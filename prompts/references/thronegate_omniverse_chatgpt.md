The vault door opened because Blake put his hand on the wrong stone.

He had been trying to steady himself in the dark. That was all. The tunnel under the red cliffs had no business existing beneath a trade road, and the old survey map he had bought in a market on Veyre Station had no business being right. Yet there he was, thirty-eight years old, knees dusty, a lamp trembling in his fist, his mutton chops wet with cave sweat, staring at a slab of black rock cut so smoothly it made the rest of the ruin look chewed. Behind him, far back in the throat of the tunnel, Anna called his name. Her voice came thin through the stone. Their children were with her, Josh trying to act older than twelve and failing, Rhea asking in a whisper whether dead kings still had teeth.

“Don’t move,” Blake called back.

He didn’t mean it as a dramatic thing. He never spoke that way. He was a merchant. Honest goods. Clear prices. No tricks. He said things the way men cut rope. Straight. “Floor’s uneven. Give me a breath.”

His palm rested on a circle carved into the slab, seven interlocked lines around a notch shaped like a crown. He had seen that pattern before. Not in any book he remembered buying. Not on any coin in circulation. Just once, years ago, on the underside of a cedar box his mother kept wrapped in cloth in the back of a cupboard. He had traced it as a boy and gotten slapped for prying. Strange that he remembered that now. Stranger still that his thumb found the notch without looking and pressed.

The stone answered.

Not with grinding. Not with dust. The door became absence so clean it hurt to look at. One heartbeat it was there, black and cold and solid. The next heartbeat it was not there at all, and a chamber waited beyond it with the stillness of a held knife. Blake felt the hair rise on his forearms. The lamp flame bent inward, toward the room, as if the dark beyond had started breathing.

Anna came up beside him anyway because she never listened when fear tried to make decisions for her. She tucked Rhea behind her skirt and put a hand on Josh’s shoulder. “What did you do?”

“Touched it.”

“That’s comforting.”

“I know.”

The chamber beyond was round and large enough to swallow a manor whole. Columns climbed into shadow. A ring of silver channels cut through the floor and met at a dais in the center. The air smelled wrong. Not stale. Not rotten. Preserved. Like linen sealed in cedar for generations. Blake lifted the lamp, and the light reached a figure lying on a slab in the middle of that impossible room.

The man looked forty-five. He looked built by a kingdom that expected its king to stand in the front rank when walls broke. Broad chest. Thick shoulders. Arms heavy with old labor and old war. Short hair. A neatly trimmed salt-and-pepper beard. Even asleep, or dead, or whatever this was, he looked not merely handsome but finished, as if someone had gone back over the shape of him and refused to leave a flaw. The face should have been softened by peace in death. It was not. It held a pressure under the skin. A waiting.

Anna saw the sword first, laid across the man’s folded hands. “Blake.”

“I see it.”

“No,” she said. “Look at the metal.”

The blade was not rusted. It was not dusty. It was not even old. It held a color Blake could not name, silver one moment and then black in the same breath, as though light kept changing its mind after touching it. On the walls behind the dais, faint lines had begun to glow. The same sevenfold knot from the door. The same crown. Josh made a small sound through his nose, not quite fear, not quite wonder, and then, before Blake could stop him, the boy said, “It’s a sanctuary.”

Blake looked at him. “How would you know that?”

Josh blinked. “I don’t. It just… sounded right.”

The man on the slab opened his eyes.

There was no waking in it. No groan. No sharp inhale. His eyes opened the way dawn opens over a field, all at once and everywhere. The lamp went out. Anna grabbed Rhea and Josh in the dark. Blake stood very still because every part of his body had become aware of itself at the same time, each finger, each tooth, each pulse in the throat. Then light returned, not from the lamp but from the air. The room filled with a low white glow, enough to show the man sitting up on the slab with the sword still across his hands.

He looked at Blake first.

Not past him. Not through him. At him. Warmth touched that terrible face so briefly Blake thought he imagined it.

Then the man stood, and the universe learned he was awake.

The chamber did not shake. Stone did not crack. Nothing theatrical happened. That made it worse. Blake felt something move through existence too large for nerves to carry. Not a sound, not a wind, not an impact. More like every locked door in creation opening at once where nobody could see them. For one impossible instant Blake knew, with the clean certainty men reserve for pain, that if this stranger wanted the sky gone the sky would simply stop. He knew the seas outside their planet, the suns beyond them, the nameless reaches past those suns, all of it, all the layered vastness people used to make themselves feel small in respectable ways, had just become fragile around this one waking man.

The stranger stepped off the slab barefoot. “Blake.”

Blake’s mouth dried. “You know me.”

“I know everything that lives beneath the current arrangement of stars.” The voice was deep and steady and had once belonged to a man who settled disputes at a table with both hands open. It belonged to something else now. “And more than stars.”

Anna tightened her hold on the children. “Who are you?”

The man turned to her and bowed his head just enough to be courteous. “Godric.”

He spoke the name like it had been buried with cities.

Rhea, eight years old, peered around her mother’s arm. “Are you a king?”

Godric looked at her a moment. Then he said, “I was. I am again.”

He closed his eyes, and Blake saw the shape of grief pass through his face so old and deep it had worn grooves into whatever he had become. When Godric opened them again, the warmth was gone. The room felt colder though the air had not changed. He turned his head a fraction, as if listening to something too large for mortals to hear, and Blake knew without understanding how that the man had just examined the whole age outside these ruins. The kingdoms. The fleets. The languages. The bloodlines. The lies history had told over ten thousand years to make room for itself.

“Thronegate,” Godric said softly.

He smiled then, and Blake wished he had not. It was not wild. Wild men can be managed. It was calm and final, the smile of a judge who has already seen the sentence carried out. “They forgot Olympus.”

No one spoke. The silver channels in the floor glimmered around his feet.

Godric lifted his sword, though he did not seem to need it, and the light in the chamber bent away from the blade. “Blake. You will come with me.”

It was not a command delivered sharply. It was simple. Certain. As if Godric had reached into Blake’s life and found the thread that mattered most.

Anna said, “My husband goes nowhere unless I know why.”

Godric looked at her again, and something changed for the better. “Because he opened the door Thorin died to close poorly. Because his face was the first kind face I saw after ten thousand years of dark. Because among all the names now alive, his is the one I choose.” He turned back to Blake. “And because in three days I will stand before the king of Thronegate and ask him to surrender the throne his line stole in blood.”

Blake swallowed. “You know their king.”

“Robert. Twenty-two. Proud because he inherited machinery he thinks is merit. He has three days.”

Josh stared up at him, trying hard not to seem afraid. “If he says no?”

Godric’s gaze moved to the boy. For a heartbeat the terrible smile returned. “Then he will learn what patience can grow in a grave.”

The chamber smelled suddenly of rain on hot stone. Blake heard Anna breathing. He heard Rhea’s small fingers rubbing the cloth of her mother’s sleeve. He heard his own heart. Godric heard all of it and more. Blake knew that too. He should have run. A sane man would have gathered his family and fled back up the tunnel until the world made sense again. But the stranger’s voice when he had said Thorin had snagged on something inside Blake, some old family silence, some habit of keeping objects wrapped and names unspoken. And under the fear there was another thing, a hard plain honesty he had lived by his whole life. This man had been wronged. Terribly. Beyond repair.

“All right,” Blake said.

Anna looked at him as if he had lost a section of his skull. “Blake.”

He did not look away from Godric. “All right,” he repeated. “But my family stays under my eye.”

“They stay under mine,” Godric said. “No harm will ever touch them while I draw existence around me.”

It should have sounded arrogant. Instead it sounded like weather announcing itself.

Far above them, deep in the galaxy that had forgotten one murdered kingdom and the man it had buried half alive, transmission arrays woke in every Thronegate system at once. Screens in palaces, barracks, mining stations, nursery wards, senate halls, gambling dens, prison blocks, chapel vaults, and war docks bloomed white. The image that appeared on all of them showed a broad-shouldered man with a salt-and-pepper beard standing in a buried chamber no one could identify. Blake stood to one side of him, pale and rigid, still holding a dead lamp. Anna held the children behind him. Godric spoke once, and every speaker in the galaxy gave his voice the same weight.

“Robert of Thronegate,” he said. “Your kingdom murdered my wife Reina, my daughter Isabel on the day she was to be wed, my daughter Hebe at fifteen, my friend Thorin, and every soul under my roof. Abdicate in three days or bring me all the strength your civilization can gather. If I defeat it, the throne is mine. If you prevail, I will kneel and let you cut my head from my neck.”

Then the transmission ended.

No one in the galaxy laughed.

Long before Thronegate had become a spread of planets and engines and obedient stars, Olympus had smelled of bread and lamp oil and horses in the winter court. That was how Godric remembered it in the first clean instant after waking, before rage rushed back in. Bread. Oil. Horse sweat. His daughters’ voices running ahead of him down a hall.

Ten thousand years earlier, on the morning Isabel was to be wed, the palace had been dressed in white silk and green boughs. Servants carried bowls of crushed mint through the corridors to sweeten the air. Musicians tuned in the lower court. Outside the western walls, the sea hit the black rocks below Olympus in patient blows that sounded like deep applause. Godric had been forty-five then and every inch a king his people could trust to lift a beam with them if a roof caved in. He was handsome in the open way that made men straighten their shoulders and women smile before they meant to. The years had thickened him in the chest, in the arms, in the neck. His beard showed silver at the chin and along the jaw, trimmed neat because Reina teased him if he let it grow wild. His hair was cut short. When he laughed, he laughed with his whole body.

On that morning he laughed often.

He stood with Thorin in the robing chamber, struggling into ceremonial blue that did not fit his shoulders unless two servants pulled the seams into obedience. Thorin, court wizard by inheritance and temperament, stood by the window in grey robes muttering over a golden clasp. He was thinner than Godric, dark-eyed, with clever hands that always looked as though invisible ink had dried on them. His family had served Olympus so long that their names were worked into the same old records as flood years and coronations.

“You look like a slaughterhouse pretending to be delicate,” Thorin said.

Godric grinned. “I’m officiating. Delicate is the point.”

“Then stop flexing at the fabric.”

“I’m breathing.”

“You breathe like a siege ram.”

Godric threw back his head and laughed. “Reina said the same.”

Thorin glanced up. “Reina says many accurate things.”

A servant girl entered with the clasp, failed to hide her smile at the sight of the king wrestling silk, and bowed herself nearly in half. Godric thanked her by name, which startled her more than the kingly robes did. He did things like that. Remembered names. Asked after fathers with bad knees. Sat with old men in courtyards and let children climb him as if he were part wall. Warmth came easily to him then. Kindness did too. His people loved him because he was powerful. They loved him more because he was not cold.

When he reached the upper garden where the ceremony had been prepared, Reina was waiting beside the cedar arch. The wind moved the dark hair at her temples. She wore green shot through with silver thread and the only jewels she liked, a pair of moonstone drops Godric had once traded three horses and a silver helm to get from a southern caravan because she had admired them and then refused to say so again. She was smiling at Isabel, who stood between maids with her wedding veil undone and a look on her face halfway between courage and panic.

Godric stopped walking.

Reina saw him and smirked. “Well?”

He put a hand to his chest. “You three have conspired to kill me.”

Fifteen-year-old Hebe, sharp as flint and never able to let a moment stay solemn, said, “Good. Then I get the hawks.”

“You get none of the hawks,” Godric said. “You forgot to feed the smallest one last week.”

“I did not forget. I delegated.”

“Thorin heard that, and now he is going to lecture you about word choice.”

Thorin arrived behind Godric with the ceremonial clasp finally in place and said, right on cue, “Delegation is just noble language for negligence when birds are involved.”

Isabel laughed, nervous and bright. She was twenty-two and looked so much like Reina that sometimes the resemblance startled Godric in doorways. She caught his sleeve as he came to her. “Father, is it obvious I’m afraid?”

“Yes,” he said.

She stared at him.

He kissed her forehead. “And beautiful. And ready. Fear is not rot, Isabel. It’s proof you understand the size of a promise.”

Reina rolled her eyes. “He saves his decent lines for the children.”

“I save nothing. I am generous by nature.”

“You are vain by nature.”

“Both can live in one man.”

Hebe made a gagging sound. Thorin snorted. The musicians below struck the first notes to summon the guests, soft strings rising through the garden. Godric looked at his wife, his daughters, his friend, and felt the thick warm fullness of a life built properly. The cedar boughs smelled green and sharp in the sun. Bees drifted above the wall flowers. Gold flashed on armor in the lower courtyard as guards took their positions. Beyond the cliffs, the sea kept speaking in patient blows.

When the first screaming began, it came from below the western gate.

At first it seemed part of the day, a horse gone wild, a dropped crate, the ordinary disorder around ceremony. Then the second scream tore higher and longer, and all the birds in the upper garden burst from the hedges at once. Godric turned toward the sound. Thorin did too. They met each other’s eyes in the same instant the ground jumped under their feet.

Not thunder. Impact.

A second later the western tower broke inward in a bloom of black fire.

The world became fragments moving too fast to fit thought. Guards shouting. Guests dropping cups and veils. A rain of hot stone. From beyond the walls came a metallic howl so huge it rattled teeth. Something hit the lower court with a force that punched the air from the upper garden. Women fell. Musicians went down under splintered wood. Godric was already moving. He ripped the ceremonial cloak off his shoulders and dragged Isabel behind the cedar arch as flaming debris came over the wall.

“Inside,” he roared.

Below them, Olympus was opening like a wound. The gates had not merely fallen. They had been atomized. Through the boiling gap came soldiers in armor Godric had never seen, dark plates seamless as insect shell, helms without visors, standards bearing the crown-mark of neighboring Thronegate. Behind them rolled engines that spat white heat. Above them, shapes moved across the sky too fast for birds, black wings and iron underbellies. Thronegate had come with devices generations ahead of anything Olympus knew, and they had come on a wedding day when half the guard was ceremonial.

Reina shoved Hebe toward the inner stairs. “Run.”

Hebe did not run. She grabbed a fallen spear from a dead guard and set her feet with both hands on it, face gone white and hard. Isabel had torn the veil from her head and was trying to pull an older aunt from under a broken bench. Godric drew the sword he had worn only for ritual and found real weight in it all at once.

“Take them!” he shouted to Thorin.

Thorin’s mouth was bloodless. “The lower passages may already be cut off.”

“Then make one!”

Another impact hit the palace. Columns in the gallery below them snapped. The cedar arch toppled behind Isabel in a crash of branches and silk. Guards rushed up from the inner court and were cut apart by a line of Thronegate soldiers who came over the wall on hooked cables, fast and silent until their blades started working. Godric met them halfway down the garden steps. He split the first at the collarbone. He smashed the second’s face into the stone rail hard enough to leave teeth there. Warm blood slicked the steps. Somebody behind him was praying. Somebody else was vomiting.

He heard Reina scream his name.

He turned in time to see a shaft of white fire punch through her chest.

There are pains that have edges and center and can be named. That was not one of them. For a moment the whole garden narrowed to the black hole burned through the woman he loved. Reina looked down as if surprised by the light inside her. Then her knees gave way. Godric moved, but Isabel moved first. She reached her mother just as the second strike came from below. It took Isabel across the throat. The sound it made was small, almost domestic, like cloth tearing in a quiet room. She fell against Reina in a folding spill of white wedding silk and blood.

Hebe made a noise no father should hear from a child. She charged the stair with the spear lowered. She was brave enough to shame old soldiers. One of the Thronegate men looked almost amused when he raised his weapon. Hebe’s body hit the wall so hard the spear shattered.

After that, whatever in Godric had once been warm cracked open and let something black through.

He killed without sequence. Without memory. Men died around him in sprays, in wet heaps, in pieces on stone. He did not feel the cuts that opened his arms and side. He did not hear Thorin shouting until the wizard seized him by the shoulders and dragged his face around.

“Godric!”

Everything behind Thorin was fire now. The upper garden was gone. Guests lay in shapes that no longer resembled people. Reina, Isabel, Hebe did not move. Godric looked at them and the last gentle thing in him burned to ash.

Thorin saw it happen. His own face changed with grief. Then it changed again into decision.

“There is one chance,” he said.

“No.”

“You’ll listen.”

“I said no.” Godric tried to turn back toward the dead, toward the soldiers, toward anything he could kill. Thorin slapped him. Hard. Godric blinked.

“My blood knows a forbidden passage,” Thorin said. The words came fast, breathless, precise. “A teleportation rite passed from court wizard to court wizard. Never written. Never shared. It was meant for the last king if the line failed. I cannot take others. Only you. And if I begin, you do not break the circle. Do you hear me?”

Godric stared at him.

“Do you hear me?”

Another blast tore through the wall behind them. Stone rained down. Thronegate troops were pouring into the upper levels now. Thorin shoved Godric into the half-collapsed chapel at the edge of the garden, where the floor mosaics still held. He cut his own palm with a knife and smeared blood across the old tiles in curves and knots older than Olympus itself. The symbols flared gold. Godric stood inside the ring because Thorin had ordered it and because the alternative was to die with blood on his boots and no reckoning left to do.

From outside came the pounding boots of invaders. The choking smoke. The hiss of fire on wet stone. Godric kept seeing Isabel falling in her wedding silk. Hebe against the wall. Reina looking down at the wound in astonishment. His mouth was open but no sound came.

Thorin began the chant.

The language was not one Godric knew, though he knew many. It sounded like light being broken into pieces. The ring around Godric brightened. The blood on the tiles lifted into the air in threads. Thorin’s hands shook as he shaped the last turns of it, fingers cutting signs so fast they blurred.

The first Thronegate soldier reached the chapel door. Godric half moved. Thorin snarled, “Stay.”

The soldier fired. Thorin twisted. The bolt should have taken Godric through the eye. It entered Thorin beneath the ribs. The wizard gasped, but the chant did not stop. More troops crowded the doorway. Another bolt struck Thorin high in the shoulder. Then a spear came through behind it and punched out between his back and chest in a wet burst.

Thorin sagged. His mouth kept working. Blood ran from between his teeth onto the gold light.

“Thorin,” Godric said. His voice sounded raw, scraped hollow.

Thorin lifted one shaking hand. Not to beg. To complete.

The last word came out broken.

The ring closed wrong.

For one exact instant all movement left Godric’s body. Not numbness. Removal. His age, forty-five years and all the slow loyal work they had done upon him, stopped there forever. The need for sleep vanished first. Then the need for breath. Then hunger. Then thirst. His heart did not cease; it became irrelevant. Time still touched him, but it could no longer spend him.

In the same instant his strength multiplied by a million and by itself, then did so again in the next second, and again in the next, and again without mercy or end. Endurance, speed, senses, wisdom, intelligence, every measurable and unmeasurable faculty of being swelled on the same hideous curve. Knowledge detonated through him. Not acquired knowledge, not learned through line and patience. Immediate knowledge. The shape of every star. The speech of unborn civilizations. The final words of men not yet dead. The hidden chambers beneath cities not yet built. Yet at the center of that infinite opening there remained one locked place, one absence where cause should have been. He knew all creation and not why this had chosen him.

A second passed.

A new power appeared in him whole and mature, potent enough at birth to erase a civilization before it knew its own name, and that power began multiplying by a million and by itself with the same relentless hunger as everything else. It was concealment. Perfect, absolute restraint. The ability to fold his presence so completely that reality itself would not split around him. Without it, the chapel, the mountain, the world, and what lay beyond the world would have gone with his first awakening breath he no longer needed.

A second passed.

Another power arrived. Then another. Then another. Each catastrophic. Each growing on the same unspeakable curve. The means to still stars mid-burn. To turn thought into ash in the skull that housed it. To deny momentum. To command plague from silence. To peel time back from flesh and leave the memory walking. They came every second. They never stopped.

Outside the broken rite, the spear pinned Thorin to the chapel floor. His eyes found Godric’s one last time. There was apology there. And loyalty. And something like relief.

Then the spell tore.

Godric vanished from the chapel in a clean vertical line of white so narrow it left the stone around it untouched. The Thronegate soldiers surged through the doorway into empty light. One of them stepped inside the fading ring and burst apart without anyone ever knowing why.

The sanctuary caught Godric in dark miles beneath the world. Stasis took him before the scream could leave his mouth. But the multiplication did not sleep. The powers did not sleep. The knowledge did not sleep. They piled upon him second by second by second for ten thousand years while kingdoms rose, while languages split, while maps changed shape and forgot him.

And because the new thing he had become admitted no weakness, the universe developed a savage courtesy around that fact. The first time, centuries into his burial, a scholar-king on a distant moon meditated upon rumors of a sleeping force below reality and wondered, idly, whether anything so vast must hide a seam. The thought had not yet reached full language in his mind when Godric’s amplification lurched into an even steeper climb. In that same unborn fraction of intention, the very notion of that imagined seam ceased to have meaning against him. Simultaneously, somewhere below all worlds, mastery of the scholar’s half-formed imagined exploit settled into Godric as naturally as a hand settling around a familiar cup. The scholar never knew what he had done. He only died years later under an ordinary rain, convinced the stars had begun staring back harder than before.

This happened again and again across ages. A machine mind in a crystal nebula considered whether concealment implied strain. A saint in a dead language dreamed of binding an invincible thing with its own memory. A war council in a bronze universe speculated over wine that any god must rely on sequence and therefore on interruption. Each thought, before maturing into plan, fed the sleeping king. Each false path hardened into permanent immunity. Each proposed wound became another weapon in Godric’s invisible hand. Sometimes, after he woke, he would permit such fools to complete their useless designs for the brief entertainment of hearing terror reshape a voice.

Three days after the challenge, the whole of Thronegate came to kill one man.

They chose the Plain of Nareth because satellites could see it from orbit and because there were no cities near enough to threaten with collateral devastation. Robert announced that publicly, hoping to sound noble. In private he had selected it because the old plain was broad and barren and far from the capital, and because something in him wanted all this impossible business kept at a distance from the palace where he still slept like a frightened child after issuing brave statements.

By sunrise the plain was gone beneath armies.

Blake stood with Anna and the children on a rise of basalt three miles from the center line, inside a circle of protection Godric had drawn with nothing visible and everything final. The grass underfoot was wet with dawn. The air smelled of engine fuel, dragon musk, charged metal, and the bitter clean sting of military sorcery. Above the horizon, troop carriers descended in black swarms. Their lights flashed in grids. Long columns of armor ground into position with the sound of continents dragging. Billions of super-soldiers in reactive plate filled the plain rank after rank until the human eye gave up on counting. War mages walked between them trailing banners of blue flame. Behind those came the robot legions, perfect chrome anatomies in serried lines so straight they looked printed onto the world. Then the dragons descended, hundreds first, then thousands, scales plated in gunmetal and gold, throats hot enough to level continents, wings beating storms into the dust. Last came the time-ninjas, almost unseen until they were seen wrong, flickering in and out along the edge of perception, fast enough to reverse local causality, fast enough to arrive a moment before they had left.

Anna stared across it all and said, “That is not an army. That is a bad idea made physical.”

Blake could not answer. His tongue had stuck to the roof of his mouth.

At the center of the plain, a single black chair had been placed on the bare earth.

Godric sat in it.

No armor. No crown. No cloak. Bareheaded under the pale morning sky, hands resting on the pommel of his sword with the point planted in the dirt between his boots. From where Blake stood, Godric might have been waiting for a late dinner guest. He did not move. Not once. Not a shift. Not a tilt of the neck. Not a tightening of the hand.

The army saw that and laughed.

The sound carried strangely over the flat land, millions of men making themselves brave together. Loudspeakers boomed Robert’s voice from command ships overhead. He was somewhere safe and shielded, of course. “Unknown claimant. You were offered honorable combat and have chosen spectacle. Thronegate will now demonstrate the cost of insolence.”

“Honorable combat,” Blake muttered. “With all that.”

Josh, face pale and rigid, said, “He’s afraid.”

“Yes,” Godric said, though he was three miles away and had not moved.

The first attack was ceremonial. Twelve champion lancers rode out on gene-forged horses in armor bright as mirrors, pennants snapping behind them. Their squadron leader lowered his lance and shouted his name to the sky. Godric sat. The horses reached full charge.

Then all twelve riders arrived at a point five feet before the black chair and ceased existing in the arrangement they had known. No flash. No impact. They simply lost the right to continue as bodies. Horses, riders, lances, armor, banners, all of it came apart into a red vapor so fine the wind took it at once. The plain fell quiet for a blink.

A second later the whole army opened fire.

The sound crushed the air flat. Rail batteries drew white scars across the morning. Siege cannons on the rear lines launched shells large as houses. Mage companies raised both hands and sent down cataracts of sun-hot force. Dragons exhaled walls of continent-breaking flame. The robot legions loosed clouds of needles, smart mines, and self-copying kill swarms. The time-ninjas vanished, reappeared, vanished again, running backward through seconds to cut the king before the command had even been given.

The center of the plain disappeared under violence.

Blake felt the heat on his face despite the protection around his family. The ridge shook. Dust rose in pillars. The sky went dark with debris. Rhea buried her face in Anna’s side. Anna kept one arm around each child and stared without blinking, jaw locked hard enough to show the tendons in her neck.

It went on for eleven minutes.

For eleven full minutes Thronegate emptied its imagination into the place where Godric sat. The plain blistered. Rock liquefied. Air caught fire. Local weather died. A ring of glass spread mile by mile from the center. Several command officers in orbit, watching telemetry, smiled for the first time that day because no living thing could remain in that furnace. Robert himself stood in his flagship’s war room with both fists on the table and told himself the shaking in his hands was adrenaline and not dread.

Then the barrage ended.

At the center of a basin fifty miles wide, in a circle of untouched earth the size of a cottage room, the black chair remained.

Godric sat in it.

Not a thread of his clothing stirred.

He had not moved a single muscle.

The silence that followed was worse than the barrage. Men in the front ranks began looking sideways at one another. Dragons shifted their claws. A few of the super-soldiers laughed again, but the sound broke in the middle. The time-ninjas reappeared far behind their intended mark, masks wet inside. One of them tore his own cowl off and vomited.

Godric spoke, and the words reached every ear without speakers. “That was your confidence.”

Then he began to answer.

He did not stand. He did not lift a finger. He did not even blink. Yet throughout the army things started happening that no doctrine had prepared anyone to watch.

In the western mechanized divisions, every robot turned its head toward the human officers who had commanded it and realized obedience could be inverted. Chrome hands closed on warm throats. Needle-launchers rotated inward. Tracks chewed through trenches filled with their own support crews. Men screamed orders that drowned beneath metal feet.

In the eastern dragon flights, the flame sacs of the first rank cooled into stone mid-breath. Their fire backed up inside them, and their chests burst open with a muffled succession of internal thunderclaps. The second rank banked in panic and found the air too heavy to hold them. They dropped. Scales hit the ground like thrown shields. Wings snapped. Riders were hurled in sprays over the glass plain. The third rank tried to climb above whatever unseen pressure was crushing their kin and discovered altitude had become a wall.

The war mages attempted countermeasures. Blake saw them from miles away, staffs raised, mouths wide in working. Blue geometries opened over their heads. Entire schools of lethal art struck Godric at once: soul-rend, memory decay, dimensional flensing, blood ignition. Each one failed before reaching him. Failed so completely that the spells looked embarrassed in the dying. Some guttered into steam. Some curved delicately aside and found the caster who had formed them. One company of archmages was reduced to blind flesh the instant they conceived of attacking his mind, each man clutching at his own face and begging for darkness.

The time-ninjas made their run.

They were elite even among absurdities, bred and trained in monasteries built around wound clocks and causality wells. They could step half a second left of the world and return with a throat already cut. They spread into a lattice around Godric’s chair, vanished, and attacked him from twelve converging versions of the immediate past.

Every single one of them stopped one inch from his skin.

They hung there in the air, feet not touching ground, blades fixed, bodies straining with impossible speed that now had nowhere to go. Their eyes moved under their masks. That was the awful part. They were conscious. Godric left them that much. One by one he robbed them of forward time. Not all time. Only enough that their bodies began aging in jerks while they remained suspended. Cloth rotted first. Then leather. Then skin shrank across jaw and knuckles. Hair went white under their cowls. Teeth loosened. Some tried to scream and had no moisture left for it. A minute later they were collapsing heaps of old bone in black cloth. A heartbeat after that the bones reversed, became infants of ash, and blew away.

Blake heard Josh whisper, “He’s holding back.”

Anna looked at him sharply. “How can you tell?”

Josh’s hands were shaking. “Because if he wasn’t, I think the sky would be gone.”

That was the moment morale broke.

Not when the champions vanished. Not when the barrage failed. Not when the ninjas turned to dust. It broke when the front infantry line, those billions of enhanced men who had marched onto the plain with jokes and songs and cameras in their helms, understood in one shared animal instant that the seated figure before them was not fighting. He was selecting. He was choosing how much of them to permit to remain long enough to feel it.

The first ranks tried to retreat. Orders from the rear shoved more men forward. Panic folded the line inward. Some fired blindly at anything near them. Others dropped weapons and ran. Overhead, command ships began maneuvering for orbital strikes. Godric noticed. The ships turned around in the sky and rammed one another nose-first with the precision of courteous docking. Metal flowered outward in white debris. Burning hulks fell into the plain and crushed thousands.

Robert’s voice returned to the speakers then, shrill now, stripped of kingly polish. “Advance! Advance, damn you! He is one man!”

Godric’s answer was simple. Every speaker, every comm link, every private channel, every neural implant in every soldier in Thronegate began playing the sounds of Olympus dying. Screams in a wedding garden. Stone collapsing. A woman gasping once around a hole in her chest. A girl falling in silk. A younger girl hitting a wall. Thorin choking on blood while he finished a broken spell. Godric did not narrate it. He made them hear. Billions heard the murder their ancestors had built themselves upon, and because his omniscience was exact, each listener heard it from the angle most likely to destroy composure.

Men dropped to their knees with their palms over their ears and could not block it. Dragons reared until riders spilled. Generals sobbed in armored command capsules. One whole legion tore off helmets, then scalp, trying to scratch the noise out.

Blake stood frozen on the ridge. Anna’s face had gone white as fresh paper. Tears ran down Rhea’s cheeks though she made no sound. Godric had promised their protection, and he kept it. They heard nothing but wind.

Then the killing became personal.

Godric looked across the plain, and lines of soldiers opened from sternum to spine without visible blade. Bodies stayed standing for a second, puzzled, before they remembered gravity. He let a full battalion keep consciousness after removing their shadows. Men screamed and crawled after shapes that would never return to their heels. He commanded a squadron of continent-burner dragons to remember every city they had ever destroyed, and the memory came on them physically, masonry and smoke and screaming civilians packed into their skulls until they went mad and bit through their own throats. A regiment of elite super-soldiers had their pain receptors magnified past sanity while their injuries remained small, a scrape becoming an apocalypse under the skin. They rolled on the glass plain begging for mothers dead for generations.

The cruelty of it sickened Blake. That mattered. Because even then, even in that devastation, Godric heard the revulsion in Blake’s silence and adjusted. The next wave died faster. Cleaner. Still utterly. But faster.

By the twentieth minute the field was no longer a battle. It was proof. Proof laid down in miles. The confidence that had arrived in banners and chants had become scattered clusters of men hiding behind the corpses of machines, behind dead dragons, behind each other. Whole units were surrendering to empty air. A general with half his face gone crawled toward Godric’s chair dragging a white flag made from the lining of his own coat. Godric glanced at him once, and the man lived. Others saw that and tried the same. Those who surrendered were spared. Those who attacked continued vanishing.

By the twenty-sixth minute no organized resistance remained.

The billions were dead, broken, or kneeling. The unmatched magical corps were silent. The deadliest robot armies in existence lay in smoking heaps or stood obediently still with blood on their hands that was not their own. The dragons twitched in piles vast enough to alter the horizon. The time-ninjas were dust mixed with glass. Above all of it, in a circle of untouched earth at the center of the plain, Godric still sat in his black chair without having moved a single muscle.

He looked toward Blake across the miles.

“Take your family home,” he said.

Blake heard himself answer, because not answering would have been a kind of lie. “I don’t think there is a home now that looks like before.”

Godric’s face changed. Not much. Enough. The old fatherliness flickered there for one bare second, rusted but not dead. “No,” he said. “There is not.”

Two days later Robert was found dead in his private chamber, wrists opened in the royal bath. The warm water had gone pink and then red and then thin again by the time servants broke the doors. Godric did not visit the body. He walked into the throne room of Thronegate’s capital wearing plain black, Blake at his right shoulder and Anna with the children in the gallery above, and he set the old crown of Thronegate on his own head with hands steady as iron.

No one objected.

What came next would have sounded like blasphemy if it had failed. Godric named it the Awakening.

He stood on the palace steps in evening light while the city below packed itself full of citizens. Men pressed shoulder to shoulder in the squares. Women filled balconies and window ledges. Children sat on roofs. The air smelled of rain, torch smoke, and the iron tang that always lingers over a capital after regime change. Godric spoke without amplification, and everyone heard him anyway.

“Thronegate was built by men who believed power entitled them to cruelty,” he said. “I will keep the power and remove the entitlement.”

He declared that from that day forward, every Thronegate man who lived beyond his twentieth birthday would increase in strength, endurance, intelligence, and wisdom tenfold for every day he continued to live. The gift would count backward for those already past twenty. It would not make them immortal. It would not touch women. Men could lower themselves to ordinary levels or raise themselves by will to any point beneath their full height. He said it as one lays a law into stone. Then he made it true.

The city changed before the cheering even finished.

A dockworker of fifty-six dropped to his knees in the square and then stood again with tears on his face because the ache in his back was gone and because he could feel, with horror and delight together, that if he forgot himself he might lift a building. A clerk of twenty-three looked at his own hands as equations he had never been taught unfolded in his mind like clean banners. Men staggered. Laughed. Wept. Some grew quiet and put both hands over their hearts as if listening to something inside them becoming immense.

Blake, thirty-eight then, went very still.

Anna noticed before anyone else. “Blake?”

He blinked and looked at his hands. “I’m all right.”

He was more than all right. The flagstones under his boots had begun to craze in thin lines. A bronze rail beside him bowed where his fingers had rested. He removed his hand at once and tucked it into his coat. Godric saw. Their eyes met. Nothing was said. Later, when three newly Awakened men together failed to stabilize a collapsing transit bridge and Blake stepped under its falling spine alone and held it up while families ran clear, Godric said nothing then either. He only watched with a private comprehension and, afterward, gave Blake a room in the old observatory tower where strange books kept finding their way onto the shelves.

Under Godric’s rule Thronegate changed its texture. Men revered him with a devotion so complete it was almost relief. The strongest among them, aged thirty-five, fifty, seventy-nine, could have pulverized old empires by themselves, yet before Godric they bowed like sons before a just father. Women walked the capital at midnight without fear. Assault vanished not because laws existed on paper but because Godric’s punishments were immediate and inventive and widely known. A magistrate who sold girls to a private ring was found alive and conscious within a cube of his own compressed greed, every coin he had taken fused into his flesh. A warlord on a frontier moon beat his wife once, only once, and the next morning woke unable to raise his hand higher than his own shame while the memory of her bruises appeared on his skin every hour. Women loved Godric for the safety. Men loved him for the strength. Children loved him because he listened when they spoke and because he never lied to them.

Only Blake saw how hard the kindness had to fight its way back in.

At first Godric’s mercy was technical. Surrender and live. Resist and die. Efficient. Brutal. Blake followed him through the first years like a man walking beside a furnace he trusted not to turn. He gave honest counsel because that was the only kind he had. “That was too much,” he would say after an execution stretched for theatre. Or, “You already won. Don’t play with it.” Nobody else on any planet would have dared. Godric never punished him. Sometimes he looked at Blake with an expression so old it ached and said, “I know.” Other times he said nothing and the next conquered city found itself spared a spectacle.

Anna understood before Blake did what he had become to the king. “He likes you because you don’t worship him,” she said one night in the tower while rain tapped the leaded windows and Josh snored over an open book at the table.

Blake shook his head. “Everyone worships him.”

“No,” Anna said. “They fear him, trust him, love him, need him. That’s different. You speak to him like a man. He remembers being one when you do.”

Josh, head on folded arms, mumbled half asleep, “Court wizard.”

Blake smiled faintly. “Go to bed.”

Josh lifted his head enough to reveal ink all over his fingers and a page dense with copied sigils he should not have understood and somehow did. “I’m not tired.”

Rhea, older now and cutting dried fruit with a scholar’s neat hands, said, “You’ve been saying that since noon.”

Godric’s expansion beyond Thronegate’s galaxy was not announced as conquest. It began as mercy. Every new universe they reached received the same offer first: surrender unconditionally, keep your customs, keep your cities, keep your dead buried where you buried them, and live under Thronegate’s law. Refuse, and be subjugated. Many refused because pride survives first contact with terror more often than sense does.

The first famous refusal came from the Brass Hymn, a universe of cathedral stars and machine choirs that had been preparing for invasion for five years before the offer arrived. They had heard of neighboring cosmoses going dark. They had seen refugees at the membrane between realities, half mad with stories of men from Thronegate who could pull moons apart with careful hands. The Brass Hymn mobilized everything. Planetary shields woven from singing metal. Armadas grown inside suns. Priests who could sanctify space until enemy matter blistered on entry. Their emperor sent back a message dressed in gold mathematics and scripture. They would not kneel.

Godric was in a poor mood that morning because a provincial governor had lied to him about grain and because the lie had insulted him more than the theft. He read the refusal, sighed, and set down his cup.

The Brass Hymn ceased resistance in less than a fraction of a second.

Godric did not travel there physically. He remained in Thronegate’s palace garden, one hand on the back of a stone bench, while winter sunlight touched the silver in his beard. Somewhere in the cathedral universe, every shield turned transparent to hostile intent. Every sun-born armada discovered that heat could remember cold and shatter under the recollection. The choirs opened their mouths for war and inhaled silence so absolute their lungs forgot how to reverse it. On the imperial world, the emperor saw the sky fill with his own defenses collapsing inward in bright slow ruin. Then the message of surrender he had rejected appeared already signed in his private seal, as though history had corrected him out of courtesy. By the time his knees hit the floor, the conquest was complete.

The second refusal came from the Sable Orchard, whose defenders believed preparation could humiliate prophecy. They had three years of warning. Their scientists folded planets into fortress seeds. Their witches planted living minefields in the dark between systems. Their champions drank distilled beasts and became mountains wearing skin. Godric did not go. He sent four men, all older than thirty-five, all kind in the way steady fathers are kind.

There was Taron, fifty-one, who baked sweet bread for his daughters every second rest day. Emil, thirty-nine, who wrote poems he never showed anyone. Cassian, forty-four, whose wife ran a clinic and made him remove his boots at the door. Oren, sixty-three, who apologized to furniture after bumping it. They stood in the transfer hall adjusting plain uniforms while an officer briefed them on the Orchard’s kill ratios and apocalyptic engines. None of the four interrupted. When the officer finished, Taron asked, “Will they surrender after a demonstration?”

“If they’re wise,” Blake said from the side of the room.

Oren rubbed the bridge of his nose. “And if not?”

“Then do what the king asked.”

They entered the Sable Orchard at dawn local time. Thirty minutes had been budgeted. They needed twenty-one.

The Orchard’s first planetary shield shattered when Emil touched it with one finger and asked it, gently, to stop pretending. Their minefields bloomed and died against Taron’s chest without marking the cloth. Cassian let three mountain-champions strike him because he disliked hurting people who had not exhausted their options; on the fourth blow he exhaled and all three men lay in a valley of their own footprints, alive but unable to command their knees ever again. Oren walked through the central war garden while vines bred to eat armies coiled around his boots and wilted, overwhelmed by the simple fact of his continued advance. The defenders had prepared for monsters, brutes, berserkers, divine avatars. They were not ready for polite men who kept saying, “Please stand down,” while reducing centuries of military theory to local embarrassment. By the twenty-first minute the Orchard’s regent was on the ground, face pressed to black soil, listening to Cassian explain in a patient voice that Thronegate would not extend the Awakening to them and that resistance now would only raise funeral costs.

The third refusal came from the Mirror Vaults, a universe run by intellects who had no gods and trusted only iteration. They had watched six neighboring realities fall and spent two full years refining a defense lattice so intricate it occupied eleven dimensions and predicted enemy action before the enemy chose it. They filed Thronegate under conqueror barbarism. They prepared equations instead of prayers.

Godric sent five men this time, each over forty, because the Vaults had no civilians concentrated in cities and surgical force would be cleaner than his boredom. Blake objected only to the youngest choice. “Marrec is thirty-six.”

“He qualifies,” Godric said.

“He also just had a son.”

Godric studied Blake a moment, then nodded once. “Replace him.”

So the squad became Taron again, Oren again, a quiet giant named Sef at forty-seven, a schoolmaster named Lin at fifty-eight, and Dagan at seventy-nine, who wore his old age with cheerful vanity and liked to say the Awakening had improved his posture more than his spirit. They entered the Mirror Vaults under white artificial light and were met by defense grids that could cut causality into ribbons. The grids fired. Nothing useful occurred. The predictive engine tried to forecast their next movements and collapsed into recursive panic because Dagan had chosen not to decide until after he had already arrived. Lin walked through a storm of anti-matter lace holding a folded surrender charter in both hands so it would not crease. Sef absorbed a moonshot array and redirected it into an empty sea because there was no need to scorch farmland. Oren dismantled the eleven-dimensional lattice by removing one support concept at a time, the way a careful man might unwind snagged thread. Taron reached the central chamber in twenty-seven minutes and found the ruling intelligences still arguing over whether they had already lost. “Yes,” he told them. “You did. Sign, please.”

Assimilated universes kept their languages, their calendars, their dead. They did not receive the Awakening. That was Thronegate’s alone. The denied gift became its own horror. Whole populations lived under the protection and taxation of a realm whose men aged into force beyond cosmological reason while they themselves remained merely whatever they had always been. They learned obedience quickly.

Resistance to Godric personally became rare not because courage disappeared from creation but because information spread. His omniscience made ambush ridiculous. His restraint made measurement impossible. His casual destructions made myth feel insufficient. Even so, sentient arrogance is renewable. Somewhere, always, there is a mind that hears impossible and translates it into challenge.

The most elaborate of those minds belonged to an external entity known in twelve doomed archives as the Curator of Knives.

It lived between universes in a region of lacquered dark where abandoned laws drifted like shed skins. It was old enough to remember creators dying and clever enough to have survived all replacements. When the Curator first studied the record of Godric, it dismissed much of it as panic-poetry. No being was without compromise. No power existed without architecture. Then it read of the hidden aura, the perfect concealment that prevented omniversal collapse, and it found what it believed to be a seam. Not in Godric’s flesh. In his restraint. If the containment could be inverted, overloaded, reflected back into itself through enough mirrored realities at once, perhaps the king’s own infinity would break him open.

The thought formed lightly. Barely more than curiosity.

And in that instant, before plan, before design, before the Curator had even selected a testing model, Godric’s amplification rate climbed again into a region language cannot survive. The imagined seam vanished from the category of possible injuries. Mastery over the reflective overload the Curator had not yet invented settled into Godric completely.

The Curator felt none of this. That was the cruelty.

It spent nine years preparing.

Nine years breeding mirror worlds with identical physical constants. Nine years seeding them with sacrificial stars. Nine years constructing a chamber whose walls were made from trapped possibilities and whose floor remembered every death by implosion ever recorded. Nine years of stolen prophets, vivisected mathematicians, chained angels, and harvested future-tense. It built a weapon called the Crown of Inversion, beautiful as frost, meant to take any force directed inward and make it rabid.

Godric knew every bolt of it. He let the work continue.

Blake learned of the Curator only when he found Godric one evening in the western observatory turning a small glass sphere between his fingers. The stars outside were still. Josh, now grown, worked at a desk copying a translation from an older script that seemed to come too easily to him. Rain clicked on the high panes.

“What is it?” Blake asked.

“An attempt,” Godric said.

“At you?”

“At my supposed necessity.”

Blake exhaled through his nose. “And you’re letting it run.”

“Yes.”

“Why?”

Godric looked at the sphere. Inside it, tiny mirrored corridors folded forever. “Because some lessons should be complete when given.”

Blake did not like that answer. He liked it less nine days later when the Curator made its move.

The ambush took place in a dead transit vein between universes, a place Thronegate used rarely because the geometry there caused ordinary navigators headaches and vivid dreams. Godric entered alone. He did that sometimes, not from recklessness but because escort implied risk transfer and there was none to transfer. The vein lit around him with mirrored suns. The Crown of Inversion descended from every direction at once, locking him inside a chamber of reflective doom built to force his hidden containment to rebound endlessly through itself. The Curator manifested outside the chamber in a body of lacquered black blades, each edge singing with stored endings.

For a fraction of a second the trap was magnificent.

Then it belonged to Godric.

He stood in the chamber and admired the craftsmanship while the Crown tried to apply a principle he had become immune to before its inventor had finished suspecting it. The reflective matrices folded inward. Not on him. On themselves. Every mirrored universe the Curator had cultivated bloomed into a clean white line and vanished. The trapped possibilities in the walls screamed as they were reduced to one. The floor forgot implosion and remembered obedience.

The Curator understood too late.

Godric looked at it through the unraveling cage. “You thought concealment implied pressure. Pressure implied vessel. Vessel implied rupture.” His voice was almost conversational. “A tidy chain.”

The Curator struck anyway, because revelation rarely cures pride fast enough. It hurled the completed exploit, the perfected inversion, the crowned reflection of infinite containment turned cannibal.

Godric used the same attack back on it with far greater elegance.

The Curator’s lacquered body split into a thousand copies, each copy becoming the chamber for the others’ destruction. It felt its own plan mastering it from the inside. Its knives turned inward. Its mirror stars fed on one another. All nine years of preparation closed like teeth around the originator. It tried to flee across discarded laws and discovered that movement itself had been denied to the route of escape it had intended. In its last coherent moment it realized the truth not as theory but as personal ruin: the first idle notion of weakness had already delivered the defeat. Everything after that had been labor performed on behalf of the man it sought to kill.

Godric let it live six seconds longer than necessary so it could fully understand.

When he returned to Thronegate, Blake was waiting in the palace court with his coat on and anger in the set of his jaw. “You could have told me.”

“I knew the outcome.”

“That’s not what I said.”

Godric stopped before him. Around them, evening bells tolled. Servants crossed the far arcade with baskets of winter citrus. The ordinary life of the palace went on under the shadow of impossible things, and Blake had learned to stand in that contrast without bowing to it.

“You were worried,” Godric said.

“Yes.”

A small smile touched Godric’s mouth, worn softer now than it had been twenty years before. “I know.”

Blake folded his arms. “Then next time say where you’re going.”

Godric considered this as if the request were weightier than all the Curator’s knives. “All right.”

That was how the redemption happened. Not cleanly. Not suddenly. In increments. In corrections. In the daily abrasions of friendship against grief too old to heal but not too old to be guided.

The warm man from Olympus did not return all at once. He returned in habits. In the way Godric began sitting in public gardens again so children could climb his knee and ask whether stars made noise. In the way he remembered birthdays among servants. In the way he let old widows complain to him about food prices and actually changed policy afterward. In the way he started laughing with his shoulders again. The cruelty remained for enemies. The ruthlessness remained for those who threatened his people. But to Thronegate itself he became what he had once been in the sea kingdom under the cedar arch: fatherly, warm, friendly, kind.

Blake was at the center of that change and never quite accepted credit. He moved from merchant to adviser to something older in outline and harder to name. The books in the observatory multiplied. So did the instruments. So did the nights he spent over old sigils with Josh leaning over one shoulder and Rhea, now a legal architect in the lower ministries, bringing tea when both of them forgot to eat. Sometimes Blake’s hands worked symbols before his mind had translated them. Sometimes a sealed room opened when he merely rested his palm against the latch. Godric watched without comment and had the ancient title restored anyway. Court wizard. Blake objected for form’s sake. Godric ignored him for practical reasons.

“You can’t just name me that,” Blake said the day the decree was drafted.

“I can. I’m the king.”

“I don’t come from a line of wizards.”

Godric’s gaze lingered on him with private amusement, as though listening to some old voice in the room Blake could not hear. “No,” he said. “Of course not.”

Years passed under absolute rule and exact peace. Present-day Thronegate spread across countless universes now, but the heart of it remained the capital where the first crown had changed heads in bloodless silence. On spring evenings the palace terraces smelled of jasmine and forge smoke from the lower districts. Women laughed on lit bridges after midnight because no hand in the realm would dare touch them wrong. Men moderated themselves down to human measure to drink in taverns without breaking tables and raised themselves again only when labor or duty required it. In the schools, boys counted the days past twenty with nervous pride. In the observatory, Josh, thirty-two and ink-stained like his father before him, instructed a circle of apprentices from old loyal families whose children kept showing uncanny patience with dead languages and forbidden knots. Nobody announced a pattern. Nobody needed to.

At sunset, from the highest balcony of the western tower, you could see the city burn gold under a hundred ordered skies. The bells of evening crossed one another in warm metal waves. Airships moved between spires with their lamps already lit. In the palace garden below, children chased each other around the black stone fountain while guards pretended not to smile.

Godric stood at the balcony rail in a dark coat, crownless, the wind moving the short hair at his temples. Twenty years had not touched his face. Forty-five forever. Handsome still. Massive still. The silver in his beard caught the last light. In profile he looked like a carved answer to an old prayer. Beside him stood Blake, fifty-eight now, shoulders thicker than they had any right to be, mutton chops flecked with grey, one hand resting on the top of a staff he claimed he did not need and plainly enjoyed carrying. Below them, Anna spoke with Rhea near the stair. Josh argued with a page over the correct pronunciation of a dead syllable and was losing graciously.

Godric watched the city and said, “They sleep easily.”

“They work hard,” Blake answered. “They’ve earned it.”

“They sleep easily because I remain.”

Blake gave him a sideways look. “That too.”

For a while they listened to the bells. Somewhere in the lower courtyards a troop of children began chanting one of the cleaner heroic songs the palace tutors preferred. Godric’s mouth twitched.

“You hate that one,” Blake said.

“It rhymes ‘majesty’ with ‘destiny’ three times.”

“Could be worse.”

“It often is.”

Blake laughed. Godric joined him, deep and real. The sound rolled out over the balcony and down into the warm evening where people looked up, saw their king laughing with his right-hand man, and smiled without fear.

Far beyond the ordered skies, beyond the conquered universes and the surrendered ones, beyond the membranes where reality changed flavor and law, the omniverse remained under Godric’s hand. No enemy approached without his knowing. No false thought of weakness formed anywhere without feeding the abyssal multiplication sleeping beneath his calm. He was still the most terrible thing creation had ever failed to contain. But on the balcony above Thronegate, with the bells ringing and the city lit below and Blake standing proudly at his side like the final proof of some ancient loyalty completed at last, he looked once more like the king Olympus had deserved before it burned.

Not gentler. Never that. Just kinder where kindness was due.

Night settled over the capital in layers of blue and gold. Windows kindled. Voices rose from the streets. The fountain in the garden whispered over black stone. Godric laid one heavy hand on Blake’s shoulder, a gesture simple enough to belong to any two old friends and weighty enough to anchor worlds. Blake did not bow. He did not flinch. He only stood there, loyal and plain and unafraid, while the king who had outlived his own grave looked over the realm he had carved from murder, grief, conquest, mercy, and impossible power.

Then together they turned from the balcony and went inside, where the lights were warm, the doors stood open, and Thronegate waited for its king and its wizard.
