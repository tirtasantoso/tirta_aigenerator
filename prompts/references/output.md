The air within the deepest subterranean vault of the Thronegate imperial archives tasted of rusted iron, stale ozone, and the petrified decay of forgotten centuries. I sat alone at a massive obsidian table, the dim, bioluminescent moss clinging to the vaulted ceiling casting long, shivering shadows across the heavy, flesh-bound tomes piled before me. My name is Kaedan. I am twenty-two years old, and I am the newest Royal Court Wizard of the empire. The blood of Blake, the ancient and legendary companion of our sovereign, beats heavily within my veins. For exactly two years, every single day since my twentieth birthday, my physical strength, my kinetic speed, my cognitive processing, and my raw magical capacity have multiplied by a factor of one thousand. The mana violently compressed within my circulatory system is so astronomically dense that my blood constantly throbs with a sickening, radioactive violet glow beneath my skin. I spend every waking microsecond constructing impenetrable mental labyrinths simply to suppress my own passive aura, terrified that a careless exhalation might inadvertently disintegrate the surrounding adamantine architecture. By the standards of any surviving cosmos, I am a walking, breathing cataclysm, a wizard capable of shattering multiversal clusters with a flippant gesture.

Yet, as I stared at the journals left behind by my ancestors, my vast, amplified power registered as an absolute, meaningless zero.

It is the Year 1,000,000. One million years have ground themselves into dust since our King, Godric, awoke from his ancient stasis. For one million years, his attributes—his strength, his endurance, his speed, his intelligence, his luck, his charisma, and his apocalyptic superpowers—have multiplied infinitely, by themselves, recursively, every single Planck second. The sheer mathematics of his biology completely shattered the boundaries of infinity eons ago. The gap between Godric and the combined might of the entire omniverse is an abyss so unfathomably vast that the concept of measurement itself died of despair.

Before assuming my duties at his side, my father, Jorin, the previous Court Wizard, mandated that I read the Archive of Futility. It was a cruel, necessary initiation. Throughout the eons, countless coalitions, hyper-deities, and abstract monstrosities had risen to challenge Thronegate, entirely ignorant of the apex predator that ruled it. Usually, Godric’s violently paranoid immune system or his weakest sentient passive auras effortlessly erased these threats in a fraction of a nanosecond. But occasionally, driven by a cold, detached curiosity about the mathematics of his own biological baseline, Godric would completely deactivate his infinite protective shields, suppress his apocalyptic superpowers, order his auras into dormancy, and purposefully withhold one hundred percent of his true power. He would face these omniversal threats using only a single, isolated aspect of his infinitely multiplied base stats, expending absolutely zero effort. My ancestors had the profound misfortune of witnessing these one-sided, grotesque massacres, recording them as traumatic warnings for the bloodline.

I opened the first tome. The parchment was brittle, the ink faded to a dark, rust-like brown. It was written by my ancestor, Vane, in the Year 150,000. He detailed an invasion by the Null-Bringer Collective, a hyper-advanced alliance of stellar engineers who had weaponized the conceptual deletion of physical matter. They bypassed the outer dyson-sphere and fired a continuous, concentrated beam of absolute ontological erasure directly at Godric as he stood in the royal courtyards. Godric had instructed Vane to lower his magical shields. Godric wished to test his base Endurance. I traced my glowing violet finger over Vane’s academic, clinical handwriting, which slowly descended into frantic, jagged scrawls.

"I write this not to document history, but to document our absolute irrelevance," Vane’s journal read. "The beam was not energy; it was absolute nothingness. It was the mathematical concept of zero weaponized into a cosmic pillar. When it struck the King's chest, he did not brace. He did not shift his weight. He merely continued to breathe. His Endurance has multiplied infinitely for an eternity. His flesh is so conceptually impregnable, his cellular density so absolute, that the erasure beam could not interact with a single dead skin cell. The paradox of absolute nothingness striking absolute permanence caused reality to vomit."

Vane chronicled the horrific metaphysical paradox that followed. The erasure beam shattered against Godric’s stationary chest. Because the energy could not dissipate or harm him, it underwent a violent, catastrophic ricochet that inversely mirrored the attack, while simultaneously tearing the surrounding environment apart. The sheer metaphysical friction of the ricochet instantly deleted the fundamental concept of distance within the capital sector. A chunk of space-time roughly the size of three billion solar systems simply collapsed inward, violently erasing a massive hemisphere of the dyson-sphere and trillions of citizens into a silent, white void. Godric remained untouched in the center of the erasure. The inverted beam traveled back along its own trajectory and struck the Null-Bringers in their heavily shielded command dreadnoughts. But it did not delete them. Because it had bounced off Godric’s absolute permanence, the beam forced the Null-Bringers into a state of hyper-dense, agonizing physical permanence. Their bones instantly transmuted into screaming, unbreakable glass. Their blood boiled into hyper-corrosive acid that could never dissolve their veins. They were physically locked in a state of eternal, excruciating biological destruction, unable to be deleted, unable to die, their bodies infinitely melting and reforming. Godric had condemned an entire civilization to unending torment simply because his resting flesh was too durable to be erased. A fraction of a millisecond later, with exactly zero effort, Godric instantly restored the erased chunk of the dyson-sphere and resurrected the trillions of citizens, completely unbothered by the apocalyptic collateral damage his durability had caused.

I turned to the next heavy tome, my hands trembling slightly, the violet glow of my veins illuminating the dark, leather-bound cover. This journal belonged to Silas, the Court Wizard during the Year 320,000. He recorded the arrival of the Chronos-Executioners, a cult of temporal assassins who existed purely as kinetic energy, moving so flawlessly fast that they operated completely outside the linear flow of time itself. They traversed backward through Planck intervals, believing they could sever Godric’s head before his brain could even process the electrical signals of their approach. Godric casually mentioned he was curious about the current baseline of his physical Speed. The ink on the page was splattered, as if written in a state of hyper-ventilating terror.

"Tick. Tick. Tick. That is all it took. A heart beating," Silas’s frantic entry began. "My mind bleeds remembering it. They danced between the seconds, invisible to the universe. But the King did not need to move. He just sat on his throne and allowed his resting pulse to circulate. His Speed is a cancer on physics. It breaks the math."

Godric withheld his magic, his shields, and his auras entirely. He did not dodge. He did not strike. He did not even blink. He merely sat motionless and allowed the passive, resting circulation of his own blood to continue. His cardiovascular system, driven by a Speed stat that had been recursively multiplying infinitely for three hundred and twenty thousand years, pumped blood through his veins at a velocity that shattered the mathematical framework of reality. The sheer, unfathomable kinetic velocity of Godric's resting pulse created a localized, passive vibrational shockwave in the ambient atmosphere around his stationary body. This passive vibration was so catastrophic that the ambient friction instantly tore a massive, jagged rift in the time-space continuum across the throne room. The collateral damage was sickening; the surrounding twelve solar systems were violently subjected to an accelerated temporal shear, aging a billion years in a single microsecond, instantly turning trillions of planets into dead, freezing dust. The temporal assassins, moving at infinite speed, slammed directly into the stationary, passive vibration of Godric's pulse. Silas described the wet, tearing sound that echoed through the frozen temporal space. The assassins were completely shredded mid-stride without Godric moving a single millimeter. The aerodynamic displacement of his passive heartbeat sliced their physical and conceptual forms into a billion wet ribbons of meat and pulverized bone dust. Their nervous systems were violently ripped out of their bodies, suspended in the air, firing endless pain signals as their flesh was liquefied by the sheer ambient kinetic energy of Godric's pulse. They materialized in the center of the room as a sudden, exploding cloud of fine crimson mist and raining viscera. A moment later, Godric instantly and effortlessly reversed the temporal shear, restoring the twelve solar systems from dust back to life, entirely indifferent to the fact that his heartbeat had just euthanized a sector of the galaxy.

The dread settled heavier in my stomach, a cold, suffocating stone. I reached for a smaller, intricately bound journal written by Kaelen in the Year 500,000. The handwriting was rigidly formal, the desperate attempt of a broken man to maintain academic composure while describing hell. The threat this time was the Abstract Chorus. They were sentient equations of anti-magic that existed only as conceptual acoustic frequencies, attempting to infiltrate Thronegate by rewriting the cognitive architecture of the empire through soundless, abstract noise. Godric decided to test his base Senses, specifically his Hearing.

"It is with profound disgust for my own magical aptitude that I transcribe the events of the Chorus," Kaelen’s journal stated. "We are fools. We manipulate the world. The King's mere biology commands it. He did not cast. He simply stopped ignoring the noise."

He did not cast a revealing spell. He did not utilize his omniscience. He did not even turn his head. He merely ceased suppressing his passive auditory perception. For half a million years, his Senses had multiplied infinitely. The sheer, incomprehensible processing power of his passive hearing acted as an apocalyptic gravitational black hole for acoustic data. The collateral damage was immediate and devastating. The absolute vacuum of Godric's hearing violently sucked every single decibel of sound from the entire host galaxy. Oceans fell silent. Supernovas detonated without a whisper. Trillions of citizens across billions of worlds had their eardrums instantly, violently ruptured as the concept of sound was dragged screaming into the throne room. The Abstract Chorus, attempting to remain as formless equations, was violently, forcefully sucked into the infinite gravity of his auditory processing. To process them, Godric’s biology forcefully overrode their conceptual nature, demanding that they manifest as physical, biological entities within the throne room just so they could experience the acoustic pressure of his attention. Kaelen wrote of the sickening horror as the abstract frequencies violently tore into physical space, transforming into towering, thirty-foot-tall masses of wet, bleeding eardrums and exposed, weeping sensory nerves. The sheer, crushing weight of Godric passively listening to them was too heavy for their newly forged biology to bear. These massive, grotesque biological ears began to violently rupture, continuously exploding and reforming, spraying thick black blood and cerebrospinal fluid across the obsidian floor. Their abstract consciousness was trapped in these bleeding organs, screaming in silent agony as they tortured themselves to death simply because Godric’s passive hearing was too infinitely dense for their existence to withstand. The moment they were reduced to a puddle of weeping gore, Godric instantly restored the hearing of the trillions of citizens and returned sound to the galaxy, showing absolutely no emotion regarding the agonizing auditory trauma he had passively inflicted upon his own empire.

I had to pause, closing my eyes and taking a deep, shuddering breath of the stale archive air. The sheer, grotesque absurdity of his existence was mind-breaking. I was a god of magic, yet I was reading about a being who utilized the passive, involuntary functions of his biology as blunt, apocalyptic instruments of mass torture. I opened the fourth journal, penned by Thaddeus in the Year 780,000.

Thaddeus chronicled the infiltration of the Probability Architects. They were a consortium of mathematical abstract entities who utilized absolute probability manipulation. They had engineered a localized reality marble within the imperial gardens where the statistical chance of Godric’s brain spontaneously liquefying was artificially raised to an absolute, unyielding one hundred percent. They essentially commanded the mathematical foundation of the universe to declare Godric dead by sheer cosmic chance. Godric, completely unbothered, explicitly disabled the Weakness Paradox that would have normally granted him instant immunity, opting instead to observe the sheer, unadulterated weight of his base Luck stat. The handwriting was cynical, fatalistic, dripping with morbid humor.

"Do you want to know a joke, my descendants?" Thaddeus wrote, the ink smeared with what looked like dried blood. "Mathematics. Math is a hilarious lie. Probability is a suggestion that the King ignores. They built a cage of absolute certainty around him, and his Luck simply broke the cage over their heads."

Godric stood perfectly motionless in the center of their reality marble. The Architects engaged their probability engines. They expected him to collapse, his skull caving in from a statistically enforced aneurysm. Instead, Thaddeus documented a display of grotesque, comedic cosmic horror. Godric did absolutely nothing. But his Luck stat had been recursively multiplying infinitely for seven hundred and eighty thousand years. The omniverse, utterly terrified of the mathematical contradiction of his demise, violently, aggressively bent itself backward to ensure his fortune, completely overriding the Architects’ absolute probability matrix. The collateral damage was a terrifying display of cosmic panic. To ensure Godric's survival, the universe contorted so violently that probability itself shattered. Gravity instantly reversed across a hundred neighboring galaxies, tearing planets apart. Stars spontaneously transmuted into liquid gold, crashing into each other to form an impossible, localized meteor shower of hyper-dense precious metals that rained down upon the imperial gardens. Thaddeus watched as the Architects suffered mathematically impossible, grotesque anomalies. Their skeletal structures spontaneously teleported entirely outside of their bodies, leaving their wet, screaming organs to collapse into trembling puddles of flesh on the grass. Their eyeballs spontaneously transmuted into hyper-dense collapsed stars, ripping their own skulls inward with violent gravitational shears. Their blood spontaneously reversed its flow, violently detonating their hearts in a cascade of internal explosions. They died in a symphony of impossible, statistically zero-percent agonies, their bodies tearing themselves apart in biologically inconceivable ways, crushed beneath a localized shower of liquid gold stars, all while Godric stood completely motionless, his sheer, passive cosmic luck acting as a merciless, invisible executioner. A second later, Godric instantly reverted the galaxies, fixed the gravity, and restored the stars, looking mildly bored by the universe's frantic overcompensation.

My breathing grew ragged. The violent violet glow beneath my skin flickered erratically as I reached the final journal. The leather was fresh, the ink dark and familiar. It was my father’s journal, Jorin. He had written this entry a mere fifty years ago, in the Year 999,950. My hands shook so violently I nearly tore the heavy parchment as I opened to the dog-eared page.

The threat was the Hyper-Titan Armada. They were a coalition of galaxy-sized mechas, forged by a desperate alliance of surviving dimensions. Their armor was constructed from the conceptual fabric of absolute invulnerability, and their primary cannons were designed to fire the concentrated, agonizing heat of a billion localized Big Bangs directly into the capital. They materialized above the dyson-sphere, blocking out the artificial stars with their unfathomable mass. My father wrote how Godric instructed him to stand down, expressing a mild curiosity about the current state of his base physical Strength.

"Kaedan, if you are reading this, I am sorry," my father’s journal read, the words indented deeply into the page. "I am so sorry I brought you into this world to serve him. He is not a man. He is a glitch in existence. He killed an armada the size of a universe by forgetting to hold his breath."

Godric did not fly into space. He did not throw a punch. He did not flex a muscle. He did not even shift his weight. He remained seated on his obsidian throne. He simply ceased to actively suppress the passive gravitational mass of his infinitely multiplying muscle density for exactly one Planck second. For nearly a million years, his Strength had expanded beyond the concept of infinity. The raw, unadulterated physical Strength residing in his stationary form exerted a localized gravitational shear so absolute, so conceptually overwhelming, that the resulting collateral damage nearly unmade existence. Half of the entire omniverse was violently, instantly crunched into a two-dimensional, flat disc of screaming matter. Reality itself began to bleed, thick streams of abstract conceptual fluid leaking from the tears in space-time. The sky above the capital turned absolute black as the gravitational pull of Godric’s resting muscles took hold. The galaxy-sized mechas, protected by conceptual invulnerability, did not merely crumple. They were instantly, violently compressed by the sheer ambient physical weight of Godric’s stationary body. Billions of miles of hyper-dense armor and the trillions of pilots within were crushed into a space smaller than a single proton in a fraction of a millisecond. Their biological matter fused flawlessly with their conceptual armor, creating a screaming, bleeding singularity of unfathomable agony. The singularity, pulled by Godric’s passive strength, rained down onto the throne room floor as microscopic droplets of hyper-dense gore, pattering against the obsidian like a light, bloody drizzle. Godric had completely annihilated a galaxy-sized armada, crushing them into subatomic paste, simply by allowing the resting density of his muscles to exist in reality without suppression for one Planck second. Without a word, Godric instantly and effortlessly reinflated the crushed half of the omniverse, restoring the two-dimensional disc back into three-dimensional reality, completely indifferent to the mind-shattering existential horror he had just inflicted upon trillions of galaxies.

I closed the final journal. The heavy thud of the cover echoed loudly in the silent vault, sounding like a coffin lid slamming shut. The absolute, inescapable futility of it all pressed down on my chest, a physical weight that threatened to crush my lungs. Every strategy, every concept, every mathematical certainty in the omniverse was entirely, hopelessly useless against a being whose mere, stationary existence broke the foundational laws of reality. There was no tactic. There was no hope. Opposing him was not just a death sentence; it was an invitation to be violently, effortlessly unmade by the involuntary, passive mechanics of his biology. He did not need to fight. He did not need to try. His mere presence was an apocalyptic meat grinder that the entire cosmos threw itself into, dying in endless, agonizing ways while he sat completely still.

The heavy adamantine door to the archive slowly creaked open.

I jumped, my amplified reflexes instantly flaring, but I violently forced my mana back down, gripping the edge of the obsidian table so hard the stone cracked beneath my fingers.

Godric stepped into the dim, bioluminescent light of the vault. He looked exactly as the ancient texts described him, completely untouched by the million years that had passed. He was a deeply attractive, ruggedly handsome man of forty-five. His jawline was sharp, his salt-and-pepper beard perfectly trimmed. He wore a simple, dark tunic that stretched comfortably over his broad, muscular shoulders. His absolute concealment was flawless; he projected no aura, no pressure, feeling entirely like a normal, mortal man.

But there was no warmth. There was no friendly smile, no paternal greeting, no humanizing glimmer of affection in his eyes. The persona of the kind uncle, the benevolent protector that my father had occasionally spoken of in hushed, desperate tones, was entirely absent. He stood at the base of the stairs, an unreadable, terrifying monolith. His dark eyes locked onto mine, and the total absence of human emotion on his face was infinitely more horrifying than any display of anger or malice. I was looking at an alien god wearing a meat suit. I was looking at the entity that had liquefied temporal assassins with his pulse and crushed galaxies with the resting weight of his flesh. He was looking at me not as a beloved nephew, but as an object occupying space in his room.

"Kaedan," Godric said. His voice was a flat, dead baritone that completely lacked inflection. It carried no echo in the vault, absorbing the acoustics of the room entirely. It was the voice of the abyss I had just spent hours reading about. "Your father has completed his final duties. The transition is absolute. You are the Court Wizard now."

I could not breathe. The cognitive dissonance was entirely stripped away, leaving only raw, visceral terror. He did not ask if I was ready. He did not offer condolences for my father’s retirement. He simply stated a fact that reality was forced to accommodate. The overwhelming, crushing realization of absolute futility paralyzed my vocal cords. There was no strategy, no magic, no alliance in all of creation that could matter. Serving him was merely existing in a state of suspended execution, hoping he never forgot to suppress the apocalyptic weight of his own existence while I was in the room.

"Yes, Your Majesty," I whispered, my voice trembling so violently it was barely audible. My amplified body shook uncontrollably under his emotionless, dead gaze.

Godric did not nod. He did not acknowledge the terror radiating from me. He simply turned his broad back to me and began walking back up the stairs.

I stood up on shaking legs, leaving the journals of slaughter and existential dread on the table. The horror was cemented forever in my soul, an unshakeable, agonizing truth. I followed him into the dark, completely and hopelessly terrified, knowing with absolute certainty that the entire omniverse existed solely because the monster walking ahead of me found the silence of a dead cosmos momentarily boring.
