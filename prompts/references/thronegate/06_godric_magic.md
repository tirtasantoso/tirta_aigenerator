The taste of raw, unshaped mana is a heavy, metallic thing, like sucking on a copper coin that has been left to bake in a radioactive sun. I can feel it coating my teeth, sliding down my throat, and settling into my stomach as a dense, coiled weight. My name is Gorm. I am twenty years old. I am the Royal Court Wizard of Thronegate, a title I inherited through the unbroken, ancient bloodline of the legendary Blake. For my entire life, I had been told tales of the Awakening, the decree our sovereign enacted millennia ago. But nothing, absolutely no ancient text or whispered warning from my father, could have prepared my physical anatomy for the reality of it. When I turned twenty, exactly one hundred and forty-two days ago, the dormant arcane pathways within my flesh violently tore themselves open. Every single day since that morning, my physical strength, my kinetic speed, my endurance, my intellect, and most importantly, my magical capacity, have multiplied by a factor of one thousand. Not added. Multiplied.

It is a staggering, suffocating gift. The mana flowing through my circulatory system is so hyper-compressed, so violently dense, that my veins constantly throb with a faint, bruised violet luminescence beneath my skin. I have to spend every waking second of my existence aggressively forcing my own power back down into my core, constructing endless mental labyrinths and reinforced arcane dams just to stop myself from accidentally disintegrating the heavy marble floors of the palace when I walk. The sheer, physical pressure of holding back a daily thousandfold amplification of magical offense and defense is a waking nightmare of absolute, unbroken concentration. I am a mortal man, destined to die around my eightieth year, yet I am rapidly becoming a walking, breathing cataclysm. By the standards of any other surviving cosmos, I am a god of arcane destruction, capable of unraveling entire solar systems with a stray, unsuppressed thought.

But in the grand, terrifying hierarchy of Thronegate, I am merely the shield. I am the arcane protector of a king who, I had always firmly and naively believed, possessed absolutely no magical aptitude of his own.

Godric has ruled for forty thousand years. To the conquered omniverse, he is the ultimate apex predator, the immortal god-king who crushed existence beneath his heel and forged an empire of inescapable, absolute order. But to me, he is just Uncle Godric. He has been a constant, warm presence in my life since I was a small child playing with carved wooden blocks on the thick palace carpets. He looks exactly as he did the day he awoke from his ancient stasis: a stunningly attractive, ruggedly handsome man of forty-five. His heavily muscled frame is usually dressed in simple, dark tunics that stretch across his broad shoulders. His jawline is sharp, framed by a thick, perfectly trimmed salt-and-pepper beard, and his short salt-and-pepper hair catches the golden light when he laughs. And he laughs often, especially with me. His warmth is not a mask. It is a genuine, unfeigned affection that radiates from his chest, a deep, abiding love for my family that spans tens of thousands of years.

Because of his absolute concealment, Godric emits zero magical aura. He projects absolutely no arcane pressure. When I stand next to him, my magically amplified senses read him as a perfectly ordinary, mundane human being. There is no leakage, no passive hum of mana, no distortion of the ambient reality. Combined with the undeniable historical fact that he has always kept a Court Wizard by his side—always a descendant of Blake, stretching back to the dawn of Year Zero—I had formed a logical, fatal assumption. Godric was a physical god, an entity of infinite strength and speed, possessing countless apocalyptic physical superpowers that made him untouchable in mundane combat. But magic? Magic was the domain of my bloodline. I assumed he kept us close not just out of love, but out of a quiet, unstated necessity. I believed he needed protection from the esoteric, the abstract, the conceptual arcane threats that his physical fists could not punch. I believed it was my sacred duty to be his magical armor.

This arrogant, naive pride was the foundation of my entire identity. I was Uncle Godric’s wizard. I was going to protect the kindest man I knew from the dark, twisting horrors of the arcane omniverse.

The shattering of this belief did not occur during a quiet conversation or a peaceful demonstration in the training grounds. It occurred in the middle of an agonizing, mind-bending slaughter in the Year 40,000.

The threat did not arrive in warships or hyper-mechas. They arrived as a conceptual infection. They called themselves the Weaver-Choir, an ancient, multi-dimensional coalition of hyper-sorcerers who had completely shed their physical bodies millennia ago. They existed purely as sentient, predatory equations of anti-magic, dwelling in the abstract, lightless spaces between realities. They did not want to conquer Thronegate physically; they sought to unweave the fundamental arcane foundation of the empire, to rewrite the magical syntax of our dimension so that Godric’s absolute rule would simply dissolve into abstract, meaningless nonsense.

I was standing with Godric in the grand astral observatory, a massive, circular chamber crafted from polished, seamless obsidian, its vaulted ceiling open to the swirling, starry expanse of the Thronegate nebula. Godric was resting his elbows on the cold stone of the balcony rail, sipping slowly from a delicate porcelain cup of jasmine tea, listening to me excitedly describe a new spatial-compression ward I had developed the night before. He was smiling, his eyes crinkling at the corners with genuine, paternal pride.

Then, the air in the observatory curdled. The ambient temperature did not drop; instead, the very concept of temperature was entirely and violently deleted from the room. The scent of ozone and burning copper slammed into my olfactory receptors with such concussive force that my nose instantly began to bleed, thick drops of violet-tinged blood spotting the immaculate obsidian floor.

The Weaver-Choir breached our reality. They did not tear a physical hole in space; they simply edited the location data of the universe to include themselves within the observatory. They manifested as six towering, geometric pillars of screaming, discordant light, their forms shifting rapidly through impossible, non-Euclidean shapes that made my amplified eyes water in pain. The sheer, naked hostility of their anti-magic was a physical weight, a suffocating, crushing pressure that immediately began to dissolve the ancient arcane wards woven into the palace walls. The heavy stone pillars of the observatory began to weep a thick, gray sludge as their conceptual integrity failed under the sheer friction of the unmaking.

Two heavily armed Royal Guards, stationed at the heavy adamantine doors, reacted with preternatural speed, raising their kinetic rifles. But physical weapons were utterly useless against abstract math. One of the geometric pillars pulsed with a sickly, jaundiced light. The guards did not die; their biological reality was simply rewritten into a state of permanent, agonizing paradox. Their armor melted into their skin, their bones turned into screaming, sentient glass, and their internal organs violently inverted, erupting from their mouths and eye sockets in a tangled, writhing mass of bloody, inside-out flesh. They collapsed to the floor in a steaming heap of impossible biology, their vocal cords emitting a continuous, wet shriek of absolute, unending torment.

I stepped directly in front of Godric. My heart hammered a frantic, terrified rhythm against my ribs, but my blood sang with the arrogant pride of my lineage. This was it. This was my purpose. The physical god behind me was defenseless against this abstract arcane horror, and I was going to shield him.

I released the tightly coiled restraints on my mana. The observatory violently trembled as the thousandfold daily amplification of my power flooded into the physical world. The violet luminescence beneath my skin flared into a blinding, radiant aura that cast stark, unnatural shadows across the obsidian. I raised both hands, my fingers weaving complex, high-dimensional geometries in the air, instantly forging a massive, interlocking bulwark of conceptual shields. I poured oceans of dense, hyper-compressed mana into the barrier, anchoring it to the fundamental gravity of the planet itself.

The Weaver-Choir unleashed their attack. It was a torrential, silent scream of anti-magic, a tidal wave of unmaking designed to erase the very concept of my existence from the timeline. The anti-magic slammed into my violet shields. The impact was not a sound, but a violent, concussive vibration that shattered every single pane of glass in the observatory and cracked the obsidian floor beneath my boots. I screamed, the veins in my neck bulging, sweat pouring down my face as I fed more and more of my amplified mana into the barrier. I could feel my magical reserves, vast as they were, boiling away under the corrosive, predatory friction of the Choir’s attack. It was an agonizing, desperate struggle. I was holding back the apocalypse with my bare, magical hands, fueled entirely by the desperate need to protect the man standing behind me.

I glanced back over my shoulder, panting heavily, my vision swimming with exhaustion and raw arcane feedback. "I've got you, Uncle Godric!" I yelled over the deafening, silent roar of the magical clash. "I'll hold them! You need to fall back!"

Godric had not moved. He was still standing exactly where he had been, holding his delicate porcelain teacup. He looked at the screaming, geometric pillars of anti-magic, and then he looked at me. His expression was not one of fear, or awe, or even mild concern. He looked at me with an expression of profound, tender warmth. He looked like a father watching his young son fiercely defend a sandcastle from the incoming tide.

He took a slow, calm sip of his jasmine tea.

"That is incredibly brave of you, Gorm," Godric said. His voice was a rich, comforting baritone, perfectly audible despite the catastrophic arcane pressure filling the room. It did not echo; it simply existed, cutting through the noise of the anti-magic with absolute, undeniable clarity. "You have your ancestor’s heart. Blake would be so incredibly proud of you. I know I am."

He lowered his teacup. "But you are straining yourself, my boy. Let me handle this."

I barely had time to register the confusion. Handle this? How? He had no magic. He possessed absolute physical supremacy, yes, but against hyper-sorcerers made of pure anti-magic, physical strength meant absolutely nothing.

Godric did not drop his absolute concealment. He did not step forward to strike them. He did not weave complex hand signs, nor did he chant ancient syllables of power. He simply allowed an infinitesimally microscopic fraction—a percentage so mathematically minute that adding an infinite string of zeros after a decimal point still would not accurately convey its smallness—of his own magical capacity to briefly express itself.

The realization did not arrive as a thought. It arrived as a total, catastrophic collapse of my entire philosophical understanding of reality.

I felt his magic.

It was not a wave. It was not a pressure. It was a sudden, absolute, crushing awareness of an infinite abyss. The mana that I had been so desperately proud of, the thousandfold daily amplification that made me a god among mortals, instantly registered in my mind as an absolute, perfect zero. My magic was nothing. The combined, accumulated magical power of the entire omniverse, across all timelines, all dimensions, and all surviving realities, was nothing.

Because Godric’s magic was not static. Just like his physical strength, his magical offense, his magical defense, his arcane comprehension, his conceptual manipulation, and his mana reserves multiplied infinitely every single Planck second. They had been multiplying infinitely, by themselves, recursively, for forty thousand years. The Weakness Paradox evolution had shattered any possible mathematical ceiling millennia ago, transforming his arcane traits into an incomprehensible engine of infinite scaling. The infinitesimal fraction of magic he allowed into the room was so vast, so heavy, so incomprehensibly dense, that it could instantly and effortlessly erase the entire omniverse, and then recreate it, and then erase it again, an infinite number of times, without him ever noticing the expenditure.

The air in the observatory simply ceased to be air. It became a solid block of Godric’s intent.

The Weaver-Choir, these ancient, multi-dimensional hyper-sorcerers of anti-magic, instantly realized the fatal, mind-shattering error of their hubris. The geometric pillars of screaming light abruptly froze. Their attack against my shield evaporated, completely consumed by the ambient, overwhelming weight of Godric’s raw arcane supremacy.

Godric did not use a specific spell. He did not use fire, or lightning, or spatial rending. He simply directed his infinitesimally small fraction of magical offense toward the abstract entities, and he decided they should experience suffering.

The abstract, non-physical entities were violently, aggressively forced to obey his arcane command. Because they lacked physical bodies to experience the pain he desired, Godric’s magic instantly, effortlessly forged physical forms for them. The geometric pillars of light were brutally, agonizingly transmuted into towering masses of raw, exposed, bleeding nerve endings and slick, pulsing muscle tissue. The transition from abstract math to wet, biological reality was an excruciating, tearing process that ripped their consciousness apart.

The newly formed, towering columns of raw meat immediately began to scream. It was a deafening chorus of absolute, unadulterated, biological agony. Blood, thick and dark, began to rapidly pool on the cracked obsidian floor, slicking the soles of my boots.

Godric watched them bleed with a look of mild, detached apathy. "You came into my home," Godric said, his voice flat, completely devoid of the warmth he had shown me a moment prior. "You terrified my nephew. You stained my floor."

With a casual, almost bored flick of his gaze, Godric’s magic ripped the towering masses of exposed nerves apart. He did not unmake them cleanly. He flayed them conceptually. He grabbed the fundamental, arcane strings of their existence and began to slowly, methodically pull. The giant columns of raw meat violently thrashed, spraying hot, arterial blood across the walls and the ceiling, showering me in a thick, metallic mist. They tore at their own newly forged flesh, trying to escape the unbearable, infinitely multiplying friction of his magical grip.

I watched, completely paralyzed, my knees shaking so violently I could barely remain standing. The smell of copper and void-rot was suffocating. I heard the sickening, wet sounds of their biological forms being crushed, twisted, and shredded by an arcane pressure that dwarfed the combined gravity of a billion black holes. Muscle tore from bone that had only existed for a few seconds. Eyes, spontaneously generated across their fleshy surfaces purely to weep tears of agony, violently popped under the strain, leaking vitreous fluid into the growing pool of gore.

Godric did not stop at their physical forms. His magic hunted down the conceptual origin of their existence. The Weaver-Choir had spent millennia hiding their true, core essence in a heavily fortified, hidden dimension outside the bounds of normal reality. Godric’s magic effortlessly bypassed their dimensional locks, shattering their abstract fortresses as easily as a man walking through a cobweb. He grabbed their core essence and dragged it into the observatory, forcing it into the bleeding, shredded meat.

"Please," a collective, gurgling voice wept from the pile of thrashing gore. It was the sound of hyper-sorcerers broken beyond the concept of repair. "Mercy. Oblivion. Please."

Godric did not grant oblivion. He simply squeezed his magical intent. The towering masses of bleeding flesh imploded violently, crushed into a microscopic singularity of pure, concentrated suffering. The singularity hung in the air for a fraction of a second, emitting a high-pitched, whining frequency of eternal torment, before Godric casually flicked it away into the void, banishing it to a pocket dimension where it would burn in that exact state of agonizing compression for the rest of eternity.

The observatory fell completely, sickeningly silent, save for the wet dripping of blood from the walls and the quiet, bubbling groans of the two mutated guards slowly dying on the floor.

Godric let out a soft, measured sigh. He turned his head, his absolute concealment immediately wrapping back around his infinite, world-ending magic, hiding the apocalyptic abyss behind the facade of the ruggedly handsome, forty-five-year-old king. The warm, kind, friendly smile returned to his face, crinkling the corners of his eyes.

He stepped carefully over a large puddle of steaming blood, his heavy leather boots leaving wet, red prints on the obsidian, and walked over to me. I was trembling uncontrollably, my breath coming in short, ragged gasps. I looked up at him, my mind violently attempting to reconcile the loving, gentle uncle I knew with the absolute, omnipotent, merciless god of magic who had just casually, effortlessly tortured a coalition of abstract entities to death using a fraction of a fraction of his power.

"Are you alright, Gorm?" Godric asked gently, reaching out a warm, calloused hand to firmly grip my shaking shoulder. His touch was incredibly grounding, a solid anchor in a reality that had just permanently lost its structural integrity.

I swallowed hard, the taste of blood and ozone thick on my tongue. "Uncle Godric," I whispered, my voice cracking, barely audible over the roaring existential dread filling my skull. "You... your magic. You... you don't need me. You don't need any of us. My magic... it's nothing."

The cognitive dissonance was a physical blade twisting in my gut. He didn't need protection. He didn't need healing. He didn't need the Court Wizards. His magic was multiplying infinitely every Planck second. He could erase the entire omniverse infinite times over without thinking. He could, I realized with a sudden, horrifying clarity, effortlessly delete the very concept of magic from ever existing in the cosmos. He could unwrite the arcane laws of reality with less effort than it takes to blink. He had not invented magic—Thorin had been a wizard before the stasis event, before Godric became this impossible thing—but Godric could unmake it forever.

Godric’s smile softened into something deeply tender, deeply nostalgic. He looked past me, his gaze seemingly piercing the veil of forty thousand years, looking back to an ancient, forgotten kingdom that no longer existed. He was looking at Thorin. He was looking at Blake.

"I have never needed protection, Gorm," Godric said softly, his thumb gently rubbing my shoulder. "But I need my friends. I need my family. Thorin was a wizard. Blake became a wizard. Your bloodline is tied to the arcane."

He looked back down at me, his eyes filled with a profound, unyielding affection. "I did not create magic, my boy. But I allow it to exist, purely because it is the language you speak. I keep you by my side because I love you, and because seeing you stand in front of me, ready to fight an abstract horror to protect an old man... it reminds me of why I let this omniverse keep spinning."

He patted my shoulder warmly, then turned and began to walk toward the exit, carefully stepping around the mutated guards. "Come along, Gorm. Let us have the servants clean this mess. I believe we have a council meeting to attend."

I stood frozen in the center of the blood-soaked observatory. My pride as the magical shield of Thronegate had been completely, utterly annihilated. My understanding of my own power, my massive, thousandfold daily amplification, had been reduced to a pathetic, microscopic joke. I was a speck of dust claiming it could cast a shadow over a sun. The existential dread was overwhelming, a heavy, suffocating blanket that I knew I would wear for the rest of my life. The gap between us was not just insurmountable; it was a mathematical impossibility.

Yet, as I watched his broad back retreat toward the heavy adamantine doors, I felt a deep, fierce surge of loyalty and love for the man. He was the ultimate apex god predator, a being of such terrifying, absolute supremacy that his slightest whim could permanently rewrite existence. But he was also Uncle Godric. He kept a universe of magic alive simply because he loved his best friend's descendants.

The horror was cemented forever in my soul. I knew, with absolute, unshakeable certainty, that opposing him was the definition of fatal futility. Any entity, any cosmos, any god that dared to stand against him would not just lose; they would be violently, agonizingly unmade in ways that defied sanity. But I was not his enemy. I was his court wizard.

I wiped the violet-tinged blood from my nose with the back of my trembling hand, took a deep, shuddering breath of the copper-scented air, and hurried to follow my king.

"Coming, Uncle Godric," I called out, stepping carefully over the steaming gore. The dread remained, cold and absolute in my chest, a permanent reminder of the impossible, merciless god I served. And I would serve him, faithfully and terrified, until the end of my days.
