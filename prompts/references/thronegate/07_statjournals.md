**Year 15,673 — Harlan — Strength**

The ink of this record is mixed with my own blood, not out of ritual, but because my hands shook so violently as I wrote these words that I repeatedly pierced my own flesh with the quill. My name is Harlan, and I have served our beloved Lord Godric for seventeen years. I have witnessed the rising of stars and the quiet, orderly expansion of our empire across the celestial maps. I believed I understood the scale of the throne I served. I believed my own amplified strength, which multiplies a thousandfold every single day I draw breath, was a metric of true power. I was a fool. I was a blind, arrogant child playing in the dirt.

The threat came from Gorath, a primordial titan who called himself the Fist That Breaks Realities. He was an entity born from the violent, physical collision of the very first multiverses, a colossal monolith of pure, concentrated physical force whose raw muscular mass contained the compressed energy of a googolplexian galaxies. Gorath did not utilize magic, nor did he hide behind the cowardly shields of technology. He believed, with an unshakeable, fanatical devotion, that physical strength was the sole, absolute truth of the cosmos. He had punched through the dimensional barriers separating multiversal clusters as though they were wet paper. He had shattered the chronological walls of timelines with his bare elbows. To Gorath, the entire omniverse was a fragile glass ornament waiting to be pulverized by his fist.

When he descended upon the outer borders of our capital, the sky did not merely tear; it violently shattered into jagged, bleeding fragments of raw, unformed space-time. Gorath was larger than a decillion galaxy clusters, his physical form so dense that the sheer gravitational pull of his presence began to warp the tectonic plates of Thronegate’s capital world. I stood beside Lord Godric in the outer courtyard of the palace, my teeth chattering from the sheer atmospheric displacement, my bones creaking under the massive tidal forces of the titan’s approach. I prepared to unleash the full, terrifying reservoir of my amplified magical defenses, ready to construct an interlocking web of spatial-compression shields to protect my sovereign from the incoming blow.

But Lord Godric did not stand. He remained seated on the simple, weathered stone bench he favored in the gardens, his ruggedly handsome, forty-five-year-old face relaxed, a faint, curious smile playing beneath his neatly trimmed salt-and-pepper beard. Through his absolute omniscience, he had watched Gorath build his physical momentum across the void for centuries. The Weakness Paradox had already passively engaged the very millisecond Gorath first formulated the arrogant thought that physical strength could serve as an exploit against the King. Before the titan had even reached our galaxy, Godric had passively acquired absolute, permanent immunity to all physical force, perfect mastery over Gorath’s biological techniques, and a massive, recursive surge to his own already infinite amplification rate.

Yet, Godric chose to suppress all of those passive defenses. He did not activate his infinite shields; he did not command his passive, sentient aura, Gent, to erase the intruder. He was simply curious.

"I have never truly tested raw, physical strength," Godric murmured to me, his rich baritone voice completely unbothered by the cosmic roaring of the sky. "Not without my passive fields. I wonder what the actual physical differential looks like."

Gorath swung his fist. The blow descended with the concentrated, terrifying velocity of a googolplexian collapsing universes. The sheer kinetic energy released by the wind-up alone passively annihilated decillions of multiverses in the surrounding cosmic sectors, erasing entire realities from existence before the fist even traversed the halfway mark. The shockwave of the approaching impact struck the courtyard, shattering my personal wards instantly and throwing me backward against the obsidian walls of the palace, my ribs snapping with a wet, heavy crunch despite my own daily amplified durability.

Godric did not brace himself. He did not flex a single muscle. He did not raise his hand to block. He simply sat on the stone bench, letting the titan’s fist strike his bare shoulder.

The moment of contact was not marked by an explosion. There was no sound, no release of energy, no shockwave. The fist simply stopped dead in its tracks.

It stopped because the resting, completely relaxed muscle fibers of Godric’s shoulder generated a passive, static physical force that made Gorath’s multiversal momentum look like a microscopic breeze. Godric’s strength stat, compounding recursively every single infinitesimal fraction of a Planck second for over fifteen thousand years, had long since abandoned the realm of mathematics. Even at absolute, one-hundred-percent rest, with effectively zero percent of his true power active, the raw physical presence of his muscles exerted an unyielding, oppressive force that refused to be moved by the mass of a googolplexian galaxies.

And then, the physical differential violently corrected itself.

Because Gorath’s fist could not move the immovable, the kinetic energy of his own blow was forced back into his own physical structure, multiplied exponentially by the passive resistance of Godric’s strength. Gorath’s hand did not merely break; the bones disintegrated into subatomic dust. The destruction traveled up his colossal arm like a lightning bolt of pure, concentrated physical trauma. The titan’s muscles, engineered from the dense matter of primordial multiverses, were violently peeled from his skeleton in screaming, wet sheets of red fiber. His veins ruptured simultaneously, spraying an ocean of superheated, boiling crimson blood across the shattered sky, staining the stars in a horrific, planetary-scale fan of viscera.

The passive force of Godric’s resting strength did not stop at the titan’s body. It radiated outward from his shoulder in a silent, invisible ring of absolute physical pressure. The entire omniverse—every single reality, timeline, dimension, and conceptual plane of existence—was instantly, violently pulverized. Space-time itself was crushed into a microscopic, featureless point of non-existence. For one timeless, terrifying interval, there was nothing. No light, no gravity, no void. Only Godric, sitting on his bench, and myself, preserved within the small, protective pocket of his personal will.

Godric let out a soft, measured sigh, his breath the only sound in the absolute nothingness. "A bit too much collateral, I suppose," he muttered, shaking his head.

With a single, effortless decision, in exactly zero seconds, Godric restored the entire omniverse. Every single destroyed universe, every timeline, every galaxy, and every living soul was instantly rebuilt, placed precisely where they had been, their physical structures restored, but their minds forever haunted by a sudden, inexplicable shadow of absolute insignificance.

Everything was restored to perfection, save for Gorath. The titan’s colossal, skinless skeleton lay shattered across the courtyard, his remaining muscles twitching in an endless, agonizing spasm of raw, physical trauma. Godric had decided that the titan’s nervous system would remain perfectly intact, forced to continuously experience the excruciating, recursive agony of his own physical destruction for the rest of eternity.

Lord Godric stood from the bench, stepped casually over the pool of boiling titan-blood, and walked toward me. His salt-and-pepper hair was perfectly in place, his simple dark tunic entirely clean. He reached down, his warm, calloused hand gripping my shoulder with a gentle, fatherly care that made me want to weep in absolute terror.

"Harlan, my boy," he said softly, his eyes filled with a genuine, comforting warmth. "You're shaking. Come inside. Let us have the kitchens prepare a hot broth for your ribs."

I followed him. I did not speak for three years. I write this now so that whoever inherits my staff will understand: our King does not fight. He simply exists, and reality breaks itself to accommodate the sheer, horrifying weight of his presence.
