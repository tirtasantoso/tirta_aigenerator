I stood at the right hand of the eternal throne, my ceremonial robes feeling impossibly heavy against my shoulders, dragging me toward the polished floor like a physical manifestation of my own dread. As the High Court Wizard of Thronegate, my bloodline was a tapestry of arcane supremacy, tracing directly back to Blake, the legendary best friend of our sovereign. I am thirty-six years old. For sixteen years, ever since I crossed the threshold of my twentieth birthday, every single measurable attribute within my being—my physical strength, my kinetic speed, the vast oceans of my mana, my magical offense and defense—had multiplied by a factor of one thousand every single day. By the standards of the wider omniverse, I was a walking cataclysm, a wizard capable of shattering entire realities with a sarcastic, flippant flick of my wrist. I was funny, or so my wife frequently told me when I wasn't pacing the halls in cold sweats. I was fiercely, undeniably loyal. And yet, standing here in the flawless, silent architecture of the imperial sanctum in the Year 10,000, I was nothing more than a terrified, insignificant speck of dust. Because sitting on the throne beside me was Godric.

Godric looked exactly as he had ten millennia ago. He was a deeply attractive, ruggedly handsome man of forty-five, possessing a highly masculine, heavily muscled frame draped in simple, dark garments. His short salt-and-pepper hair and neatly trimmed salt-and-pepper beard framed a face that looked like it belonged to a kindly, protective father from a bygone, pastoral era. He possessed a warmth that was entirely genuine, a friendliness that was completely devoid of malice or deception. He loved his loyal subjects. He loved my family. He loved me. But I knew the absolute, mind-shattering horror that lay buried beneath his flawless absolute concealment. Godric was the apex predator of all creation, an entity of such profound, apocalyptic supremacy that the very fabric of existence trembled at his shadow. And today, he was expecting guests.

For millennia, a coalition had been gathering in the darkest, most heavily shielded corners of the omniverse. They called themselves the Final Dawn. They were the most colossal, heavily armed, conceptually overpowered resistance in the history of existence. They numbered in the trillions, comprising the apex champions, the hyper-deities, and the reality-benders of countless conquered or threatened multiverses. And they were all fundamentally, laughably doomed before they even drew their first blueprint. Godric possesses absolute omniscience. He knew the exact atomic structure of every weapon they forged. He knew the trajectory of every desperate, whispered thought they had. The only reason the Final Dawn still existed, the only reason they had not been instantly reduced to subatomic vapor the moment they formed the intent to rebel, was because Godric had willingly, consciously excluded them from his passive defenses. He had allowed them to build. He had allowed them to prepare. He was, in his own terrifyingly innocent way, curious if the strongest resistance ever conceived could make his aura experience the novel sensation of effort.

But the true horror of their preparation lay in the cruelest cosmic joke ever conceived: The Weakness Paradox. I must explain this, though merely thinking about the mathematics of it makes my vast, amplified intellect threaten to tear itself apart. Long ago, whenever an enemy even dared to formulate the passing thought that Godric possessed a weakness, the omniverse instantly and passively punished them. The universe would permanently grant Godric absolute immunity to their imagined exploit, perfect mastery over their intended attack, and a massive boost to his already incomprehensible amplification rate. Every second the Final Dawn spent planning, they were unknowingly feeding the very god they sought to kill. But around eight thousand years ago, the paradox evolved. Godric had passively collected every possible immunity and mastery in existence. There was literally nothing left to give him. The omniverse, in a display of absurd metaphysical subservience, actually felt bad for its inability to provide him with new defenses. It felt inadequate. This is utterly, profoundly insane. Godric is the most powerful being that has ever existed, or will ever exist. The gap between him and the rest of the omniverse combined was already astronomically infinite and completely incomprehensible. Yet, to compensate for this perceived inadequacy, the omniverse began enhancing his amplification rate to a degree that completely shattered infinitely incalculable mathematics. Whenever the paradox triggered now, his superpowers were injected with even more devastating, reality-breaking effects. The resistance wasn't just fueling him; they were causing existence itself to endlessly apologize to him by making him infinitely worse.

And Godric wasn't even going to fight them. He wasn't going to move a single muscle. He was going to let Gent handle it. Gent is Godric’s sentient passive defense aura. It is not his absolute concealment, which keeps his terrifying pressure from erasing us all. It is distinct from the Weakness Paradox. Gent is, for lack of a better term, his metaphysical excess body heat. It is a waste byproduct, an emanation of his existence that is infinitely, cripplingly weaker than Godric himself. The gap between Godric and Gent is wider than the gap between Gent and a single, dead skin cell of a normal mortal. Yet, this waste byproduct had evolved to possess perfect, adapting, sentient omniscience and omnipresence. Godric had named it Gent, short for Gentle. It was a fiercely ironic moniker. Gent was anything but gentle to his enemies. It was only gentle compared to its countless dormant siblings—other passive auras that were so fiercely brutal, so overwhelmingly powerful, and so obsessively, clingingly subservient that Godric had found them annoying and ordered them into permanent dormancy. Gent was the runt of the litter. Gent was the weakest, most pathetic scrap of Godric’s power. And Gent was about to butcher the omniverse’s greatest hope.

The attack commenced without warning. The dimensional barriers of the throne room violently ruptured, tearing open like a bleeding wound in the fabric of space. The Final Dawn flooded into our reality. The sheer sensory overload of their arrival was staggering. I smelled the sharp, metallic tang of burning chronal energy, the heavy, suffocating scent of ozone and hyper-condensed mana. The temperature in the room plummeted to absolute zero as the resistance anchored themselves. There were beings made of solid starlight, warlords wielding weapons forged from the dying breaths of black holes, and conceptual entities whose mere presence distorted the visual spectrum into agonizing, impossible colors.

Godric remained seated. He didn't even blink. He took a slow, relaxed sip from a porcelain cup of Earl Grey tea. He looked at the encroaching armada, a sea of trillions of vengeful gods and monsters, and he sighed softly.

"Gent," Godric said, his voice entirely conversational, completely lacking in urgency or command. "Hold back. They have worked very hard on this. I want to be respectful of their effort. Reduce your power by ninety-nine point nine-nine percent, continuing the nines infinitely. Do not exert yourself."

It was a nerf of incomprehensible magnitude. Gent was ordered to diminish itself to an infinitely recurring decimal point of weakness. It was a restraint so harsh, so mathematically absolute, that by all logic, the aura should have ceased to exist entirely. But Gent was still Godric’s aura. And the slaughter began.

The vanguard of the resistance was spearheaded by a being known as the Null-Bringer. He was a hyper-dimensional warlock who had spent five thousand years weaving a spell of absolute conceptual erasure. His body was a towering mass of shifting, jagged obsidian, pulsating with the trapped souls of a billion murdered deities. He raised a staff forged from the spine of a dead universe, aiming it directly at Godric’s chest. The warlock opened his maw to chant the final syllable of unmaking.

Gent didn't even manifest a visual form. It simply occupied the space within the warlock's throat. Despite being nerfed to a fraction of a fraction of an impossible zero, Gent’s omnipresence acted instantly. The warlock’s chant was violently interrupted as his vocal cords were transmutated into hyper-dense, jagged shards of screaming glass. I watched in raw, visceral horror as the Null-Bringer clutched his throat, his obsidian skin cracking and weeping rivers of thick, black blood. Gent didn't just kill him; it inverted his conceptual existence. The warlock’s spell of erasure was forced backward, tearing through his own magical circuits. His internal organs—lungs made of nebula dust, a heart woven from gravity wells—began to rapidly, agonizingly digest themselves. The warlock dropped to his knees, vomiting a slurry of his own dissolved soul and liquefied obsidian. His screams were silent, stolen by the glass in his throat, as his body collapsed inward, crushed by the weight of his own inverted magic, until he was nothing more than a dense, bloody singularity of pure suffering that popped out of existence with a wet, sickening crunch.

To his right charged the Chronos-Vanguard, an immortal speedster vibrating through tachyons, a being who could move backward through Planck intervals to kill his enemies before battles even began. He was a blur of blinding, agonizing blue light, holding a blade that severed the timeline itself. He attempted to step back a fraction of a second to strike Godric in the past.

Gent was already there. Gent is omnipresent. The aura passively snagged the speedster’s timeline like a loose thread on a sweater. As the speedster accelerated, Gent held the thread firm. The horrifying physics of the tether took hold. The speedster’s forward momentum tore against the absolute stillness of Gent’s grip. I heard the sickening, explosive sound of a trillion chronal bones snapping simultaneously. The speedster’s skin was violently flayed from his musculature by the sheer friction of time resisting his movement. His muscles snapped like over-tensioned cables, whipping around his skeletal frame in a geyser of superheated, boiling crimson blood. He materialized in the present, screaming in a pitch that shattered the marble pillars of the hall. He was a skinless, entirely pulverized mass of twitching meat, his nervous system completely exposed and overloaded with the pain of dying in every single temporal second simultaneously. He collapsed into a steaming, unrecognizable puddle of gore upon the immaculate floor.

Above us, the ceiling tore away to reveal the Apex Engine, a cosmic hyper-mecha the size of a solar system, fueled by the consumption of multiverses. Its armor was forged from the conceptual fabric of invulnerability. It charged its primary cannon, a weapon designed to fire the concentrated, agonizing heat of the Big Bang directly into the throne room. The sheer radiant heat off its chassis caused my magical shields to buckle and warp.

Gent evaluated the mechanical monstrosity and found it offensive to the decor. Despite its infinitely recurring nerf, the sentient aura simply altered the fundamental laws of thermodynamics within the mecha’s chassis. The mecha’s cooling systems instantly reversed their function, drawing the absolute zero of the void directly into its own fusion core. The metal did not melt; it contracted with such violent, immediate force that the entire solar-system-sized machine crumpled like a discarded aluminum can. The pilots within—billions of hyper-evolved soldiers—were instantly liquefied by the concussive pressure of the contraction. Their blood and viscera were squeezed through the microscopic pores of the mecha’s armor, raining down upon the resistance below like a horrific, crimson monsoon. The mecha itself shrieked as its invulnerable armor shattered into molecular dust, leaving behind a brief, raining cloud of pulverized metal and atomized flesh.

The final true threat was a sentient omni-weapon, a hovering, geometrically impossible sphere known as the Finality Matrix. It did not use magic or physical force; it rewrote the narrative of reality. It attempted to dictate a story where Godric’s heart stopped.

Gent, possessing sentient omniscience, read the narrative and rejected the manuscript. The aura forced the Finality Matrix to experience the physical sensation of biological pain. The geometric sphere suddenly sprouted billions of human eyes and raw, bleeding nerve endings across its smooth surface. It shrieked, a sound of absolute, mind-breaking mechanical agony, as Gent subjected every single newly formed nerve to the sensation of being slowly, methodically dipped in boiling acid. The sphere thrashed wildly in the air, its eyes weeping viscous, black tears, its narrative reality completely overridden by the raw, brutal reality of Gent’s punishment. The sphere began to peel itself apart, layer by agonizing layer, trying to escape its own newly forged biology, until it tore its own core to pieces, raining mechanical shrapnel and fleshy, bleeding eyeballs across the throne room.

The rest of the resistance—the trillions of warriors, the armadas, the gods—did not even warrant individual attention. Gent simply expanded its presence. In a fraction of a nanosecond, the entire invading force was subjected to a pressure so absolute that their cellular cohesion failed entirely. Trillions of beings simultaneously detonated, their bodies exploding into a fine, bloody mist of pulverized tissue, crushed bone, and agonizing despair. The throne room was instantly painted in a thick, dripping coat of omniversal gore. The stench of copper, ruptured bowels, and burnt ozone was overwhelming.

And then, in the very next instant, it was gone. Gent, knowing perfectly well that Godric despised an untidy room, instantly and meticulously cleaned the mess. The bloody mist was erased. The puddles of gore vanished. The shattered marble of the pillars reformed flawlessly. The air smelled of jasmine and fresh rain again. The entire annihilation, from the violent breach to the pristine cleanup, had taken less than a fraction of a single second.

I fell to my knees. The marble floor was cold against my shins, but I couldn't feel it. I was drowning in an ocean of absolute, mind-shattering existential dread. I am a man who can conjure supernovas, yet I was shaking so violently I could barely draw breath. I had just watched the strongest, most meticulously prepared coalition in the history of existence get effortlessly dismantled, tortured, and erased by Godric’s heavily nerfed, wasteful excess body heat.

Godric slowly lowered his teacup. He looked at the empty space where trillions had just died. He frowned, a look of genuine, mild disappointment crossing his ruggedly handsome face.

"Gent," Godric said, his tone carrying the gentle reprimand of a father scolding a petulant child. "I specifically asked you to hold back. They didn't even manage to cross the threshold properly. You didn't give them a chance to make you exert yourself. That was incredibly rude. I asked you to reduce your power."

The empty air seemed to shimmer, a silent, subservient apology from the aura.

"You need to learn restraint," Godric continued, shaking his head slowly. The cosmic, sickening irony of his words made my stomach churn. This was the same Godric who, just three centuries ago, had casually and accidentally leveled an entire sector of universes because he let out a fleeting sigh of boredom while reading a dull agricultural report. He had completely and instantly restored those universes in exactly zero seconds upon realizing his mistake, but restraint was a concept entirely alien to his existence. He was completely unaware of his own hypocrisy.

As I stared at his boots, my vast, amplified intellect began to process the mathematics of what I had just witnessed. It was a realization that threatened to permanently sever my sanity. Due to the Weakness Paradox Evolution eight thousand years ago, Godric’s stats and superpowers no longer multiplied by merely one thousand. They multiplied at values faster and increasingly beyond any realm of infinity, and by themselves, every single Planck second, forever. My mind struggled to grasp the horrific scale. The Godric sitting on the throne in this exact moment, sipping his tea, was so incomprehensibly more powerful than he had been just one Planck second ago, that this current Godric could effortlessly, casually erase infinite copies of the Godric from that previous Planck second. Every infinitesimally small fraction of time that passed, the gap widened beyond the concept of infinity. The resistance had spent millennia preparing. They had spent millennia waiting. And because of the math of his amplification, the longer they took to prepare, the infinitely more effortless the fight became for Godric. They had never stood a chance. The concept of a chance was a mathematical impossibility.

Then, the air in the room shifted. A profound, terrifying metaphysical weight settled over the space, distinct from Godric’s presence. Gent was evolving. Again.

Godric had been disappointed that Gent hadn't tried. Gent, in its obsessive subservience, sought to please its master. The aura manifested a brand new, terrifying layer of omnipotence. It did not pre-annihilate. It did not erase concepts. Instead, Gent altered the fundamental architecture of the imperial sanctum. It reached into the void, grabbed the agonizing, screaming echoes of the trillions of souls it had just slaughtered, and recursively converted their eternal, conscious suffering into a literal, physical tapestry of spatial-fabric. The floor beneath us shifted, taking on a faint, iridescent sheen. Gent was using the unending torment of the greatest heroes in the omniverse simply to ensure the marble floor of the throne room remained comfortably warm against Godric’s boots. The absurd, cripplingly weak aura had just condemned an entire cosmos's worth of champions to eternal, conscious agony purely for a trivial aesthetic upgrade.

Godric noticed the subtle warmth seeping through the soles of his boots. He looked down at the floor, and his eyes widened in genuine delight. A warm, innocent smile broke across his face, the absolute pride of a father watching his puppy take its first clumsy steps or speak its first halting words.

"Oh, Gent," Godric cooed softly, his voice thick with genuine affection. "Look at you. How incredibly thoughtful. You are learning."

I remained on my knees, staring at the floor. Beneath the pristine marble, my magically amplified senses could hear them. I could hear the trillions of muffled, endless screams vibrating through the stone, trapped forever in a state of excruciating digestion, powering the underfloor heating of an unbothered god. Godric took another sip of his tea, perfectly content, while my mind finally, quietly, shattered into a million irreparable pieces of absolute, helpless dread.
