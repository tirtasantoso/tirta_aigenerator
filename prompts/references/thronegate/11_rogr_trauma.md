The morning sun of Thronegate always filtered through our bedroom window with a measured, flawless warmth. It was a golden, gentle light, meticulously calibrated by the orbital defense matrices to ensure the capital’s citizens awoke in absolute serenity. I sat on the edge of our plush, imported silk-weave mattress, a cup of steaming jasmine tea trembling in my hands, and watched my husband, Rogr, stare at the wall. Ten years had passed since the incident in Year 28, yet in his eyes, not a single second had advanced. He sat in the corner of the room, his knees drawn tightly to his chest, his once-broad shoulders curled inward as if trying to collapse his own physical form into a microscopic point of non-existence. His teeth clicked together in a rapid, endless staccato, a wet, bone-on-bone rhythm that had become the terrible metronome of my life. A cold, clammy sweat perpetually slicked his graying skin, smelling sharply of copper and stale ozone. He was a hollowed-out husk, an empty, rotting shell of the proud, articulate diplomat I had married.

Our son, Pete, bounded into the room, his small feet pattering against the polished mahogany floorboards. Pete was twelve now, a child of the empire, raised entirely under the immaculate, unyielding shadow of our sovereign. Pete carried a small, beautifully carved wooden figurine of King Godric, a toy sold in every market square across the capital. My chest tightened with a vicious, tearing cognitive dissonance as Pete ran past his shivering, broken father to show me the carving. Pete idolized the King. Everyone idolized the King. And why wouldn’t we? Godric’s rule was the definition of absolute, terrifying perfection. Under his reign, the streets were entirely devoid of crime. As a woman, I enjoyed a level of divine protection so absolute that the mere concept of being harmed was a laughable impossibility; the King’s brutal, agonizing punishments for any who dared harm a woman were legendary. He was just, he was boundlessly generous, and he was known to walk among the commoners with a warm, friendly smile that reached his eyes. I loved my King. I revered him with every fiber of my being. Yet, as I looked from the smiling wooden face of the toy to the vacant, terror-blown pupils of my husband, a silent, agonizing scream clawed at my throat. It was the King who had done this to him.

Rogr had been part of the delegation from our newly subjugated universe. Our cosmos had surrendered immediately upon receiving Thronegate’s offer, sparing us the apocalyptic annihilation that befell so many others. Rogr, full of pride and nervous energy, had traveled to the royal palace to formally present our treaties. The delegates had arrived with preconceived notions, expecting a blood-soaked tyrant, a merciless warlord who crushed galaxies beneath his heel. Instead, they had met Godric. I only know what happened because of the fractured, sobbing hysterics Rogr had managed to vomit out in the first few weeks before his vocabulary completely abandoned him. Godric had looked at them. And for a fraction of a second, the King had decided to play the part they expected. He had feigned a tyrant’s mild displeasure.

Rogr could never properly articulate the metaphysical horror of that infinitesimal fraction of a second. He told me it felt as though the fundamental mathematics of reality had violently inverted inside his skull. Because Godric’s attributes—his presence, his pressure, his sheer existential mass—multiplied by a thousand, and then by itself, every single Planck second, an eternity of amplification had compounded before the delegates could even blink. A microscopic, almost non-existent expression of Godric’s annoyance carried the psychic weight of a billion dying supermassive black holes. Rogr said the absolute concealment usually shrouding the King had slipped by a percentage so small it lacked a mathematical definition, but it was enough to permanently fracture his soul. The overwhelming dread wasn’t just the fear of death; it was the sudden, undeniable realization of his own absolute, pathetic insignificance, a crushing, suffocating awareness that his existence was permitted solely by the whimsical mercy of an apex god predator. Rogr’s mind had simply snapped, unable to process the unfathomable gap between his mortal consciousness and the recursively amplifying infinity of the King.

I could not accept this fate. I loved Rogr with a desperate, bleeding ferocity, and over the course of ten agonizing years, I had exhausted our considerable wealth seeking a cure. My attempts became increasingly absurd, driven by a hopeless, blinding despair. I absolutely refused to seek out the King or his Court Wizard, Blake. How could I? To approach the source of the trauma felt like dragging a burn victim back into the heart of a stellar furnace. Instead, I looked to the subjugated cosmos. My first attempt involved hiring the Neuro-Splicers of the Vaelen cluster, a hyper-advanced collective of cybernetic organisms whose medical technology bordered on the miraculous. They set up their humming, silver machinery in our living room, inserting microscopic, diamond-tipped mind-probes into the base of Rogr’s skull. The chief splicer, a being of cold logic and synthetic flesh, initiated the psychic dive to untangle the knotted trauma in Rogr’s cortex. The absolute moment the machine registered the residual imprint of Godric’s gaze locked within Rogr’s memories, the chief splicer’s synthetic eyes rolled back and exploded into showers of sparks and bloody tears. The machine let out a high-pitched, almost human shriek of pure, unadulterated terror, its internal logic cores self-destructing rather than processing the echo of the King’s aura. The Splicers fled our home screaming, their minds permanently corrupted by proxy-terror.

Years later, I spent a fortune to acquire a celestial artifact, a Weeping Stone from a dead, esoteric galaxy, renowned for absorbing all negative psychic energy and existential rot. I placed the smooth, obsidian stone gently upon Rogr’s trembling chest. For a moment, the room grew cold, and the stone began to glow with a soft, pulsing violet light. Then, it turned completely pitch black, vibrated violently, and emitted a sound that resembled a whimpering dog before it abruptly evaporated into a cloud of foul-smelling gray ash, leaving a scorch mark on Rogr’s shirt. My final, most desperate attempt involved summoning an ancient, conceptual entity of pure peace from the deep astral planes. The entity manifested in our bedroom as a column of soothing, iridescent light, radiating an aura of absolute tranquility. It floated toward Rogr, its light reaching out to bathe him in restorative calm. But as the light touched the edge of Rogr’s aura, the entity paused. I watched in sheer, mind-breaking disbelief as this ancient, immortal concept of peace realized exactly whose shadow had cast the darkness over my husband. The entity did not fight. It did not try to heal. It voluntarily and instantly dissolved its own consciousness, choosing to cease to exist entirely rather than share a cosmos with the being that had inflicted such incomprehensible pressure.

Each failure drove me deeper into an abyss of absolute despair. I was a woman drowning in a sea of cognitive dissonance. I walked the safe, beautiful streets of Thronegate, buying groceries from cheerful vendors, paying taxes to a generous crown, and thanking the heavens for the King’s perfect rule, only to return home to wipe the sour drool from the chin of the man I loved, a man broken by the very same King. Pete continued to grow, continuing to praise Godric’s name at the dinner table, completely unaware of the monstrous irony that his father’s shattered mind was the direct result of a passing, playful glance from his idol. We were trapped in a paradox of reverence and horror, unable to hate the hand that fed us, even though it had casually crushed the soul of our family.

Then came the tenth year. Year thirty-eight of Godric’s reign. I was in the kitchen, aggressively scrubbing a perfectly clean plate to keep my hands busy, when a heavy, authoritative knock echoed from the front door. I dried my hands on my apron, my heart thumping a dull rhythm against my ribs, and opened the door. Standing on my porch was Blake, the Royal Court Wizard. He was a man of legend, the former merchant who possessed the ear of the King, easily recognizable by his thick, friendly mutton chops and the aura of staggering, thousandfold daily amplification that he kept tightly suppressed beneath his simple traveler’s coat. But he did not look like a mighty wizard today. He looked incredibly burdened, his shoulders slumped, a deep, miserable frown etched into his forty-eight-year-old face. He smelled faintly of pipe tobacco and cosmic ozone. I ushered him inside, offering tea, my voice trembling with a mixture of awe and terrifying anticipation. He declined the tea with a sharp, dismissive wave of his calloused hand, his short temper warring with a profound, suffocating guilt.

Blake walked into the living room and stopped dead in his tracks. He stared at Rogr. Rogr was curled into a tight ball on the rug, his fingernails digging bloody crescent moons into his own forearms, his eyes rolled back so far only the whites showed. A heavy, suffocating silence descended upon the room. When Blake finally spoke, his voice was thick with self-disgust and a simmering, misplaced anger. He told me the truth. He confessed everything. Ten years ago, just before the delegates arrived, Blake had made an offhand joke. He had suggested that Godric play into the delegates' fears, to act the part of the merciless tyrant for a bit of fun. Godric, who trusted Blake completely, who considered this mortal man his absolute best friend, had taken the suggestion to heart. He played the prank.

The words hung in the air, heavy and absurd. A prank. A joke. My husband’s ten-year descent into a vegetative state of pure, uninterrupted panic was the punchline to a gag gone wrong. I felt my knees weaken, the breath leaving my lungs in a ragged gasp. Blake, his face flushed with anger, explained that Godric had casually mentioned Rogr’s condition to him just that morning. Godric possessed absolute omniscience. He knew every single atom in Rogr’s body, he knew every failed, desperate attempt I had made to cure him, and he knew exactly how much Rogr was suffering. But Godric had not intervened. Blake told me, his voice trembling with fury at his King, that Godric genuinely underestimated the horror. Godric sincerely believed he had held back nearly one hundred percent of his power. He thought he was just giving them a mild scare. Furthermore, Godric believed in mortal resilience. He remembered being a mortal man ten thousand years ago, and he honestly thought Rogr could just tough it out, that he would eventually snap out of it. The King of the Omniverse, the apex predator of creation, fundamentally lacked the context to understand that his mildest joke was an apocalyptic event for a lesser being.

Blake dropped to his knees before me, a man of unimaginable power bowing to a tired housewife. He confessed his role, his voice breaking, and he asked for my permission. He asked if he could have Godric cure Rogr.

I looked at Pete, who was standing in the hallway, his eyes wide, holding his wooden carving of the King. I looked at Blake, whose guilt was a palpable, heavy thing in the room. And finally, I looked at Rogr, my beautiful, broken husband, bleeding onto the rug. The cognitive dissonance roared in my ears, a deafening waterfall of love and terror. I took a shuddering breath, the air burning my throat, and I nodded. "Yes," I whispered. "Please."

The syllable had not even finished leaving my lips. There was no flash of light, no tear in the fabric of space, no sound of displaced air. The universe simply rearranged its fundamental truth to accommodate him. Godric was suddenly standing in the center of our living room. He appeared exactly as the statues depicted him: a forty-five-year-old man, ruggedly handsome, his jawline sharp beneath a neatly trimmed salt-and-pepper beard, his short hair immaculate. He wore simple, dark garments. Through his absolute concealment, I felt absolutely no magical pressure, no aura, no crushing weight. He felt entirely, wonderfully normal. Yet, my soul screamed in the presence of the ultimate apex predator. My instincts, honed by millions of years of mammalian evolution, demanded that I throw myself to the floor and weep in absolute subjugation. I was looking at a being who could erase the omniverse with a stray, idle thought, standing on my cheap woven rug.

Godric did not speak. He did not chant a spell. He did not weave intricate hand signs, nor did he pull any mystical reagents from his coat. He did not even move a single muscle. He simply looked down at Rogr, his eyes soft and entirely, genuinely warm. In that imperceptible fraction of a second, Godric simply decided that Rogr should be healthy.

The change was violently abrupt, a grotesque display of terrifying, effortless omnipotence. Rogr’s violent shivering ceased instantly. The sickly, ozone-smelling sweat evaporated from his pores, leaving his skin clean and dry. The gray, necrotic pallor of his flesh was immediately banished, replaced by a deep, thrumming, radiant vitality that seemed to glow from beneath his skin. The muscles in his arms and legs, which had severely atrophied from ten years of absolute inactivity, instantly and silently engorged, inflating with perfect blood flow and dense muscle fiber. He didn't look younger, he didn't de-age, but he looked impossibly, ridiculously healthy. He became the very picture of unattainable physiological perfection, his body repaired and optimized beyond the limits of mortal biology as an accidental side-effect of a God's passing thought.

Rogr blinked, the madness entirely wiped from his irises. He took a deep, shuddering breath, filling his perfectly functioning lungs, and looked up at me. "Rosa?" he whispered, his voice deep, clear, and steady, completely devoid of the chattering terror that had defined him for a decade. He stood up, moving with the fluid grace of a master athlete, and wrapped his arms around me. He smelled like clean linen and the warm sun.

I buried my face in his chest, sobbing uncontrollably. An overwhelming mixture of profound, staggering happiness and boundless gratitude washed over me. I had my husband back. The nightmare was over. I looked up at Godric, my heart swelling with an immense, unshakeable love for the King who had restored my life. But beneath that love, a dark, freezing undercurrent of prolonged existential dread firmly anchored itself in my soul. I remembered the screaming neuro-splicer. I remembered the weeping stone. I remembered the conceptual entity of peace committing suicide. All those absurd, desperate lengths, all those years of agonizing hell, completely rendered meaningless because the being standing in my living room could fix it with zero effort. The terrifying ease of it shattered my understanding of reality. We were nothing. We were dust motes floating in the beam of his attention.

Godric offered a warm, apologetic smile, looking genuinely contrite, like a father who had accidentally stepped on a child’s toy. He spoke, his voice a rich, comforting baritone that vibrated perfectly in my chest. "Rosa. I apologize for the inconvenience. Please, allow me to offer you full compensation for the troubles you have endured." He casually offered the absurd. He offered me a private, pocket dimension forged entirely of self-replenishing chronal-diamond. He offered a dynasty of unbroken, absolute perfection and fortune for my bloodline lasting ten thousand generations. It was an astronomical overcompensation, a staggering wealth that could buy galaxies, but to him, it was less than the lint in his pocket.

I clung to Rogr’s incredibly healthy torso, trembling. The dread was a physical weight in my mouth. I couldn't take it. To accept his gift was to remain tethered to his attention, to invite the infinite gaze of the omniverse's master into our small, fragile lives. "No," I said, my voice barely a squeak. "No, Your Majesty. Thank you. But having my husband back is... it is more than enough."

Godric looked at me for a long moment. His absolute omniscience surely dissected every layer of my terror, my love, my gratitude, and my dread. He simply nodded, respecting my decision with absolutely no fuss. "Very well," he said gently. He looked at Pete, who was staring at him with wide, shining eyes, clutching the wooden toy. Godric offered the boy a small, kind wink. Pete gasped in absolute awe. In that moment, despite the horror, despite the ten years of stolen life, I knew we revered him more than ever. The cognitive dissonance was complete. He was the monster who had broken our world, and he was the benevolent savior who had healed it, and we would love him unconditionally until the end of our days, terrified of the very love we felt.

Blake and Godric turned toward the front door. I stood perfectly still, clutching Rogr, listening as they stepped out onto the wooden planks of our porch. Through the open window, the afternoon breeze carried their voices back to me, clear and distinct.

Godric let out a soft, deep chuckle, clapping a heavy hand onto Blake’s shoulder. "Well, that sorted itself out nicely, didn't it, old friend?" Godric mused, his tone light and entirely devoid of the cosmic gravity he possessed. "You know, the delegates from the Vespera galaxy are arriving next week. I was thinking, perhaps we could try the tyrant routine again. Just for a moment. I could make my eyes glow red this time. Just a fraction. What do you think?"

I heard Blake suck in a sharp, hissing breath, the sound of a mortal man pushed past the breaking point of his temper. "Godric, I swear to the forgotten ashes of Olympus," Blake snarled, his voice thick with visceral terror and exhausting rage, "if you even so much as furrow your brow at them, I will personally find a way to strangle you with my bare hands."

Godric just laughed, a warm, rich, and utterly terrifying sound that echoed across the perfectly safe streets of his empire, leaving me trembling in the arms of my husband, profoundly grateful to be alive, and hopelessly terrified of tomorrow.
