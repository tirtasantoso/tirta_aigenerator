No light reflected from its surface. No sound escaped its depths. A casual observer would dismiss it as a paperweight, a curio, a bit of clutter that the King had forgotten to remove.

But Clive knew what it was. He had read Osvald's account. He knew that within that marble was the original omniverse—the real stars, the real galaxies, the real Earth, the real Thronegate, the real Blake, the real everything—compressed into an infinite mass smaller than a quark, contained by the barest whisper of Godric's will. And he knew that the reality he inhabited, the air he breathed, the body he occupied, was a copy. Perfect, indistinguishable, but a copy.

The deep, existential horror of this realization was a cold, living thing in his chest. If the original could be compressed and a copy restored, then what was the difference? How many times had Godric done this? How many copies of the omniverse had he created and discarded across the eons? How many versions of Clive had existed before this one, their existences erased and replaced without anyone ever knowing?

He looked at the marble, and for a moment, he thought he could hear the faintest, most distant echo of screaming—the compressed agony of every original soul, trapped forever in an infinite density of suffering, preserved as a trophy for a being who had found the ultimate attack of all fiction to be merely disappointing.

Godric's voice broke the silence. "I see you've been reading Osvald's account."

Clive spun in his chair. The King was standing in the doorway, his salt-and-pepper hair catching the light, his kind eyes crinkled with a gentle smile. He was holding a cup of tea, steam curling upward in lazy spirals. He looked, as always, like a warm, friendly father figure who had wandered into the library to check on his child.

"Your Majesty," Clive breathed. "I—yes. I was reading about the last opposition."

Godric nodded, stepping into the room and pulling up a chair. "Ah, yes. The fictional coalition. That was a memorable day. Such lovely music. It's a shame they couldn't back it up with a decent attack." He took a sip of his tea, his expression thoughtful. "I still have the marble, you know. I keep it on the shelf over there. I sometimes take it down and hold it, just to remember. Not because of the power—their combined force was so weak it would take an infinite number of them just to register as a rounding error relative to my current strength. But because of the hope. They really believed they could win, Clive. They believed it with every fiber of their fictional souls. And that belief, compressed into that marble, is a fascinating thing to contemplate."

Clive's throat was dry. "Your Majesty... Osvald wrote that the restored omniverse is a copy. That the original is still in the marble. Is that... true?"

Godric met his eyes, and for a moment, there was something ancient and unfathomable in his gaze. Then he smiled, a warm, reassuring smile that did nothing to ease the terror in Clive's heart.

"Does it matter?" Godric asked gently. "The copy is perfect. Every atom, every memory, every soul—it's all exactly as it was. If the original and the copy are indistinguishable, then what is the difference?"

"The difference," Clive whispered, "is that one is real, and the other is not."

Godric tilted his head, considering this. "And who defines what is real, Clive? I do. I have always defined what is real. If I say the restored omniverse is the real one, then it is. The original in the marble is merely an artifact now—a souvenir of a day when the last courageous fools in existence tried to oppose me. The reality you inhabit is as real as any reality has ever been. You are as real as you have ever been. The fact that your original atoms are compressed in that marble does not diminish your existence. It merely... contextualizes it."

He took another sip of tea, his expression softening. "I understand that this is difficult to accept. Osvald struggled with it as well. He wrote that he never stopped screaming inside. But Osvald also continued to serve me, loyally and faithfully, for the rest of his mortal life. Because he understood, eventually, that the question of original versus copy is a distraction. What matters is the present—the relationships, the love, the purpose. You are my court wizard, Clive. You are the descendant of Blake. You are real to me. And that is more real than any abstract philosophical distinction."

Clive looked at the marble again. The faint echo of screaming seemed louder now, or perhaps it was only the beating of his own heart. He thought about the googolplexian years that had passed since Osvald's account. He thought about the countless generations of court wizards who had served and died, their journals filling the shelves of the archive. He thought about his son, who would one day take his place, and who would read this journal and others, and who would grapple with the same impossible, existential dread.

And he thought about Godric—the warm, kind, patient Godric who sat across from him, sipping tea and offering gentle reassurance. The Godric who had destroyed the original omniverse and recreated it as an afterthought. The Godric who held the compressed mass of all original existence in a marble and called it a souvenir. The Godric who had, in his disappointment at a disappointing boss battle, accidentally ended all opposition forever.

The last opposition. After this, no one dared. The silence that had stretched across the googolplexian years was not the silence of peace. It was the silence of absolute, soul-shattering terror, the silence of a creation that had finally, completely, understood the futility of defiance.

And yet, Godric was kind. Godric was warm. Godric loved his court wizards and his subjects and his gardens and his bisque. The paradox was the fundamental truth of existence: the most terrifying being in all of reality was also the most loving, and the love was as genuine as the terror was absolute.

Clive bowed his head. "Thank you, Your Majesty. I think I understand."

Godric smiled, his eyes crinkling at the corners. "Good. Now, come. Vorthan has been working on a new arrangement in the garden, and I promised we'd stop by to see it. You can bring your journal. I know you've been meaning to write your own account."

He rose, clapping Clive on the shoulder with the same hand that had compressed all original existence into a marble, and walked toward the door. Clive followed, as he always did, as he always would, his heart a cold stone of eternal dread and unwavering loyalty.

Outside, the eternal afternoon of Thronegate continued its golden progression, and the marble sat on its shelf, silent and dark, holding the screams of the original omniverse in its infinite compressed mass.

No one dared oppose Godric anymore. After that day, the very concept of opposition had died. And the Godric of this era, the Godric who would eventually become the far-future Godric Prime who created the Stasis Event, continued to grow, continued to amplify, continued to ascend beyond any possible measure, the gap between him and the rest of creation widening with every Planck instant into an abyss that could never be bridged.

The last opposition had ended, and the silence that followed was the most profound terror of all.
