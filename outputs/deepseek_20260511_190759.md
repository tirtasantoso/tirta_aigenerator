Because he, bless his immortal heart, had forgotten entirely. In its place, he’d filled the emptiness with us, his loyal pets, and he’d called it family.

I drank my wine, and it tasted like the universe’s last prayer.

When you have amplified every Planck second for five hundred thousand years, speed becomes something else entirely—it becomes a state of being in which you exist in all possible moments simultaneously, not as a traveler but as a native. The Chronarchs fired their weapon, which propagated backward through time at infinite velocity, and it found His Majesty already there. In every moment it tried to erase—every single Planck frame of the past five hundred thousand years—His Majesty was present, not as a target, but as an occupant. The Annihilator, designed to erase a sequence of moments, instead collided with an infinite number of His Majesties, each one existing with perfect simultaneity across the entire span of eternity.

The temporal backlash was catastrophic.

It happened so fast that fast is the wrong word. Speed implies a duration, and the destruction of the Chronarchs' civilization had no duration. It was simply *already finished* before the Annihilator fired, because His Majesty's speed had already retroactively applied the consequence before the cause occurred. The Chronarchs, every single one of them across all their timelines, all their temporal colonies, all their past and future selves, experienced their deaths in a single instant that was also stretched across infinity.

I saw—with temporal sight I had never used before that day and have never used since—the Unmaking of the Chronarchs. Their bodies were subjected to a velocity differential that tore them apart at the atomic level. But not quickly. Oh, Kaedan, not quickly. His Majesty's speed allowed him to apply different velocities to different parts of each Chronarch simultaneously. An arm might experience one million years of motion while the attached torso experienced one second. The resulting shear forces did not merely dismember—they turned flesh and bone and soul into a fine mist of screaming particulate. A Chronarch general, a being who had once conquered the time-stream of seventeen multiverses, had each of his internal organs accelerated to different temporal frames. His heart beat at ten thousand times per second. His lungs expanded and contracted over a period of a decade. His brain, trapped between the two, registered every dissonant sensation with perfect, undying clarity for a subjective period that His Majesty's speed stretched into forever.

The collateral damage: the temporal shockwave propagated across all of causality. For a span of exactly zero seconds, every timeline in every universe experienced simultaneous temporal desynchronization. Beings found themselves living their lives in reverse while their neighbors aged forward. Children were born old and died young. Civilizations that had lasted aeons were compressed into moments and then stretched back out. The fabric of cause-and-effect snapped across the entire omniverse, and for a single, eternal instant, everything was chaos.

His Majesty, moving—no, *existing*—at a speed that made the concept of motion irrelevant, repaired every single timeline simultaneously. He did this before the chaos could register on any mortal mind, before any living being could experience the horror I had witnessed. The Chronarchs were not restored. "They attacked time," His Majesty said when I asked. "Time is my garden. I don't let people burn my garden." Then he asked if I wanted to see the roses in the east courtyard. They were blooming early, he said. Lovely shade of crimson.

I did not want to see the roses. I went anyway. They were, indeed, lovely.

**ENTRY THE FIFTH — Year 700,000**
*Recorded by Malkor, High Court Wizard, age forty-one*

You know what's funny, Kaedan? Not a damn thing. But if you don't laugh, you'll scream, so I'll tell you about the luck incident with the appropriate amount of gallows humor. Gallows humor is what you develop when you've watched probability itself bend over and present itself to a man who wasn't even paying attention.

The Strategos were the most brilliant military minds in the history of the omniverse. That's not hyperbole. That's a verifiable fact. They were a collective consciousness formed from the merged intellects of every greatest general, every finest tactician, every most subtle spymaster who had ever lived across all realities. Their combined IQ was, by any meaningful measure, infinite. They had studied His Majesty's reign for seven hundred thousand years. They had analyzed every recorded engagement. They had identified—or so they believed—a statistical weakness. His Majesty could not be everywhere at once. His attention, while omniscient, could theoretically be directed. If they could create a scenario where a perfect storm of distractions occurred simultaneously across every plane of existence, they could slip a single, undetectable assassination vector through the chaos.

They spent twenty thousand years engineering the perfect coincidence. They calculated every variable. They accounted for every possible interference. They were, by any standard, flawless.

And then their lead general, the morning of the attack, slipped on a metaphysical patch of ice and broke his conceptual neck.

I am not joking. The ice was not real. It was a metaphor, a poetic construct in the general's meditation chamber meant to represent the fragility of mortal effort. But His Majesty's luck—a stat that has been multiplying by itself for seven hundred thousand years—does not distinguish between real accidents and metaphorical ones. The general fell. In a realm where falling was impossible, he fell. His neck—which was not a neck but a psychic conduit—snapped. The resulting neural cascade propagated through the collective, introducing a single, tiny error in their otherwise perfect calculations.

That error was a misplaced decimal point, a fraction so small it should have been negligible. But His Majesty's luck inflated that decimal point into an avalanche of catastrophic coincidences. The distraction-creation fleets, positioned across seventeen million universes, all experienced simultaneous, inexplicable equipment failures. Not the same failure—that would have been too merciful. Each fleet failed in a way uniquely suited to its own destruction. The plasma conduits on one ship vented into the crew quarters, flash-boiling the soldiers in their bunks. The teleportation arrays on another inverted their polarity and deposited the strike team into the heart of a neutron star. The psychic amplifiers on a third resonated at a frequency that, by sheer random chance, matched the resonant frequency of the pilots' skulls, and their heads exploded in a synchronized spray that the surviving crew members later described as "a bloody symphony."

The assassination vector itself—a needle of compressed oblivion, supposedly impossible to detect—drifted off course by exactly the right angle to strike the Strategos' own command center. It detonated. The collective consciousness, every genius mind ever harvested, was subjected to an oblivion so absolute that they were not merely killed but unmade, their contributions to history erased, their names forgotten, their very existence scoured from the record of reality.

His Majesty, when the last echo of their non-existence faded, looked up from his lunch and said, "Malkor, did you feel something? I had the strangest sense that someone just had a very bad day." He hadn't done anything. He hadn't even been aware of the attack—or rather, his omniscience had noted it, catalogued it, and dismissed it as irrelevant because his luck had already handled it. The luck, Kaedan. The passive, ambient, zero-percent-of-his-power luck. It had butchered the greatest military minds in existence without him needing to so much as frown.

The restoration was instant, as always—the collateral damage across seventeen million universes was reversed before the blood finished cooling. But the Strategos were not restored. His Majesty, when I asked why, simply said: "They tried to use probability against me. Probability is embarrassed enough as it is without me undermining its lessons." Then he asked if I'd tried the roasted pheasant. He wasn't trying to be cruel. He just wasn't trying not to be.

**ENTRY THE SIXTH — Year 850,000**
*Recorded by Darian, High Court Wizard, age thirty-three*

This will be my only entry. I cannot write another. My hand trembles even now, and it has been forty years since that day, but the memory does not fade because His Majesty's voice does not fade. It echoes. It has been echoing in my skull for four decades, and it will echo until I die.

The Grand Rebellion of Year 850,000 was the largest coalition ever assembled—larger than the one during the Gent incident, larger even than the Unified Coalition that triggered the immune system apocalypse. They had learned, these rebels. They had learned not to target His Majesty's body, not to target his mind, not to hide from his senses, not to challenge his speed or his strength or his luck. They simply refused to acknowledge his rule. Their weapon was not a weapon. It was a declaration, a statement signed by the leaders of every free reality that remained, a document of total, absolute rejection. They did not attack. They simply denied his authority.

His Majesty attended their gathering in person. He walked into their council chamber—a colossal hall built in the space between multiverses—alone, unarmed, wearing his simple grey tunic. He stood before the assembled leaders, forty trillion of them, each one the representative of a reality that had pledged to resist him forever. And he spoke a single word.

It was not a spell. It was not a command. It was just a word. His voice—that warm, paternal voice that I had heard a thousand times over pastries and tea and chess games—carried that word into the air, and the word was: "Please."

Not a plea. Not a request. Just the word, spoken with the infinite, gentle sadness of a father who has been disappointed by his children for the millionth time and still loves them. And the word was amplified by His Majesty's charisma—a stat that has been multiplying by itself every Planck second for eight hundred and fifty thousand years, a charisma that makes the concept of devotion look like a mild preference.

The council chamber erupted.

Not in violence—not at first. In adoration. The leaders closest to His Majesty turned on their own guards, weeping, screaming that they had been fools to oppose him. They tore at their own flesh, offering wounds as tokens of their newfound loyalty. A diplomat from the Azure Cluster, a being of pure logic and reason, gouged out his own eyes with his ceremonial stylus, crying that he was unworthy to look upon his king. A war-queen from the Thorn Galaxies drew her blade and, instead of striking His Majesty, opened her own throat in an act of ecstatic self-sacrifice, her arterial blood painting the declaration of rebellion in a crimson wash of nullification.

The contagion spread. Charisma, when amplified to the level His Majesty possesses, is not persuasion. It is a law of nature. It is gravity. It is the fundamental force of loyalty, and the rebels had placed themselves directly in its path. Those who did not immediately turn to self-harm turned on their neighbors. Allies of ten thousand years drew weapons and butchered each other in His Majesty's name, their faces slack with a devotion so absolute it had erased every other thought. Generals ordered their own fleets to open fire on their own positions. Scientists activated experimental weapons that consumed their own realities. Children—there were children among the civilian delegations, Kaedan, children brought as symbols of the innocent future the rebellion would protect—threw themselves at His Majesty's feet and wept, begging for the honor of serving him, their small bodies trembling with a zeal that had been implanted not through magic but through the sheer, overwhelming weight of his presence.

His Majesty stood in the center of the carnage, not moving, not speaking further. His face—I saw it through my scrying lens—was not cruel. It was sad. Genuinely, honestly sad. But he did not stop the slaughter. He did not revoke the charisma that was killing them. Because they had opposed him. And his mercy, his warmth, his kindness—those are reserved for the loyal. For the opposition, there is only what they have earned.

The collateral damage was not collateral. It was the point. The rebellion's heart-realms—the universes that had signed the declaration—were consumed by the same loyalty-plague. Civilizations that had sworn eternal resistance suddenly declared themselves vassals of Thronegate, their populations overcome with a devotion so intense that it manifested as physical illness if they spent more than a moment without praising His Majesty's name. The rebellion collapsed not into defeat but into a worship so fevered that it made defeat look like mercy.

His Majesty returned to Thronegate an hour later. He found me in the library, shaking, my eyes unable to focus. "Darian," he said, placing a warm hand on my shoulder, "you look like you've seen a ghost. Let's get you some tea." He guided me to the kitchen, chatted about the upcoming harvest festival, and never once mentioned the forty trillion leaders who had just torn each other apart because he had spoken one polite word.

I drank my tea. It tasted like the screaming of a universe that had just learned it was loved too much.

---

I closed the compendium. My hands—my twenty-two-year-old hands, trained in a thousand spells, heir to a bloodline of wizards who had protected the throne for forty thousand generations—were shaking so badly I could barely see the cover. The study's wards had kept my screaming contained, though I hadn't realized I'd been screaming. My throat was raw. My eyes were wet. I had pissed myself at some point during the fourth entry, and I hadn't even noticed.

My father was in the doorway. He had been there for a while, I think. His face was the face of a man who had watched his son learn the truth he himself had learned forty years ago, and his eyes held no comfort, only recognition.

"Now you understand," he said, not asking.

I understood nothing. I understood everything. Uncle Godric—the man who had taught me to fish in the palace pond, who had attended my naming ceremony, who had asked about my homework and ruffled my hair and slipped me extra pastries when my mother wasn't looking—was also the being who had butchered the omniverse with a fraction of a fraction of a single stat. The warmth was not a mask. That was the worst part. The warmth was real, and it existed alongside a cruelty so absolute that it made the void look hospitable.

"Tomorrow," my father continued, "you will stand at his side. You will call him Your Majesty, or Uncle, as you prefer. You will love him, because he loves us, and his love is genuine, and it is the only reason our bloodline still exists. But you will never, for a single moment, forget what you read tonight. Because if you forget—if you start to believe that the friendly old king is all there is—you might make the same mistake they made." He gestured at the compendium. "And His Majesty does not forgive opposition. Not ever."

I looked at the book, at the dozens of other entries I hadn't yet read—later wizards, earlier ones, fragments from eras between the ones I'd sampled—and I knew I would read them all. I would read them until I could recite them from memory. Because I was Kaedan, son of Kaelor, descendant of Blake the Loyal, and I had just learned the only lesson that mattered in this omniverse.

Opposing Godric is not death. It is a demonstration. And the demonstration always ends the same way: with a king who forgets the blood before the tea has cooled, and a wizard who will never forget at all.

I turned the page. There was more. There was always more.
