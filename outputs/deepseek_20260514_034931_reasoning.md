Godric lounged upon His throne, His heavily muscled frame draped in what appeared to be a comfortable, slightly faded tunic of simple grey linen. His salt-and-pepper hair was, as always, immaculate, and His neatly trimmed beard framed the rugged, handsome face of a man who looked no older than forty-five. He had been staring at the ceiling for the past three years, not out of melancholy or introspection, but simply because He found the shifting patterns of the frozen star-matter embedded in the vaulted obsidian to be mildly more entertaining than the endless, sycophantic reports from the newly conquered Xeranthian Expanse.

Beside Him, standing at rigid attention, was Theron, His current Court Wizard. The boy—for he was a boy of twenty-two, the latest in the unbroken line of Blake—was still fresh in his office, his formal robes stiff with new embroidery, his face a pale mask of carefully controlled reverence. Godric liked him. He had the same blunt, short-tempered expression as his ancestor, though he was too terrified to express it yet.

“Theron,” Godric said, His voice a deep, resonant baritone that vibrated pleasantly through the obsidian. “Do you know what the most amusing thing is about absolute omniscience?”

The young wizard flinched at the sudden address, then quickly shook his head. “No, my King. I… I cannot pretend to understand the scope of Your awareness.”

Godric chuckled, a warm, fatherly sound that seemed entirely at odds with the cold dread that permanently lingered in the throne room. “It’s that nothing surprises you. Every possible outcome of every possible action, stretching forward an infinite number of Planck seconds—I have already perceived it all. I have watched the birth and death of every universe, every civilization, every single atom, a decillion times over. I have seen every possible version of your life, your death, your choices. It is, in its own terrible way, profoundly boring.” He turned His gaze from the ceiling to meet Theron’s wide eyes. “So, to keep myself entertained, I sometimes choose to… ignore. I pick a reality, a universe, a single mote of dust in the endless grinding of the omniversal gears, and I deliberately do not look at its future. I give myself the gift of suspense. Do you understand?”

Theron’s throat bobbed. “You blind yourself, my King? But Your omniscience—”

“Is still active. I merely choose to restrain myself from extrapolating forward. I can still see every atom of that universe in the present moment, but I do not let myself see the next Planck second until it arrives. It is a form of meditation. And today, I feel like exercising that little gift. There is a universe I have not touched since before the Fall of Olympus. A universe so primitive, so isolated, so blissfully unaware of the larger omniverse that its inhabitants have invented their own gods, their own myths, their own quaint little philosophies. They call their home ‘Earth.’” Godric’s lips curled into a smile that did not quite reach His golden-brown eyes. “I think it’s time I introduced Myself.”

Theron, to his credit, did not immediately vomit. He had read the Stat Journals, the Superpower Codices, and the grim memoirs of a thousand Court Wizards before him. He knew exactly what happened when the King expressed an interest in a new universe. “My King,” he said carefully, “shall I prepare the formal terms of subjugation? The standard offer of unconditional surrender, with a thirty-minute grace period before the—”

“No, no,” Godric waved a hand dismissively. “No formal terms. No grace period. I’m feeling… mischievous today.” He rose from the throne with the fluid, silent grace of a predator, His bare feet echoing softly on the cool obsidian. “This will be a game. They will try to kill Me, of course—they always do, these proud little mortals. And I will let them. I will give them every opportunity, every desperate strategy they can conceive. And then, one by one, in the most creative and excruciating manners I can devise, I will kill them all. Not a quick death. Not a clean erasure. I want to hear them scream, Theron. I want their agony to echo across their local dimension for a million years. I want the universe to remember their suffering as a fundamental law of its own physics.”

Theron’s face had gone the color of old milk. He swallowed hard. “Is there… a reason this universe has earned Your particular displeasure, my King?”

Godric paused, His head tilting slightly as if considering the question. “No. None at all. That’s what makes it fun.”

He took a single step forward, and the fabric of space folded around Him like a cloak. The throne room vanished, replaced in a flicker by the cold, endless void of a universe that had never known the touch of a god.

---

Godric materialized in the upper thermosphere of Earth, His body suspended effortlessly among the wispy trails of dying satellites and the faint, blue curvature of the planet below. He looked down at the spinning marble of continents, the swirling white storms, the glittering lights of cities turning on as night crept across the hemisphere. He allowed His omniscience to wash over the planet in a single, gentle wave, absorbing every detail of its civilization—their languages, their wars, their art, their desperate little dreams, and above all, their fiction. Oh, how rich their fiction was! They had written stories about gods, about demons, about all-powerful entities from beyond the stars. They had imagined cosmic horrors, alien invasions, and even a few scattered tales that came eerily close to the truth. Godric read every book, every screenplay, every half-formed idea in the mind of a daydreaming child, and He felt a genuine, warm amusement.

“They’ve been waiting for Me,” He murmured to Himself, a soft chuckle vibrating through the empty void. “They just don’t know it.”

He descended. Not as a meteor, or a beam of light, or a majestic sky-chariot, but as a man. He simply allowed gravity to reassert its gentle pull, and He drifted downward like a falling leaf, His simple grey tunic fluttering in the thin upper winds. He passed through the stratosphere, the troposphere, until He could smell the faint pollutants, the salt of the ocean, the exhaust of a billion combustion engines. He landed softly, His bare feet touching the cracked asphalt of a minor road in a small town in the American Midwest. The sun was setting, painting the sky in shades of orange and purple, and a few cars hummed lazily along the distant highway.

Godric looked around at the humble buildings, the grain silos, the faded billboards advertising cheap beer and car insurance. He took a deep breath, savoring the air—it was polluted, yes, but it was real, unfiltered by arcane wards or dimensional shielding. And then, with the gentle, paternal smile of a father about to play a game with his children, He released His absolute concealment—not fully, never fully, but just enough for them to hear His voice.

Every human on Earth simultaneously froze. The voice did not come from the sky or from radios or from the earth. It simply manifested inside their minds, bypassing the ears entirely, in whatever language they spoke most intimately. It was a warm, resonant baritone, gentle and polite, like a neighbor asking to borrow a cup of sugar.

“Citizens of Earth,” Godric said. “My name is Godric, King of Thronegate, Apex Predator of the Omniverse. I am here to formally annex your world into my dominion. In one hour, I will begin the process of subjugation. You are, of course, welcome to resist. I encourage it. Please, use every weapon, every strategy, every clever little trick your species has devised over its brief existence. I will be waiting in the town of Oakhaven, Nebraska.”

The voice faded, leaving behind a silence that was immediately, violently shattered by global panic.

---

The hour was a spectacle that Godric watched with genuine, childlike curiosity. His omniscience, though deliberately restrained from forward-extrapolation, allowed Him to witness every corner of the planet simultaneously. He saw the United Nations Security Council convene in emergency session, their faces pale and sweating as they debated whether the voice was a global hallucination or a genuine extraterrestrial contact. He saw the Pentagon scrambling to locate Him, satellites swiveling their lenses toward the coordinates that had been psychically imprinted on every human mind. He saw religious leaders falling to their knees, some proclaiming the Apocalypse, others declaring Him the Antichrist, still others weeping in ecstatic rapture because their gods had finally spoken.

And He saw the soldiers. The first ones arrived within minutes, a convoy of armored vehicles roaring down the empty highway toward Oakhaven. The town itself had already been evacuated; Godric had psychically nudged the residents to leave, not out of mercy, but because He wanted a clear stage. The soldiers fanned out, their assault rifles trained on Him as He stood in the middle of the town square, His hands clasped behind His back, His expression placidly curious.

“Unknown target!” a voice boomed over a loudspeaker. “Kneel with your hands on your head immediately!”

Godric tilted His head, amused. He did not kneel. Instead, He lifted one hand and gave a small, friendly wave. “Hello. I’m not going to do that. But I’d like to see your weapons. Please, fire at will.”

The order came a moment later, and the air was filled with the deafening roar of gunfire. Bullets—hundreds of them, of varying calibers—streaked toward Godric’s body. He did not flinch. He did not raise a shield. He simply let them hit. The kinetic force of the rounds, minuscule compared to the infinite mountain of His strength, transferred to His skin with less effect than a gentle summer breeze. The bullets didn’t bounce; they simply evaporated on contact, their mass converted to harmless photons in a faint puff of violet light. The soldiers, watching their finest small-arms fire achieve less than nothing, began to scream in a mixture of terror and disbelief.

“That was excellent,” Godric said warmly. “Now, please escalate. I know you have nuclear weapons. I’ll wait.”

The next twenty minutes were a crescendo of human desperation. Godric allowed them to throw everything they had at Him, not because He needed to test anything—He already knew the precise outcome of every weapon they could ever conceive—but because He was savoring the slow, dawning horror in their collective psyche. They launched cruise missiles, then hypersonic glide vehicles, then experimental directed-energy cannons. Each attack struck with enough force to level a small city, and each dissolved into harmless light upon touching His skin. Godric stood in the center of the cratered, burning town square, His grey tunic unstained, His salt-and-pepper hair barely rustled.

The nuclear strike came from a submarine, a single MIRV warhead that split into six reentry vehicles, each descending toward Oakhaven with the destructive power of several megatons. Godric watched the bright, blinding flashes with a nostalgic fondness, reminded of a supernova He had once casually consumed for its mild, peppery flavor. The nuclear fireball engulfed Him, the shockwave flattening everything for miles around. And still, He stood. The mushroom cloud rose, and He was still there, arms now crossed over His broad chest, His expression mildly disappointed.

“That’s it?” He called out, His voice effortlessly carrying across the smoke and radiation to the distant drone cameras that were relaying the footage. “That’s the best you have? Your scientists have been dreaming of antimatter weapons, quantum disintegration, biological agents. I’m right here. Try them. I can wait.”

They tried. Over the remaining minutes, a frantic, desperate race of scientific madness unfolded. Labs around the world were commanded to deploy every experimental weapon. Godric allowed a few of them to actually strike Him—a prototype antimatter bomb detonated in high orbit, a cloud of weaponized smallpox, even a crudely aimed laser designed to induce a false vacuum decay in the local quantum field. He absorbed them all with the same casual indifference, and slowly, He allowed the reality of their futility to sink into every human mind. Not through psychic manipulation, but simply by letting them watch the footage, letting them analyze the data, letting them understand that nothing worked.

The hour ended.

“Time’s up,” Godric said, and the entire world heard Him.

Then the game truly began.

---

Godric had not lied when He said He wanted their deaths to be agonizing. He began with the soldiers who had fired on Him—the ones who were still alive, huddled in the ruins of their armored vehicles, their minds broken. He lifted a single finger, and every soldier in a fifty-mile radius simultaneously felt their nervous systems become hyper-aware. Every nerve, every pain receptor, was amplified a billionfold. The gentle pressure of their own uniforms against their skin became unbearable torment. The air itself, moving against their exposed flesh, felt like a constant, grinding sandstorm of razor blades. They screamed. They clawed at their own bodies, trying desperately to escape the sensations that had become pure, unending agony. And Godric watched, His expression placid, as they slowly killed themselves in their thrashing—ripping off their own clothes, then their own skin, then their own muscles, driven to a frenzy of self-destruction just to make the pain stop. It took hours, because He would not let them die quickly. Their hearts kept beating, their lungs kept gasping, long after their bodies should have failed.

And that was only the beginning.

He expanded His attention to the United States military command structure. Every general, every admiral, every politician who had authorized the attack—He reached into their minds and extracted their deepest, most personal fears. For one, it was the fear of being buried alive. Godric obliged, opening a chasm beneath the man’s feet that swallowed him into a pocket dimension of absolute, suffocating darkness, where he would remain conscious and gasping for air for the next ten thousand years, compressed seconds into eons. For another, it was the fear of his children dying. Godric appeared beside the man in his bunker, took the form of his youngest daughter, and then, while the man watched, He slowly, methodically peeled the image’s skin off, layer by layer, each scream precisely calibrated to be indistinguishable from the real daughter’s voice. When the man’s heart finally gave out from sheer terror, Godric restarted it and did it again, three times, before letting him die permanently.

Then He turned to the wider population.

Godric was not indiscriminate. He did not simply snap the planet out of existence, though it would have been laughably easy. He wanted each death to be a unique, personalized masterpiece of suffering. He began with the leaders of the nations who had refused to surrender—picking them up, one by one, with invisible hands, and forcing them to experience the sum total of all the pain they had ever inflicted on others, amplified and condensed into a single, eternal moment. But He quickly grew bored of the leaders. They were too few. So He expanded His reach to the cities—the great metropolises of Earth, the teeming billions who, in their collective ignorance, had not yet fully grasped what was happening.

He chose Tokyo first. In an instant, He psychically linked every human in the greater metropolitan area into a single, shared sensory mesh. And then He sent a pulse of what He called, in His own mind, a “suffering cascade.” One person’s stubbed toe became everyone’s stubbed toe, recursively amplified through the mesh until the sensation was a continuous, shrieking wave of phantom agony. People collapsed in the streets, clutching their feet—and then their hands, their heads, their hearts—as the cascade spread, each new pain adding to the collective scream. The mesh fed on itself, and Godric, gentle as a composer conducting a symphony, adjusted the frequencies to ensure that the agony never killed them too quickly. He let it play out over twelve hours, the entire city an orchestra of writhing, dying, still-conscious bodies. By the time the last heart stopped, Tokyo was a silent, glistening field of broken flesh, steam rising from the tangled masses as the residual neural energies dissipated.

He repeated the performance in Delhi, in New York, in London, in Shanghai—each with a different, creative twist. In Delhi, He altered their perception of temperature so that the very air felt like molten lead pouring into their lungs, while their bodies remained physically unharmed. In New York, He made every person see their own reflection as a rotting, maggot-infested corpse, driving them to suicide by hurling themselves through windows or into traffic, only to find that He wouldn't let them die—they lay, shattered and broken on the streets, conscious and in agony, until He finally released them. In London, He twisted their sense of time, so that each second lasted a subjective year, and during that year they experienced every grief, every regret, every moment of existential despair they had ever suppressed, until their minds simply collapsed.

The global military, in its final, desperate hours, turned to more exotic solutions. A secret cabal of occultists, who had long dabbled in the fringe rituals of a forgotten era, attempted to summon a "greater demon" to oppose Godric. He found this so delightful that He allowed their summoning to actually work—except that He possessed the summoned entity, wore it like a meat puppet, and used its claws to personally disembowel the cultists while laughing in their own language. Another group of physicists attempted to create a kugelblitz, a black hole made of pure radiation, hoping to swallow Him. Godric helpfully let them complete the device, then swallowed the black hole Himself and burped out a stream of gamma rays that cooked the physicists where they stood, their bodies flash-frying in an instant but their consciousnesses preserved, screaming inside their own skulls, for a subjective eternity.

He saved the children for last—not out of any mercy, but because their fear was the purest, the most exquisitely unfiltered. Godric appeared to them in the forms of their favorite cartoon characters, their beloved superheroes, and while they stared in bewildered joy, He transformed those forms into shambling, rotting horrors that devoured their parents in front of them, then turned to the children themselves. The terror in their eyes, He noted, was a rare vintage, a flavor He had not savored in millennia.

Throughout the entire ordeal, which stretched across a subjective week of Earth-time but only a few hours of Godric's relaxed, leisurely pace, He maintained the same warm, friendly demeanor. When survivors begged Him for mercy, He smiled gently and explained that He was giving them exactly what they had asked for: a chance to kill Him. They had tried. They had failed. Now they were paying the price. It was, He said, only fair.

By the end, the surface of Earth was unrecognizable. The oceans had boiled from the residual energies of His various torments, though He had kept the planet physically intact to prolong the suffering of those who fled to underground bunkers. The atmosphere was a churning soup of ash, radiation, and the lingering psychic screams of ten billion humans, each preserved as a frozen echo in the quantum substrate of the planet. Godric, standing on the peak of what had once been Mount Everest, surveyed His handiwork with a quiet, paternal pride.

He did not restore them. He did not feel the slightest flicker of remorse. These ants had been offered the chance to submit peacefully, and they had chosen defiance. The price of defiance was, and always would be, absolute.

He turned His attention to the rest of the universe—the handful of fledgling civilizations on a few distant exoplanets, the microbial life drifting in the void, the potential for future intelligence. He considered, for a moment, whether to let them live. They had not personally opposed Him, after all. But they were part of the same universe, the same seed of arrogance. He decided to be thorough.

With a single, infinitesimal flex of His molecular manipulation superpower—using a fraction so small that calling it zero percent was a grandiose overestimation—Godric issued a single command to every atom in the universe: *forget your cohesion.* The fabric of matter across the entire cosmos, from the burning hearts of stars to the cold dust of distant nebulae, instantly dissolved into a fine, luminous mist. Every solid, liquid, gas, and plasma, every complex molecule, every life-form on every world, was simultaneously and irrevocably unraveled. The universe went dark, all energy dissipating into a uniform, featureless void.

Godric stood alone in that void, the only entity in existence, surrounded by the faint, fading heat of a cosmos that had just been unceremoniously deleted. He took one last, nostalgic look at the spot where Earth had been—where the screams still echoed in the depleted quantum foam—and He smiled.

“That was fun,” He murmured to Himself. Then He folded space and stepped back into the throne room.

Theron was still standing exactly where he had been hours earlier, his face pale as death, his hands trembling. The young wizard had, Godric knew, been watching the entire event through an astral projection the King had kindly left open for him. A learning experience, He’d called it.

“Well, my boy,” Godric said, settling back into His throne with a satisfied sigh. “That was an invigorating diversion. They had such creative imaginations. I’ll have to visit a few more untouched universes soon. Remind me, won’t you?”

“Y-yes, my King,” Theron whispered, his voice barely audible.

Godric beamed at him, the warm, fatherly smile returning in full force. “Don’t look so glum. Come, have some tea. I’ve brought back a souvenir.” He produced from thin air a small, charred, yet perfectly intact smartphone, still displaying a frozen, terrified face on its cracked screen. “They called this a ‘selfie.’ I think it captures the moment rather well, don’t you?”

Theron fainted. Godric chuckled, poured Himself a cup of jasmine tea, and settled in for a pleasant evening of reflection. The Apex Predator was, for the moment, content.
