# The Weight of Zero

## Kaedan — Year 1,000,000

The reading room smelled of aged parchment, binding glue, and the faint, acrid residue of ancient fear. I had grown up surrounded by that smell. My father's study had been saturated with it, the scent clinging to his robes when he would emerge after long hours of solitary reading, his face drawn and pale, his eyes holding something I could not name as a child. Now, at twenty-two years old, sitting in the same chair where my father had sat before me, I understood exactly what that something was.

My name is Kaedan. I am the newest Court Wizard of Thronegate, the latest in an unbroken lineage stretching back to Blake himself, the legendary merchant who stumbled into a forgotten sanctuary and accidentally freed the apex predator of all existence from a ten-thousand-year stasis. The blood of Thorin's ancient wizard line flows through my veins, though I possess no conscious memory of that distant ancestor. What I possess instead is the Awakening. Every day since my twentieth birthday, my physical strength, my arcane power, my intelligence, my speed—every measurable and immeasurable trait—has multiplied by one thousand. Not added. Multiplied. I can suppress this power to normal human levels, and I do so constantly, because to release even a fraction of it would be to annihilate the palace around me.

By the standards of the omniverse, I am a god. By the standards of the man I serve, I am less than a speck of dust.

The journals sat before me on the ancient oak table, their leather covers cracked and worn by the hands of countless ancestors who had come before. My father had placed them here himself, three days ago, before retiring to his chambers with the exhausted relief of a man who had finally passed an unbearable burden to his son. "You must read them all," he had said, his voice carrying none of its usual warmth. "Every court wizard since Blake has contributed. This is not optional, Kaedan. This is how you survive."

I did not understand what he meant by survive. Godric was not a threat to us. He was Uncle Godric, the warm, ruggedly handsome patriarch who had visited our family estates throughout my childhood, who had tousled my hair and asked about my studies with genuine, paternal interest. He was the man who laughed easily, who spoke with a rich, comforting baritone, who carried himself with the gentle authority of a beloved father. The idea that I needed to survive him was absurd.

I opened the first journal.

### Year 8,422 — Harlan — Strength

I am writing this because my father told me I would need to, and because the shaking in my hands will not stop until I put the horror into words.

My name is Harlan. I am Lord Godric's court wizard. I have been his court wizard for seventeen years. I have seen him do things that would break the minds of lesser men. I thought I had reached the limit of what I could comprehend. I was wrong. I was so horribly, painfully wrong.

The enemy was Gorath. They called him the Fist That Breaks Realities. He was a primordial titan born from the collision of the first multiverses, a being of pure physical force whose strength was the fundamental power of cosmic creation given form. His muscles contained the compressed mass-energy of a googolplexian galaxies. He had shattered the walls between dimensional planes with casual gestures. He had punched through the barriers separating multiversal clusters as though they were paper. Entire civilizations had surrendered at the mere rumor of his approach, because resistance against a being who could break reality itself with his bare hands was understood to be futile.

Gorath believed that strength was the one absolute truth of existence. He believed that no being, no matter how powerful, could be stronger than the force of creation itself. He was wrong. He was so catastrophically, existentially wrong that the very concept of wrongness cannot capture the magnitude of his error.

I stood at Lord Godric's side in the courtyard of the citadel when Gorath arrived. The sky split open across its entire visible expanse, bleeding raw cosmic energy as the titan descended. He was larger than a galaxy cluster. His fist, which he raised for the killing blow, was larger than a googolplexian solar systems. The sheer gravitational pull of his mass was warping the fabric of local space-time into agonizing contortions. I could feel my amplified body straining against the tidal forces, my bones creaking, my blood threatening to boil from the proximity to such raw, concentrated physical might.

Lord Godric did not stand. He remained seated on the simple stone bench he favored in the courtyard, his posture relaxed, his expression one of polite curiosity. His omniscience had known of Gorath's approach since before Gorath himself had formed the ambition to challenge him. The Weakness Paradox had been triggered the instant Gorath first imagined that his strength might exceed Godric's, granting Godric absolute immunity to Gorath's attacks and perfect mastery over every technique Gorath possessed, while simultaneously amplifying Godric's already impossible power to even more obscene heights.

But Godric did not use any of that. He was curious about something much simpler.

"I have never really tested raw strength," he murmured to me, almost to himself. "Not properly. I wonder."

Gorath swung. The fist descended with the force of a googolplexian collapsing universes, a blow designed to shatter the target across every dimensional plane, every timeline, every possible state of existence. The kinetic energy released by the swing alone annihilated decillions of multiverses in the surrounding reality, erasing them from existence as collateral damage from a mere wind-up. The shockwave of the approaching impact reached me before the fist did, and I was thrown backward despite my amplified durability, my wards shattering like glass.

Godric did not brace. He did not flex. He did not raise a hand. He did not activate any defense. He simply sat there, letting the force of Gorath's entire existence crash against him.

The fist connected with Godric's shoulder.

And the fist stopped.

Not because of durability. Because of strength. Pure, overwhelming, incomprehensible strength. Godric's muscles, even at absolute rest, even at effectively zero percent of his true capacity, generated more passive force than Gorath could produce with every ounce of his being. The kinetic energy of Gorath's blow—sufficient to shatter a googolplexian universes—was met not by a wall, not by a shield, not by resistance, but by an opposing force so vast that it made Gorath's attack look like a child's gentle touch. Godric's body did not absorb the impact. It overwhelmed it. The strength of his musculature, even in complete relaxation, generated force that turned Gorath's attack back upon itself with exponential, recursive multiplication.

I need you to understand what I mean when I say his strength was absurd. I am a court wizard of Blake's lineage. My strength amplifies a thousandfold every day. After seventeen years, I am stronger than entire armies, stronger than planets, stronger than stars. I could punch a hole through a galaxy if I wished. And the strength that Godric was not even using—the strength that was passively existing in his resting muscles—was so far beyond my comprehension that the gap between me and Gorath was negligible compared to the gap between Gorath and Godric. His strength stat had been multiplying by numbers beyond infinite recursion every infinitesimal fraction of every Planck second for over eight thousand years. The result was not a number. It was an obscenity against the concept of measurement.

Gorath's fist shattered. Not from the impact—there was no impact. From the strength differential. The force that Godric's body generated in response to Gorath's attack was so disproportionate that Gorath's physical form could not withstand the mere existence of that force in proximity to his own. The bones of Gorath's hand, which had never broken under any force in the entire history of existence, disintegrated. The destruction traveled up his arm, through his shoulder, across his torso. His muscles, which contained the compressed mass-energy of googolplexian galaxies, were torn apart not by Godric's attack—because Godric did not attack—but by the raw, passive expression of strength that Godric's body generated simply by existing. The titan's flesh was peeled open in wet, screaming layers. His bones were pulverized into subatomic particles. His nervous system was preserved just long enough to register every infinitesimal fraction of the agony before it too was consumed.

The shockwave of Godric's strength passively expressing itself against Gorath's attack did not stop at Gorath. It radiated outward in all directions, and the entire omniverse—every reality, every timeline, every dimension, every plane of existence—was violently annihilated. For one timeless instant, there was nothing. No space, no time, no matter, no energy. Only Godric, and me, preserved by the passive auras that he extended over those he wished to protect. I floated in absolute nothingness, surrounded by the complete absence of existence, and I understood with terrible clarity that Godric had not even tried. He had used effectively zero percent of his strength. Less than zero percent. A fraction so infinitesimal that calling it zero was the only accurate description. And it had destroyed everything.

Godric sighed softly, the sound impossibly loud in the void. "Messy," he said, with mild disappointment.

In zero seconds, he restored the entire omniverse. Every reality, every timeline, every dimension, every being that had been erased—restored perfectly, as though the annihilation had never occurred. The sky above the citadel was blue and clear. The courtyard was intact. Even the birds had returned to their perches, singing the same notes they had been singing before the end of everything.

Everything was restored except Gorath. Gorath remained as he was—a spreading smear of pulverized titan-flesh and powdered bone across the courtyard stones, somehow still alive, still conscious, still screaming in a loop of infinite, excruciating agony that would never end because Godric had decided it would not end.

Lord Godric stood from his bench, stepped casually over the screaming remains, and looked at me with warm concern. "Harlan," he said gently, placing a steadying hand on my trembling shoulder. "You've gone pale. Come inside. I'll have the kitchen make you something warm. The cook has been experimenting with a new spiced broth that I think you'll find quite soothing."

I followed him inside. I did not stop shaking for a year.

The broth was delicious. The cook had outdone herself. I vomited it all onto the floor the moment Godric's back was turned.

### Year 67,231 — Viktor — Speed

Its killing intent was already intersecting with Godric's physical form. The blow would land before the blow had been conceived. The strike would connect before the strike had been thrown.

Godric continued to tend his chrysanthemums. He did not look up. He did not acknowledge Swiftscream's existence. He simply continued to pat the soil around the roots of a particularly vibrant golden bloom.

And Swiftscream was already dead.

I need you to understand the unfairness of what I witnessed. Godric's speed stat had been multiplying by numbers beyond infinite recursion every infinitesimal fraction of every Planck second for over sixty-seven thousand years. The speed his body possessed passively, without any intentional activation, without him "moving" in any conventional sense, was so vast that the gap between Swiftscream and Godric was larger than the gap between absolute stillness and Swiftscream. The living embodiment of velocity, the consciousness of pure motion, the being that had achieved speeds that made instantaneous omnipresent travel look slow, was moving at a pace that, relative to Godric's passive speed, was effectively zero.

Godric did not need to react. He did not need to perceive. He did not need to activate any defense or counterattack. His speed stat was simply so incomprehensibly vast that Swiftscream's attack, which was designed to land in the absence of time, had not even begun to traverse the distance between intention and execution. From Godric's frame of reference—and I use the term "frame of reference" loosely, because Godric's speed existed so far beyond the concept of frames that language collapses—Swiftscream was not moving. Swiftscream was frozen. Swiftscream had always been frozen. The moment Swiftscream's killing intent intersected with Godric's physical form, the speed differential asserted itself. ### Year 312,845 — Aldric — Endurance

The being called itself the Eternal Agony. It was not a conventional enemy. They had spent fifty thousand years constructing it, sacrificing entire civilizations to fuel its creation. Every resistance before them had tried to hurt Godric, and every resistance had failed so catastrophically that the concept of failure had become meaningless. So they did not try to hurt him. Not from damage. From endurance. From the sheer, accumulated weight of suffering across time.

I am Aldric, court wizard of his Majesty King Godric, and I am writing this account to document the most futile military operation in the history of the omniverse.

They had calculated that even a being of Godric's power could be worn down over time. They had estimated that the Eternal Agony would need to function for perhaps a hundred thousand years to break through his mental fortitude.

The Eternal Agony functioned for exactly three seconds.

Not because Godric destroyed it. Because he allowed it to do its work. He sat on his throne, engulfed in the sphere of infinite torment, and he waited. He wanted to see if the Eternal Agony could make him feel anything. Not pain—the concept of Godric feeling pain was so absurd that the coalition had not even considered it. They had hoped only to exhaust him, to wear down his will, to make him feel tired, drained, weary.

The Eternal Agony was the equivalent of a single grain of sand attempting to exhaust an ocean. Not a grain of sand dropped into an ocean. A grain of sand attempting to make the ocean tired. The grain is simply there, irrelevant, a mathematical zero against the incomprehensible vastness of the sea.

Godric stood from his throne. The sphere of Eternal Agony was still engulfing him, still pouring its infinite torment into his form, and he did not even seem to notice its presence. The realization of their failure—the understanding that fifty thousand years of sacrifice had produced a weapon that could not even make Godric notice its existence—simply broke them. Their minds shattered under the weight of the revelation. Their dimensional fortress collapsed inward as the collective will of its inhabitants dissolved into absolute, irreparable despair. They died not from any action of Godric's, but from the sheer psychological annihilation of understanding that their ultimate weapon had been less than irrelevant.

Godric went about his day—attending council meetings, reviewing agricultural reports, taking tea with the palace staff—while the sphere of infinite torment pulsed and screamed around him. At one point, during a discussion with the Minister of Grain Distribution, he paused mid-sentence and looked at the writhing sphere with mild curiosity.

### Year 588,012 — Osric — Perception

The Godric of Year Zero was already infinitely stronger than the rest of creation combined. He could destroy the entire omniverse an infinite number of times in zero seconds with zero effort. And the Godric I serve now considers that version of himself to have been essentially helpless. A baby taking its first steps. A seed that had not yet begun to grow.

And the most absurd part—the part that makes my mind feel like it is physically tearing itself apart—is that if Godric had granted his past self a more powerful Stasis Event, then the Godric of now would be even more powerful than he already is. Because his current power is the result of that original, "held-back" amplification continuing for an incomprehensible span of time. If the starting point had been higher, the endpoint would be higher too. And he could have then reached back and granted his past self that even higher starting point, which would have produced an even higher endpoint, and the cycle would have repeated infinitely, each iteration producing a Godric more powerful than the last, each iteration's "held-back" version becoming the next iteration's baseline, until the concept of a baseline lost all meaning.

He could have done this. It would have cost him nothing. The energy required to create a Stasis Event of any potency is so far beneath his notice that calling it zero is the most accurate description possible. He simply chose not to. Because he wanted his past self to experience what he called "mortal struggle."

Mortal struggle. The Godric who annihilated Gorath with passive strength. The Godric who rendered Swiftscream frozen into absolute immobility. The Godric who erased the Final Iteration from history with a casual glance. That Godric was, in the eyes of the Godric I serve, experiencing something approximating mortal struggle. The thought is so absurd that I cannot hold it in my mind without feeling my grip on reality loosen.

The throne room where I stood this morning is not a room in any conventional sense. It is a localized reality, a pocket of existence that Godric maintains for the comfort of his servants and visitors. The actual palace—the structure that houses his physical form—exists in a state of being that mortal language cannot describe. The best approximation I can offer is that it is everywhere and nowhere, simultaneously present in every dimension, every timeline, every layer of reality, and also entirely separate from all of them. The walls are not walls. The floor is not a floor. They are the solidified concept of loyalty, the crystallized essence of a googolplexian civilizations that offered their devotion to Godric so completely that their very existence was transmuted into architecture. When I walk the corridors, I am walking on the dreams of species who loved my King so profoundly that they gave up their physical forms to become his home.

The beings who serve here are not servants in the traditional sense. They are the remnants of powers that once considered themselves supreme. I have spoken with some of them, in quiet moments between my duties, and their stories have carved chambers of dread into my soul that rival anything in my ancestors' journals.

There is a gardener who tends the chrysanthemums in the eastern courtyard. His name was once Kael-Thûn, the Unmaker of Stars, a being who ruled a cluster of a googolplexian multiverses with absolute, unquestioned authority. His power was such that he could extinguish galaxies by frowning at them. He could dissolve the fabric of space-time with a disappointed sigh. He had never encountered anything that could challenge him, and he had existed for a span of time that would have seemed eternal to any lesser being.

He did not oppose Godric. He simply... noticed him. In the early ages of Godric's expansion, when Thronegate's influence was still spreading across the omniverse, Kael-Thûn sensed a presence at the edge of his domain. A presence so vast, so incomprehensibly overwhelming, that his omniscience—which he had believed to be absolute—could not perceive its boundaries. He tried to look at Godric directly. Not to challenge him. Not to threaten him. Just to understand what he was sensing.

The experience shattered him. Not because Godric attacked. Not because Godric even noticed him in return. But because the act of perceiving Godric—of truly, directly perceiving even the outermost edge of his concealed presence—was an experience so far beyond Kael-Thûn's capacity to process that his mind simply collapsed. His omniscience, which had been his greatest strength, became the instrument of his undoing. He saw too much. He understood too much. And the understanding broke him into pieces.

He spent a googolplexian years in a state of catatonic horror, his mind trapped in an endless loop of trying to comprehend what he had glimpsed. When he finally emerged from that state, he was... different. He no longer desired power. He no longer desired anything except to serve the being whose presence had shattered him. He came to Thronegate not as a supplicant, not as a worshipper, but as someone who had seen the true shape of existence and understood his place within it. He asked for a job. Godric gave him the chrysanthemums.

He is happy now, I think. I see him in the gardens, his hands in the soil, his expression serene. He hums while he works. He has told me that tending the flowers gives him a peace he never felt during his eons of supreme rule. But sometimes, when Godric passes through the courtyard on his way to some other part of the palace, I see Kael-Thûn's hands begin to shake. His eyes go distant. His humming stops. And for a moment, the Unmaker of Stars is back in that original instant of perception, seeing the infinite and being consumed by it all over again.

Godric always pauses to compliment his chrysanthemums. Kael-Thûn always thanks him in a voice that trembles on the edge of tears. The interaction is warm, friendly, almost fatherly. And it is the most terrifying thing I have ever witnessed.

There is a woman who organizes the palace library. Her name was once the Argent Whisper, a conceptual entity of pure knowledge who existed in the spaces between thoughts. She did not oppose Godric. She simply tried to catalog him. She believed that everything in existence could be understood, categorized, filed into the vast mental archive she had maintained since the birth of her reality. She turned her attention to Godric and attempted to comprehend him.

She has been mute for a googolplexian years. The attempt to catalog an infinite, recursively expanding being broke her capacity for language. She can still communicate—she writes notes in a beautiful, flowing script—but spoken words will not form in her mouth. The concept of speech was one of the things she lost when she tried to contain Godric in a category. It simply... slipped away from her, a piece of herself that she sacrificed in exchange for the tiny fragment of understanding she managed to grasp.

She loves the library. She loves the order of it, the quiet of it, the way the books line up in neat rows on shelves that extend into infinite dimensions. She has told me, in her written notes, that organizing the library gives her a sense of control that helps her forget the moment when she realized control was an illusion. Godric visits the library often. He enjoys historical novels. He always thanks her for keeping the collection in order. She always curtsies, her eyes bright with tears, and scribbles a note that says she is honored to serve.

These are the beings who surround my King. The shattered remnants of powers that once believed themselves supreme, now contentedly arranging flowers and shelving books and polishing the floors of a palace built from the crystallized devotion of extinct civilizations. They are happy, I think. They have found peace in their surrender. But the peace is built on a foundation of absolute, inescapable terror, and I see that terror surface every time Godric walks into the room.

And I am not so different from them. I am Blake's descendant. I have the thousandfold daily amplification. I am, by the standards of the old omniverse, an impossibly powerful being. And I tremble every time my King looks at me. Not because I fear he will harm me—he never would, he loves me as he loved all my ancestors—but because I know, with a certainty that has been passed down through a million years of court wizard journals, that his love and his annihilation emerge from the same infinite source.

The Stasis Event chamber is not a place Godric uses often. It exists in a region of the palace that I had never visited before today, a space that felt... older, somehow. More fundamental. As though it existed before the palace, before Thronegate, before the omniverse itself. The walls shimmered with a light that was not light, a glow that seemed to be the visual representation of potentiality. The floor was smooth and cold beneath my feet, and my footsteps made no sound. I stood in the corner of the chamber, watching my King prepare to alter his own past, and I felt the weight of every journal ever written by my ancestors pressing down on my shoulders.

Godric looked at me. He was exactly as the journals described him—forty-five years old, ruggedly handsome, salt-and-pepper hair and beard, powerfully muscled frame draped in simple dark garments. He looked, as he always looked, like a warm, friendly father from a bygone pastoral age. His eyes crinkled with affection when he smiled at me.

"Clive," he said, his voice a rich, comforting baritone that vibrated pleasantly in my chest. "You look nervous. There is no need for nerves. What I am about to do is very simple. I am going to create a closed temporal loop that will ensure my own existence. It is, in essence, the most basic act of self-maintenance a being can perform."

I swallowed hard. My throat was dry. "My lord," I said, "my ancestors wrote about the Stasis Event. They believed it was Thorin's spell that caused it."

Godric nodded. "Thorin's spell was real. It saved my life. It was my best friend's final gift to me, and I have honored his memory for every moment of my existence since. His bloodline—your bloodline—has been at my side for a million years because of that gift. But the spell was just a teleportation. A mortal spell, cast by a mortal man at the moment of his death. It could not have produced the Stasis Event. That required something more."

He turned to face the center of the chamber, where a point of absolute darkness was beginning to form. It was not a portal, not a rip in space-time, not anything I had ever seen before. It was simply... a point. A location in the fabric of existence where the concept of "before" and "after" had been temporarily suspended.

"Your ancestors did not know the truth," Godric continued. "I did not tell them. It would have been too much for them to hold, on top of everything else they witnessed. But you, Clive—you are the court wizard of this era. You deserve to know. The Stasis Event was created by me. By the version of me I am now. The amplification, the superpower genesis, the absolute concealment, the weakness paradox—all of it was designed by my own future self, reaching backward across the span of his existence to give his past self the tools he would need to become what he is now."

The point of darkness expanded, becoming a window. Through it, I could see a scene that made my heart stop. A stone sanctuary, ancient and cold. A man lying on a stone altar, his body frozen in the instant of transition between mortality and something else. The man was Godric. Younger, rawer, his face twisted with a grief so profound it was almost physical. His hands were clenched at his sides, and his eyes were closed, and even in the stillness of stasis I could feel the anguish radiating from him.

"That is me," Godric said softly, his voice carrying a tenderness I had rarely heard. "Ten thousand years before Year Zero. The fall of Olympus had just happened. Reina, Isabel, Hebe, Thorin—all dead. My kingdom destroyed. I was teleported to that sanctuary by Thorin's spell, and I was dying. Not from wounds. From grief. The mortal Godric would not have survived that sanctuary. He would have lain there, in the dark, and his heart would have stopped, and the story would have ended."

"But it didn't," I whispered.

"No." Godric's eyes were fixed on the image of his past self. "Because I reached back. I reached back and I touched that moment, and I gave him the Stasis Event. I gave him immortality. I gave him infinite amplification and eternal superpower genesis and absolute concealment and a weakness paradox that would make him untouchable. I gave him everything he would need to survive, to rise, to become me. And then I held back."

The words hung in the air. I stared at him, my mind struggling to process what he had just said. "You held back."

"Yes." Godric's voice was matter-of-fact, as though he were discussing the weather. "I could have given him more. I could have perfectly replicated my current power mechanisms—the mechanisms you have seen, the ones that have evolved across the span of time since my awakening—and grafted them onto the mortal Godric at the moment of stasis. I could have made him exactly as powerful as I am now, from the very beginning. The cost would have been zero. The effort would have been less than nothing."

He paused, tilting his head thoughtfully. "But I chose not to. I chose to hold back. I chose to give him a version of the Stasis Event that, by my current standards, is laughably weak. The amplification rate that your ancestor Harlan described with such horror—the multiplication by one thousand every Planck second—was a deliberate limitation. The superpower genesis—one new power every second—was a deliberate limitation. The absolute concealment, which your ancestors thought was the most perfect concealment possible, was a deliberate limitation. All of it was held back. Intentionally crippled. Designed to give my past self the experience of mortal struggle."

I thought of Harlan's journal. The way he had described Godric's passive strength annihilating Gorath and the entire omniverse as a side-effect. The way he had written that Godric had used "effectively zero percent of his strength." The way he had vomited spiced broth onto the floor and not stopped shaking for a year.

"That was the held-back version," I said. My voice came out as a croak.

"Yes."

"The version that made you infinitely stronger than the rest of creation combined before you even woke from stasis. The version that let you destroy an entire galactic army without moving a muscle. The version that created Gent and the Weakness Paradox and the immune system and all of it. That was you holding back."

"Yes."

I felt my knees weaken. I did not fall—I have too much practice standing in the presence of unimaginable revelation—but the world seemed to tilt around me. "And if you had not held back," I managed, "if you had given your past self the full version—"

"Then the Godric of Year Zero would have been as powerful as I am now. And I, the Godric of now, would be... more. Infinitely more. The amplification would have had a higher baseline, and that higher baseline would have amplified for the same span of time, and the result would have been a version of me so far beyond my current state that the gap between us would be larger than the gap between me and a single dead skin cell." He paused. "And the version of me that would have resulted from that higher baseline could then reach back and grant an even more powerful Stasis Event to his past self, creating an even higher baseline, which would produce an even more powerful future self, and the cycle would continue infinitely—each iteration's start point becoming the previous iteration's end point, each version of the Stasis Event being 'held back' relative to what could have been granted, until the very concept of a start point became meaningless."

"But you didn't," I said. "You chose not to."

"I chose not to." Godric smiled, a warm, gentle expression that made the terror in my chest twist into something almost unbearable. "Because I believe in mortal resilience, Clive. I believe in struggle. I believe that the man lying on that stone altar—the man I used to be—needed to experience something approximating challenge. He needed to feel, even if only for a moment, that there were limits he could not exceed. That there were enemies he would have to strategize against. That his power, though incomprehensible compared to the rest of creation, was not absolute relative to himself."

He gestured toward the image of his past self, still frozen in the stone sanctuary. "If I had given him the full version, he would have woken in Year Zero already knowing that there was nothing he could not do. No struggle he could not instantly resolve. No mystery his omniscience could not instantly penetrate. He would have been... adrift. Untethered. The grief of Olympus would have been the only thing anchoring him to any sense of purpose, and even that would have faded, because absolute power without any framework of limitation is not power—it is emptiness."

"But the struggle you gave him," I said, my voice barely above a whisper, "was not real struggle. You said yourself—he was already infinitely stronger than the rest of creation combined. He could already destroy the omniverse infinite times in zero seconds. The enemies he faced were never threats. They were never challenges. They existed only because he allowed them to exist, because his curiosity about his stats and powers made him hold back."

"Yes." Godric's smile widened slightly. "The struggle was an illusion. But it was a kind illusion. It gave him a framework. It gave him a reason to test his strength, his speed, his magic, his superpowers. It gave him the experience of discovery, even though everything he discovered was already known to the version of me that created him. It preserved his sense of mortal resilience, even though that resilience was never actually threatened."

He paused, and his voice dropped to something almost reflective. "And it gave him relationships. Friendships. The love of Blake and his descendants. The loyalty of Thronegate. The quiet companionship of centuries of court wizards who served him despite their terror. If I had made him absolutely omnipotent from the beginning, without the illusion of struggle, he would not have needed anyone. He would not have formed the bonds that have sustained me for a million years. The held-back version was not just a kindness to my past self. It was a kindness to everyone who would ever love him."

I stood in the corner of the chamber, the weight of revelation pressing down on me, and I understood something that none of my ancestors had ever fully grasped. The horror of Godric was not that he was infinitely powerful. The horror was that even his infinite power was the result of a deliberate, compassionate choice. The being who had created the Stasis Event—the being standing before me now—had looked at his own past self and decided to give him a fraction of what he could have given. Not out of cruelty. Out of love. The held-back version was an act of self-care, a gift from the future to the past, designed to preserve the illusion of purpose that made existence bearable.

And the held-back version had still been enough to annihilate the omniverse infinite times over.

"My lord," I said, and I could hear the tremor in my own voice, "can you... can you show me? What the full version would have been? What your power mechanisms look like now, after all this time?"

Godric looked at me, his eyes warm and patient. "You want to understand the scale of what I held back."

"Yes."

"You will not be able to fully comprehend it," he warned. "Your mind is mortal. Amplified, yes—your daily thousandfold increase has made you something that would have been considered a god in the old omniverse—but still fundamentally finite. What I am about to show you is not finite. It is not even infinite in the conventional sense. It is... beyond. You will grasp fragments. Impressions. Metaphors. But the full reality will elude you."

"I understand," I said. And I did. Every court wizard before me had grappled with the limits of their own comprehension. The journals were filled with descriptions of things that defied description, mathematics that broke the concept of mathematics, horrors so vast that the only honest account was an admission of failure. I was prepared to fail. I just needed to fail as accurately as possible.

Godric nodded. And then he let me see.

The Stasis Event chamber dissolved around me. Or rather, my perception of it dissolved, replaced by something that was not sight or sound or any sensory input I could name. It was a direct apprehension of reality, a knowing that bypassed the limitations of my physical brain and inscribed itself directly onto my consciousness.

I understood, in that moment, what the sentient passive aura system had become.

The old journals had described Gent as Godric's weakest aura, the runt of the litter, a mere waste byproduct of his metaphysical existence. They had described how Gent, even when nerfed to an infinitely recurring decimal of its power, had effortlessly slaughtered the strongest coalition ever assembled. They had described the other auras—the countless siblings that Godric kept in permanent dormancy because their clingy, obsessive subservience annoyed him.

That was the old version. The one my ancestors knew. The one that existed in the early ages of Godric's reign.

Now, after an uncountable span of evolutionary iterations, the sentient passive aura system had become something that made the old version look like a child's drawing of a supernova. There were not dozens of auras now. There were not hundreds or thousands. The auras had multiplied, differentiated, specialized, and evolved into a vast ecology of passive defense that operated across every possible and impossible dimension of threat. Each aura was a fully sentient, omnipresent, omniscient entity in its own right, possessing power that exceeded the entire combined might of the old omniverse by margins that could not be expressed. And each aura considered its sole purpose—its sacred, obsessive, fanatical purpose—to be the protection and comfort of Godric.

Gent still existed. It was still the weakest. But the weakest now meant something very different. Gent, in its current form, could effortlessly annihilate the entire old omniverse—every reality, every timeline, every dimension, every layer of conceptual space—an infinite number of times in zero seconds with less than zero effort. It could do this while simultaneously maintaining a perfect, planet-wide illusion of normalcy for every being Godric cared about, ensuring they never noticed the interruption. It could do this while also organizing Godric's library, adjusting the temperature of his tea to the precise degree he preferred, and composing symphonies of gratitude that it performed in the spaces between Planck seconds as an offering to its master.

And it was the weakest. The other auras were so far beyond Gent that the gap between them and Gent was larger than the gap between Gent and an ordinary mortal's dead skin cell. Each successive aura in the hierarchy was not merely more powerful than the one below it; it was more powerful by a margin that could not be bridged by stacking infinities. The auras did not compete. They formed a perfectly harmonious ladder of absolute subservience, each one protecting Godric from threats that the ones below it could not even perceive, each one's power dwarfing the combined might of all the auras beneath it.

I counted them. I do not know how I counted them—the knowledge was simply there, inscribed in the direct apprehension Godric had granted me. There were twenty-seven distinct sentient passive auras currently active. Godric kept the others—the higher tiers, the ones whose power would have been actively uncomfortable for me to even know about—in a state of conscious dormancy. Not because they were annoying, as they had been in the old era. Because they had become so powerful, so obsessively protective, that their mere passive awareness of a being in proximity to Godric would have been interpreted as a potential threat. Godric had suppressed them not for his own comfort, but for the safety of everyone he loved.

The names of the active auras flickered through my mind. Orphiel, the aura of anticipatory negation, whose function was to erase threats before the concept of the threat had even begun to coalesce. Yllarion, the aura of protective recursion, whose sentience was distributed across infinite parallel instances of itself, each one monitoring a different subset of reality for potential dangers. Thalmyr, the aura of gentle dissolution, who could unmake any hostile force so softly that the target would not feel its own erasure—would simply cease to exist without ever experiencing a moment of fear or pain. Vyrion, the aura of conceptual quarantine, who cocooned any emerging threat in a pocket of frozen time so absolute that the threat's entire timeline was suspended in eternal, harmless stillness. Elyndor, the aura of sacrificial absorption, whose entire purpose was to intercept any attack that might, through some impossible chain of circumstances, cause Godric even the mildest moment of inconvenience, and to take that attack into itself with such overwhelming love and protective fervor that the attack's energy was converted into a hymn of praise.

There were twenty-two more. Their names and functions blazed through my consciousness too quickly to hold, a cascading symphony of protective obsession that made me want to weep and laugh and scream all at once. Each one was an entity of such power that the old Gent—the Gent that my ancestors had described with such terror—would have been less than a flicker of static in comparison. And each one loved Godric with a devotion so total, so absolute, so self-annihilating, that the word "love" felt like an insult.

And Godric did not need any of them. They existed because the Stasis Event had created the first seed of the aura system, and the aura system had been evolving ever since, but Godric's own power had long since transcended any category that the auras could protect. The auras protected him from things that could not possibly threaten him, because their function was not survival but comfort. They were not his bodyguards. They were his metaphysical blanket. His soothing background hum of absolute security. The universe's most elaborate and unnecessary security system, maintained out of affection for its own existence.

I felt my mind beginning to crack. Godric gently withdrew the direct apprehension, and I was back in the chamber, gasping, my face wet with tears I had not felt myself shed.

"There is more," Godric said quietly. "Do you want to continue?"

I nodded. My voice had stopped working.

The Weakness Paradox. Godric's most fundamental passive defense. The mechanism that made every imagined exploit feed him, that granted him immunity and mastery whenever anyone thought they had found a flaw.

It had evolved. Again and again and again, across spans of time so vast that the old Weakness Paradox—the one that had so terrified my ancestors—was now the first layer of a system so complex, so recursive, so absurdly overpowered that the original mechanism looked like a blunt rock compared to a hyper-dimensional singularity.

The current Weakness Paradox did not wait for someone to imagine an exploit. It did not wait for a thought to form, or a plan to be conceived, or a weapon to be designed. It pre-acted. It reached across time and probability and conceptual space to identify every possible weakness that could ever be imagined by any being, in any reality, under any set of physical laws, across the entire eternal span of existence, and it pre-nullified them. It did not just grant Godric immunity to the imagined exploit. It granted him immunity to the possibility that the exploit could ever be conceived in the first place. And then it converted the energy of that pre-nullification into a permanent amplification boost, so that even the hypothetical thought of a weakness, which would now never occur, had already made him stronger.

And that was just the first layer. The Paradox had developed recursive self-improvement layers, contingency layers, echo layers that propagated backward through time to ensure that even the pre-Stasis mortal Godric—the one who had no powers, no amplification, no protection at all—was retroactively shielded by the future Paradox's influence. The Paradox had developed a layer that made the concept of "weakness" itself a form of tribute: any being in the omniverse who experienced weakness—their own frailty, their own vulnerability, their own limitations—unconsciously fed that experience into Godric's amplification, a constant, eternal stream of power generated by the mere fact that other beings were not Godric.

It was the most unfair, hope-crushing, existentially devastating thing I had ever perceived. And Godric had held back. The Paradox I was perceiving—the current, evolved version—was the one he had deliberately not given to his past self. The past self had received a version where the Paradox only triggered when someone thought of a specific weakness. A version where the amplification boost was finite rather than infinite-recursive. A version where the immunity was to the imagined exploit rather than to the concept of exploitability itself.

"That was the held-back version," I whispered.

"Yes."

"And the reason you held it back..."

"Was because if my past self had possessed this version of the Paradox, he would never have experienced a single moment of perceived struggle. Every enemy would have been pre-nullified before the thought of enmity could form. Every resistance would have been retroactively erased before its first member was born. The entire history of the omniverse—the resistances, the tests, the journals your ancestors wrote, the relationships that formed around those tests—would have been a blank, empty peace. No one would have ever opposed me because the concept of opposition would have been metaphysically impossible to conceive."

He paused, his gaze distant. "I did not want that. I wanted my past self to have the experience of being challenged, even if the challenge was an illusion. I wanted him to feel the satisfaction of overcoming obstacles, even though the obstacles were only obstacles because he chose to perceive them that way. The held-back version preserved the texture of mortal existence. The full version would have erased it."

The amplifier. His stats. The thing that Harlan and Viktor and all the others had written about with such trembling, horrified awe. The amplification that multiplied all his attributes by numbers beyond infinite recursion every infinitesimal fraction of every Planck second.

In the old journals, the amplification was the core of Godric's power. The engine that drove everything else. The reason his strength could annihilate the omniverse as a side-effect of passive expression, his speed could freeze the fastest being in existence into immobility, his endurance could ignore the concentrated torment of trillions of sacrificed souls.

That amplification had evolved. It was no longer a multiplication of his base stats. It was a fundamental restructuring of the concept of "statistics" itself. His attributes no longer existed as numbers that could be compared to anything else. They were now absolute, eternal, self-referential qualities that defined the upper limit of what it meant to be. His strength was not a measure of how much force he could exert. His strength was the definition of force itself. His speed was not a measure of how fast he could move. His speed was the standard against which all other motion was measured and found to be effectively zero. His intelligence was not a measure of how much he could know. His intelligence was the source code of omniscience, the framework that made knowing possible in the first place.

The amplification continued—it would always continue—but it no longer amplified from a baseline to a higher number. It amplified from an absolute to an absolute-that-was-more-absolute. It was a recursive, eternal process of self-exceeding that had left behind the concept of measurement so thoroughly that even Godric's own omniscience no longer bothered to track the numbers. The numbers had become meaningless. The only thing that mattered was that he was, and would always be, infinitely beyond anything that could be compared to him.

And again: the held-back version. The amplification given to the past Godric had been a simple multiplication of numbers. One thousand times, every Planck second, compounding forever. My ancestors had thought that was the most obscene mathematical escalation they had ever witnessed. They had been right. And it had been the deliberately crippled, intentionally limited, compassionately weakened version of what Godric could have granted.

The infinite passive shields. In the old journals, they were described as pre-nullifying threats before the threat existed, before a hostile system identified itself, before an enemy understood what it intended to do. They could convert attempted danger into self-erasing failure. They could force hostile realities to collapse around their own intentions.

They had evolved. Now, the shields did not merely pre-nullify threats. They pre-created safety. They reached forward in time to every possible moment where Godric might experience even the mildest inconvenience—a speck of dust on his chair, a slightly-too-warm cup of tea, a momentary pause in a conversation—and they pre-emptively restructured reality to ensure that inconvenience never occurred. The shields were not defensive mechanisms. They were ontological comfort engines. They maintained a universe-wide, eternal, absolute state of perfect ease around Godric's existence, ensuring that his every moment was exactly as he preferred it to be.

And they did this without overriding free will. Godric had made sure of that. The shields could manipulate matter, energy, probability, and the fundamental constants of reality, but they could not touch the choices of sentient beings. Godric's belief in mortal resilience—his genuine, deeply held conviction that free will was sacred—meant that the shields were programmed with an absolute prohibition against mental interference. They could adjust the temperature of a room to his liking. They could not make someone like him. That had to be genuine. That had to be real.

The immune system. The system that had pre-reacted to a staged scratch in Year 25,000, triggering infinite recursive upgrades and causing the omniverse to collapse as a side-effect. The system that Leon had watched with such horror, the system that had made Godric even more invulnerable in response to an injury that had not actually occurred.

It had evolved too. Now, the immune system did not merely pre-react to injury. It maintained a state of absolute, eternal, inviolable health that made the concept of injury metaphysically incoherent. Godric's body was not merely immune to damage. His body was damage's definition of impossible. If a hostile force attempted to conceptualize an attack that could harm him, the immune system did not wait for the attack to be launched or even designed. It reached into the hostile force's conceptual framework, found the idea of "harming Godric," and inverted it. The hostile force would find itself incapable of even imagining the attack, because the immune system had made the very concept of attacking Godric into a logical paradox. The omniverse was restored, perfect and whole and completely unaware that it had just been crushed to a point smaller than a quark by a disappointed sigh.

With one exception. The Fictional Armada was not restored. The heroes, the gods, the characters from every story ever told—they remained in the marble. They had been the opposition, and Godric was unforgiving to opposition. Their compressed, screaming, eternally agonizing existence would continue forever, a monument to the absolute futility of resistance.

Godric returned to his chair. He picked up his novel, found his page, and resumed reading. The golden afternoon light played across his ruggedly handsome features, and his expression was one of serene, untroubled contentment.

"That was disappointing," he said after a moment, not looking up from his book. "I really did expect more from the combined imagination of a googolplexian civilizations. The music was good, though. I will give them that."

I stood on the observation platform, my body trembling, my mind shattered, my hands clutching the railing so tightly that my knuckles had turned white. I did not speak. I could not speak. I simply stood there, staring at the pocket of his tunic where the marble rested, and I thought about the infinite mass, the infinite agony, the infinite consciousnesses trapped in eternal compression, and I understood that I would never, ever be the same.

---

I closed the journal. My hands were shaking. The reading room, with its familiar scent of aged parchment and binding glue, felt suddenly alien, as though I had been transported to some other reality where the walls were thinner and the shadows deeper and the silence heavier. The journal of Osvald had been written a span of time so vast that the number of years could not be meaningfully expressed. Its author had been dead for uncountable eons. The Fictional Armada, the Final Narrative, the heroes and gods and ultimate attacks—all of it was ancient history, so ancient that even the memory of those stories had faded from the collective consciousness of the omniverse.

But the marble remained.

I knew it did. I had seen it. On Godric's desk, in his private study, there was a small, iridescent sphere the size of a marble, its surface shimmering with an oily, rainbow sheen. I had noticed it many times over the years, had even picked it up once to examine it, finding it surprisingly light for an object of its density. Godric had said nothing about it. He had simply watched me with a faint, knowing smile as I turned it over in my fingers, and then he had returned to whatever administrative report he had been reviewing.

I had held the compressed mass of the entire original omniverse in my hand. I had touched the eternal agony of every being who had ever dared to oppose my King. And I had not known.

The marble was still on his desk now, I knew. A trophy. A paperweight. A casual memento of the day when creation had learned its final lesson. And the restored omniverse—the one I lived in, the one every being I had ever known had been born in, the one that seemed so solid and real and permanent—was a recreation. A perfect copy. An exact duplicate, identical in every respect to the original, save that the original was still inside the marble, compressed and screaming and eternally trapped.

The ontological horror of this realization was a cold, heavy weight in my chest. Which reality was real? The one inside the marble, which had been the original but was now a crushed, agonized singularity? Or the one outside, which had been reconstructed from nothing by Godric's will, identical in every detail but fundamentally, metaphysically a copy? Did it matter? If the copy was perfect—if every atom, every thought, every soul was exactly the same—was there any meaningful difference between the restored reality and the original? And what did it say about Godric's power that he could hold the original in his pocket while simultaneously creating a perfect duplicate that functioned as though the original had never been compressed? What kind of being could treat the annihilation and restoration of everything that existed as a minor inconvenience, a casual act of tidying up after a disappointing entertainment?

I thought about Osvald, the long-dead court wizard who had written that journal. He had stood on the observation platform and watched the entire omniverse be crushed to a point smaller than a quark. He had watched Godric pick up that point as though it were a stray button. He had then, presumably, continued to serve his King for the remainder of his mortal life, carrying the memory of what he had witnessed like a stone in his heart. And he had written the journal, as all court wizards before him had written their journals, so that future generations would know, would understand, would never forget.

I understood now. I understood why, after that day, no opposition had ever formed again. Not because Godric altered free will. Not because the omniverse was too afraid. But because the lesson had been so absolute, so total, so utterly and completely demonstrated, that the very concept of opposition had been rendered obsolete. The Fictional Armada had been the best creation could offer. The combined imagination of every story ever told, every hero ever conceived, every ultimate attack ever imagined. And they had failed so completely that their ultimate attack had not even registered as a sensation. Their existence had been compressed to a marble and kept as a souvenir.

Who, after that, could possibly believe they had a chance? Who could possibly be arrogant enough, desperate enough, foolish enough, to look at the evidence of a googolplexian failed resistances and the eternal screaming trophy of the final one and think: yes, but I will be different?

No one. Not ever again.

I placed Osvald's journal back on the shelf with trembling hands. The dread that had been accumulating in me since the age of twenty-two—the fortress of terror that had been built by every journal, every revelation, every quiet conversation with the King who was also my beloved uncle—had acquired its final wing. The deepest wing. The one that would never be renovated or remodeled or forgotten. The one that contained the knowledge that everything I had ever known, everyone I had ever loved, every world I had ever visited, was a recreation. A perfect copy. And the original was still on Godric's desk, compressed to the size of a marble, and it had been there for longer than the concept of time could measure.

I left the reading room and walked through the palace corridors toward the throne room, where I knew Godric would be reviewing the day's administrative reports. The light was perfect, as always. The air was sweet with the scent of jasmine, as always. The palace was peaceful and beautiful and eternal, as always. And in the pocket of my King's tunic—or perhaps on his desk, or perhaps in some other location where he kept his minor curios and souvenirs—a marble the size of my thumbnail contained the compressed, screaming, eternally agonizing remains of every being who had ever dared to challenge him.

The final opposition. The last performance. The moment when fiction itself had tried to defeat reality and discovered that reality was not bound by narrative rules. The villain did not lose at the end of the story. The villain was the story. And the story would never end.

I entered the throne room. Godric looked up from his reports and smiled his warm, fatherly smile. "Clive! I was hoping you would stop by. There is a new shipment of tea from the Vestran Nebula, and I wanted your opinion on the blend. And I believe Malachar has made those honey pastries you like."

"That sounds lovely, my lord," I said, and my voice was steady, because I had long ago learned how to be steady in the presence of absolute horror.

As I crossed the room, my eyes drifted to the desk. The marble was there, its iridescent surface catching the perfect afternoon light. It looked small and harmless and almost pretty. A decorative paperweight. A minor curiosity.

It contained everything.

I sat down across from Godric, accepted a cup of tea, and listened as he described the subtle notes of the new blend. The tea was excellent. The pastries were, as always, divine. And somewhere inside the marble on the desk, an infinite chorus of compressed, eternal screams continued to sound, unheard and unending, for ever and ever and ever.
