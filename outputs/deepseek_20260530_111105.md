Every single day. Kaedan had asked why. His father had not answered with words. He had simply pointed at the first shelf and closed the heavy stone door behind him.

Now Kaedan understood. He had been reading for three days without sleep, his amplified constitution rendering rest unnecessary but doing nothing to protect his mind from the accumulating weight of what he was absorbing. The journals were not merely records. They were confessions. They were attempts at self-preservation through documentation. And when it finally ended—when the last echoes of the choir faded, when the light dimmed, when the fictional armies collapsed in exhaustion, their narrative reserves completely depleted—there was silence.

A long, terrible, absolute silence.

Godric was still sitting in his chair.

His tunic was not singed. His skin was not marked. His tea cup, still resting on the side table, had not even developed a crack. He was blinking, his expression one of mild surprise—not the surprise of someone who had been hurt, or even someone who had been touched, but the surprise of someone who had been expecting something and had received nothing at all.

He looked down at his chest, where the beam had struck. He brushed a bit of imaginary dust from the fabric. And then he looked up at me, and his face was filled with an emotion I had never seen on him before.

Disappointment.

"That was it?" he asked.

The words were soft. Quiet. Almost plaintive. They carried across the void with the weight of absolute, crushing finality. The fictional armies, still recovering from their exertion, heard them. The archetypes, still flickering from the depletion of their narrative mass, heard them. The choir, their voices hoarse and broken, heard them.

"That was the ultimate attack? The combined narrative mass of all fictional existence? The great crossover event to end all crossover events?" Godric shook his head slowly, his salt-and-pepper beard swaying gently. "There were no phases. The music was good, but it never shifted into the second movement. The light show was spectacular, I will grant you that, but there was no substance behind it. No weight. No impact. I felt nothing. Not even a tickle. Not even the faintest suggestion of a tickle. It was like being struck by a particularly aggressive sunbeam."

He stood up from his chair. The movement was casual, unhurried. He brushed off his tunic again, as if trying to remove a stain that did not exist. "I waited for subjective eons," he said, his voice carrying a note of genuine, wounded disappointment. "I cleared the stage for you. I distracted myself from my omniscience so I could be surprised. I let you charge your ultimate attack without interference. I removed all my passive defenses. I let the beam hit me directly in the chest. I did everything I could to give you the best possible chance to put on a good show."

He looked at the fictional armies, his eyes—those ancient, terrifying, infinitely wise eyes—sweeping across the assembled legions of heroes and villains and gods and monsters. "And you gave me nothing. A pretty light show and some nice music. That is all. You are the greatest storytellers in creation. You have moved audiences to tears and laughter and righteous fury for eons. And when you were given the opportunity to tell the greatest story of all—the story of the final battle against the ultimate unbeatable boss—you forgot the most important rule of storytelling."

He paused, letting the silence stretch. "You forgot to make the boss feel something."

And then, in his disappointment, he accidentally let slip a fraction of his strength.

---

I must be precise in describing what happened next, because the precision is the only thing that keeps the memory from overwhelming me entirely.

Godric did not attack. He did not swing a fist or unleash a superpower or activate any of his evolved mechanisms. He simply—in the moment of his disappointment, in the instant of his letdown, in the emotional anticlimax of a show that had failed to deliver—stopped holding back. Not consciously. Not deliberately. The restraint he maintained around his true capacity, the infinite self-suppression that kept the omniverse from collapsing under the weight of his existence, flickered. Just for an instant. Just for a fraction of a fraction of a Planck instant.

And zero percent of his strength expressed itself.

The effect was not gradual. It was not incremental. It was absolute, instantaneous, and total.

The fictional armies had erected shields during their preparation. They were not ordinary shields. They were narrative shields, forged from the concept of invulnerability itself, woven from the accumulated belief of every sentient being who had ever wanted the heroes to survive. They were layered infinite times, each layer representing a different story, a different myth, a different desperate last stand where the shield had held against impossible odds. They were, by any rational standard, indestructible. They had taken subjective eons to construct. They were powered by the hope of all fictional existence.

They did not slow the pressure of Godric's strength by even a Planck instant.

The shields did not shatter. They did not crack. They simply ceased to be. The concept of invulnerability was overridden by a force so immense that the concept itself became meaningless. You cannot be invulnerable against a pressure that does not recognize the distinction between something and nothing. The shields were not broken. They were invalidated. They were made irrelevant by the simple, undeniable fact of Godric's existence.

And the pressure continued.

The fictional armies—the heroes and villains and gods and monsters, the warships and giant robots and magical constructs, the archetypes and the choir and everything they had brought with them—were caught in the expanding field of Godric's accidental strength. They did not die immediately. Death was too slow for what happened to them. The pressure was so immense, so complete, so utterly beyond any possible threshold of resistance, that their physical forms were instantly, simultaneously de-atomized.

I must describe this in detail, because the detail is what will haunt me for the rest of my existence.

The de-atomization was not a simple disintegration. It was a violent, agonizing unraveling of every particle of their being. Their atoms did not merely separate. They were crushed. Compressed. Forced together by the sheer gravitational weight of Godric's passive strength, even as the pressure was tearing them apart. The heroes who had shattered planets with their fists felt their own bones collapse inward, their muscles liquefy, their skin peel away in layers of screaming, bloody mist. The warships the size of planets crumpled like paper, their hulls folding and refolding into increasingly dense configurations of crushed metal and pulverized flesh. The giant robots, their pilots still inside, were compressed into spheres of fused organic and inorganic matter, the screams of their occupants trapped inside the collapsing metal like insects in amber.

The gods—the beings who had been worshipped by trillions across fictional realities—experienced the full, agonizing dissolution of their divine forms. Their ichor boiled. Their celestial flesh rendered into superheated gas. Their consciousness, which had endured for subjective eternities, was forced to experience every individual moment of their own destruction, stretched across a subjective duration that made the charging period of their ultimate attack feel like a fleeting instant. They felt their divinity being stripped away, layer by layer, until nothing remained but the raw, screaming awareness of their own mortality.

The choir—the voices that had sung of hope and triumph and the indomitable spirit of heroes—kept singing. Not because they chose to. Because the pressure was so absolute that it trapped the sound waves inside their disintegrating bodies, forcing their vocal cords to vibrate even as their lungs collapsed and their throats filled with blood. The music that had been so beautiful, so triumphant, so full of hope, warped into a discordant, agonizing shriek of pure, unadulterated torture. It was the sound of a symphony being murdered. It was the sound of every story ever told being erased in a single, infinite moment of crushing, compressive agony.

And the archetypes—the original heroes, the templates of all fictional virtue—suffered worst of all. Because they understood. They understood, in the final moments of their compressed consciousness, exactly what they had done. They had gathered all of fiction, all of imagination, all of the creative spirit of every sentient being who had ever existed, and they had thrown it against a being who could not be touched. They had sacrificed everything for a show that the audience had found wanting. They had written the ultimate crossover event, and the final boss had given it a bad review.

Their flesh, their bones, their divine essences, their narrative cores—all of it was crushed together, compressed by the zero-percent expression of Godric's strength into a single, infinitesimal point. The pressure did not stop. It continued, driving the mass of all fictional existence into a space smaller than an atom, smaller than a quark, smaller than any measurement that mortal science had ever conceived. The entire collective of fictional reality—every hero, every villain, every god, every monster, every warship, every robot, every magical construct, every archetype, every choir member—was squeezed into a point of infinite density, a singularity of compressed narrative mass that contained the screaming, agonized echoes of every story that had ever been told.

And that point, smaller than a quark, heavier than the entire omniverse, hung in the void where the fictional armies had once stood.

Godric, still standing beside his chair, looked at it. His expression had shifted from disappointment to mild, almost sheepish embarrassment. "Ah," he said. "I may have overreacted."

---

The collateral damage was absolute.

The zero-percent expression of Godric's strength did not stop with the fictional armies. It propagated outward, across the void, through the dimensional barriers, through the abstract planes, through every reality and timeline and multiverse and meta-dimensional layer. The entire omniverse—everything that existed, could ever exist, had ever been conceived or would ever be imagined—was caught in the expanding pressure wave.

The de-atomization of the fictional armies was only the beginning. Every galaxy, every universe, every multiversal cluster experienced the same violent, instantaneous compression. Stars collapsed into singularities and then into smaller singularities and then into points smaller than the smallest possible measurement. Planets and their inhabitants were crushed together, their matter forced into configurations that violated every law of physics. The gods of every pantheon, the abstract entities, the cosmic titans, the temporal architects, the sentient civilizations scattered across the infinite reaches of existence—all of them were subjected to the same agonizing, inescapable compression.

I watched it happen. Gent, Godric's sentient passive aura, had extended a protective bubble around me, preserving my consciousness even as the rest of existence was being crushed into an infinitesimal point. I watched the omniverse die. Not as an abstract concept, but as a visceral, immediate, horrifyingly detailed reality. I heard the screams of quadrillions of quadrillions of sentient beings, their voices blending into a single, continuous shriek of absolute, inescapable agony. I saw their bodies torn apart at the atomic level, their flesh rendered into superheated plasma, their bones pulverized into subatomic particles, their consciousness trapped in the collapsing architecture of their own nervous systems, forced to experience every Planck instant of their destruction.

And at the center of it all, the focal point of the compression, was the singularity that had once been all of fictional existence. It was the seed around which the omniversal collapse was organized. Every atom of every reality was being drawn toward it, crushed into it, added to the infinite mass of the compressed narrative core. The singularity grew heavier with every passing instant, its gravitational pull intensifying beyond any possible measurement, but it did not grow larger. It remained a point smaller than a quark, a point of infinite density that contained the screaming, compressed remains of everything that had ever been.

The process took less than a Planck instant. And then it was over.

There was nothing. No space. No time. No matter. No energy. No reality. No omniverse. Only the void, and the protective bubble around me, and Godric, standing beside his chair, looking at the infinitesimal point of infinite mass that now contained the entirety of all possible existence.

He reached out with his right hand—casually, unhurriedly, as if he were picking up a pebble from a garden path—and he took the singularity between his thumb and forefinger.

There was no strain. There was no effort. The point of infinite mass, the compressed remains of everything that had ever existed or could ever exist, weighed nothing to him. Less than nothing. He held it up to the light, peering at it with the curious, slightly disappointed expression of a man examining a flawed gemstone.

"Messy," he murmured. "Very messy. I really did not intend to do that." He sighed softly. "I let my emotions get the better of me. How embarrassing. Blake would have scolded me for hours."

He looked at the singularity for another moment, then made a small, casual gesture with his free hand. The singularity expanded. Not to its original size—that would have unleashed the infinite mass back into the void—but to the size of a marble. A small, perfectly spherical marble, dark as the void itself, pulsing with a faint, inner luminescence that was the compressed light of every star that had ever existed.

"A trophy," Godric said, his voice regaining some of its earlier warmth. "To remember the occasion. The final opposition. The last time anyone dared to try. I should have something to commemorate it, even if the show was a disappointment." He rolled the marble between his fingers, the infinite mass inside it contained by a barrier of pure, absolute will. "I will put it on my desk. It will make a fine paperweight."

Then, without any visible effort, without any gesture or word or flicker of intention that I could perceive, he restored the omniverse.

Every galaxy, every universe, every multiverse, every timeline, every abstract plane. Every sentient being that had been crushed into the singularity was restored to their original state, their memories intact, their existence seamless. They would not remember being compressed. They would not remember the agony. They would simply continue their lives, unaware that the entire omniverse had been destroyed and recreated in less than a Planck instant.

Except the fictional armies. They remained compressed in the marble. The marble still contained their infinite mass, their narrative essence, their collective existence. Godric had not restored them. He had decided they would remain a trophy.

And that was when the final horror struck me.

If the marble contained the compressed remains of everything—the fictional armies, yes, but also the entire omniverse that had been caught in the collateral damage—then what had Godric just restored? The original matter of existence was still inside that marble, crushed to infinite density, screaming in eternal, silent agony. The omniverse I was now perceiving, the reality that surrounded me, was a copy. A recreation. An identical duplicate, generated from Godric's infinite memory, indistinguishable from the original in every possible way, but not the original. The original was in his hand, reduced to a paperweight.

The implications shattered something inside me. If he could do this—if he could compress all of existence into a marble and then recreate it from scratch, seamlessly, effortlessly, in zero seconds—then what was reality? What was existence? Were we all just copies? Were we the original restored, or were we something new, something fabricated, something that existed only because the original had been destroyed and the King had decided to make a replacement? Did it matter? Did the distinction between original and copy hold any meaning when the being who made the copy was also the being who had made the original, who had made everything, who was the source and the substance and the end of all that was?

I do not know. I will never know. But I know this: the marble is still on Godric's desk. It contains everything that ever was, crushed into a sphere the size of a child's toy, held together by a barrier of pure will. And the being who holds it does not even notice its weight.

---

After the restoration, Godric returned to his chair and picked up his tea cup. The tea was still warm. The lemon cakes were still fresh. He took a sip, and then he looked at me with that warm, fatherly smile.

"Well," he said, "that was a disappointment. But not a total loss. I did enjoy the music, at least until it turned into screaming. And the marble will look quite nice on my desk, once I clear some space." He paused, tilting his head slightly. "Are you all right, Osvald? You look pale. Perhaps you should sit down. Have a lemon cake. They are very good. The chef used a new recipe—something about stellar nucleosynthesis, I believe. I did not quite follow the details, but the results are excellent."

I sat down. I did not eat a lemon cake. I simply sat, staring at the marble in his hand, and I felt the full, crushing weight of what I had witnessed settle into my soul.

The last opposition. The final attempt. The greatest crossover event in the history of fiction, and it had ended in a single, accidental expression of zero percent of Godric's strength. The heroes and villains and gods and monsters of every story ever told had been compressed into a paperweight. And the only reason the omniverse still existed was because Godric had decided to make a new one.

I am writing this journal in the hope that some future court wizard will read it and understand. Understand that opposing Godric is not impossible. It is not merely futile. It is a category error. It is the mistake of thinking that the boss can be beaten, when the boss is not part of the game. The game is part of the boss. The game has always been part of the boss. And the boss can end the game whenever he wishes, and start a new one, and keep the old one as a souvenir.

I do not think anyone will try again. After this, after the total, absolute, instantaneous annihilation of everything that ever was, I think the lesson will finally stick. No one will dare. No one will even think about daring. The concept of opposition will become a fossil, a curiosity, a relic of a more primitive age when sentient beings still believed they had a chance.

And Godric will continue to sit in his chair, drinking his tea, eating his lemon cakes, growing more powerful every Planck instant, forever. The marble will remain on his desk. The copy will remain indistinguishable from the original. And I will remain here, writing this journal, trying to capture in words an event so vast and so terrible that the words themselves seem to recoil from the page.

I do not think I will ever stop trembling.

*— Osvald, Court Wizard to King Godric*

---

Clive closed the journal.

His hands were shaking. The preservation wards in the archive were humming with that strange, unfamiliar frequency he had noticed when he first opened the book. The leather binding, scorched and resealed, felt warm against his palms. He stared at the final page for a long, silent moment, and then he looked up at the shelves around him.

Thousands of journals. Tens of thousands. A million years of recorded dread. And this one—Osvald's journal—marked the end. The last opposition. The final time anyone had dared to raise a hand against King Godric. After this, the shelves fell silent. No more coalitions. No more desperate strategies. No more ultimate attacks with banger boss music and multiple phases. Just the quiet, peaceful, orderly records of an empire that had learned, at last, the absolute futility of resistance.

Clive thought about the marble. He had seen it. On Godric's desk, in the private sanctum, a small dark sphere that seemed to absorb the light around it. He had assumed it was a decoration. A trinket. A memento from some forgotten era. He had never asked about it. He had never even looked at it closely.

An identical duplicate, generated from Godric's infinite memory, indistinguishable from the original in every way that mattered. But the original still existed. Compressed. Contained. Trapped forever in a sphere the size of a child's toy, held together by the King's casual will.

No one would ever oppose King Godric again. Not because they could not. Not because their will had been altered or their capacity for defiance removed. But because they had learned. All of creation had learned. The lesson had been taught in the compressed screams of every fictional hero who had ever inspired hope, and it had been preserved in a marble that sat on a desk, unnoticed and unremarked, for eons beyond counting.

Forever.
