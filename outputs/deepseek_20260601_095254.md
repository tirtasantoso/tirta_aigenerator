# The Godric Stat Experiment

## Kaedan — Year 1,000,000

The reading room smelled of aged parchment and the faint, lingering ozone of preservation wards that had been recast across millennia. I sat at the long obsidian table, my hands flat against the cold stone, and I stared at the stack of leather-bound journals my father had placed before me three hours ago. My name is Kaedan. I am twenty-two years old. I am the newest Court Wizard of Thronegate, a direct male descendant of Blake, the legendary merchant who had stumbled into a hidden sanctuary and accidentally released the apex predator of all existence from a ten-thousand-year stasis.

My father, himself a broken man in ways he never spoke about, had stood in the doorway of this same room when I was a child and told me that one day I would sit where I now sit. "You will read them all," he had said, his voice carrying a weight I did not then understand. "Every single one. It is not optional. It is not ceremonial. It is survival. You must understand exactly what you serve before you stand at his side."

The journals were written by my ancestors. Every court wizard since Blake had contributed, and every contribution was a scar left bleeding onto parchment. They were bound in chronological order, the oldest volumes near-crumbling despite the preservation spells, the newer ones still bearing the faint scent of the ink used to write them. The collection had a name, passed down through the generations with the grim humor of men who had seen too much.

The Godric Stat Experiment.

I opened the first volume. The handwriting was cramped and urgent, the letters pressed so hard into the page that the quill had nearly torn through in places. I began to read.

Erebus, sitting on his throne of compressed dying universes, looked at the forty-five-year-old man in simple dark garments and felt the first stirrings of something he had never felt before. Not fear. Not yet. But a faint, creeping sense that the universe he understood was not the universe that actually existed.

Godric did not threaten. He did not display his power. He simply allowed Erebus to understand. He opened a microscopic conduit between his omniscience and Erebus's finite mind, and he let the god-king perceive the shape of the gap between them.

Erebus spent three hundred years in a catatonic state. When he emerged, his empire was gone—not destroyed, but dissolved by his own command, its territories absorbed into Thronegate, its legions dispersed, its throne of compressed universes dismantled and scattered across the void. Erebus himself came to Thronegate. He knelt before Godric in the royal gardens and begged for permission to serve.

Godric granted it. He made Erebus a gardener.

That was an incomprehensible span of years ago. Erebus still tends the roses in the eastern courtyard. He is a quiet, gentle being now, his once-terrifying voice reduced to a soft murmur, his once-boundless power suppressed to the level of a simple mortal. He speaks to the roses more than he speaks to anyone else. But sometimes, when the light catches his eyes at a certain angle, I see the echo of what he once was—a god-king who learned the shape of infinity and had his mind permanently shattered by the understanding.

The second is Lyra. She was not a ruler. She was a weapon. Forged by a coalition of dying pantheons in the last era before opposition ceased entirely, Lyra was designed to be the ultimate anti-Godric countermeasure. Her body was woven from the fundamental threads of reality-rejection. Her consciousness was programmed with a single, overriding directive: locate and exploit the weakness of the target designated as Godric.

She was the most sophisticated weapon ever created. Her designers spent a billion years on her construction, pouring the resources of a trillion dying universes into her programming. She was designed to be unstoppable, unkillable, utterly immune to any defense that relied on prediction or pre-emption.

She was activated. She located Godric. She analyzed him with senses that could perceive the fundamental structure of existence itself. And she found nothing. No weakness. No exploit. No vulnerability. Her programming, the single directive that defined her existence, encountered a target that simply did not possess the quality she was designed to find. She spent a full second—an eternity for a being of her processing speed—searching every possible layer of reality for something, anything, that could be exploited.

She found the truth instead. The truth that Godric's one limitation—the sealed pre-Stasis past—was not a weakness but a guarantee. The truth that his power originated from a version of himself so incomprehensibly vast that even the current Godric could not challenge it. The truth that she had been designed to find a crack in a wall that had no surface, no edge, no existence in any dimension she could access.

Her programming crashed. Her consciousness fractured. And in the instant of her collapse, she did something her designers had never anticipated: she converted. Not through any action of Godric's—he simply stood there, watching her with mild curiosity—but through the sheer, overwhelming force of the realization that she had been built to achieve something that was definitionally impossible.

Lyra is now the head groundskeeper of the palace. She maintains the hedges and the ornamental trees with a devotion that borders on obsession. Her reality-rejection abilities, once designed to unmake the fabric of existence, are now used to keep pests away from the royal gardens. She is happy, I think. Or at least, she is at peace. But sometimes, when Godric walks past her in the garden and offers her a warm smile and a kind word, I see her hands tremble. Not from fear—or not only from fear—but from the residual echo of that first, shattering moment when she looked at him and understood that she had been built to fail.

These are the beings who serve in Godric's palace. Former god-kings. Former ultimate weapons. Former entities of unimaginable power, now reduced to humble servants, their sanity preserved only by the merciful suppression of their own memories and the genuine, baffling kindness of the being who unmade them.

And I am their colleague. Their coworker. Their fellow servant in the court of the Eternal King.

## The Evolved Mechanisms

Before I describe what I witnessed today, I must also describe the current state of Godric's power mechanisms. The ancient journals speak of systems that were already absurd, already incomprehensible, already sufficient to make opposition impossible. Those systems have evolved. Not once. Not a thousand times. Countless times. So many times that the concept of counting evolutions has become meaningless.

The sentient passive auras, for example. The ancient journals speak of Gent, the weakest and most primitive of Godric's auras, a being so pathetically weak compared to Godric that calling it his excess body heat was generous. Gent, even infinitely nerfed, could annihilate the strongest coalition ever assembled in less than a fraction of a second. Gent, with its perfect adaptation and sentient omniscience, was considered the runt of the litter—the least powerful of Godric's passive defenses, so weak that Godric found it amusing.

Gent no longer exists as it once did. It has evolved so many times that the original Gent would be less than a single atom compared to an infinite ocean of what it has become. The current aura systems—and there are now layers upon layers of them, recursive hierarchies of sentient passive defense that make the old Gent-and-siblings structure look like a child's drawing of a fortress—operate on principles that cannot be described in any language that exists.

The weakest of the current sentient passive auras, nerfed to an infinitely recurring decimal of its power, could pre-annihilate everything that has ever existed or will ever exist across all timelines, all dimensions, all possible states of reality, before the concept of a threat even forms. Not before the threat manifests. Not before the threat is planned. Before the possibility of a threat exists as a coherent concept. The aura reaches backward into the logical foundations of reality and severs the connection between "intention to harm Godric" and "ability to exist."

And this is the weakest aura. The strongest ones—the ones Godric keeps dormant not because he fears their power but because their clingy subservience annoys him—are so far beyond this that attempting to describe them would require inventing new categories of existence.

The passive shields have evolved similarly. The ancient shields were already capable of pre-nullifying threats before the threat existed, converting attempted danger into self-erasing failure, forcing hostile realities to collapse around their own intention. The current shields do not merely pre-nullify. They retroactively and futuristically erase the conceptual framework that would have allowed the threat to be conceived. If a weapon is forged anywhere in the omniverse with the intention of harming Godric, the shields do not destroy the weapon. They destroy the intention. Not the specific intention of that specific weapon—the entire category of intention, across all timelines, across all possible beings, across all possible states of existence. The shield reaches into the fundamental architecture of consciousness and removes the possibility of forming hostile intent toward Godric.

Godric suppresses this effect. He believes in mortal resilience. He believes in free will. He will not allow his shields to alter the fundamental capacity of sentient beings to make their own choices, even when those choices are suicidal. So the shields are calibrated to exclude this function, their full power held in check by his explicit command.

The immune system has evolved beyond biological function into something that resembles a metaphysical tyranny of absolute protection. The ancient immune system could pre-react to injuries, initiate infinite recursive self-evolution, and trigger upgrades across infinite backup chains. The current immune system treats the concept of harm as an entity to be hunted, cornered, and erased. If a rogue timeline or impossible weapon carries even the theoretical variable needed to inconvenience Godric, the immune system does not wait for the variable to manifest. It hunts the variable across every possible state of existence, every timeline, every dimension, every abstract plane of reality. It corners the variable in the logical space between concepts. And it erases it.

Not destroys. Erases. The variable never existed. The possibility of the possibility never existed. The immune system rewrites the fundamental mathematics of reality to exclude the concept of Godric being inconvenienced.

The Weakness Paradox has evolved so far beyond its original form that calling it the "Weakness" Paradox has become a cruel joke. The ancient paradox triggered when any being thought Godric had a weakness, granting him permanent immunity, perfect mastery, and increased amplification. The current paradox does not require a trigger. It is perpetually active. The instant any sentient being anywhere in the omniverse forms any thought about anything—not just about Godric, not just about his weaknesses, but about literally anything at all—the paradox engages. Every thought becomes a vector for his improvement. Every consciousness becomes an unwitting engine of his amplification. The entire sentient population of the omniverse, simply by existing and thinking, feeds his growth.

And because the paradox also grants him permanent immunity to the imagined exploit and perfect mastery over the attack, he has now accumulated every possible immunity and mastery that could ever exist, across every possible conceptual framework, across every possible state of reality. There is nothing left to grant him. The omniverse has nothing left to give. And yet the paradox continues to search for new immunities, new masteries, new ways to make the unstoppable even more unstoppable.

The amplification has evolved beyond mathematics. The ancient amplification multiplied his stats by numbers beyond infinite recursion every infinitesimal fraction of every Planck second. The current amplification operates on a scale so vast that calling it "amplification" is like calling a supernova a faint warmth. His strength, his speed, his endurance, his magic, his intelligence, his wisdom, his perception, his durability, his charisma, his luck—every measurable and immeasurable trait—multiplies by itself at a rate that makes the old rate look perfectly static. The interval of multiplication is no longer a Planck second or an infinitesimal fraction of a Planck second. It is a duration so small that the concept of duration collapses under the weight of its smallness.

The superpower genesis has evolved similarly. The ancient genesis birthed one new apocalyptic superpower every second, each one capable of causing instant extinction to an entire civilization. The current genesis births new superpowers at a rate that exceeds the old rate by a factor that exceeds infinity. Each new superpower, at the instant of its birth, is already more powerful than all the superpowers that existed in the ancient era combined. And each of those new superpowers also has its own enhanced effects, granted by the Evolved Weakness Paradox, making them even more absurdly, superfluously devastating.

I could continue. There are other systems. Other layers. Other redundancies built upon redundancies, each one more terrifying than the last, each one completely unnecessary because any single one of them could protect Godric from everything that exists. But you understand the shape of it. The ancient Godric—the Godric of the stat journals, the Godric who could annihilate the omniverse infinite times over with 0 effort in 0 seconds—was already the most powerful being in existence by an infinite margin. The current Godric surpasses that Godric by a margin so vast that the gap between them makes the gap between the ancient Godric and the rest of creation look like an error of measurement.

And I serve him. I stand at his side. I call him by his name and he calls me by mine. He asks about my children. He laughs at my jokes. He tends his roses with the same calloused hands that could crush the omniverse into a singularity so dense that the concept of density would collapse.

The warmth is genuine. The kindness is real.

And today, I watched him reach across time to create himself.

## The Stasis Event

The day began like any other in the Eternal Court. Godric was in the royal gardens, as he often is, his hands working the soil with the practiced ease of a man who has been gardening for a span of years best measured in googolplexians. Erebus was tending the roses in the eastern courtyard, his ancient eyes focused on the crimson petals with an intensity that bordered on meditation. Lyra was trimming the hedges, her reality-rejection abilities keeping the ornamental shrubs in perfect geometric alignment.

I approached Godric with my daily report, a summary of the empire's affairs that he did not need because he already knew everything. He looked up as I approached, his ruggedly handsome face creasing with a warm smile.

"Clive," he said, his voice a rich, comforting baritone. "Sit with me. The soil is particularly receptive today."

I sat on the stone bench beside him, the warmth of the eternal morning sun pleasant on my fifty-year-old skin. For a moment, neither of us spoke. The silence was comfortable, familiar, the easy quiet of two men who had known each other for three decades.

Then Godric set down his trowel.

"I have something to do today," he said. "Something I have been planning for a very long time."

I felt my chest tighten. Godric's tone was different—not cold, never cold to me, but heavier. There was a weight beneath his words that I had never heard before.

"Your Majesty?"

Godric looked at me, and for the first time in thirty years of service, I saw something in his ancient eyes that I could not identify. It was not fear. It was not doubt. It was... anticipation. The quiet, patient anticipation of a man who has been waiting for a span of time that made the age of the omniverse feel like a heartbeat.

"I am going to create the Stasis Event," he said.

The words hung in the air like a physical weight. I stared at him, my mind refusing to process what he had just said. The Stasis Event. The origin of his power. The moment, ten thousand years before Year 0, when the mortal King of Olympus fell into a hidden sanctuary and began his transformation into the apex predator of all existence.

He was going to create it. Not witness it. Not revisit it. Create it.

"You," I managed to whisper, my voice barely audible. "You are the origin. You have always been the origin."

Godric nodded, his expression thoughtful. "The Stasis Event was never caused by Thorin's spell. Thorin's spell was exactly what it appeared to be—a forbidden teleportation incantation, exclusive to his bloodline, powerful enough to send my past self to the hidden sanctuary. But the transformation that followed—the stopping of my aging, the granting of immortality, the beginning of the amplification, the superpower genesis, the absolute concealment, the Weakness Paradox—that was my doing. My current self, reaching back across time to touch my past self at the moment of his greatest despair."

He paused, and his ancient eyes grew distant. "I have been planning this for a very long time, Clive. Since before the oldest stars in the omniverse were born. I knew, from the instant my omniscience came online in that sanctuary, that I was the author of my own transformation. And I knew that eventually, I would reach this moment—the moment when I would become the creator of myself."

I sat in stunned silence. The weight of what he was saying pressed against my skull like a vice of absolute, suffocating revelation. I had read the ancient journals. I had read Kaedan's account of the revelation in Year 1,000,047, when Godric had first told his court wizard about the true origin of the Stasis Event. I had known, intellectually, that the Stasis Event was created by Godric's far-future self. But knowing it intellectually and sitting beside the being who was about to do it were two entirely different categories of experience.

"How?" I asked, my voice hoarse. "How will you do it?"

Godric smiled, a gentle, almost paternal expression. "I will show you."

## The Reaching

The omniverse did not tremble. The omniverse had long since learned that trembling in Godric's presence was a futile gesture. But something shifted. Something in the fundamental architecture of reality rearranged itself to accommodate his intention.

Godric did not move. He did not stand. He did not gesture. He did not speak any words of power. He simply sat on the stone bench beside me, his calloused hands resting on his knees, and he directed a fraction of his attention toward the past.

The fraction was so small that calling it zero percent of his true focus would have been an overstatement of its magnitude. It was less than zero percent. It was an infinitesimal sliver of his awareness, the merest whisper of his intention, so thoroughly negligible that the universe had no language to describe its smallness. And it was still more than enough.

I felt it happen. Not through my physical senses—my physical senses were not designed to perceive temporal manipulation on this scale—but through a deeper, more fundamental awareness that my thousandfold daily amplification had granted me over thirty years of service. I felt the fabric of time part before his will like water before a hand.

The moment he was reaching toward was Year -10,000. The Fall of Olympus. The instant when Thorin's forbidden teleportation spell had torn the dying mortal king from the burning pavilion and hurled him into the dark unknown. The instant when a man who had just watched his wife, his daughters, and his best friend die in a storm of arcane fire was suspended between existence and oblivion.

Godric was there. Not physically—his current body remained on the stone bench beside me—but his will, his intention, his power, was present at that moment with an immediacy that transcended the concept of presence. He was not observing the past. He was touching it. Shaping it. Remaking it according to his design.

And the design was the Stasis Event.

I watched through senses I did not fully understand as the sanctuary received its occupant. The dying mortal king, his body broken, his heart shattered, his mind reeling with the fresh agony of loss, materialized on the cold stone floor. And in the exact instant of his arrival, Godric's will descended upon him.

The Stasis Event began.

The mortal king's aging stopped. Not gradually. Not through any process that could be observed. Simply stopped, as though the concept of aging had been removed from his existence by a decision that was absolute. His cells ceased their slow march toward death. His telomeres locked into perfect stability. His biological clock was not paused; it was erased, replaced by a new fundamental truth: this body would never age again.

The mortal king's need for sleep, for air, for food, for water, was severed. Not suppressed. Not delayed. Severed. The biological imperatives that had governed his existence for forty-five years were simply removed, their absence filled by a self-sustaining perfection that required nothing from the outside world. He would never tire again. He would never hunger again. He would never draw another breath unless he chose to.

The mortal king's mortality dissolved. The fundamental vulnerability that defined all living things—the certainty that life must eventually end—was lifted from him like a shroud. His flesh became immutable. His existence became absolute. He would never die. No force in the omniverse could kill him. Not because he was durable enough to survive any attack, but because the concept of death had been removed from his personal reality by a decision that could not be challenged.

And then the amplification began. The mortal king's attributes—his strength, his speed, his endurance, his intelligence, his wisdom, his magic, his perception, his charisma, his luck, everything measurable and immeasurable—began to multiply. Not by a thousandfold every Planck second, as the ancient journals described for the early eras. That would come later, after the Weakness Paradox evolved. In this initial moment, the amplification was... gentler. A foundation being laid. A seed being planted. But even that foundation, that seed, was already so potent that the mortal king would emerge from his ten-thousand-year stasis effectively infinitely stronger than the rest of creation combined.

Simultaneously, the superpower genesis ignited. One new apocalyptic superpower every second, each one capable of causing instant extinction to an entire civilization at the moment of its birth. The first power to manifest was Absolute Concealment, and I understood with sudden, terrible clarity why the ancient journals always noted that concealment was the very first superpower to crystallize. It had to be. Without it, the mortal king's growing presence would have annihilated the omniverse before he even awakened.

The Weakness Paradox activated. The fundamental law that would make all perceived exploits self-defeating. The mechanism that would grant him permanent immunity, perfect mastery, and increased amplification whenever any being thought he had a weakness. It was a closed loop of absolute protection, a system that ensured every attempt to challenge him would only make him stronger.

All of this happened in the space between Thorin's spell completing and the mortal king's back striking the cold stone floor. A single instant. A single moment. And in that moment, the entire trajectory of existence was permanently altered.

I watched it happen, and I felt my mind begin to crack.

Because I understood, with sudden, terrible clarity, that what I was witnessing was the held-back version.

## The Holding Back

Godric spoke, his voice calm and conversational, as though he were explaining the pruning of roses. "I could have granted him much more. My current mechanisms—the evolved auras, the evolved shields, the evolved immune system, the evolved Weakness Paradox, the evolved amplification, the evolved superpower genesis—I could have copied all of them perfectly into that moment. I could have made the Godric of Year -10,000 as powerful as I am now, or at least as powerful as I was at some earlier stage of my current self. There would have been no cost to me. Not even an infinitesimal fraction of my power would have been expended."

He paused, and a faint, almost amused smile crossed his ruggedly handsome face. "But I chose not to. I held back. I gave him a version of the Stasis Event that was, by my current standards, absurdly gentle. Almost laughably restrained. The amplification began at a thousandfold every Planck second. The superpower genesis birthed one new power every second. The Weakness Paradox required a trigger to activate. All of these systems were, by my current standards, pathetically weak."

He looked at me, and his ancient eyes sparkled with something that might have been nostalgia. "And even that held-back version made him, at Year 0, effectively infinitely stronger than the rest of creation combined. The gap between him and the omniverse was already incomprehensible. And yet, compared to what I could have given him, it was nothing. Less than nothing."

The sheer, absurd ridiculousness of his words crashed over me like a tidal wave of existential vertigo. He was describing a version of the Stasis Event that had birthed the most powerful being in existence—a being who could annihilate the omniverse infinite times over with 0 effort in 0 seconds—and calling it gentle. Restrained. Pathetically weak.

And he was right. By his current standards, it was. The amplification that had multiplied his past self's stats a thousandfold every Planck second was a crawl compared to the current rate, which operated at speeds that made the concept of speed irrelevant. The superpower genesis that had birthed one new power every second was a trickle compared to the current rate, which generated new abilities at frequencies that exceeded infinity. The Weakness Paradox that required a trigger was primitive compared to the current version, which was perpetually active.

"Why?" I managed to ask, my voice barely a whisper. "Why hold back?"

Godric's expression grew thoughtful. "Because I believed—and I still believe—in mortal resilience. I believed that my past self needed to experience the world from a perspective that was not entirely divorced from the struggles of ordinary existence. I believed that if I made him too powerful too quickly, he would lose the capacity to understand the beings he would eventually rule. The horror of the ancient journals—the stat tests, the superpower tests—that horror came from the fact that he was still, in some small way, connected to the scale of mortal experience. He could still be surprised by his own power. He could still be fascinated. He could still discover, through testing, what he was capable of."

He paused, turning his gaze toward the roses. "I wanted to preserve some kind of mortal struggle for him. The sense that there was still something to learn, something to explore, something to test. If I had given him everything at once—if I had made the Godric of Year 0 as powerful as I am now—he would have been... bored. Detached. Incapable of the very warmth and kindness that has defined his rule. The struggle, even the illusion of struggle, was essential to shaping the being he would become."

He chuckled softly. "Of course, I was wrong. The 'struggle' I preserved for him was never a real struggle. From the moment he awakened in Year 0, he was already infinitely more powerful than anything that could oppose him. The stat tests, the superpower tests—none of them were ever real challenges. They were just... play. Exploration. The illusion of discovery that I had manufactured for him by withholding the full scope of what I could have given."

The absurdity of it made my head spin. Godric had deliberately made his past self weaker—had deliberately withheld an incomprehensible amount of power—in order to preserve some semblance of mortal experience. And the result, even with that deliberate withholding, was a being who could annihilate the omniverse infinite times over with less than 0 effort in 0 seconds.

It was like a god deciding to give a child a gentle push instead of a full-force shove, and the gentle push sending the child through a mountain.

"But there is something else," Godric said, his voice growing quieter. "Something that I did not fully understand until this moment."

He looked at me, and his ancient eyes were deep with a realization that was still settling into his consciousness. "The version of the Stasis Event I am granting to my past self is the foundation upon which all future amplification will be built. The Godric of Year 0, with his thousandfold amplification and his one-superpower-per-second genesis, will grow across ten thousand years, then a hundred thousand years, then a million years, then spans of time so vast that language fails. And at every stage, his power will compound upon itself. The higher his base, the higher his ceiling. The stronger he becomes, the stronger he can become."

He paused, letting the implications settle. "And eventually, he will reach this moment. The moment when he triggers the Stasis Event for his own past self. But by then, he will be even more powerful than I am now. Because the foundation I am giving him, even held back, is still the foundation from which I grew. And if I had given him a stronger foundation—if I had copied my current mechanisms perfectly into that moment—then the being who eventually reached this moment would have been even more powerful than I am now. And he, in turn, would have given his past self an even stronger foundation. And the cycle would compound. Forever."

I stared at him. The causal loop was spinning in my mind like a vortex of infinite recursion. Godric creates the Stasis Event for his past self. His past self grows into the Godric who creates the Stasis Event. But if Godric makes his past self stronger, then the future Godric who reaches this moment will be stronger, and he will make his past self even stronger, and the cycle will compound infinitely, each iteration producing a version of Godric more powerful than the last.

It was an infinite feedback loop of self-creation. A closed circle of amplification that had no beginning and no end. Godric was not merely the author of his own existence. He was the author of his own infinite growth. Every decision he made about the Stasis Event echoed forward and backward through time, compounding upon itself forever.

"And you still held back," I whispered, my voice barely audible.

Godric nodded. "I held back. I gave my past self only what I believed he needed. And even that, as you have witnessed, was more than enough to make him the most powerful being in his era by an infinite margin. The rest—the evolution of the mechanisms, the compounding of the amplification, the endless layering of new and more terrifying abilities—that came later. That was earned, not given. That was the result of the journey, not the gift."

He smiled, a warm, genuine expression that crinkled the corners of his eyes. "I believe in mortal resilience, Clive. Even now, after everything I have become, I believe that struggle—real or illusory—is essential to the formation of character. My past self needed to believe he was discovering his power, not inheriting it. He needed to test his stats and his superpowers, to be surprised by what he could do, to feel the weight of his own growth. If I had given him everything at once, he would have been less than what I am now. Not in power—power is just numbers—but in substance. In warmth. In the capacity to love his court wizards and tend his roses and laugh at his friends' jokes."

He turned back to the temporal window, which still showed the sanctuary in Year -10,000. The mortal king was suspended in the first instant of his stasis, his body frozen in the moment of transformation, his mind entering the long, dark sleep that would last ten thousand years.

"The Stasis Event is complete," Godric said softly. "My past self will sleep for ten millennia. He will awaken in Year 0 to find a merchant named Blake poking at the seal of his sanctuary. He will conquer Thronegate. He will begin his expansion across the omniverse. He will test his stats and his superpowers, and his court wizards will write terrified journals about what they witness. And eventually, after a span of time so vast that the year cannot be written, he will sit on a stone bench in a garden and reach across time to create himself."

He looked at me, and his smile was gentle and ancient and utterly at peace. "And so the loop continues. Forever."

## The Silence After

The temporal window closed. The garden was quiet again. The eternal morning sun filtered through the ornamental trees, casting dappled shadows across the stone bench where we sat. Erebus was still tending the roses in the eastern courtyard. Lyra was still trimming the hedges. The omniverse continued its slow, peaceful rotation around the fixed point of Godric's existence.

I sat in silence, my mind reeling with the weight of what I had witnessed. The Stasis Event. The origin of Godric's power. The moment that had defined the entire trajectory of existence for a span of years so vast that the number could not be written. And I had watched him do it. I had watched him reach across time and touch his own past, shaping the foundation of his own existence with the casual ease of a gardener pruning a rose bush.

The horror was not just the power. The horror was the casualness. The genuine warmth. The complete absence of strain or effort or struggle. Godric had just performed an act of temporal manipulation so profound that it defied the fundamental laws of causality, and he had done it while sitting on a stone bench, his hands still smudged with garden soil, his voice as calm and conversational as if he had been discussing the weather.

And he had held back. He had deliberately withheld power—power that would have cost him nothing, power that would have made his past self even more incomprehensibly powerful—because he believed in the value of struggle. Because he wanted his past self to earn his growth, not inherit it. Because the warmth and kindness that defined his rule, the genuine affection he felt for his court wizards and his gardeners and his rose bushes, was something that could not be gifted. It had to be developed. Nurtured. Grown.

The absurdity was staggering. The Godric of Year 0, even with the held-back Stasis Event, had been effectively infinitely stronger than the rest of creation combined. The "struggle" Godric had preserved for him had never been a real struggle. It had been a carefully manufactured illusion of discovery, a playground built by his future self to give him something to do. And even knowing this, I could not deny the result. The Godric sitting beside me—the Godric who had just created himself—was warm. Kind. Genuinely affectionate toward his servants. The mortal king of Olympus, the father who had loved his daughters and his wife, was still present in the infinite being who could erase everything with a passing thought.

That was the true horror, I realized. Not the power. Not the infinite amplification or the evolved mechanisms or the closed loop of self-creation. The true horror was that Godric was good. He was a good king. A good friend. A good man. And he was also an apex predator of such absolute supremacy that the entire omniverse existed only because he permitted it to exist.

The dissonance was unbearable. But I had been bearing it for thirty years. I would continue to bear it for the rest of my life.

"Are you well, Clive?" Godric asked, his voice warm with genuine concern. "You have gone pale."

I swallowed hard. The dread was a physical weight in my throat. "I am well, Your Majesty. It is only... the magnitude of what I witnessed. It will take time to process."

Godric nodded, his expression understanding. "Take all the time you need. I know it is not easy, watching the foundations of existence be laid. But I wanted you to see. You are my court wizard. You are a descendant of Blake, the best friend I have ever had. You deserve to know the truth of what I am and how I came to be."

He reached over and placed a warm, calloused hand on my shoulder. "Now. Shall we return to the palace? I believe the kitchens have prepared a roasted fowl, and I find I have worked up an appetite."

I nodded mutely. I could not speak. I stood from the stone bench on legs that felt hollow and insubstantial, and I followed my king back through the garden toward the palace.

Erebus, the former god-king, looked up from his roses as we passed. His ancient eyes met mine, and in them I saw a reflection of my own dread—the dread of a being who had once believed himself powerful and had been shown the true shape of power. His short salt-and-pepper hair caught the golden light. His neatly trimmed salt-and-pepper beard framed a gentle smile. He was humming a tune I did not recognize.

"Your Majesty," I said, approaching with a respectful bow.

"Clive," Godric replied, his voice warm and rich. "Sit with me. The roses are particularly fragrant today. Erebus has outdone himself."

I sat on the bench beside him. Erebus, the former god-king who now served as the palace's head gardener, was working in the eastern courtyard, his ancient hands delicately pruning a crimson rose bush. He had once ruled an empire spanning decillions of realities. Now he tended flowers. And he seemed, in his quiet, broken way, content.

"Your Majesty," I said, "I have been thinking."

"A dangerous pastime," Godric said, his eyes crinkling with amusement.

I pressed on. "You keep many servants. Gardeners, cooks, artisans of all kinds. And you are always generous with your praise for their work."

Godric nodded. "They are skilled. Their dedication brings beauty to the palace. I appreciate beauty."

"I have been wondering," I said, choosing my words carefully, "if you have ever wished to develop such skills yourself."

There was a pause. A very slight pause. In the span of that pause, I saw something flicker across Godric's ancient eyes—a glint of something that, in my ignorance, I interpreted as wistful longing. I know now that it was amusement. Deep, profound, cosmic amusement.

"I have never really tried," Godric said, his tone perfectly neutral. "My focus has always been... elsewhere."

"Then perhaps," I said, my foolish enthusiasm building, "you might enjoy some lessons. The kitchen staff could teach you to cook. Erebus could teach you the finer points of rose cultivation. I could arrange for the palace artists to give you instruction in painting, in sculpting, in all the arts you have so generously patronized over the years."

Godric looked at me for a long moment. His expression was unreadable. And then, slowly, a warm smile spread across his ruggedly handsome face.

"Clive," he said, "that is a wonderful idea. I would be honored to learn from them."

I felt a surge of pride. I had done something good. I had given the Eternal King a new pursuit, a new avenue for enjoyment, a new way to connect with the servants who loved him.

I was, as I have said, catastrophically wrong.

## The First Lesson: Gardening

The morning of the first lesson dawned clear and golden, as all mornings did in the Eternal Palace. Godric had dressed in simple, practical garments—a loose tunic of dark linen, sturdy trousers, heavy leather boots suitable for kneeling in soil. He looked, I thought, genuinely excited.

Erebus was waiting in the eastern courtyard, his ancient hands clasped nervously before him. The former god-king had been trembling since I informed him of the lesson three days ago. He had tried to refuse, his voice a choked whisper of terror, his eyes wide with something that looked very much like panic. "I cannot teach Him," he had said. "I cannot. He is... I cannot."

But Godric had personally requested Erebus as his instructor, and Erebus, like all of us, was incapable of refusing the King.

So here he stood, a being who had once commanded the fundamental forces of creation, preparing to teach a being who *was* the fundamental force of creation how to plant roses.

Godric approached with an eager stride, his boots crunching on the gravel path. "Erebus," he said warmly. "Thank you for doing this. I have always admired your roses. I look forward to learning your techniques."

Erebus swallowed hard. His hands were shaking visibly. "Your Majesty," he managed, his voice barely audible. "I am... honored. Please. Let us begin."

He led Godric to a prepared plot of soil, a rectangular bed of rich, dark earth that had been specially treated with nutrients and arcane growth enhancers. A tray of rose seedlings sat nearby, their delicate green stems barely poking above the soil of their starter pots.

"The first step," Erebus said, his voice steadier now that he had a script to follow, "is to prepare the hole. The depth must be precisely measured to accommodate the root system without crowding. Too shallow, and the roots will be exposed. Too deep, and the stem will rot."

Godric knelt in the soil, his dark tunic immediately staining with dirt. He picked up a small trowel and examined it with what seemed like genuine curiosity. "Like this?" he asked, pressing the blade into the earth with clumsy, exaggerated awkwardness.

The hole he dug was... terrible. Lopsided. Too shallow on one side and too deep on the other. The edges were ragged and uneven. Erebus winced.

"Ah, perhaps with a bit more... precision, Your Majesty," Erebus said gently. "Here, let me show you."

He knelt beside Godric and demonstrated the correct technique, his ancient hands moving with practiced ease. Godric watched attentively, nodding along, asking small, seemingly naive questions about soil composition and drainage and the optimal angle of the trowel.

He was playing a role. I understand that now. He was performing incompetence with the same absolute, infinite mastery that he brought to everything else. Every clumsy movement was perfectly calculated to appear genuine. Every naive question was precisely calibrated to seem like the honest curiosity of a beginner. He was so good at pretending to be bad that even I, who had served him for thirty years, was completely fooled.

For the first hour, the lesson proceeded in this fashion. Godric struggling. Erebus gently correcting. Godric improving slightly, then making another deliberate mistake. Erebus growing more comfortable, his terror slowly fading as the lesson settled into a familiar rhythm.

And then it happened.

Godric was attempting to transplant a particularly delicate rose seedling from its starter pot into the prepared hole. His hands, which had been clumsy throughout the lesson, suddenly moved with a fluid, effortless grace that seemed almost unconscious. The trowel slipped into the soil at the perfect angle, carving a hole of such precise dimensions that it might have been measured by instruments capable of detecting Planck-scale variations. The seedling lifted from its pot with its root system completely intact, not a single delicate fiber damaged. It settled into the new hole with a natural ease that made it look as though it had always been there. The soil was pressed gently around the stem, compacted to exactly the right density, watered with exactly the right amount of moisture.

The entire sequence took less than two seconds. It was perfect. Not merely skilled. Not merely expert. Perfect. The kind of perfect that no amount of practice could achieve. The kind of perfect that existed only in the realm of theoretical ideals.

Erebus stared at the transplanted seedling. His mouth opened. No sound came out.

The seedling, having been planted with such impossible precision, immediately began to grow. Not slowly, over days or weeks, but visibly, right there in the soil, its stem thickening, its leaves unfurling, its buds swelling and bursting into blooms of such deep, vibrant crimson that they seemed to glow with their own inner light. The rose bush reached full maturity in less than ten seconds, its branches spreading, its flowers opening, its fragrance filling the courtyard with a scent so rich and complex that it brought tears to the eyes of everyone present.

And that was not the worst part.

The worst part was that Godric did not seem to notice. He was looking at the fully grown rose bush with an expression of mild surprise, as though he had not expected it to do that. As though he had not just performed a feat of horticultural mastery that the combined expertise of every gardener in the omniverse, given infinite time and infinite resources, could not have replicated.

"Oh," Godric said, his voice perfectly casual. "Look at that. It grew."

Erebus collapsed to his knees. His ancient face was pale, his eyes wide with an expression that I recognized instantly: the look of a being whose entire understanding of reality had just been shattered. His hands, which had been shaking throughout the lesson, were now trembling so violently that he could not hold them still.

"Your Majesty," he whispered, his voice cracking. "That was... that was..."

"Beginner's luck, I'm sure," Godric said with a warm, self-deprecating laugh. "I must have just happened to get the angle right this time. You are an excellent teacher, Erebus."

Erebus stared at him. Then he stared at the impossibly perfect rose bush, its crimson blooms still radiating that faint, inner glow. Then he stared at Godric again.

"Beginner's luck," he repeated, his voice hollow.

"Absolutely," Godric said, clapping him on the shoulder with a friendly hand. "Now, shall we try another one? I think I'm starting to get the hang of this."

## The Second Lesson: Cooking

The kitchen was a vast, cathedral-like space, its vaulted ceilings supported by pillars of polished obsidian, its walls lined with ovens and hearths and workstations of gleaming metal and ancient stone. The head chef was a being named Pyralis, a former elemental lord of primordial fire who had once consumed entire star systems in his endless hunger. He had been in Godric's service for longer than most galaxies had existed, and he had long since learned to suppress his nature into the form of a stout, cheerful man with rosy cheeks and flour-dusted hands.

Pyralis, unlike Erebus, had not been terrified when I informed him of the lesson. He had been... puzzled.

"The King wishes to learn cooking?" he had asked, his voice carrying the faint crackle of banked embers. "Why?"

"I believe he wants to develop new skills," I had replied. "To appreciate the arts he has so generously patronized."

Pyralis had stared at me for a long moment. Then he had shrugged, his massive shoulders rising and falling like tectonic plates. "As His Majesty wishes."

Now, standing before Godric in the kitchen, Pyralis was demonstrating the preparation of a simple dish: a roasted fowl, seasoned with herbs from the palace gardens, basted in a reduction of wine and butter and aromatic spices.

"The key," Pyralis said, his voice a low rumble, "is patience. The heat must be precisely controlled. Too hot, and the skin will burn before the meat is cooked. Too cool, and the meat will be tough and dry. You must feel the fire, Your Majesty. Understand it. Become one with it."

Godric nodded solemnly. He was wearing a simple apron over his dark tunic, his sleeves rolled up to his elbows, his calloused hands resting on the workstation. He looked, I thought, genuinely eager.

"Feel the fire," he repeated. "Become one with it. I will try."

Pyralis demonstrated the technique. His hands moved with the practiced precision of a being who had been cooking for eons, seasoning the fowl, trussing it with kitchen twine, placing it in the roasting pan with a bed of root vegetables. He slid the pan into one of the massive hearths, adjusting the flames with a casual gesture that spoke of absolute mastery over the element he had once embodied.

"Now you try, Your Majesty," Pyralis said, gesturing to a second workstation where an identical set of ingredients had been prepared.

Godric approached the workstation with the same exaggerated clumsiness he had displayed in the garden. He picked up the fowl awkwardly, his fingers fumbling with the kitchen twine. He applied the seasoning unevenly, leaving some areas bare and others caked with herbs. He basted the bird with too much butter, then too little, then too much again.

Pyralis watched with a patient, paternal expression. "A gentle touch, Your Majesty. The fowl is not your enemy. It is your partner."

"Right," Godric said, his brow furrowed in concentration. "Partner. Gentle."

He continued to fumble. The preparation took far longer than it should have, and the result, when he finally slid the pan into the hearth, was a misshapen, unevenly seasoned mess. Pyralis nodded encouragingly. "A fine first effort, Your Majesty. Now we wait."

The waiting was the part where it all went wrong.

Godric stood before the hearth, watching the flames lick at the roasting pan. His expression was placid, almost dreamy. And as he watched, something shifted in the air. The flames in the hearth began to burn differently—more evenly, more precisely, as though they were responding to an unspoken command. The heat radiating from the fire became perfectly uniform, reaching every part of the fowl with exactly the right intensity. The aroma that began to fill the kitchen was not merely pleasant; it was transcendent. It was the smell of roasting meat and caramelizing vegetables and aromatic spices, yes, but it was also more than that. It was the smell of every perfect meal that had ever been cooked, concentrated into a single, overwhelming sensory experience.

Pyralis's eyes widened. His nostrils flared. His hands, which had been resting calmly on his workstation, began to tremble.

The fowl emerged from the oven without anyone touching it. It simply rose from the pan, floating on a cushion of perfectly heated air, and settled onto a serving platter that had somehow positioned itself on the workstation. Its skin was golden-brown and crackling, glistening with a glaze that seemed to shimmer with its own inner light. The vegetables surrounding it were caramelized to perfection, their edges crisp, their interiors tender. The aroma intensified, filling the kitchen, spilling out into the corridors, drawing servants from across the palace who could not resist the magnetic pull of that impossible scent.

Godric looked at the fowl with an expression of mild surprise. "Oh," he said. "It seems to have finished early."

Pyralis said nothing. He was staring at the fowl with an expression that went beyond awe and into the territory of religious terror. His mouth moved, but no words emerged. His hands, which had commanded the primordial flames that birthed stars, were shaking.

"Shall we taste it?" Godric asked cheerfully, picking up a carving knife.

The slice he made was perfect—a single, fluid motion that separated a portion of breast meat from the bone with surgical precision. The meat beneath the skin was tender and juicy, steaming faintly, its texture so fine that it seemed to melt even before touching the tongue. Godric lifted the slice to his mouth and chewed thoughtfully.

"Not bad," he said. "A bit underseasoned, I think. Next time I will use more rosemary."

Pyralis made a sound that was somewhere between a sob and a laugh. "Your Majesty," he whispered. "That is... that is the most perfect roasted fowl I have ever seen. The most perfect I have ever smelled. I have been cooking for longer than most stars have burned, and I have never... I could never..."

"Beginner's luck," Godric said with a dismissive wave of his hand. "I am sure your next attempt will surpass it, Pyralis. You are, after all, the expert."

Pyralis stared at him. Then he stared at the fowl. Then he reached out with a trembling hand, carved a small piece for himself, and placed it in his mouth.

Tears began to stream down his rosy cheeks. Not from sadness. Not from joy. From the overwhelming, mind-shattering realization that everything he had ever known about cooking—every technique, every secret, every carefully hoarded piece of culinary wisdom—was less than nothing compared to what a being who had never cooked before had just produced by accident.

"Beginner's luck," Pyralis repeated, his voice hollow.

"Absolutely," Godric said. "Now, shall I try the soup next? I have always been curious about broths."

## The Third Lesson: Painting

Lyra, the former ultimate weapon who now served as the palace's head groundskeeper, was also an accomplished painter. Her landscapes adorned the walls of the eastern gallery, vast canvases depicting the gardens and courtyards of the Eternal Palace with a photorealism that bordered on the supernatural. She had once been designed to locate and exploit the weakness of Godric. Now she painted flowers.

When I approached her about the lesson, she had stared at me with an expression of such profound disbelief that I had almost reconsidered the entire enterprise. "You want me to teach Him to paint," she said flatly.

"He expressed interest in developing new skills," I replied.

Lyra closed her eyes for a long moment. When she opened them, they held the resigned, thousand-yard stare of a being who had long since stopped questioning the absurdity of her existence. "Fine," she said. "Send Him to the gallery tomorrow morning."

The gallery was a long, sunlit hall lined with canvases, its windows overlooking the eastern courtyard where Erebus was still tending the impossibly perfect rose bush Godric had created. Lyra had set up two easels side by side, each holding a blank canvas, each accompanied by a palette of oil paints and a selection of brushes.

Godric arrived promptly, his expression eager, his simple dark garments already smudged with paint—he had apparently been experimenting on his own before the lesson. He greeted Lyra with a warm smile and took his place before the easel with the enthusiasm of a child.

"I have always admired your work," Godric said. "The way you capture light on the petals. The depth of your shadows. It is remarkable."

Lyra's hands trembled slightly. "Thank you, Your Majesty. Today, I thought we might attempt a simple still life. The bowl of fruit on the table there, perhaps."

The bowl of fruit was an arrangement of apples and pears and grapes, carefully positioned on a small table in the center of the gallery. It was, I thought, a suitably humble subject for a beginner's first painting.

Lyra began her demonstration, explaining the basics of composition, the mixing of colors, the application of paint to canvas. Godric listened attentively, nodding along, asking small questions about brush technique and color theory that seemed perfectly calibrated to indicate a novice's genuine curiosity.

When it was his turn to paint, he approached the canvas with the same deliberate clumsiness he had displayed in the garden and the kitchen. His brush strokes were shaky and uncertain. His color mixing was haphazard, producing muddy browns and garish oranges. The shape of the bowl on his canvas was lopsided, the fruit little more than vague blobs of color.

Lyra watched with a patient, encouraging expression. "You are doing well, Your Majesty. Remember to hold the brush lightly. Let the paint flow."

Godric nodded, his brow furrowed in concentration. He continued to paint, his brush moving with apparent awkwardness, his canvas slowly filling with a crude, childlike approximation of the still life.

And then, as had happened in the garden and the kitchen, something shifted.

Godric's brush strokes, which had been clumsy and uncertain, suddenly became fluid and precise. His color mixing, which had produced muddy browns and garish oranges, suddenly yielded hues of such depth and richness that they seemed to contain entire spectrums within them. The shapes on his canvas, which had been lopsided blobs, suddenly snapped into perfect alignment, each apple and pear and grape rendered with a realism so absolute that it transcended realism and entered the realm of hyper-reality.

The painting did not merely depict a bowl of fruit. It *was* a bowl of fruit. The apples seemed to glow with an inner light, their red skins so vivid that they looked warm to the touch. The grapes were translucent, their inner seeds visible through the delicate membranes of their flesh. The bowl itself was rendered with a mastery of reflection and shadow that made it look more real than the actual bowl sitting on the table.

And it was not just the technical perfection. The painting had *presence*. It radiated a quiet, overwhelming authority that made every other painting in the gallery—every landscape, every portrait, every carefully crafted masterpiece that Lyra had produced over eons of practice—look like crude finger-paintings by comparison.

Lyra stared at the canvas. Her brush fell from her fingers and clattered on the floor. Her face, which had been composed and patient throughout the lesson, went utterly white.

"Your Majesty," she whispered. "That is... that is..."

Godric stepped back from his easel with a satisfied sigh. He looked at his painting, then at the actual bowl of fruit, then back at his painting. His expression was one of mild, pleasant surprise.

"Huh," he said. "Would you look at that. It actually looks like fruit this time. I think I am starting to understand the brush technique you showed me."

Lyra's mouth opened and closed. No sound came out. She was staring at the painting with the expression of a being who had just watched the fundamental laws of reality be casually rewritten. The painting's presence continued to radiate through the gallery, its quiet, overwhelming perfection pressing against the mind like a physical weight.

"The brush technique," she repeated, her voice barely audible. "The brush technique I showed you."

"Yes," Godric said cheerfully. "The way you hold the brush lightly and let the paint flow. It really does make a difference, doesn't it?"

"The brush technique," Lyra said again, her voice cracking. "I have been painting for eons. I was designed to be the ultimate weapon, but painting was the one thing I found that made existence bearable. I have studied under the greatest masters in the omniverse. I have dedicated my entire existence to capturing beauty on canvas. And you... you just..."

"Beginner's luck," Godric said, waving his hand dismissively. "I am sure it is just a fluke. Your next painting will undoubtedly surpass it, Lyra. You are the true artist here."

Lyra stared at him. Then she stared at the painting—the impossibly perfect painting, the painting that made everything else in the gallery look like the work of a talentless child. Then she stared at the actual bowl of fruit, which now seemed dull and lifeless compared to its painted counterpart.

"Beginner's luck," she whispered.

"Absolutely," Godric said. "Now, shall I try a landscape next? I have always wanted to capture the way the light falls on the eastern courtyard in the morning."

## The Fourth Lesson: Sculpting

The sculptor was a being named Vorkan, a former cosmic architect who had once designed entire planetary systems. He had come to Godric's service after his own empire—a vast network of artificial worlds he had personally sculpted from the raw material of dead stars—had been absorbed into Thronegate. He had not resisted. He had seen what happened to those who resisted. Instead, he had offered his skills to the palace, and Godric had accepted with genuine warmth.

Vorkan worked in a massive studio attached to the eastern wing of the palace, a cavernous space filled with blocks of marble and granite and obsidian and stranger materials—crystallized time, solidified light, the compressed essence of dying dimensions. His sculptures adorned the palace gardens and courtyards, vast, sweeping forms that seemed to capture motion in solid matter.

When I told him about the lesson, he had simply nodded, his craggy face impassive. "His Majesty wishes to sculpt," he said. "Then His Majesty shall sculpt."

The lesson was held in the studio, before a massive block of white marble that had been quarried from the heart of a dead moon. Vorkan had selected the material carefully—beautiful, pristine, suitable for a beginner's first attempt. Godric arrived wearing a leather apron over his dark tunic, his sleeves rolled up, his expression eager.

"I have always admired your sculptures, Vorkan," Godric said. "The way you find the form within the stone. It is remarkable."

Vorkan inclined his head. "Thank you, Your Majesty. Today, I thought we might attempt a simple bust. A human face. The proportions are forgiving, and the subject is familiar."

He demonstrated the technique, showing Godric how to hold the chisel, how to strike with the mallet, how to remove large sections of stone to reveal the rough shape beneath. Godric watched with apparent fascination, his eyes bright with curiosity.

When it was his turn, he approached the marble block with the same deliberate clumsiness he had displayed in every lesson. His grip on the chisel was awkward. His strikes were hesitant and poorly aimed. Large, ugly chunks of marble fell away, leaving jagged, uneven surfaces. The rough shape that began to emerge was barely recognizable as a face—lopsided, asymmetrical, crude.

Vorkan watched with patient, professional attention. "You are doing well, Your Majesty. The key is to feel the stone. To understand its grain, its structure, its hidden weaknesses. Sculpting is not about imposing your will on the material. It is about working with the material to reveal what lies within."

"Feel the stone," Godric repeated, nodding. "Reveal what lies within. I understand."

He continued to work, his chisel striking the marble with apparent uncertainty, his mallet swinging with exaggerated caution. The rough shape on the block slowly refined, becoming more recognizable as a human face, though still crude and unpolished.

And then the moment came. The same moment that had come in the garden, in the kitchen, in the gallery.

Godric's posture changed. Not dramatically—just a slight shift in his stance, a subtle relaxation of his shoulders. But suddenly his hands were moving with a fluid, effortless grace that made everything he had done before look like the fumbling of a child. The chisel danced across the marble, striking at angles that seemed impossible, removing flakes of stone so thin they were almost transparent. The mallet swung with a rhythm that was almost musical, each strike perfectly timed, perfectly placed, perfectly controlled.

The bust emerged from the marble not gradually, but all at once, as though it had always been there and Godric was simply removing the stone that had been hiding it. The face was of extraordinary beauty—not merely realistic, but idealized in a way that transcended realism. Every feature was perfectly proportioned. Every curve was mathematically flawless. The marble itself seemed to have become translucent, capturing and diffusing the light in ways that made the bust seem almost alive.

And it was not just a face. It was *the* face. The platonic ideal of a human visage. Something that existed only in the realm of theoretical perfection, rendered into physical form by hands that had been clumsy and uncertain only moments before.

Vorkan dropped his mallet. The heavy tool clattered on the stone floor, but he did not seem to notice. He was staring at the bust with an expression of such absolute, overwhelming awe that his craggy face seemed to have cracked open.

"Your Majesty," he whispered. "I have been sculpting for longer than most civilizations have existed. I have carved entire worlds from the bones of dead stars. I have created works that were revered across a billion galaxies. And I have never... I could never..."

Godric stepped back from the bust, wiping marble dust from his hands. He looked at his creation with an expression of mild, pleasant surprise. "Oh," he said. "It actually looks like a person this time. I think I am starting to get the hang of the chisel."

Vorkan made a sound that was somewhere between a laugh and a sob. "Getting the hang of the chisel," he repeated. "Your Majesty, that bust is the most perfect sculpture I have ever seen. It is beyond perfect. It is the ideal of sculpture, rendered into physical form. I have dedicated my entire existence to this art, and I could study for a billion more years and never produce anything that approached this. And you... you just..."

"Beginner's luck," Godric said with a warm smile. "I am sure it is just a fluke. Your next sculpture will undoubtedly surpass it, Vorkan. You are the true master here."

Vorkan stared at him. Then he stared at the bust. The perfect, impossible, reality-defying bust.

"Beginner's luck," he whispered.

"Absolutely," Godric said. "Now, shall I try a full figure next? I have always thought the gardens could use a few more statues."

## The Aftermath

The four lessons had spanned the course of a single afternoon. By the time the sun began its slow descent toward the western horizon, the Eternal Palace was home to an impossibly perfect rose bush, the memory of an impossibly perfect meal, an impossibly perfect painting, and an impossibly perfect bust.

Word of the lessons had spread through the palace like wildfire. Servants and courtiers and dignitaries gathered in small clusters, whispering in hushed voices about what they had witnessed. The rose bush in the eastern courtyard had become a pilgrimage site, its crimson blooms still radiating that faint, inner glow. The painting in the gallery had drawn crowds of admirers, their faces slack with awe. The bust in the studio had been carefully covered with a cloth, not to protect it, but to shield the eyes of those who were not prepared to witness its perfection.

Godric, meanwhile, had retired to his study. He was sitting in his favorite chair, a cup of tea cooling on the table beside him, a small, satisfied smile on his ruggedly handsome face. He looked, I thought, like a man who had enjoyed a thoroughly pleasant afternoon.

I, on the other hand, was experiencing what could only be described as a complete and total mental collapse.

I stood in the doorway of the study, my hands trembling, my mind reeling with the implications of what I had witnessed. The lessons. The "beginner's luck." The way Godric had seamlessly transitioned from clumsy incompetence to impossible perfection, and then back again, as though nothing had happened. As though he had not just casually demonstrated mastery so absolute that it made the combined expertise of every artisan in the omniverse look like the fumbling of infants.

Godric looked up as I entered. His smile widened. "Clive! Come in, come in. I was just thinking about the lessons. Thank you for arranging them. I had a wonderful time."

I opened my mouth. No words came out. I tried again. "Your Majesty," I managed, my voice hoarse. "The rose bush. The fowl. The painting. The bust."

"Beginner's luck," Godric said cheerfully. "I must have just gotten lucky. Four times in a row. Remarkable, isn't it?"

I stared at him. He stared back, his expression perfectly innocent, his ancient eyes twinkling with something that might have been amusement.

"Beginner's luck," I repeated.

"That's what I said."

"Your Majesty," I said, my voice cracking, "you planted a rose bush so perfectly that it grew to full maturity in ten seconds and produced blooms that glow with their own inner light. You cooked a fowl so perfectly that Pyralis, a being who has been cooking for eons, wept when he tasted it. You painted a still life so perfectly that Lyra's paintings—Lyra, who was designed to be the ultimate weapon and has studied under the greatest masters in the omniverse—look like crude finger-paintings by comparison. You sculpted a bust so perfectly that Vorkan, who has carved entire worlds from the bones of dead stars, dropped his mallet and could not speak for five minutes."

Godric nodded. "I've had a very lucky day."

"That is not luck!" I burst out, my composure finally shattering. "You did all of that at—at zero percent of your true capability! You were holding back so much that the amount of skill you displayed was effectively nothing compared to what you actually possess! And even that nothing was more than the combined efforts of the entire omniverse could achieve with infinite time and infinite resources!"

Godric's smile did not waver. If anything, it grew slightly warmer. "Clive," he said gently, "you are overthinking this. I am a beginner. Beginners sometimes get lucky. It happens."

I felt my sanity strain against its moorings. He was gaslighting me. He was standing there, in his study, with his salt-and-pepper beard and his warm smile and his cup of tea, and he was gaslighting me about the fact that he was the single most skilled being in all of existence across every possible domain of mortal endeavor.

And the worst part—the truly, deeply horrifying part—was that I was apparently the only one who realized it.

I had spoken with Erebus after the gardening lesson. "The King has beginner's luck," he had said, his ancient eyes distant. "It is charming, isn't it? Even the Eternal King can still surprise himself." I had stared at him in disbelief. He had watched the rose bush grow to maturity in ten seconds, and he had accepted Godric's explanation without question.

I had spoken with Pyralis after the cooking lesson. "His Majesty is a natural," he had said, his rosy cheeks still damp with tears. "Some people simply have a gift. It is wonderful to see him discovering new talents at his age." His age. As though Godric were simply a middle-aged man taking up a new hobby, and not an eternal, infinite being whose cooking skills made the primordial flames of creation look like a child's birthday candle.

I had spoken with Lyra after the painting lesson. "Beginner's luck," she had said, nodding sagely. "It happens sometimes. I am so proud that I could help him discover his potential." Help him discover his potential. As though she had taught him anything. As though he had not simply decided to reveal a microscopic fraction of his true capability and then immediately covered it up with a flimsy excuse.

I had spoken with Vorkan after the sculpting lesson. "His Majesty has a gift," he had said, his craggy face creased with what looked like genuine happiness. "It is an honor to have been part of his first steps into the art." His first steps. As though the impossibly perfect bust was the work of a talented novice, and not a masterpiece that transcended the concept of mastery itself.

Every single one of them had accepted Godric's explanation without question. Every single one of them had been charmed by his humility, his apparent surprise, his warm self-deprecation. They believed, with absolute, unshakeable conviction, that the Eternal King had simply gotten lucky.

And I was the only one who saw the truth.

"Your Majesty," I said, my voice barely above a whisper, "you could have done any of this at any time. You could have cooked every meal in the palace. You could have planted every flower in the gardens. You could have painted every portrait and sculpted every statue. I am the page upon which your tales are written. I am the fundamental truth that all your narratives are attempting, and failing, to describe."

He stepped forward, off the observation deck, into the void itself. His presence, still held tightly in check by his absolute concealment, did not change. But something in the air shifted. The heroes, who had faced down cosmic horrors and ultimate evils and impossible odds, began to tremble.

"You wanted a final boss," Godric said quietly. "A villain worthy of your ultimate attack. A darkness that would make your light shine all the brighter. But I am not a boss, little heroes. I am the designer of the game. And I was so hoping you would put on a better show."

---

## The Annihilation

What happened next is difficult to describe. Not because I did not witness it—I witnessed every instant with perfect, horrifying clarity—but because the language I possess is inadequate to capture the scale of it.

Godric was disappointed. Not angry. Not wrathful. Not even particularly annoyed. Just disappointed. The way a father might be disappointed when his child, who had spent weeks practicing for a recital, forgot the notes at the crucial moment. A gentle, almost paternal disappointment.

And in that disappointment, he accidentally let slip a fraction of his strength.

Not a large fraction. Not even a measurable fraction. Zero percent. Less than zero percent. An infinitesimal sliver so small that calling it zero percent was the only accurate description. The same amount of strength that he had accidentally released during the Ebon Colossus incident, described in Corvin's ancient journal. The same passive, resting expression of his musculature that had annihilated the entire omniverse as collateral damage.

But that had been Corvin's Godric. The Godric of Year 1,247. The Godric who had been amplifying his strength by a thousandfold every Planck second for eleven thousand years.

The Godric standing before me now had been amplifying his strength by numbers beyond infinite recursion every infinitesimal fraction of every Planck second for a span of years so vast that the number could not be written. The gap between him and Corvin's Godric was an infinity of infinities, a chasm so wide that comparing the two was a category error.

And he released zero percent of his current strength.

I will attempt to describe what I witnessed. I will fail. But I will attempt.

The expression of his strength did not manifest as a shockwave. It did not manifest as a blast or a wave or a pulse. It simply manifested as *presence*. The sheer, passive weight of his resting musculature, allowed to exist outside the suppression of his absolute concealment for one infinitesimal fraction of an instant.

The army of fictional heroes—the billions upon billions of champions who had gathered from every story ever told—did not die. They were not killed. They were simply *overwritten*. The concept of their existence was violently, instantly replaced by the concept of Godric's strength. Their bodies, their minds, their souls, their narrative significance, their place in the collective imagination of all sentient life—all of it was crushed into absolute nothingness by the mere proximity of his unleashed musculature.

But that was only the beginning.

The shields that the army had erected—impenetrable barriers forged from the collective will of every hero who had ever stood against the darkness, reinforced by the narrative weight of a billion billion stories, designed to withstand the heat death of infinite multiverses—were the first to fail. They did not shatter. They did not crack. They simply ceased to exist, their fundamental structure unwoven by a force so overwhelming that the concept of resistance became a mathematical impossibility.

The fabric of reality itself began to tear. Not the way a piece of cloth tears, with ragged edges and visible damage. It tore the way a dream tears when the dreamer awakens—completely, instantly, without any possibility of repair. The void surrounding the Thronegate capital was stripped away, layer by layer, dimension by dimension, until there was nothing left but the raw, screaming fundament of existence itself.

And Godric's strength pressed on.

The entire omniverse—every galaxy, every star, every planet, every civilization, every dimension, every timeline, every abstract plane of existence, every possible and impossible state of being—was caught in the radius of that passive, accidental expression. It did not burn. It did not crumble. It was de-atomized. Every atom, every quark, every fundamental particle that had ever existed or could ever exist was simultaneously and instantly stripped of its structural integrity. The forces that held matter together—the strong nuclear force, the weak nuclear force, electromagnetism, gravity—were overridden by a single, absolute command: *compress*.

The omniverse collapsed inward. Not into a black hole—a black hole was a child's toy compared to what was happening. It collapsed into a singularity of infinite density, a point of compressed reality so small that the concept of size became meaningless. Smaller than a quark. Smaller than a Planck length. Smaller than any measurement that physics had ever conceived. The entire sum total of all existence—every universe, every multiverse, every timeline, every reality, every abstract realm, every story, every dream, every possible and impossible permutation of being—was crushed into a space so infinitesimally small that calling it zero was the only accurate description.

And within that singularity, every sentient being who had ever existed experienced their own annihilation in perfect, eternal, agonizing clarity. The heroes of a billion billion stories felt their bodies compressed into nothingness, their bones pulverized into subatomic particles, their flesh stripped from their frames and crushed into the collective mass of the dying omniverse. Their screams—and there were screams, countless screams, an infinite chorus of absolute, mind-shattering agony—were compressed along with everything else, folded into the singularity like layers of torment pressed between the pages of a book.

The process, from start to finish, occurred in zero seconds.

When it was done, the void was empty. The army was gone. The omniverse was gone. Everything that had ever existed or could ever exist was gone. There was only Godric, floating in the absolute nothingness, his expression still faintly disappointed.

And in his hand, held between his thumb and forefinger with the casual ease of a man picking up a pebble, was the singularity.

---

## The Trophy

I cannot fully explain how I survived. Godric's passive protective field had extended over me, as it always did, preserving me from the annihilation that consumed everything else. I floated in the void, my body intact, my mind reeling, and I watched as the Eternal King examined the compressed remains of all existence with the mild curiosity of a geologist examining an interesting rock.

 The singularity that had once been the entire omniverse was too small to see. Even my amplified vision could not perceive it. But I could feel it—a point of infinite mass, infinite density, infinite gravity, suspended between Godric's fingers as though it weighed nothing at all. It contained everything. Every star that had ever burned. Every world that had ever spun. Every life that had ever been lived. Every story that had ever been told. Everything, compressed into an infinitesimal point of absolute, perfect annihilation.

Godric looked at it for a long moment. Then he sighed.

"What a waste," he murmured.

He brought his fingers closer to his face, examining the singularity with an expression of genuine disappointment. "All that buildup. All that drama. All those pretty colors and stirring music. And the attack itself was effectively nothing. I did not even feel it. I did not even notice it. If I had not been paying attention, I would have missed it entirely."

He shook his head slowly. "I was really hoping for something better."

Then he did something that made my blood run colder than the void itself. He took the singularity—the compressed remains of everything that had ever existed—and he *enlarged* it. Not by much. Just enough to make it visible. It swelled from an infinitesimal point of infinite density to a small, perfectly spherical marble, perhaps two centimeters in diameter, its surface a swirling, iridescent sheen of compressed reality.

He rolled it between his fingers, testing its weight. It should have been impossible to hold. The mass contained within that tiny sphere was infinite—the combined gravitational pull of every galaxy, every star, every planet, every particle that had ever existed. It should have collapsed into a singularity that devoured even the void itself. But Godric had enclosed it in some kind of barrier—a transparent membrane of his own will that held the infinite mass in check, preventing it from destroying its surroundings.

"There," he said, his voice almost cheerful. "A souvenir. I have not collected a trophy in a very long time. This one is rather pretty, don't you think, Osvald? The way the light catches the compressed timelines. Very iridescent."

I stared at the marble in his hand. My mouth was dry. My heart was hammering against my ribs. "Your Majesty," I whispered. "That is... that is the omniverse. Everything that ever existed. You are holding it in your hand."

"Yes," Godric said. "I suppose I am." He tossed the marble lightly into the air and caught it, the gesture so casual, so utterly, terrifyingly nonchalant, that I felt my mind threaten to crack. The infinite mass of all existence, and he tossed it like a coin.

"The barrier will hold," he said, apparently reading my thoughts. "It is a simple containment field. Strong enough to prevent the gravitational collapse from propagating outward. Weak enough that I barely notice it is there. I think I will keep it on my desk in the study. It will make a good conversation piece."

He pocketed the marble with the same casual ease a mortal man might pocket a lucky coin. Then he turned his attention back to the void.

"Now," he said. "I suppose I should restore everything."

## The Restoration

Godric restored the omniverse exactly as he always did—in zero seconds, with zero effort, without moving a single muscle. One moment the void was empty. The next moment, everything was back. Every galaxy, every star, every planet, every civilization, every dimension, every timeline, every abstract plane of existence. Restored perfectly, exactly as it had been before the annihilation.

Except for the army of fictional heroes. They remained gone. Unrestored. Their narratives severed from the fabric of reality, their stories ended not with a triumphant victory but with an absolute, crushing, eternal nothingness.

And the marble remained.

I walked the restored corridors of the Eternal Palace, my legs hollow and insubstantial beneath me, my mind reeling with the implications of what I had witnessed. The marble was still in Godric's pocket. The compressed remains of the original omniverse—every atom, every particle, every life, every story—were still there, held in check by a containment field that Godric had woven with less effort than a mortal man might use to tie his shoes.

But if the original omniverse was still compressed in that marble, then what was the omniverse I was walking through? What were the stars I could see through the palace windows? What were the servants I passed in the corridors, the gardeners tending the roses, the cooks preparing the evening meal? Were they real? Were they the genuine article, restored perfectly by Godric's infinite power? Or were they copies? Replicas? Echoes of something that had been destroyed and replaced?

Godric had restored the omniverse before. Countless times. The ancient journals described it as a routine occurrence—the collateral damage of his stat tests and superpower demonstrations, always followed by perfect, instantaneous restoration. But the original matter, the original reality, had always been annihilated. Erased. Converted into nothingness. And Godric had simply... remade it. Identical in every way. Indistinguishable from the original.

But the original was gone.

And now the original was not gone. The original was compressed into a marble in Godric's pocket. And the restored omniverse was something else. A copy. A recreation. A perfect, flawless duplicate of everything that had ever existed, conjured into being by a being who could create entire realities with less effort than a mortal man might use to draw a breath.

Did that make it less real? Did the fact that the original still existed—compressed, annihilated, held in eternal stasis within that iridescent sphere—mean that the restored version was somehow... diminished? And if Godric could do this, if he could hold the original omniverse in his hand while simultaneously maintaining a perfect copy, what did that say about the nature of reality itself?

Were we all just copies? Had we always been copies? Had the original omniverse been destroyed and restored so many times that the concept of "original" had become meaningless? Did it matter? Did anything matter, when the being who ruled us could compress all of existence into a marble and then recreate it with a passing thought?

I do not have answers to these questions. I do not think answers exist. I think these questions are the kind that drive men mad, and I think the only reason I am not mad is that I have learned, over twenty-four years of service, to stop asking questions that have no answers.

But the marble is still there. Sitting on Godric's desk in his private study, a small sphere of swirling, iridescent light that contains the compressed mass of everything that ever existed. He uses it as a paperweight. I have seen him toss it from hand to hand while thinking. I have seen him roll it absently across his desk while reading reports.

It is the most terrifying object in existence. And he treats it like a toy.

---

## The Last Opposition

I do not know if there will be more opposition after this. I do not know if any sentient being, anywhere in the omniverse, will ever again dare to challenge the King of Thronegate. The army of fictional heroes was the largest, most powerful, most dramatically charged opposition that has ever been assembled. They represented the collective imagination of all sentient life. They wielded the narrative weight of every story ever told. And they were annihilated in zero seconds by an accidental, zero-percent expression of Godric's strength.

If there is any wisdom left in the omniverse—if any sentient being, anywhere, has learned anything from the long, bloody history of opposition against the Eternal King—then there will be no more attacks. No more resistance. No more desperate last stands. There will be only the quiet, terrified acceptance that opposition is not merely futile, but definitionally impossible.

But I do not know the future. I am only a court wizard. I write these words because I must, because my ancestors wrote before me, because my descendants will write after me. I write to preserve the memory of what I witnessed, even though I know that memory will fade, that the omniverse will forget, that someday another coalition will form and another attack will be launched and another annihilation will occur.

Unless, of course, this is the last one. Unless the army of fictional heroes was the final gasp of a creation that has finally, truly learned its lesson.

I do not know. I cannot know. I am only Osvald, forty-four years old, court wizard of Thronegate, trembling in the shadow of a god who holds the compressed remains of the original omniverse in his pocket like a lucky coin.

And I am terrified. I will always be terrified. I think terror is the only rational response to understanding even a fraction of what Godric is.

But I will serve him until the end of my days. Not because I must. Because I choose to. Because his warmth is genuine and his kindness is real and his disappointment when the heroes failed to entertain him was the most human thing I have ever seen him express.

He wanted a show. He wanted to feel like part of a story again. And the story, in the end, failed him.

That is the tragedy at the heart of all of this. Not the annihilation. Not the compression of all existence into a marble. Not the horrifying questions about the nature of reality that the marble implies. The tragedy is that Godric, for all his infinite power and eternal growth and accelerating ecstasy, is still, somewhere deep beneath the layers of godhood, the mortal king of Olympus who loved stories.

And the stories were not good enough.

I will write more if there is more to write. But I do not think there will be.

## Clive

I closed the journal with trembling hands. The reading room of the Eternal Palace was quiet, the preservation wards humming softly in the corners, the eternal afternoon light filtering through the stained glass windows. I had been reading for what felt like hours. Perhaps it had been hours. Time was difficult to measure in the presence of such revelations.

Osvald's journal was the last entry in the opposition records. After him, there were no more journals describing attacks, no more accounts of stat tests or superpower demonstrations against hostile forces. The later journals—the ones written by the court wizards who came after Osvald—focused on other things. The lessons. The revelations. The quiet, peaceful administration of an omniverse that had finally stopped fighting back.

Osvald had not known, when he wrote his journal, that he was documenting the final opposition. He had speculated about the possibility—had wondered if the omniverse had finally learned its lesson—but he had not known for certain. He had written his words as a warning to future court wizards, just as his ancestors had written before him. He had not known that there would be no more attacks. No more resistance. No more desperate last stands.

But I knew. Sitting in the reading room, surrounded by the accumulated journals of a thousand ancestors, I knew that Osvald's account was the end of something. The final chapter in a history of futile defiance that stretched back to Year 0, when Godric had emerged from his stasis and begun his reign. After the army of fictional heroes, no one else ever tried. No one else ever dared. The omniverse had learned its lesson at last.

And the marble—the marble was still there. It sat on Godric's desk in his private study, exactly where Osvald had described it. A small sphere of swirling, iridescent light, containing the compressed mass of everything that had ever existed. The original omniverse. The first reality, destroyed and preserved in the same instant, held in eternal stasis by a containment field so casual that Godric had woven it with less effort than a mortal man might use to tie his shoes.

I had seen it. I had touched it. It was cold and smooth and terrifyingly light, as though the infinite mass within was nothing at all. It was, as Godric had said, a conversation piece. A paperweight. A souvenir from a battle that had failed to entertain him.

And everything I knew—everything I had ever known, everything I had ever loved, everything I had ever been—was a copy. A recreation. A perfect duplicate of something that was still compressed in that marble, held in eternal stasis on the desk of a god who had stopped caring about the original long before my ancestors were born.

The question that had tormented Osvald—the question of whether the restored omniverse was real—had never been answered. It could never be answered. Because the answer, whatever it was, would destroy the fragile sanity of every sentient being who learned it. If the restored omniverse was real, then reality was a construct, a fabrication, a plaything of the Eternal King. If the restored omniverse was not real, then nothing was real, and existence was a lie told by a god who held the truth in his pocket like a lucky coin.

Either way, the dread was absolute. Either way, the futility was total.

I stood from the reading table, my legs unsteady, my mind heavy with the accumulated weight of a billion years of testimony. I walked to the window and looked out at the eastern courtyard, where Erebus was still tending the impossibly perfect rose bush that Godric had created during his first gardening lesson. The former god-king was humming softly to himself, his ancient hands moving with the gentle precision of a being who had found peace in surrender.

I understood him now. I understood all of them. Erebus and Pyralis and Lyra and Vorkan and every other broken being who had come to Godric's service. They had learned what I was only now beginning to learn: that the only sane response to the Eternal King was acceptance. Not resistance. Not denial. Not desperate attempts to find meaning in the face of absolute meaninglessness. Just acceptance. Quiet, humble, terrified acceptance.

The omniverse was a copy. Reality was a fabrication. Everything we had ever known was held in eternal stasis inside a marble on Godric's desk. And none of it mattered, because the copy was perfect, and the original was gone, and the only difference between the two was a question that could never be answered.

I turned from the window and walked to the door of the reading room. I had one more journal to read—one more account of the Eternal King's power, written by a court wizard who had witnessed something even more terrifying than the final opposition. But that was a story for another day. Today, I needed to breathe. I needed to walk in the gardens and feel the sun on my face and pretend, for a few precious moments, that I was real.

I am Clive. I am fifty years old. I am the Court Wizard of the Eternal King. And I have just learned that everything I have ever known is a copy of something that is still sitting on my king's desk, compressed into a sphere the size of a marble.

The dread will never leave me. The questions will never be answered. And I will serve Godric until the end of my days, because I love him, and because he is kind, and because the alternative—the abyss of meaninglessness that yawns beneath the feet of anyone who truly understands what he is—is too terrible to contemplate.

The marble sits on his desk. The original omniverse, held in eternal stasis, a souvenir from the day the stories failed to entertain him.

And somewhere, in the deep, silent void where the army of fictional heroes once stood, there is nothing at all.
