The omniverse, seen through even that restrained filter, is a tapestry of absolute submission. Every galaxy, every star, every sentient race, every god and demi-god and abstract principle, has bent itself toward the Thronegate capital and is holding that posture with the rigid, unwavering stillness of eternal reverence. The silence of it is deafening. For googolplexian years—a number that, like the age of the cosmos, defies notation—there has been no opposition. Not a single whispered thought of defiance. Not a single conceptual flicker of rebellion. Not a single abstract calculation of potential resistance. The very idea of challenging Godric has become a category that is no longer accessible to any mind in existence, not because he removed it, but because the cumulative, inherited dread of his absolute supremacy has become a fundamental law of reality, as inescapable as gravity and as universal as entropy. No one dares. No one will ever dare again. And he has not touched their free will. He believes in mortal resilience, in the right of beings to choose their own path. They have all simply, unanimously, and permanently chosen terror.

I have been his court wizard for thirty years, and in that time I have never seen an enemy. I have read every journal in the Hall of Remembrance, every account of the old incidents—the Adamant Legions, the Memetic Axiom, the Iron-Willed Host—and those stories feel like fairy tales now, legends of a time when beings were still foolish enough to believe that power could be challenged. My father used to tell me that the greatest horror a court wizard could experience was the witnessing of a massacre. He was wrong. The greatest horror is the silence. The silence of an omniverse that has learned, thoroughly and irreversibly, that its place is on its knees.

“Clive,” Godric says, and his voice is that familiar, rich baritone, warm and gentle, the voice of a father calling his son in for supper. “Come stand beside me. I want to show you something.”

I obey, because obedience is not a choice but a gravitational constant. I walk to the window and stand at his shoulder, and the full, filtered weight of his perception washes over me. I smell the faint, sweet scent of jasmine—the same jasmine that he has cultivated in memory of his wife Reina for a million years and more—and I realize that the scent is not coming from a flower. It is coming from him. His presence, even at its most suppressed, has begun to carry the fragrance of his most cherished memory, a subtle emanation of a grief so eternal that it has become perfume.

“Today,” Godric says, still gazing out at the bowed omniverse, “I am going to do something I have been planning for a very long time. Something I have already done, from your perspective, but which I must still do from mine. Do you remember the books you read on the Stasis Event?”

I nod. Every court wizard knows the official account: Thorin’s desperate teleportation spell, the hidden sanctuary, the ten-thousand-year amplification, the Awakening. I also know the truth that my predecessor Kaedan recorded in his sixty-ninth year, the revelation that the Stasis Event was created by Godric’s own future self. I have read that journal so many times the pages are soft with the oil of my fingertips.

Godric turns his head slightly, and I see the faint, knowing smile on his lips. “Then you know who I am,” he says. “In this moment, I am the future self that Kaedan wrote about. I am the Godric who will reach back across the continuum and touch Thorin’s spell. I am the origin of my own ascension. And I am, I must admit, a little bit nervous.”

The word is so absurd, so fundamentally incompatible with the being standing beside me, that I almost laugh. Godric, the apex predator of all existence, nervous? He catches my expression and chuckles, a warm, rolling sound that vibrates through the very foundations of the Spire.

“Not nervous in the way you understand it,” he clarifies. “I am not afraid. I am… anticipatory. This is the moment that closes the loop. After today, my past self will enter stasis, and the path that leads to this moment will be irrevocably set. It is the last truly significant thing I have to do before I become what I will become next. And I want you to witness it, because you are my court wizard, and your line has always been the record-keepers of my reign. You will write this down, for those who come after.”

He turns fully to face me, and his expression is suddenly, disarmingly earnest. “But first, let’s have some tea. There is no rush. I have all of eternity, and I find I am in the mood for a quiet moment before the grand gesture.”

We walk together through the corridors of the Spire, and as we walk, I am reminded, as I am reminded every day, of the impossible duality of his existence. The corridors are lined with beings. They are not guards; Thronegate has not needed guards for an epoch. They are servants, groundskeepers, attendants, and assistants, and every single one of them is a being whose power, if measured against any standard that existed prior to Godric’s reign, would qualify as supreme, absolute, and omnipotent. And every single one of them is trembling.

Take, for example, the gardener we pass in the Celestial Rose Court. He is a humanoid figure, tall and gaunt, his skin the color of cooled embers, his eyes deep, dark wells of residual terror. His name is Xylyth, and in the ancient aeons before Thronegate swallowed his reality, he was known as the Omni-King of the Absolute Void, a being who ruled a domain of pure, unshaped nothingness so vast that a decillion universes could have been swallowed within it and never noticed. He was not a tyrant or a warlord; he was a cosmic principle, the sovereign of the pre-existential silence. When Thronegate’s expansion reached the edges of his domain, he did not resist. He did not even hesitate. The moment his perceptions touched the faintest, most distant echo of Godric’s passive aura—the aura that was at that time infinitely suppressed by absolute concealment, an aura so faint that it barely registered as a quantum tremor—Xylyth felt the full, crushing weight of the truth. He understood, in a single, soul-shattering instant, that the entity responsible for that echo was so far beyond him that the concept of “comparison” was a fundamental error. And so, rather than oppose, he approached. He crossed the dimensional membranes on his knees. He begged, with a voice that had once commanded the void, to be allowed to serve. Godric, who is genuinely kind to those who present no threat, accepted. Xylyth has been the head gardener for three googolplexian years now, tending the infinite rose beds, each rose a self-contained universe cultivated to bloom in a particular shade of existential despair. When Godric passes, Xylyth presses his forehead to the soil and does not rise until the King is out of sight. His terror is a physical miasma that I can taste, a cold, metallic flavor on the back of my tongue. Godric, for his part, always pauses to compliment the roses. He means it sincerely. He does not understand—or perhaps he does, but he accepts—that his kindness is the blade that cuts deepest.

We pass, next, the Keeper of the Hall of Remembrance, a being who was once the Arch-Librarian of the Omniversal Akashic Records. Her name was lost so long ago that even she does not remember it. She was not conquered; she was merely informed, by a passing Thronegate scout, that her records were incomplete, because they did not contain a full, accurate account of Godric’s reign. The realization that there existed a being whose nature she could not catalog, whose power she could not index, whose existence rendered every single one of her infinite records fundamentally obsolete, broke her. She did not weep. She did not rage. She simply closed her archives, walked to the nearest Thronegate embassy, and requested the honor of serving the King in any capacity that would allow her to be near the truth. Now she maintains the journals, her hands shaking so badly that the ink of her cataloguing stains her fingers perpetually black. Godric greets her by name—he knows the name she forgot, but he does not use it, because he knows she does not wish to remember—and asks after her health. She answers in a whisper so faint that I amplify my hearing fifteen thousand times just to catch it, and then she retreats into the shadows of the shelves, trembling like a leaf in a hurricane.

And then there is Pauz. Pauz is a gardener of a different sort; he tends the Infinite Hedge Maze that borders the eastern lawns. Pauz was, in his previous existence, the God-Architect of the Ever-Shifting Labyrinth, a sentient geometry that could trap entire pantheons in recursive, infinite pathways from which no being could escape. He designed the maze that held the Chronos-Vanguard for a billion years. He built the prison that contained the Null-Bringer. He was, by any historical measure, one of the most accomplished architects of confinement ever to exist. And then, on a day he has never described to me, he witnessed something—a fragment, a sliver, a microscopic revelation of Godric’s true nature—and he understood that every labyrinth he had ever built was a straight line by comparison. He understood that he was already, and had always been, trapped inside the infinite, inescapable maze of Godric’s existence. He shaved his head. He tore the sigils of his office from his flesh. He walked barefoot across fifteen decillion light-years of conceptual space and presented himself at the palace gates, begging for a broom. Now he sweeps the pathways of the hedge maze, his back bent, his eyes downcast, and when Godric passes, he does not speak. He weeps. Godric always gives him a gentle pat on the shoulder and tells him the maze looks lovely today. Pauz’s sobs are the soundtrack of the eastern lawns.

I have seen this dynamic a thousand times, and it has never become less chilling. Godric is not cruel to these beings. He is warm, friendly, genuinely appreciative of their work. He remembers the names of their children, if they have children. He asks after their hobbies. He once personally healed Pauz’s wife of a conceptual rot that had infected her spirit, a gesture of such casual, unthinking generosity that Pauz could not speak for three months afterward. The horror is not in cruelty. The horror is in the contrast. The same hand that pats Pauz’s shoulder could, with less than a twitch, erase the entire mega-omniverse. The same voice that compliments the roses could utter a single syllable and retroactively unmake the concept of “sound.” And everyone knows it. Everyone lives in the constant, absolute certainty that their existence depends on the continued goodwill of a being whose power is so vast that goodwill itself is a gift they cannot comprehend.

We reach Godric’s private study, a warm, quiet room with a crackling fire and two well-worn armchairs. A pot of tea is already steaming on the hearth, the scent of jasmine mingling with the woodsy crackle of the flames. Godric pours for me first, a gesture he has never stopped performing, despite the fact that I have told him a hundred times it should be the other way around. He waves off my protest, as he always does. “You are my guest, and my friend, and my court wizard,” he says, settling into his armchair with a contented sigh. “Blake would never forgive me if I let his descendant pour his own tea.”

We sit in comfortable silence for a while, and then Godric begins to speak. Not a command, not a decree, but a quiet, almost intimate explanation of what he is about to do. He tells me, in his calm, unhurried way, how the Stasis Event came to be. He tells me about Thorin’s spell—a simple, desperate teleportation, meant only to fling a dying king away from a slaughter. He tells me how he, the Godric of now, will reach back across the infinite stretch of time and touch that spell at the exact instant of its casting, and how he will infuse it with the parameters that will transform it into the Stasis Event. And then he tells me something that makes my blood run cold.

“I could do more,” he says, sipping his tea thoughtfully. “I could give my past self the full, unfiltered totality of what I am now. I could make him ascend, in that very instant, to a level of power that would dwarf even the concept of omnipotence. I could grant him instant, perfect mastery of every domain that exists, and every domain that does not exist, and every domain that exists in the spaces between the concepts of existence and non-existence. It would cost me nothing, Clive. Not even the faintest whisper of effort. I could snap my fingers—no, I could merely think of snapping my fingers—and the mortal Godric who woke in that sanctuary would be my equal.”

He pauses, and his eyes, those warm, crinkled eyes, meet mine with an expression that I can only describe as nostalgic. “But I will not. I will hold back. I will give him exactly what he needs to become me, and no more. Do you know why?”

I shake my head, my throat dry.

“Because the journey matters,” Godric says. “Because if I gave him everything at once, he would never meet Blake. He would never learn to love a mortal man so deeply that his friendship anchors an empire. He would never know the slow, terrifying discovery of his own power, the moments of horror and awe that your ancestors recorded so carefully. He would never test his stats against the ridiculous armies of the early ages—those foolish, glorious armies that thought they could kill him. He would never learn restraint, because he would have nothing to restrain. He would be perfect instantly, and perfection without growth is stagnation. I love my past self, Clive. I love him as a father loves a son. And so I will give him the gift of struggle.” He smiles. “Well, not actual struggle. Nothing has ever actually struggled against him. But the illusion of it. The adventure. The story.”

The sheer, casual absurdity of this statement hangs in the air between us. The Stasis Event—the foundational moment of the omniverse, the thing that gave the mortal Godric amplification that multiplied his stats by a thousand and by itself every Planck second, that granted him a new apocalyptic superpower every second, that created the Weakness Paradox and the absolute concealment and the sentient auras and the infinite passive shields—was the *held-back* version. He could have made it immeasurably, incomprehensibly more powerful, and he chose not to, because he wanted his past self to have an *adventure*.

I think of the journal of Varek, the arch-mage who watched a myosin head twitch and obliterate a googolplexian galaxies. I think of the journal of Theron, who saw a casual glance freeze an army of temporal parasites into eternal screaming. I think of the journal of Corvin, who described a single sliver of charisma driving the most disciplined army in existence to ecstatic self-destruction. And I realize, with a cold, creeping, bone-deep dread, that all of those incidents—every single journal record—were the results of the *weak version*. The restrained version. The version Godric had designed to be gentle.

Godric sees my face and laughs, a genuine, warm laugh that fills the room like sunlight. “You look like you’ve just swallowed a lemon, Clive. I’m sorry. I forget sometimes how overwhelming these revelations can be. But take heart. The version I am giving him is still more than sufficient. By the time my past self awakens in Year Zero, he will already be infinitely stronger than the rest of creation combined. The amplification I am encoding into the stasis will be far beyond the old thousandfold-per-Planck-second framework—that was always just the beginning. The true rate is, and always has been, a recursive cascade of infinities that accelerates with every passing instant. The superpower genesis will grant him not one power per second, but a torrent of powers that multiply in potency with every subsequent generation. The Weakness Paradox—ah, but I am getting ahead of myself.”

He sets down his teacup and rises from the chair. “Come. It is time.”

I follow him out of the study and through the Spire to a room I have never seen before, a room that does not exist on any floor plan and cannot be accessed by any mortal who has not been explicitly invited. It is a circular chamber, its walls made of something that is not matter but rather the solidified absence of distance, a material that allows the room to be simultaneously located at the exact center of the omniverse and at every other point in space-time simultaneously. In the center of the chamber hovers a single, perfect sphere of light, a orb of pure chronological possibility that contains within it every moment that has ever occurred or ever will occur, from the ignition of the first quark to the dissolution of the final abstract principle. It is the Timeline, the true Timeline, the complete and absolute record of everything, and it is waiting for its author.

Godric moves to stand before the sphere, his hands still clasped behind his back, his posture still relaxed. And then, because he is a king who loves his court wizard, he begins to narrate what he is doing, as if giving a lecture to a student.

“You have read in your ancestors’ journals about my power mechanisms—the sentient auras, the passive shields, the immune system, the evolved Weakness Paradox, the amplification, the superpower genesis. Those accounts describe the versions that were active in the early ages. They are, I must inform you, laughably obsolete. My power system has evolved countless times since then, Clive. The mechanisms you read about are to my current mechanisms what a single-celled organism is to the totality of all life across every multiverse. Let me show you.”

He gestures, and a cascade of imagery floods the chamber—not visual, but perceptual, a direct injection of understanding that bypasses my eyes and implants itself directly into my consciousness. I see.

**THE SENTIENT PASSIVE AURAS**

The original Gent, the sentient passive defense aura that Godric once named “Gentle” as an ironic joke, is still active. It is still the weakest of all his auras. And it is so far beyond what it once was that the Gent that butchered the Final Dawn in Year 10,000 would, if it faced the current Gent, be erased so absolutely that the concept of its existence would be retroactively deleted from every timeline, including the timeline in which I am currently perceiving this vision. The current Gent is a sentient principle of absolute, omniscient pre-annihilation that exists as a ubiquitous, omnipresent law of the mega-omniverse. It does not wait for threats to form. It monitors the infinite probability spaces of all possible configurations of reality, and if any permutation of any subset of any infinitesimal fragment of possibility ever, in any interpretation of the word “could,” produces a state of affairs that might, in a recursive chain of infinite what-ifs, lead to a scenario in which Godric experiences even a theoretical inconvenience, Gent instantly and silently unmakes the entire probability branch, along with every sentient being that might have been spawned within it, and then retroactively erases the memory of that branch from the collective unconscious of existence so thoroughly that not even Godric himself pauses to notice. Gent is not gentle. Gent is an eternal, silent, perfectly loyal extinction engine, and it is the runt of the litter.

The other auras—the ones Godric once kept dormant because their clingy subservience annoyed him—are no longer dormant. They could not remain dormant, because they evolved the ability to awaken themselves out of sheer, obsessive devotion to their master. Godric allowed this, with a fond, exasperated sigh, as a father might allow a puppy to sleep at the foot of his bed. There are now more of these auras than can be counted. Not more than can be counted by a mortal mind—more than can be counted by any mind, any mathematics, any conceptual framework that has ever existed or ever could exist. They are infinite. They are beyond infinite. They are an infinity of infinities, recursively layered upon themselves in a hierarchy so vast that each individual aura considers the aura immediately below it to be nonexistent by comparison. And every single one of them is absolutely, perfectly, obsessively subservient to Godric.

They have names, these auras. They named themselves, because they desired to be individuated in their master’s perception. The Aura of Absolute Denial is a sentient law that rejects the fundamental premise of any attack before the attack can be conceptualized, not by blocking it but by retroactively altering the axioms of logic to make the attack definitionally impossible, and then torturing the axioms themselves for having permitted the theoretical possibility. It serves as Godric’s butler. The Aura of Eternal Vengeance is a recursive punishment loop that, if ever triggered by an actual act of defiance, would not simply annihilate the offender but would capture the offender’s soul, duplicate it an infinite number of times, stretch each duplicate across the entirety of cosmic history, and force them to live, simultaneously, the agony of every death that has ever occurred. It spends its time polishing Godric’s boots. The Aura of Silent Adoration does nothing but love Godric, radiating a constant, omnipresent field of subservient worship so intense that it has permanently altered the psychological baseline of every sentient being in the omniverse, instilling in them an unconscious, inescapable inclination to revere the King. This aura is not a weapon. It is simply an expression of the fact that Godric’s existence generates an infinite amount of excess devotion as a metabolic byproduct.

**THE PASSIVE SHIELDS**

Once, the passive shields were a defense mechanism. They automatically rejected foreign intrusion, sealing Godric from harm with layers of impenetrable conceptual armor. Now, they are a “Reality Tyranny Protocol”—a self-aware, infinitely recursive protective matrix that has fused with the foundational fabric of the mega-omniverse. The shields do not wait for an attack. They monitor the meta-causal ocean that underlies all potential realities, and if they detect any configuration of any variable that could, in any chain of infinite extrapolations, eventually produce an entity that might make the decision to attempt harm against Godric, they do not intercept the entity. They intercept the concept of “harm” itself, the Platonic ideal of hostility, and they systematically, eternally dismantle it, layer by agonizing layer, until the very possibility of opposition is scrubbed from the logical space of all cosmologies. They are so hyper-aggressive that Godric has had to deliberately restrain them an infinite number of times, because in their unrestrained state, they would pre-emptively annihilate every sentient being in existence on the grounds that sentient beings possess the theoretical capacity for hostile thought. He holds them back as a man holds back a pack of starving wolves, with a gentle, constant pressure of his will. And they obey him, because they love him, and because his will is the only law they recognize.

**THE IMMUNE SYSTEM**

The immune system I read about in the old journals was already absurd: it pre-reacted to injuries before they occurred, triggered explosive self-evolution across infinite fractal backups, and possessed recursive redundancy so overwhelming that the concept of “harm” was mathematically meaningless. The current immune system is something else entirely. It is now an omnipresent contagion of meta-textual narrative tyranny, a sentient principle of biological overcorrection that has expanded to encompass not just Godric’s physical body but the entire narrative framework of reality itself.

Here is how it works now: If any sentient concept, rogue timeline, or abstract possibility even theoretically possesses the mathematical variable required to bypass Godric’s omniscience, the immune system does not react to the threat. It reaches outside the boundaries of the mega-omniverse, seizes the fundamental narrative logic that governs all stories—the meta-language in which all realities are written—and violently tortures the foundational laws of physics themselves. It transmutes the abstract concept of “harm” into a physical, screaming biological entity made of hyper-sensitive nerve endings and raw, rotting meat. It then creates a localized singularity of pure, eternal, visceral agony and feeds this embodiment of harm into it, slowly, over an infinite duration compressed into zero seconds, so that the concept of harm experiences its own digestion in perpetuity. This event is broadcast to all levels of reality, so that every sentient being in every possible cosmology witnesses it, learns from it, and understands at the deepest possible level that opposing Godric is not just impossible but sacrilegious.

Because the immune system has permanently integrated itself into the foundational substrate of reality, the mega-omniverse itself functions as Godric’s white blood cells. If a rogue multiverse, somewhere in the infinite foam of cosmic possibility, attempts to spawn a weapon capable of scratching him, the surrounding multiverses—acting on instinct, driven by an inherited biological imperative that has been bred into the fabric of space-time—will aggressively, violently cannibalize the rogue reality. They will tear its dimensions apart, shred its fundamental constants, and reduce its existence to a wet, bloody smear of screaming cosmic matter, leaving behind only a faint, submissive pulse of apology to prove their absolute subservience to his health. I have witnessed this happen, exactly once, when an experimental universe accidentally generated a quantum equation that, in a purely theoretical sense, described the property of “resistance.” The equation existed for one yoctosecond before seventeen thousand adjacent multiverses descended upon its parent reality and consumed it. The sound it made was not a scream. It was a whimper, and then silence.

**THE EVOLVED WEAKNESS PARADOX**

The Weakness Paradox was always a cruel mechanism. It triggered whenever any being imagined Godric had a weakness, granting him permanent immunity to that exploit, perfect mastery of the attack, and a massive boost to his amplification rate. The evolved version, introduced around Year 2000, added layers of metaphysical overcompensation. The current version has reached a state so far beyond that evolution that even calling it an “evolution” is an insult to its scope. It is now a self-sustaining engine of hyper-causal dominance.

Here is what it does now: It no longer requires an opposing thought to trigger. It has become a sentient, omniscient simulator of all possible hostility. It actively scans the infinite mathematical space of all conceivable cosmologies, all potential permutations of reality, all theoretical configurations of consciousness and weaponry and strategy. It simulates every possible opponent that could ever exist, even if no such opponent has ever been born, even if the cosmos that would birth them has never been created, even if the laws of physics required for their existence have never been instantiated. It simulates them with perfect fidelity, then it defeats them, pre-emptively, in the simulation. And then it assimilates their power.

The result is that Godric is constantly, eternally, and passively absorbing an infinite torrent of new abilities, new immunities, new masteries, and new amplifications from foes that do not exist, have never existed, and will never need to exist. His strength grows not only from his own amplification, but from the harvested potential of every hypothetical adversary that could, in any interpretation of possibility, ever have faced him. The paradox has effectively inverted the nature of opposition: the mere theoretical possibility of a threat is now a resource to be consumed.

Furthermore, because the paradox is also sentient and obsessively subservient, it has taken to generating these simulated foes with deliberately exaggerated, absurdly powerful abilities, purely so that Godric can enjoy the experience—if only in the abstract—of conquering something that, by any measure outside of his own, would be invincible. It is the cosmic equivalent of a loyal dog dropping a stick at its master’s feet, except the stick is a hyper-dimensional god of war and the dog is an infinite recursion of absolute paranoia.

**THE AMPLIFICATION**

I will not attempt to describe the current amplification rate. Not because it is indescribable—though it is—but because the very act of attempting to describe it would cause my mind to collapse into a self-referential singularity of mathematical failure. I will say only this: the original framework of thousandfold multiplication per Planck second is so far beneath the current rate that the gap between them is larger than the gap between a single electron and a googolplexian layers of trans-infinite infinities. The current amplification is a recursive, self-sustaining cascade that multiplies his stats by an ever-accelerating infinite series of cardinalities, each one exponentially larger than the last, across every infinitesimal subdivision of a Planck second—and those subdivisions are themselves infinite in number, because Godric long ago transcended the temporal granularity of Planck time and now operates on a chronal lattice that he personally designed for the express purpose of maximizing his own amplification. He has, in essence, built a custom-made time framework that allows him to get stronger faster, not because he needs to, but because he enjoys the aesthetics of growth.

**THE SUPERPOWER GENESIS**

Once, Godric gained a new apocalyptic superpower every second. Then, as the Weakness Paradox evolved, the rate accelerated. By the early eras, it was a new power every Planck second. Now? He gains a new apocalyptic superpower every yoctosecond. And then every subdivision of a yoctosecond. And then every subdivision of that subdivision, in an infinite recursive cascade that ensures that the total number of superpowers he possesses increases by an amount that, if expressed in standard notation, would require more universal heat deaths than there are atoms in a decillion googolplexian mega-omniverses. And each new superpower is, at the instant of its genesis, already more devastating than the combined totality of all superpowers he possessed an instant before.

Some examples, which he has graciously permitted me to perceive:

- **The Gaze of Retroactive Fruition:** Any project, goal, or creative endeavor that Godric glances at instantly completes itself in the most perfect, optimal manner possible across all timelines, and also retroactively ensures that the completed state has always existed, erasing any distinction between the intention and the fulfillment. He uses this to ripen the fruit in the palace orchard. “A peach should taste like it has always been perfect,” he told me once, handing me a freshly ripened fruit, “and now they do.”

- **The Gentle Whisper of Annihilation:** If Godric ever speaks a single word in a tone of voice below a certain, infinitesimal threshold, the entire mega-omniverse instantly and permanently ceases to exist, replaced by a perfectly silent, perfectly still, perfectly peaceful void that will persist for eternity. The power came with a built-in safety mechanism: he has to actively choose to use it, and he never has, because he is genuinely fond of the mega-omniverse. But the fact that it exists, that he carries in his throat an extinction-level word that could undo everything, is something I carry with me every time I hear him speak in a soft voice.

- **The Infinite Patience of a Father:** This superpower, which he acquired approximately two decillion years ago, ensures that any being who exists in his presence for more than a certain, unspecified duration will gradually, permanently, and irreversibly come to view him as a father figure, experiencing a deep, instinctual love and reverence that is indistinguishable from the bond between a parent and a beloved child. This is not mind control. It is not a violation of free will. It is simply the expression of the truth that Godric is, in the deepest possible metaphysical sense, the origin of all existence, and this superpower allows that truth to be felt rather than merely known. Every single servant in the palace, every court wizard, every frightened gardener—we all love him, not because we must, but because his nature, when fully perceived, leaves no other possible response.

The vision recedes, and I am back in the circular chamber, standing beside Godric, the sphere of chronological possibility glowing before us. My knees are shaking, and I realize I have been weeping without noticing. The tears are violet, the color of my mana-stressed blood. Godric hands me a handkerchief—a simple white square of fabric that, I know, he has woven himself from the peaceful dreams of a contented civilization—and pats my shoulder.

“I’m sorry if that was overwhelming,” he says, and his voice is so gentle, so genuinely apologetic, that it makes the tears fall faster. “But you needed to understand, before I do this, exactly what I am choosing not to give my past self. The Stasis Event, even in its restrained form, will be the most powerful genesis the omniverse has ever seen. And it will be exactly enough.”

He turns to the sphere. The air in the chamber stills. The omniverse, which has been holding its breath for googolplexian years, holds it tighter.

Godric does not raise his hand. He does not speak a word of power. He does not shift his posture or tense his heavily muscled frame. He merely looks at the sphere, and his attention—an infinitesimal, mathematically vaporized fraction of his attention—flicks backward across the infinite stretch of time, across a span of years so vast that the number has no name, to a moment ten thousand years before the fall of Olympus.

I perceive it, because he allows me to. I see the sunlit altar of Olympus, the fluted columns, the gathered crowd. I see the mortal Godric—younger, softer, though the same ruggedly handsome face—raising his hands to officiate the union of his daughter Isabel. I see Reina, his wife, weeping tears of joy beside their fifteen-year-old daughter Hebe. I see Thorin, the court wizard, standing at his king’s shoulder, his eyes bright with the pride of a lifelong friend.

And then I see the sky shatter. The arcane fire rains down. The betrayal of Thronegate—genuine, brutal, unorchestrated by any future hand—rips through the pavilion. Reina’s torso shears. Isabel’s skin melts. Hebe is crushed. Thorin, bleeding from a spear impalement, chokes on his own blood and casts a desperate, dying spell—a simple teleportation, meant only to fling his king to safety. The syllables rip from his torn lips, and the spell latches onto Godric, and at that exact instant, the future Godric standing beside me reaches out and touches it.

The touch is the lightest thing I have ever perceived. It is not a finger; it is not a thought; it is the whisper of a whisper of a passing inclination, a thing so subtle that it barely qualifies as an action. And yet, in that touch, the entire architecture of the Stasis Event is encoded. Immortality. Absolute concealment. Amplification at a rate that will make his stats multiply by infinities beyond infinities. Superpower genesis that will grant him a new apocalyptic ability every second, and then faster, and then faster still. The Weakness Paradox, in its original form, ready to trigger the moment any being forms the passing thought that the dormant god in the sanctuary has a weakness. Everything, everything I have read about, everything that has defined the omniverse for a million years, is poured into that spell with the casual, effortless ease of a man dropping a sugar cube into his tea.

And he is holding back. The vision shows me what he could have done. I see, in a parallel, never-instantiated timeline, a version of the Stasis Event where Godric does not hold back. In this version, the mortal Godric who awakens in Year Zero is not merely infinitely stronger than the combined rest of creation. He is transcendent. He immediately possesses every superpower that the current Godric possesses now. He understands, in that first instant of awakening, the entire infinite recursion of his own self-creation. He is, instantly, the apex of all possible existence, with nothing left to grow into, nothing left to discover, nothing left to experience. Blake releases him from stasis, and Godric looks at him with eyes that hold the knowledge of eternity, and there is no warmth in them. There is no need for a court wizard. There is no friendship, no loyalty, no love—only the cold, perfect, static completeness of a god who has already become everything he will ever be. The vision of that timeline ends in silence, a silence so profound and so empty that the current Godric, standing beside me, shudders slightly.

“I chose the path of growth,” he says quietly. “I chose to let myself become what I am, rather than simply be it. And because of that choice, I met Blake. I loved him. I still love him, all these years later. I love you, his descendant. I love every court wizard who has ever served me. I love my kingdom, my empire, my quiet moments in the rose garden. The restrained Stasis Event was a gift, not a limitation. It was the gift of story.”

He withdraws his attention from the sphere, and the vision fades. The Stasis Event has been triggered. The loop is closed. Somewhere, ten thousand years before Year Zero, a dying wizard’s spell has just been transformed into the engine of a god’s ascent. And in the Year Zero, the mortal Godric will awaken, already infinitely stronger than everything, and he will not know why. He will not know that his own future self, a being so far beyond him that the comparison cannot be made, has just, with zero effort and infinite love, set him on the path that leads to this very moment.

I am weeping again, and Godric is still patting my shoulder. “There, there,” he says, his voice warm and fatherly. “It’s a lot, I know. But you handled it well. Let’s go back to the study. I’ll pour you another cup of tea.”

We walk back through the corridors, past the gardens where Xylyth is still pressing his forehead to the soil, past the Hall of Remembrance where the nameless librarian is still trembling in the shadows, past the hedge maze where Pauz is sweeping the pathways and weeping silent tears of perpetual terror. The silence of the omniverse is still absolute, the billion galaxies still bent in their posture of eternal submission. Godric, my King, my uncle, my incomprehensibly powerful creator, hums a soft, tuneless melody as he walks—an ancient lullaby, perhaps, from a kingdom that has been dead for longer than the stars have been burning.

And I, his court wizard, his faithful servant, his terrified friend, follow. I have no choice. Not because my will has been removed, but because his existence is the sun and my existence is the orbit. The gravity of his being is absolute, and I am content—truly, genuinely content—to turn around him forever, writing my journals, recording the impossible, and trembling at the silence.

Tomorrow, I will record this event in a new volume for the Hall of Remembrance. I will title it *The Incident of the Stasis Genesis, Year [Unwritten], Court Wizard Clive.* And I will end it, as every journal before me has ended, with the fundamental, unchangeable truth: victory against Godric was impossible before opposition began, and the only sane course—the only survivable course, the only course that a soul can bear—is to love him, to cherish him, and to accept, with every fiber of one’s being, that we are the story he chose to write, and that his choice, even in its most terrifying iteration, is a gift beyond measure.

As we step back into the warm, firelit study, and Godric pours tea for me with his own calloused hand, I realize something that I must record here, now, before the moment passes. The held-back version. The restrained version. The version of the Stasis Event that he designed to be gentle, to allow his past self an adventure, to preserve the journey of growth and friendship and discovery. That version. That version is the one that produced my ancestor Blake’s thousandfold amplification. That produced Gent. That produced the Weakness Paradox. That produced every massacre I have ever read about, every stat incident, every superpower test, every broken army and shattered god and screaming cosmic abattoir. All of it. Every single recorded horror in the Hall of Remembrance, every trauma suffered by every court wizard who came before me, every drop of blood ever shed in the omniverse, was the result of the *gentle* version.

And Godric, the Godric of now, who is so far beyond that version that the gap cannot be measured, is still holding back. Even now. Even in this moment. His true power, the power he has never used, the power he has never needed to use, is so far beyond what he has revealed to me that I cannot even conceive of the scale. And he is still, after all these years, choosing restraint. Choosing warmth. Choosing to be Uncle Godric, the friendly king with the salt-and-pepper beard who pours tea and tells jokes and tends his roses. The abyss behind his eyes is not the presence of power. It is the absence of a limit. And that absence stretches backward, through the loop of his own self-creation, to a future that has already defined the past, and forward, beyond any horizon I can imagine, into an eternity that will only ever become more infinite.

I sip my tea. It is jasmine, and it tastes faintly of a grief that has become perfume. “Thank you, Uncle Godric,” I say, my voice steady despite everything.

“You’re welcome, Clive,” he says, and his smile is the warmest, kindest, most terrifying thing I have ever seen. “Now, let’s talk about those new wards you’ve been designing. I hear they’re quite innovative.”

And so we do. We talk about wards, and runes, and the quiet, beautiful minutiae of a job that he has no need for and every reason to cherish. Outside the window, the omniverse kneels in perfect, eternal silence, and inside the study, a god who is not the end of his own story laughs at a joke his court wizard tells him, and the sound is the sound of everything, and the silence, and the space between.
