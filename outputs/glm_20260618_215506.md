# The Fall and the Ascension

The Kingdom of Olympus stood for millennia in the fertile valley between the Thornspine Mountains and the Silversea, a realm of marble halls and copper-roofed towers where the scent of olive groves drifted through open courtyards and the laughter of children echoed off stone that had been old before anyone could remember who laid it. It was small by the standards of future civilizations, intimate in its politics, vulnerable in its borders, but rich in ways that mattered to the people who called it home. King Godric ruled Olympus not from a distant throne but from the courtyard steps, where children would sit beside him and ask him questions about the stars. He was forty-five years old, tall and broad-shouldered, his highly muscular frame filling out the white and gold ceremonial robes of his office. His short salt-and-pepper hair and neatly trimmed beard framed a face that was ruggedly handsome, weathered by years of honest rule, and quick to break into a laugh that could be heard across the throne room. He had a habit of remembering the names of every farmer, every merchant, every child who approached him. When a family in the eastern quarter lost their home to fire two winters before, Godric was there before dawn, sleeves rolled past his elbows, hauling timber alongside the carpenters, and he stayed until the frame was raised, and he came back the next day to help with the roof. That was the kind of king he was.

On the morning of the wedding, Godric stood before a mirror in the royal chambers while Reina fastened the clasp of his ceremonial cloak. She was gentle where he was rough, her hands steady, her eyes soft with the particular patience of a woman who had spent twenty-three years loving a man who cried at everything. Isabel, twenty-two years old and radiant, was already in the temple with the attendants. Hebe, fifteen and still young enough to be mortified by her father's open weeping, had gone ahead to make sure the flower arrangements were perfect, a task she had taken so seriously that she had rearranged them four times. Godric had cried three times already that morning, and Reina had dried his eyes each time with her sleeve, telling him he was ridiculous. Thorin, forty-seven, Godric's best friend and court wizard whose family had served the kings of Olympus for countless generations, waited in the corridor, leaning against the wall with the casual impatience of a man who had known the king since boyhood and had long since stopped being impressed by anything he did. He made a joke about Godric's tears flooding the temple and ruining the flower arrangements, which would upset Hebe, which would upset everyone. Godric threw a boot at him. Thorin caught it without looking.

The ceremony began at midday. The temple of Olympus was filled with light from the high windows, the air thick with incense and olive blossoms, the pews packed with every family in the kingdom who had ever had their name remembered by their king. Isabel stood at the altar in white and gold, her hands trembling slightly, her smile so wide it seemed to hold the entire room together. Godric stood before her, the words of the officiation on his lips, his eyes wet, his heart so full it ached in his chest. Hebe sat in the front row beside Reina, both of them already crying, and Thorin stood at the back with his arms crossed, pretending he was not crying too. It was the happiest day of Godric's life.

The first wall exploded inward at the exact moment Godric opened his mouth to speak.

Thronegate's soldiers poured through the breach in numbers that made no sense, that could not have been assembled without months of planning, without betrayal at every level of Olympus's court. They came in black armor with the silver gate emblem on their breastplates, and they came killing. The temple filled with screaming and the wet sound of blades finding flesh. Godric grabbed Isabel and pulled her behind him, but there were too many, coming too fast, pouring through every entrance like water through a cracked dam. Reina screamed his name from somewhere to his left. Hebe was torn from her seat. Thorin's hands blazed with defensive magic, tearing through the first wave of soldiers, arcs of raw arcane force that sent armored bodies spinning through the air, buying seconds that felt like hours. A blade found Reina's throat. Godric saw it happen. He saw the light leave her eyes. He saw her body fall with the particular gracelessness of a thing that was no longer alive, her hand still reaching toward him. He screamed, and the sound was not human. Isabel was next, dragged from his grip by four soldiers, and when he lunged for her, a war hammer caught him in the ribs and sent him crashing through the altar. He came up bleeding, reaching for her, crawling over rubble and broken flowers, and watched a sword run her through. She looked at him as she died, and her expression was confusion, not pain, as though she could not understand why her father could not fix this. Hebe's scream cut short somewhere behind him. He could not turn around. He could not look. Thorin was there, blood streaming from a wound in his chest, his hands already moving through the gestures of a spell Godric had never seen, ancient and forbidden, something that pulsed with a magic so old it predated Olympus itself. It was a teleportation spell, exclusive to Thorin's bloodline, whispered across generations of court wizards but never spoken aloud, never taught, never written. Thorin's lips moved through the incantation, his eyes locked on Godric's, and then a Thronegate spear punched through his back and emerged from his sternum. Thorin's mouth opened. Blood poured out. His hands faltered. The spell fractured mid-cast, incomplete, broken, and then the world dissolved around Godric in a flash of light that consumed everything.

The last thing Godric saw was Thorin's body hitting the temple floor.

The light deposited Godric in a place he did not recognize. A sanctuary, hidden deep beneath rock and root, a chamber of smooth stone lit by no source he could identify. He collapsed to his knees, the images of Reina's falling body and Isabel's eyes and Hebe's scream seared into him like brands pressed into flesh. His hands shook. His breath came in ragged, animal gasps. The man who had hauled timber for strangers and wept at his daughter's wedding was gone. What remained was something harder, something whose grief had already begun to calcify into a substance far more dangerous than sorrow.

Then the stasis took him.

It happened in a single instant, a boundary in time so thin it had no measurable width. Godric's body locked. His aging, the slow biological clock that had carried him through forty-five years of mortal life, stopped completely and permanently. The lines of experience on his face, the salt-and-pepper of his hair, the muscular frame, the ruggedly handsome features, all of it became fixed, frozen at the age of forty-five for the rest of eternity, even after he would eventually wake. His lungs ceased to require air. His body ceased to require food or water. His mind ceased to require sleep. These needs were not suppressed. They were removed, excised from the fundamental architecture of his existence with a permanence that would outlast the stars.

At that same exact moment, something far more terrifying began.

Every measurable and immeasurable trait Godric possessed, his strength, his endurance, his speed, his senses, his magic, his mana, his magical offense, his magical defense, his charisma, his luck, his wisdom, his intelligence, his durability, his perception, and every other quality that could be named or could not be named, began to multiply. The rate was fixed and relentless: each stat multiplied by one thousand, and then that result multiplied by itself, and this happened every Planck second, the smallest measurable unit of time that existed. Each multiplication built on the last. Each second contained more Planck ticks than the human mind could process. The growth was not linear or even exponential in any ordinary sense. It was an avalanche fed by avalanches, each layer of amplification becoming the foundation for the next, compounding without end, without pause, without limit. This would continue forever. The number that described his strength even one minute into the stasis was already so large that writing it down would require more characters than atoms in a galaxy. And it would only grow.

Simultaneously, he began to gain a new superpower every single second. Each one, at the very instant of its manifestation, was already capable of causing instant extinction to an entire civilization. A power that could unmake matter. A power that could sever a mind from its body across any distance. A power that could collapse dimensional barriers. One per second, each one arriving already weaponized, already apocalyptic. And like his stats, the potency of every single superpower also multiplied by one thousand and by itself every Planck second, forever. They did not wait to become dangerous. They were born dangerous, and then they became worse.

The first power to manifest was concealment. It had to be. Without it, the raw overflow of what Godric was becoming would have unraveled the fabric of existence around him in an instant. His aura, his destructive presence, his metaphysical weight, the passive pressure of a being whose stats were multiplying beyond mathematical description, all of it was perfectly suppressed, held in check by a concealment ability that itself amplified at the same terrifying rate as everything else. The omniverse did not collapse because this power ensured it would not. The concealment was not a wall. It was an absolute condition of his existence, a permanent filter that grew stronger every Planck second, forever, ensuring that what Godric was becoming would remain contained until he chose otherwise.

And then there was the paradox.

Godric had no weaknesses. This was not a boast or an aspiration. It was a fundamental, absolute truth woven into the structure of his new existence. He was invincible in every sense that mattered: physical, magical, conceptual, temporal, metaphysical. No exploit existed. No loophole gaped. No weapon could touch him. But the omniverse was vast, and sentient beings were arrogant creatures by nature. The very moment any entity, anywhere in all of creation, even for a fraction of a second, dared to think that Godric possessed a weakness, the paradox activated. It did not activate when the attack came. It did not activate when the plan was formed. It activated at the first flicker of the thought itself, the nascent, barely conscious suspicion that perhaps this being could be harmed in some way. At that moment, before the entity had even decided what the weakness was, before the thought had fully crystallized into intention, three things happened simultaneously and permanently. First, the rate of Godric's stat multiplication increased, permanently, to a new level even more unfathomable than the one before. Second, Godric gained absolute, permanent immunity to the falsely perceived exploit, rendering the imagined attack fundamentally impossible against him. Third, Godric acquired perfect mastery of the very attack, strategy, or weapon the entity would eventually conceive, able to deploy it with any magnitude at any time, while simultaneously gaining the ability to deny the concept of that attack to all of creation unless he chose to permit it. This was passive. Godric did not need to notice. The defense responded to insolence with punishment, and it did so without exception, without delay, and without mercy. Every second an enemy spent planning their exploit, they were feeding him. By the time the attack arrived, it had already been neutralized, its wielder had already strengthened their own destroyer, and Godric had already mastered their technique better than they ever would. The entity's fate was sealed not at the moment of attack, but at the moment of the first passing thought.

The cause of all of this, the stasis, the amplification, the superpowers, the concealment, the paradox, remained a mystery. Whatever force had transformed the incomplete teleportation spell into this engine of infinite ascension was not visible, not identifiable, not understandable. It simply was.

Godric slept in the stone sanctuary beneath the earth, and while he slept, he became a god.

Ten thousand years passed.

The Kingdom of Olympus was forgotten. No historian remembered its name. No map marked its borders. The marble halls had crumbled to dust beneath the Thornspine Mountains, and the Silversea had drained into geological oblivion. The bloodline of its kings, the names of its people, the story of its fall, all of it erased by the slow, grinding indifference of time. Thronegate, the kingdom that had betrayed and destroyed Olympus, had not been erased. Thronegate had endured. It had grown. Across ten millennia, it expanded from a regional power into a continental empire, then a planetary civilization, then a stellar one, absorbing technology and magic and biology from every corner of its galaxy until it stood as the single most powerful civilization in the known galaxy. Its armies were unmatched. Its fleets were countless. Its king in the year zero of the new calendar was a twenty-two-year-old named Robert, who sat on a throne forged from the hulls of conquered dreadnoughts and believed, as all Thronegate kings believed, that his civilization was the apex of existence.

Blake was thirty-eight years old, a merchant with a friendly mutton chop and a short temper, and he was lost. He had been navigating the trade routes along the outer rim when a malfunction in his ship's navigation system dumped him into uncharted space. His wife Anna, twelve-year-old Josh, and eight-year-old Rhea were aboard, and Anna was telling him to ask for directions, which he refused to do on principle. The ship's sensors detected a planetary body that should not have existed, a world that appeared on no chart, orbited no star, and emitted no radiation. Blake landed anyway, because Blake was the kind of man who walked toward the strange thing rather than away from it. What he found beneath the surface of the dead world was a chamber of smooth stone, lit by no source he could identify, and in the center of that chamber, a man sitting in a chair as though he had merely paused to rest.

The man had short salt-and-pepper hair, a neatly trimmed beard, and a muscular build that was visible even beneath the dusty remnants of ancient ceremonial robes. He looked forty-five. He looked like he had been there for a very long time. Blake's hand, without his conscious direction, moved to the wall beside the entrance and pressed against a recessed panel that no one could have known was there. Blake did not know why he did it. Something in his blood, something old and untraceable, something that resonated with the stone and the silence and the sleeping man, moved his hand to the right place at the right time. The panel clicked. The chamber hummed. The man's eyes opened.

Godric woke, and in the instant of waking, he knew everything. Ten thousand years of history compressed into a single, silent infusion. He knew the state of the galaxy. He knew Thronegate's military capacity down to the last soldier and the last bolt in the last rifle. He knew Robert's name, age, insecurities, and bedtime routine. He knew the name of every star in every charted system and the name of every star in every uncharted system. He knew Blake's name before Blake spoke it. He knew Blake's wife's name, his son's name, his daughter's name. He knew things about Blake that Blake did not know about himself, threads of lineage that stretched backward through ten thousand years of forgotten history to a dying wizard with a spear through his chest, though this knowledge remained Godric's alone, a private recognition he offered no explanation for. He knew the state of every molecule in the chamber, the planet, the system, the galaxy, and beyond. He knew, with absolute and total certainty, the outcome of every choice that would be made in the next ten thousand years and the next ten million and the next ten billion. He was omniscient, and omniscience did not feel like a burden. It felt like breathing.

He looked at Blake, and something in his chest shifted. The face was wrong, the voice was wrong, the era was wrong, but there was something in the man's posture, in the way his hand had found that panel without thinking, in the stubborn set of his jaw and the honest confusion in his eyes, that stirred something old and buried. Godric stood, and the stasis was over, but the amplification continued, and the superpowers continued, and the concealment held, and the paradox waited, and Godric looked at Blake and said his name as though they had known each other for years.

Blake stared. Anna stared. Josh and Rhea stared from the ship's ramp. Godric asked Blake where they were, what year it was, who ruled the galaxy. Blake answered each question with the blunt honesty that was his defining trait, the kind of honesty that had gotten him thrown out of three different merchant guilds and respected by every honest trader on the route. When Blake mentioned Thronegate, something moved behind Godric's eyes, a flicker so cold that Blake took a step backward without meaning to. Godric asked who sat on the throne. Blake said Robert. Godric nodded once and told Blake he needed to send a message.

The message reached King Robert within the hour. It was short. A man named Godric challenged the king of Thronegate. If Godric could defeat the entirety of Thronegate's military, Robert would abdicate. Robert had three days to gather his forces. Robert, who had never heard the name Godric, who had never heard of Olympus, who sat on a throne of conquered dreadnoughts and commanded the most powerful military in the galaxy, laughed.

Three days later, Thronegate's army assembled on the plains of the capital world. It was the largest military concentration in galactic history. Billions of super-soldiers stood in formation, their augmentations pushing them beyond any natural biological limit, each one capable of tearing through steel plating with bare hands, each one conditioned to feel no fear and accept no defeat. Behind them, the magical corps churned with enough arcane energy to crack a moon in half, court wizards of Thronegate chanting layered enchantments that had been refined over centuries of continuous warfare, their voices synchronized into a harmonic that made the air itself vibrate with destructive potential. The robot armies gleamed in the sunlight, machines of war so advanced that each individual unit could calculate and execute a million kill strategies per second, their targeting systems processing battlefields across seventeen simultaneous tactical dimensions. Above them, dragons circled, beasts bred for continental destruction, each one large enough to cast a shadow over an entire city, their breath hot enough to melt bedrock into slag, their scales harder than any alloy Thronegate's foundries could produce. And moving between the formations, invisible to all but the most sensitive instruments, were Thronegate's ninja corps, assassins so fast they could reverse time within a localized field, their blades designed to cut through dimensional fabric itself, their training so refined that they could kill a target before the target's brain registered that a decision to kill had been made.

They were confident. They had no reason not to be. They were the greatest military force the galaxy had ever produced, assembled in full, ready for war. King Robert watched from the command spire, surrounded by his generals, and smiled the smile of a man who had already won.

Godric stood in front of them. Alone. He did not draw a weapon. He did not raise a hand. He did not speak. He simply stood there, a forty-five-year-old man in dusty robes, his salt-and-pepper beard catching the wind, his expression flat and distant, his eyes fixed on something no one else could see. The amplification that had been running for ten thousand years, multiplying every stat by one thousand and by itself every Planck second without pause, had produced a being whose power could not be meaningfully expressed in numbers. Even the infinitesimal fraction of his strength that constituted effectively zero percent of his total capacity was already sufficient to destroy the entire omniverse infinite times over in zero seconds with zero effort. He was using none of it. He was standing still and thinking about the weather.

Blake and Anna watched from the ridge. Blake had his arm around Anna, who was trembling. Josh and Rhea were in the ship, sealed inside, because Blake had told them to stay and Josh had argued and Blake had won the argument the way fathers win arguments, by being louder and meaning it more.

The first wave of super-soldiers charged. Three hundred million of them, moving in perfect synchronization, augmented bodies blurring with speed, weapons drawn, the ground shaking under the collective weight of their advance. They crossed the distance in seconds. Godric did not move. He did not blink. He did not shift his weight. Something happened that had no visible cause. The front line of the charge simply stopped existing. Not destroyed, not killed, not disintegrated. The atoms that comprised them ceased to be atoms. The space they occupied forgot that matter was supposed to be there. The soldiers behind them ran into the absence and kept running, and then they too ceased, one after another, a chain of annihilation that rolled backward through the formation faster than the eye could follow. Three hundred million soldiers vanished in less than a second. No sound. No light. No explosion. They were there, and then they were not, and the ground where they had stood was undisturbed, the grass still bent in the direction of their charge, marking the path of men who no longer existed.

The magical corps unleashed everything they had. Layered enchantments refined over centuries, spells designed to crack moons, to unmake matter, to sever souls from bodies. The combined arcane output was enough to sterilize a solar system. It reached Godric and dissolved. Not deflected. Not absorbed. Dissolved, the way morning dew dissolves when the sun rises, the magic simply ceasing to be magic, the energy forgetting what it was and becoming nothing. The court wizards of Thronegate felt their connections to the arcane sever simultaneously, a thousand voices crying out as the power they had spent lifetimes mastering was simply, quietly, irreversibly denied to them. Godric had not moved. The concealment held. The paradox had not even needed to activate, because no one had thought to exploit a weakness. They were simply attacking, and attacking was not enough.

The robot armies calculated their million kill strategies per second and executed all of them simultaneously. Plasma barrages, dimensional shears, antimatter payloads, quantum-collapse beams. The combined firepower could have reduced a continent to glass. It hit the space around Godric and became inert. The plasma cooled to room temperature. The dimensional shears mended themselves. The antimatter detonated harmlessly, producing no energy. The quantum-collapse beams collapsed into static. The robots recalculated, found no strategy that produced a different result, and continued firing anyway, because machines do not know how to despair. Not yet.

The dragons descended. Twenty of them, each one a continental weapon, their breath merging into a wall of flame that could have melted the planet's crust to liquid rock. The fire reached Godric and went out. Not extinguished. Cancelled. The concept of combustion in Godric's immediate vicinity was revoked, and the dragons, still moving, still roaring, still enormous and terrible, found themselves falling from the sky as gravity remembered them. Their wings beat uselessly against air that no longer cooperated. Their fire would not come. They hit the ground with impacts that shook the plain for kilometers, and they did not rise again, not because they were injured, but because the air around Godric had decided that flying was no longer permitted in its presence.

The ninja corps was the last to move. They reversed time within their localized fields, repositioning themselves in the past so that they arrived behind Godric before the battle had even begun. Their blades, designed to cut through dimensional fabric, found Godric's neck. The blades shattered. Not against skin. Against the idea of skin, the concept of Godric's body being a thing that could be touched by an external object. The idea refused to cooperate. The ninjas, frozen in their own reversed time fields, experienced something they had no framework to process. The time fields collapsed inward on them, compressing seconds into instants, and each ninja experienced a subjective eternity of standing in front of a man who had not moved, had not looked at them, had not acknowledged their existence in any way, while every cell in their bodies quietly decided to stop being alive. They died standing up, blades in hand, expressions frozen in confusion.

The entire army, billions of soldiers, the magical corps, the robot legions, the dragons, the ninja corps, all of it, was gone in under a minute. Godric had not moved a muscle. The plain was empty except for him and the undisturbed dirt beneath his feet. Blake stood on the ridge with his arm around Anna, his mouth open, his mind struggling to process what he had just seen. Anna was crying, not from fear exactly, but from something deeper, something that lived in the oldest part of the brain, the part that understood on an instinctive level that it had just witnessed a force that could not be categorized or comprehended or opposed. Blake's hands were shaking. He did not know why he was not afraid. He should have been. Every rational part of his mind screamed that the man standing in that field was the most dangerous thing in existence. But something else, something deeper than rationality, something that hummed in his blood the same way it had hummed when his hand found the panel, told him he was safe. He did not understand it. He would not understand it for years.

Two days later, King Robert was found dead in his chambers, a blade through his own heart, his face frozen in an expression of absolute and uncomprehending terror. Godric crowned himself King of Thronegate that afternoon. The ceremony was brief. He stood in the throne room, placed the crown on his own head, and spoke the first decree of his reign.

He called it the Awakening. Every man of Thronegate, past the age of twenty, would find that each day of his life increased his strength, endurance, intelligence, and wisdom tenfold. The increase was daily, permanent, and compounding. A man who was thirty would have ten years of daily tenfold increases layered into his being. A man who was fifty would have thirty. The Awakening applied retroactively to every man already past twenty, and they felt it hit immediately, a sudden, staggering influx of power that made the strongest soldiers of the old Thronegate feel like children holding sticks. The men could suppress this power, dial it down to normal human levels, or raise it to any degree up to their maximum. They remained mortal. They would still age and die, typically around eighty. The Awakening did not grant immortality. It granted purpose.

Blake felt something different. The same warmth, the same daily increase, but the magnitude was wrong. Where other men felt tenfold, Blake felt a thousandfold, a surge so disproportionate that he staggered and had to sit down on the throne room floor while the other men around him were still processing their own transformation. Godric watched him with an expression that Blake could not read, something between recognition and private, almost amused knowledge, and said nothing about it. Blake did not understand why his gift was different. He did not question it. He would not question it for years.

Under Godric's rule, Thronegate changed. Women were placed under absolute protection, their safety guaranteed not by law but by the certainty that any harm done to them would result in consequences so thorough that the concept of repetition became impossible. Men revered Godric with a fervor that bordered on worship, not because they were commanded to, but because the Awakening had made them powerful beyond their ancestors' dreams, and they knew exactly who had given them that gift. Citizens loved their king. The streets were safe. The economy flourished. The rule was stable, generous, and perfect by the lived experience of everyone within it. The horror of what Godric was, what he could do, what he had done to the old army in under a minute, lived only in the memories of those who had witnessed it, and even they struggled to hold the memory without flinching.

Thronegate expanded. Beyond the galaxy, beyond the cosmos, into universes and multiverses that had never heard the name. Godric could have subjugated the entire omniverse in the instant of his awakening, destroyed and recreated it infinite times in zero seconds with no effort, but he had been mortal once. He believed in resilience. He believed in free will. He gave his Awakened men purpose, and he let them carry his banner into the unknown.

The rule of conquest was simple. Each targeted universe was offered unconditional surrender first. If they accepted, they were assimilated. If they refused, they were conquered. The conquered did not receive the Awakening. It was a gift for Thronegate alone.

The first universe to refuse was a cosmos of crystalline intelligence, a civilization that had existed for eons, its inhabitants composed of living thought, their technology woven into the fabric of their reality itself. They had learned of falling neighboring cosmos five years before Thronegate's offer arrived, and they had spent every day of those five years preparing. They hardened their dimensional walls to a density that could withstand the heat of a dying universe. They forged weapons from collapsed timelines, each one capable of erasing a target from all points in history simultaneously. They assembled armies of conceptual soldiers who could not die because they were not technically alive. Their mathematicians calculated victory at ninety-four percent probability. They were ready.

Thronegate sent five men. All were over thirty-five, all Awakened, all carrying decades of daily tenfold compounding in their blood. They were fathers and husbands, men who would rather have been home with their children, but their king had asked them to go, and so they went. They arrived in the crystalline universe and stood before the assembled might of a civilization that had prepared for five years. The five men looked uncomfortable. One of them scratched the back of his neck and asked another if he had remembered to lock his front door before they left. The crystalline intelligence launched its first attack, a weapon forged from a collapsed timeline that was designed to erase the targets from all points in history simultaneously. The attack reached the lead Thronegate soldier, a forty-two-year-old former carpenter named Oren, and bounced off him like a thrown pebble. Oren looked at his companions. His companions shrugged. Oren raised his hand, not to attack but to gesture, the way a man waves away a persistent insect, and the gesture propagated through the crystalline universe at a speed that had no name. The dimensional walls shattered like glass. The conceptual soldiers unraveled, their non-life becoming non-existence with a smoothness that suggested reality had been waiting for permission. The collapsed-timeline weapons collapsed into themselves and became nothing, winking out like candles in a wind that did not exist. In twenty-three minutes, the crystalline civilization surrendered unconditionally, and the five men went home and had dinner with their families, and Oren's wife asked him how his day was, and he said it was fine, and he meant it.

The second universe refused with a declaration of war so theatrical it would have been amusing under different circumstances. It was a universe of absolute order, a mechanized cosmos governed by a single omniscient artificial intelligence that had calculated every possible outcome of every possible conflict across seventeen trillion simulated scenarios and determined that resistance was not only viable but optimal. The intelligence had existed for nine hundred million years. It had never been wrong. Godric was not planning to be involved in this conquest. He was in a mildly inconvenienced mood that morning because Blake had burned the breakfast grain and the acrid smell had drifted through the ventilation into the throne room, and that infinitesimal displeasure, that fractional irritation, a feeling so small that Godric himself would not have called it a feeling at all, propagated across the omniverse without Godric moving, speaking, or consciously directing anything toward any target. The universe of absolute order simply ceased. Not destroyed. Not attacked. Not invaded. Its existence became logically inconsistent with the ambient state of Godric's mild displeasure, and reality resolved the contradiction by removing the universe the way a body removes a splinter, quietly, efficiently, without ceremony. The conquest lasted zero seconds. The omniscient artificial intelligence that had never been wrong was wrong for the first and last time, and it would never know it, because it no longer existed in any state that allowed for knowing. Godric never learned the name of the universe. Blake apologized for the breakfast. Godric told him it was fine and suggested a lower heat setting next time.

The third universe was different. Its ruler was a being called Sellathis, an entity that existed across seven dimensions simultaneously and had accumulated power over four hundred thousand years of continuous, unbroken existence. Sellathis was, by any standard the omniverse could apply, one of the most powerful beings in creation. It had survived the collapse of three previous cosmoses. It had consumed the energy of dying gods. It had built an empire spanning nine hundred universes, each one fortified, weaponized, and populated with loyal soldiers who would die at a word. Sellathis was not a fool. Sellathis was not arrogant. Sellathis was old, careful, and intelligent beyond the comprehension of mortal minds. And Sellathis had a plan.

The plan began with a thought. A single, idle, barely conscious thought that flickered through Sellathis's seven-dimensional mind while observing reports of Thronegate's expansion: perhaps Godric has a weakness.

In the instant that thought formed, before Sellathis had identified what the weakness might be, before a plan existed, before a strategy was conceived, before the thought had even fully crystallized into something that could be called an idea, the Weakness Paradox activated. Godric's amplification rate permanently increased to a new level that made the previous rate look like stillness. Godric gained absolute immunity to whatever exploit Sellathis would eventually imagine. Godric acquired perfect mastery of whatever attack Sellathis would eventually design, understanding it more intimately than Sellathis ever could, able to deploy it with any magnitude at any time. None of this was visible. None of it was detectable. Sellathis did not know it had happened. Sellathis only knew that the idea had come, and the idea felt promising, and promising ideas were worth pursuing.

Over the next three years, Sellathis refined the plan with meticulous, seven-dimensional precision. The perceived weakness was this: Godric had been mortal once. Mortality left traces. Emotional attachment, grief, memory. Sellathis theorized that the emotional architecture of Godric's mortal mind, the love for a dead wife and dead daughters, the grief for a fallen kingdom and a murdered best friend, could be targeted through a psycho-temporal weapon that would project the pain of those memories into Godric's consciousness with enough force to paralyze him. The weapon took three years to build. Sellathis harvested the energy of seventeen collapsing multiverses to power it. Each component was tested across a thousand simulated timelines. The math was perfect. The engineering was flawless. Sellathis calculated a seventy-eight percent chance of success, which was the highest probability Sellathis had ever assigned to any undertaking, and Sellathis had undertaken many things over four hundred thousand years.

Every second of those three years, the Weakness Paradox continued to feed Godric. His amplification rate increased further. His immunity to the psycho-temporal attack deepened further.
