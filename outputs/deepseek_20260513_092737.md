# The Journals of Futility

## Year 1,000,000

The heavy bronze doors of the ancestral archive groaned shut behind me with a sound like the sealing of a tomb. I stood alone in the vast, circular chamber carved deep into the bedrock beneath the royal palace of Thronegate, my breath forming faint wisps of vapor in the cold, still air. My name is Kaedan. I am twenty-two years old. For seven hundred and thirty days, ever since I crossed the threshold of my twentieth birthday, every single measurable attribute within my being has multiplied by a factor of one thousand. My physical strength, my cognitive speed, the vast oceans of my mana, my magical offense and defense—all of it, doubling and redoubling daily like a relentless tidal wave of arcane apocalypse crashing against the shores of my mortal flesh. I am the newest Royal Court Wizard of Thronegate, the latest in an unbroken bloodline stretching back to Blake himself, the legendary best friend of our sovereign. And today, as my father had informed me with a grim, haunted look in his ancient eyes, I was required to read the journals.

The archive stretched before me in concentric rings of obsidian shelves, each one bearing rows upon rows of leather-bound volumes, their spines cracked with age and their pages yellowed by the slow crawl of millennia. These were the personal journals of every single court wizard who had served before me, stretching back to the dawn of Year Zero. My father, a man of seventy-two years whose thousandfold daily amplification had made him a walking cataclysm by any external standard, had handed me the key to this chamber with trembling hands. He had not spoken a word of warning. He had simply looked at me with an expression of profound, aching pity, and then he had turned and walked away, his shoulders hunched beneath the weight of memories he had never shared.

I walked slowly along the first ring of shelves, my fingertips brushing against the cracked leather spines. The earliest volumes were barely legible, their ink faded to ghostly impressions, their pages brittle as dead leaves. But as I moved deeper into the archive, the journals became more recent, their bindings more intact, their handwriting more desperate. I paused before a shelf marked with a small bronze plaque: *Year 28 to Year 38 – The Rogr Incident*. My father had mentioned this one once, late at night, when he thought I was asleep. He had been weeping.

I pulled the volume from the shelf and began to read.

## Journal of Blake, First Royal Court Wizard of Thronegate

### Year 38

I have committed a sin of such catastrophic magnitude that the weight of it will press upon my soul until the merciful darkness of death finally claims me. I am writing this account so that my descendants, the future court wizards of this empire, may understand the terrifying, absolute truth of the being we serve. Godric is my best friend. I love him with a fierce, unyielding devotion that has defined the entirety of my existence since the day I stumbled into that forgotten sanctuary and shattered his stasis. But love does not blind me to the horror. Love does not excuse what my careless words have wrought.

Ten years ago, in Year 28, I made a joke. It was a casual, offhand remark, the kind of idle banter that passes between old friends who have shared decades of companionship. A delegation from a newly subjugated universe was arriving to present their formal treaties. They expected to meet a blood-soaked tyrant, a merciless warlord who crushed galaxies beneath his heel. They had heard the stories of the Luminal Expanse, of the crystalline entities, of the Axiom Mind. They were terrified. And I, in my infinite stupidity, suggested that Godric play into their fears. I told him to act the part of the merciless tyrant for a bit of fun. Just a prank, I said. Just a moment of theatrical menace to make them squirm.

Godric trusted me. He followed my suggestion.

The delegates arrived. Godric looked at them. And for a fraction of a second—a span of time so infinitesimally small that it defies measurement—he feigned mild displeasure. He held back, by his own estimation, nearly one hundred percent of his power. He believed he was giving them a gentle, harmless scare.

I was standing in the shadows of the throne room when it happened. I felt the absolute concealment slip by a margin so minuscule that no mathematical language can describe it. The fraction was smaller than the smallest conceivable decimal, an unquantifiable whisper of his true presence bleeding through the veil. And in that instant, I understood exactly what my joke had done.

One of the delegates collapsed immediately. His name was Rogr. His brain hemorrhaged, his neural pathways physically tearing apart under the psychic weight of Godric's infinitesimal displeasure. The other delegates screamed, their minds fracturing, their souls permanently scarred by the sudden, crushing awareness of their own absolute insignificance. Rogr fell into a vegetative state of pure, uninterrupted terror. For ten years, he sat in his home, trembling, drooling, staring at the wall, his consciousness trapped in an endless loop of existential horror. His wife Rosa spent their entire fortune trying to cure him. Every attempt failed. Neuro-splicers fled screaming from the residual echo of Godric's gaze. Celestial artifacts shattered. An ancient conceptual entity of peace voluntarily dissolved its own consciousness rather than share a cosmos with the being who had inflicted such pressure.

Ten years. And then, this morning, Godric casually mentioned Rogr's condition to me. He had known all along, of course. His absolute omniscience meant he had watched every failed cure, every desperate prayer, every tear Rosa shed. He simply had not intervened. When I demanded to know why, he looked genuinely confused. He told me he believed Rogr would eventually recover. He remembered being mortal, ten thousand years ago, and he thought mortals were resilient. He fundamentally lacked the context to understand that his mildest joke was an apocalyptic event.

I fell to my knees before Rosa and confessed my role. I begged her permission to have Godric cure her husband. She gave it. And Godric, standing in her living room without having moved a single muscle, simply decided that Rogr should be healthy. The madness vanished instantly. Rogr's atrophied muscles engorged with perfect vitality. He stood up, whole and restored, because the apex predator of the omniverse had casually willed it so.

But I will never forget the moment after, when Godric clapped me on the shoulder and asked, with genuine, innocent warmth, if we should try the tyrant routine again for the next delegation. He had learned nothing. He could not learn. The gap between his existence and ours is so vast that empathy itself becomes a mathematical impossibility. I screamed at him. I threatened to strangle him. And he just laughed, that warm, rich, terrifying laugh, and told me I was a good friend.

I am writing this as a warning to all who come after me. Godric loves us. That love is genuine and absolute. But his love does not, and cannot, protect us from the catastrophic consequences of his casual, unthinking whims. Never joke with the King. Never forget what he is. Never forget that the only reason we still exist is because he wills it, and that a single, passing thought of displeasure could unmake everything we know.

My son Josh will one day take my place. I pray he reads this and understands.

"Enter," I call out, my rich baritone carrying easily through the heavy obsidian door.

The door swings open, and Tom steps into the study, his ceremonial robes rustling softly against the polished floor. He is taller than his father was, broader in the shoulders, his hair now completely gray after five decades of service. His eyes, however, are the same as they have always been—sharp, intelligent, carrying the faint, haunted shadow that every court wizard acquires after witnessing enough of my power.

"My King," he says, bowing his head respectfully. "I have brought my son, as we discussed. He is ready to begin his service."

Jim steps into the study behind his father, and I feel a genuine, unforced warmth spreading through my chest. The boy has grown into a man since I last saw him—taller now, his shoulders broader, his face losing the soft roundness of childhood and taking on the sharper angles of adulthood. His hair is the same dark brown as his mother's, his eyes the same bright blue as his father's. He wears the simple robes of an apprentice court wizard, the fabric still crisp and new, not yet worn soft by years of service.

"Uncle Godric," he says, and his voice carries a note of genuine, unforced affection that makes my smile widen. He has never feared me. Not truly. Even after reading the journals, even after learning the full, horrifying scope of my power, he has somehow retained the simple, childlike love he felt for me when he was young. It is a rare gift, that kind of love. I treasure it more than he will ever know.

"Jim," I say, stepping forward to clasp his shoulder with a warm, calloused hand. "Welcome. I have been looking forward to this day for a long time."

---

The morning passes in a blur of official duties, the standard responsibilities that every court wizard must master. I show Jim the palace wards, the ancient arcane matrices that have protected Thronegate since the earliest days of my reign. I explain the protocols for receiving diplomatic delegations from subjugated universes, the careful balance of firmness and fairness that must be maintained when dealing with newly conquered civilizations. I walk him through the archives, the vast, circular chamber where the journals of his predecessors are stored, and I watch his expression shift as he passes the shelves where his father's journal will one day be placed.

Tom observes all of this with the quiet, watchful patience of a father who has spent decades teaching his son the basics of their shared profession. But I can perceive the tension in his shoulders, the faint, suppressed anxiety that hums beneath his calm exterior. He is waiting for something. He is waiting for the lesson that cannot be taught in a classroom, the lesson that his own father taught him on his first day of service, the lesson that every court wizard learns eventually.

I decide to oblige him.

"Jim," I say, my voice carrying a note of casual, conversational curiosity. "Have you ever wondered what happens to the petitions that I reject?"

Jim blinks, caught off guard by the question. "Petitions, Uncle Godric? You mean the requests from subject civilizations?"

"Yes. The petitions. Every day, thousands of requests arrive from across the omniverse—appeals for intervention, demands for resources, pleas for mercy, offers of tribute. Most are handled by the imperial bureaucracy. But a select few are forwarded to me for personal review." I pause, allowing my expression to shift fractionally, my smile fading into something more serious. "And some of those petitions offend me."

Tom's posture stiffens almost imperceptibly. He knows what is coming. He has witnessed this before.

"Offend you, Uncle Godric?" Jim asks, his voice carefully neutral.

"Yes. There are certain requests that I find... distasteful. Civilizations that ask for things they do not deserve. Leaders who demand privileges they have not earned. Species who petition for exemptions from the laws that govern all my subjects equally." I walk to my desk, where a small stack of holographic documents hovers above the polished obsidian surface. "Today, I received a petition from a civilization in the Vaelen Cluster. They requested permission to resume the practice of ritual sacrifice—a practice that I banned throughout the empire seven hundred thousand years ago."

Jim's expression shifts with dawning understanding. "And you rejected the petition."

"I did. But rejection is not always sufficient. Sometimes, a civilization needs to understand exactly why their petition was rejected. Sometimes, they need a demonstration." I raise my hand, a casual, effortless gesture, and the holographic documents vanish, replaced by a shimmering image of a small, green planet orbiting a distant star. "This is their homeworld. Beautiful, isn't it?"

Jim stares at the image, his eyes wide. "Yes, Uncle Godric. It's... it's lovely."

"It is. And it has been lovely for three billion years. But its inhabitants have grown complacent. They have forgotten why ritual sacrifice is forbidden. They have convinced themselves that the old ways were better, that my laws are an imposition on their cultural heritage." I pause, allowing my expression to harden fractionally. "They need to be reminded."

I allow my absolute concealment to slip by an infinitesimal, incomprehensibly small fraction—a percentage so close to zero that calling it zero would be a grotesque overestimation. Not enough to harm anyone. Not enough to cause the kind of catastrophic collateral damage that has accompanied my more dramatic demonstrations. Just enough to let the inhabitants of that small, green planet feel the faintest, most distant echo of my displeasure.

The effect is immediate. On the shimmering holographic image, I watch as the population of the planet—a species of tall, graceful humanoids with pale blue skin and feathered wings—collapses simultaneously. Not physically. Their bodies remain standing, their wings still spread, their eyes still open. But their minds are suddenly, irrevocably flooded with the absolute, crushing awareness of my disapproval. They feel it as a physical weight, a pressure against their souls, a cold, undeniable certainty that they have made a terrible, catastrophic mistake.

"It will pass," I say, my voice calm and conversational. "In a few hours, they will recover. Their minds will still carry the echo of my displeasure, but they will be able to function again. And they will never, ever petition me for the restoration of ritual sacrifice again."

Jim stares at the holographic image, his face pale, his breath coming in short, shallow gasps. "Uncle Godric... you just..."

"I just reminded them of their place in the grand hierarchy of existence," I say. "That is part of my responsibility as their king. And it is part of your responsibility as my court wizard to understand when and how such reminders should be administered." I turn away from the hologram, fixing Jim with a calm, steady gaze. "Your father has taught you the official duties of your position. But the unofficial duties—the hidden responsibilities—are just as important. You must learn to anticipate my moods. To recognize when a situation requires my intervention. To understand the delicate balance between mercy and punishment that governs my rule."

Tom steps forward, his expression carefully neutral. "My King, if I may... this is perhaps a bit much for his first day."

I turn to Tom, my expression softening. "You are right, old friend. I am getting ahead of myself." I gesture, and the holographic image vanishes, the small green planet and its catatonic inhabitants disappearing into nothingness. "Jim, I apologize. I did not mean to overwhelm you. But I wanted you to understand, from the very beginning, exactly what your service will entail. You are not just a court wizard. You are part of my family. And part of being in my family is knowing the truth of what I am."

Jim swallows hard, his voice trembling slightly when he speaks. "I understand, Uncle Godric. I... I think I understand."

"Good," I say, and I allow my smile to return, warm and paternal and genuine. "Then let us continue with your first day."

---

The afternoon passes more smoothly. I take Jim to the imperial menagerie, where the living weapons of conquered civilizations are kept in careful, permanent stasis—a reminder of the price of defiance. I show him the Hall of Trophies, where the shattered remnants of the God-Ender and the Infinite Fist and the World-Scream are displayed in crystalline cases. I walk him through the Chamber of Echoes, where the final screams of the Resonant Host still reverberate in eternal, silent quietus.

With each display, I watch Jim's expression shift. The simple, childlike affection he has always felt for me is still there—I can perceive it, warm and steady at the core of his emotional state—but it is now layered with something else. Something more complex. The dawning, horrified understanding of exactly what his "Uncle Godric" is capable of. The knowledge, still fresh and raw, that the kindly father figure who bounced him on his knee and taught him to skip stones has also unmade entire multiverses with a casual, effortless thought.

Tom watches his son's transformation with the quiet, knowing patience of a father who has been through the same experience himself. I remember his first day, fifty-one years ago. I remember the way his eyes went wide when I showed him the remnants of the Nascent Ones, the Sentient Aromatics who had been dissolved into a thin, odorless mist by my passive sense of smell. I remember the way his hands trembled when he wrote his first journal entry, the ink smudged by the sweat of his palms. And I remember how, over the years, he grew into his role. How he learned to manage my moods, to anticipate my needs, to be the steady, calming presence that I have relied upon since the days of Blake himself.

He has been a good court wizard. One of the best. And I suspect his son will be even better.

---

The evening finds us in my private dining chamber, a small, intimate room that I reserve for meals with my court wizards. The table is set with simple, elegant dishes—roasted meats and fresh vegetables and crusty bread, the same kind of food I ate when I was still a mortal king in Olympus. I do not need to eat, of course. My immortal body requires no sustenance, no sleep, no air. But I enjoy the ritual of dining with my family. It reminds me of the old days, when Blake and Anna and their children would gather around my table and we would talk and laugh and forget, for a few precious hours, the weight of the omniverse.

Jim sits across from me, his appetite subdued, his mind still processing the events of the day. Tom sits beside him, his expression carefully neutral, his eyes flicking between his son and his king with the watchful attention of a man who is prepared to intervene if necessary.

"Jim," I say, breaking the comfortable silence that has settled over the table. "Do you remember the stone-skipping lessons I gave you when you were a child?"

Jim looks up, his expression brightening slightly at the memory. "Yes, Uncle Godric. In the reflection pool. You taught me how to find the flat stones and how to throw them at just the right angle."

"You were very good at it. Better than your father, certainly." I allow a faint, teasing smile to cross my lips. "Tom could never get more than three skips. You managed seven on your first try."

Tom snorts, a reluctant, almost involuntary sound of amusement. "I was never very athletic, my King. My talents lie in other areas."

"Indeed they do," I agree. "Your talents lie in keeping me from accidentally destroying the omniverse with a stray thought. It is a skill that requires a different kind of athleticism."

The joke lands—Tom chuckles, and Jim's expression relaxes slightly. This is part of the hidden curriculum as well, I know. The ability to laugh in the face of existential horror. The ability to share a meal with the apex predator of all creation and find genuine joy in his company. It is not something that can be taught. It is something that must be learned, over time, through the slow, patient process of living in my shadow and discovering that the shadow is not as cold as it first appears.

"Uncle Godric," Jim says, his voice hesitent but curious. "Can I ask you something?"

"Of course."

"Earlier today, when you... when you disciplined the civilization in the Vaelen Cluster. You said that rejection is not always sufficient. That sometimes a civilization needs a demonstration." He pauses, choosing his words carefully. "But if you already knew that the demonstration would work, if you already knew that they would never petition you again, why did you need to do it at all? Why not just reject their petition and be done with it?"

It is a good question. A thoughtful question. The kind of question that his father would have asked, fifty-one years ago, when he was still learning the hidden curriculum himself.

"Because fear is a more reliable teacher than law," I say, my voice calm and measured. "The law against ritual sacrifice has existed for seven hundred thousand years. Every civilization in the empire knows it. Every leader has sworn to uphold it. And yet, the Vaelen Cluster still petitioned me for an exemption. The law was not enough. The knowledge of the law was not enough. They needed something more personal. More immediate. More memorable." I pause, my expression hardening fractionally. "Now they have it. Now they will remember, for the rest of their existence, exactly what happens when they petition me for something they do not deserve. And the memory of that fear will be passed down through their generations, a cautionary tale that will keep them compliant for another seven hundred thousand years."

Jim absorbs this, his expression thoughtful. "So the demonstration was not just for them. It was for everyone. A reminder of what you are capable of."

"Yes. And it was also for you." I fix him with a calm, steady gaze. "You needed to see what your service will entail. You needed to understand that being my court wizard means witnessing things that will haunt your nightmares for the rest of your life. You needed to know, from the very beginning, exactly what you are signing up for."

Jim holds my gaze for a long moment. His hands are still trembling slightly—I can perceive the faint tremor in his fingers, the elevated pulse in his wrists—but his eyes are steady. "I understand, Uncle Godric. And I am not afraid. Not anymore."

I raise an eyebrow. "Not anymore?"

"I was afraid earlier. When I saw what you did to that planet. When I realized exactly how powerful you are, and exactly how easily you could destroy everything I know." He pauses, his voice strengthening. "But then I remembered something. I remembered all the times you came to my birthday parties. All the times you let me ride on your shoulders through the palace gardens. All the times you told me stories about Blake and the old days and the kingdom of Olympus. And I realized that the power you showed today—the terrifying, overwhelming power—is the same power that has protected our family for over a million years. It is the same power that keeps Thronegate safe. It is the same power that will keep my children safe, and their children, and their children after them."

He stands up from the table, his young face set with a determination that reminds me, suddenly and powerfully, of Blake. "I am not afraid of you, Uncle Godric. I am grateful to you. And I am honored to serve you, in whatever capacity you need, for the rest of my life."

The dining chamber falls silent. Tom stares at his son with an expression of profound, unvarnished pride, his eyes glistening with the faint threat of tears. I sit motionless in my chair, my heavily muscled frame perfectly still, my ruggedly handsome face unreadable.

And then I smile. Not the cold, predatory smile. Not the calm, clinical detachment. The other smile. The genuine one. The smile of a father whose child has just made him prouder than he ever thought possible.

"Thank you, Jim," I say, my rich baritone carrying a note of genuine, unforced emotion. "That means more to me than you will ever know."

---

Later that night, after Tom and Jim have retired to their chambers, I sit alone in my study. The fire has burned down to glowing embers. The palace is silent, its million inhabitants asleep in their beds. The omniverse spins on, vast and endless and filled with infinite possibilities for amusement and chaos and destruction.

I could perceive Jim's dreams, if I chose to. I could watch the images flickering through his sleeping mind, the memories of the day being processed and catalogued and integrated into his understanding of the world. I could see exactly how his subconscious is grappling with the revelation of my true nature, the terrifying power that his kindly uncle has always kept hidden behind a warm smile and a gentle touch.

But I choose not to. Some things, I have learned over a million years of existence, are better left unperceived. Some intimacies are too precious to be invaded, even by the absolute awareness of an omniscient god.

Instead, I simply sit in my armchair—the one Blake gave me, all those years ago—and I allow myself to feel something that I have not felt in a very long time.

Hope.

Not the desperate, grasping hope of mortals who pray for miracles. Not the fragile, fleeting hope of civilizations that dare to dream of freedom. Something deeper. Something more fundamental. The hope of a father who has watched his family grow and flourish across a million years of existence, who has mourned the loss of his best friend and celebrated the birth of his descendants, who has protected and provided and nurtured and loved with every ounce of his infinite, immortal heart.

The hope that the family will continue. That the line of Blake will never end. That there will always be a court wizard at my side, a descendant of my oldest friend, a young man or woman who calls me "Uncle Godric" and means it.

Jim will be a good court wizard. One of the best. And someday, when Tom has retired and Jim has grown old and gray in my service, his own son will come to the palace for his first day. And the cycle will continue. And I will still be here, forty-five years old, salt-and-pepper beard immaculately trimmed, sitting in the same armchair, watching another generation of my family begin their service.

The fire has died completely now, the embers fading to ash. The study is dark, lit only by the faint, amber glow of the arcane sconces. I rise from my chair, my heavily muscled frame moving through the darkness with the silent, effortless grace of a predator who has no need to announce his presence.

Tomorrow, I will continue Jim's training. I will show him the rest of the hidden curriculum, the secret responsibilities that his father has spent fifty-one years mastering. I will teach him how to manage my moods, how to anticipate my needs, how to keep the apex predator of the omniverse content and calm and focused on the welfare of his subjects rather than the destruction of his enemies. It will be difficult work. Traumatizing work, at times. The kind of work that leaves scars on the soul that never fully heal.

But Jim will be equal to it. I know this, with the same absolute certainty that I know everything else. He is his father's son. He is Blake's descendant. He is my family.

And I will love him, and protect him, and watch over him with the same fierce, unyielding devotion that I have shown to every court wizard who has ever served me.

Because that, in the end, is the true hidden curriculum. The lesson that no journal can capture, no training can impart, no father can teach his son. The simple, profound, terrifying truth of what it means to be loved by the apex predator of the omniverse.

I pause at the door of my study, my hand resting on the cold obsidian frame, and I look back at the empty room. The armchair. The hearth. The shelves of ancient books. The artifacts and mementos and treasures that I have accumulated over a million years of existence. All of it meaningless. All of it replaceable. All of it nothing compared to the warm, steady glow of a young man's gratitude and a father's pride.

"Thank you, Jim," I whisper into the darkness, my rich baritone barely audible. "For not being afraid."

And then I step through the door and make my way through the silent palace, my footsteps echoing on the polished obsidian floors, my heart—that ancient, immortal heart that has beaten for over a million years—feeling just a little bit lighter than it did before.
