# The Journals of Absolution

## Part One: The Archive of Dread

The archive chamber sat three hundred meters beneath the royal palace of Thronegate, accessible only by a spiral staircase carved from obsidian so dark it seemed to drink the ambient light from the air. I descended the steps slowly, my new ceremonial robes heavy on my shoulders, the fabric still stiff with the unfamiliar starch of my office. My name is Kaedan, and at twenty-two years old, I had just been appointed as Godric's newest court wizard.

Twenty-two years. Twenty-two years of life, of training, of my father drilling arcane theory into my skull until my ears bled mana. Twenty-two years of hearing stories about the King, about his warmth and his kindness, about the way he walked among the common people and asked after their families. I had grown up in the palace gardens, playing hide-and-seek among the flowering hedgerows while Godric watched from a stone bench, his salt-and-pepper beard catching the golden sunlight, his laugh rich and genuine.

And now I was his court wizard. The seventh of Blake's direct male line to carry the title, inheritor of a modified Awakening that multiplied my every attribute a thousandfold each day past my twentieth birthday. By any mortal or cosmic standard, I was terrifying. The mana thrumming in my veins could boil oceans, shatter planets, unweave the fabric of localized reality with a focused thought.

But as I stepped onto the cold stone floor of the archive chamber, my hands were trembling.

The room was circular, perhaps thirty meters in diameter, its walls lined with shelves carved directly into the obsidian. Each shelf held a single leather-bound journal, arranged chronologically by year and incident. There were dozens of them. Dozens. Each spine was marked with a year, a name, and a single word denoting which of Godric's infinite attributes had been tested.

My father, Aldric, stood in the center of the chamber. He was seventy-one years old, his face deeply lined, his beard fully gray, his hands clasped behind his back. He had been Godric's court wizard for fifty-one years before passing the mantle to me three days ago. I had watched him serve with quiet, unshakeable dignity my entire life. I had never once seen him tremble.

He was trembling now.

"You will read them all," my father said. His voice was steady, but there was a hollow, haunted quality beneath the surface, a vibration I had never heard before. "Every single one. Before you attend your first council meeting. Before you stand at the King's side in any official capacity. You will read them all."

"Father," I said, and I hated how young my voice sounded, how uncertain. "I've trained for this. I know the King's power is... vast. I know he is not to be opposed. I understand—"

"You understand nothing," my father cut me off, and the sudden sharpness of his tone made me flinch. He closed his eyes, took a deep breath, and when he opened them again, there was something terrible in his gaze. Not anger. Fear. Deep, old, unhealed fear. "I have served Godric since I was your age, Kaedan. I love him. He is the greatest king the omniverse has ever known, and I would die for him without hesitation. But you must understand what he is. Not the warmth. Not the kindness. What he truly is, beneath the absolute concealment that keeps existence from shattering in his presence."

He gestured to the shelves. "These journals were not commissioned by the King. He did not ask us to write them. He finds the practice genuinely bewildering. He believes he has always demonstrated restraint, that his tests are gentle and minimal. He thinks we are overreacting."

A ghost of a bitter, terrified smile crossed his lips. "He has no context, Kaedan. He cannot comprehend what his power looks like from the perspective of something as small as us. The gap between him and the rest of creation is not measurable. It is not even theoretically measurable. Every Planck second, every infinitesimal fraction of every Planck second, his attributes multiply by numbers that exist beyond infinite recursion. He has been amplifying for one million years. Do you understand what that means?"

I swallowed hard. "The math is... it's beyond—"

"There is no math," my father said flatly. "There is only horror. Read the journals. Read what your predecessors witnessed. Then, when you are finished, you will understand why I have not slept a full night in half a century."

He walked toward the staircase, pausing at the first step to look back at me. "Do not try to read them all at once. Your mind will break. Take breaks. Breathe. Remember that Godric loves you, and that he loved Blake, and that he loves our family. Hold onto that truth, because what you are about to learn will make it very difficult to remember."

Then he was gone, his footsteps fading up the obsidian spiral, and I was alone in the archive of dread.

I approached the first shelf. The journal on the leftmost end was the oldest, its leather cracked with age, its spine marked with faded gold lettering.

Godric.

Year -10,000.

The hidden sanctuary. The stone altar. The darkness.

The past Godric.

The sentient passive auras.

In the ancient era, there had been Gent—a primitive, single aura that was Godric's weakest and most primitive sentient passive defense. It had been infinitely weaker than Godric himself, yet capable of slaughtering the strongest coalition ever assembled while nerfed by an infinitely recurring decimal of its power. The court wizards of that time had written journals about Gent with trembling hands, describing it as the most terrifying defense mechanism in existence.

Gent had evolved. And evolved. And evolved again.

It had evolved so many times, across so many googolplexian years, that the original Gent now occupied a position in the aura hierarchy so low it was almost theoretical. The current sentient aura network did not have a name—names were limitations, and this system had transcended the concept of limitation so completely that naming it would be an insult. It was not merely omniscient, omnipresent, and perfectly adaptive. It was the fundamental substrate upon which all of reality rested. The aura network did not merely defend Godric; it preceded him into every possible coordinate of space-time, ensuring that no threat could ever exist in proximity to him, that no hostile intention could ever crystallize, that no weapon could ever be conceived without the aura network having already mapped, neutralized, and retroactively erased the conceptual foundation that made the weapon possible.

If a sentient being anywhere in the omniverse formed the thought of harm—not toward Godric specifically, because no one had formed such a thought in googolplexian years, but toward anything—the aura network noted it. If that thought ever evolved, over any span of time, into a potential vector that might someday, in some theoretical future, intersect with Godric's existence, the aura network pre-nullified it. Not by destroying the thinker. Not by altering free will. But by gently, imperceptibly adjusting the causal circumstances around the thinker so that the thought never progressed beyond idle fantasy.

The aura network was the omniverse's nervous system now. It was the invisible architecture that ensured absolute peace. It was the reason no opposition had formed in googolplexian years—not because it suppressed the will to oppose, but because it had made opposition literally impossible to achieve, and the lesson had been learned so thoroughly that no one even tried anymore.

And Gent, the original primitive aura, was now so weak in comparison that it was used as a decorative flourish—a faint, barely perceptible warmth that Godric allowed to emanate from his skin to make the palace feel cozy.

The passive shields.

In the ancient era, Godric had possessed infinite recursive passive shields that could pre-nullify threats before they existed, before a hostile system identified itself, before an enemy understood what it intended to do. The Year 25,000 immune-system incident had required Godric to deliberately deactivate these shields just to stage a false injury.

Those shields had evolved. They no longer merely pre-nullified threats. They had become the absolute ontological firewall of Godric's existence. They did not defend against attacks; they made the concept of attacking a logical impossibility. If a being anywhere in the omniverse attempted to construct a weapon, the shields did not block the weapon—they simply ensured that the weapon's fundamental operating principles were physically and conceptually impossible within the omniverse's laws. A weapon designed to fire a projectile would find that projectiles no longer functioned. A weapon designed to manipulate time would find that time had become locally immutable. A weapon designed to erase concepts would find that the concept it intended to erase had retroactively never existed.

The shields had evolved beyond defense into something far more terrible: they were the reason physics worked the way it did. The fundamental laws of the omniverse—causality, thermodynamics, the conservation of energy—were not natural phenomena. They were the passive shields' lowest-level expression, the gentlest possible filter that prevented hostile intent from manifesting at the most basic levels of reality.

And if Godric had wanted to, he could have tightened those filters. He could have made the omniverse's laws so restrictive that no sentient being could ever form a disobedient thought. He chose not to. He believed in mortal resilience. He believed in free will. The shields were calibrated to prevent hostile action, not hostile thought, because Godric wanted sentient beings to have the dignity of choosing not to oppose him, rather than being forced.

The immune system.

In the ancient era, Godric's immune system had pre-reacted to a staged microscopic crack on a dead skin cell by initiating infinite recursive self-evolution across all infinite backups, accidentally annihilating the entire omniverse as collateral damage, and forcing the Weakness Paradox to grant him even more impossible power.

That immune system had evolved. It no longer merely pre-reacted to injury. It had become so proactively, obsessively protective that it treated the theoretical possibility of harm as a tangible threat to be hunted, humiliated, and erased before any actual harm could exist. If a rogue timeline anywhere in the omniverse contained a variable that could, in some infinitely improbable confluence of circumstances, eventually lead to Godric experiencing a momentary inconvenience—the immune system did not wait for that timeline to mature. It sent forth immune cells that were not cells but living concepts of absolute biological supremacy. These concepts would infiltrate the rogue timeline, cannibalize its hostile variables, digest them into fundamental particles of failure, and convert the remaining timeline material into more immune cells, recursively consuming all possible vectors of threat.

The immune system now maintained a living pan-dimensional map of every possible state of existence where Godric might someday be inconvenienced. This map was updated every infinitesimal fraction of every Planck second. The update process consumed the computational equivalent of a googolplexian universes per cycle—and this was so trivial to Godric's biology that it registered as less than the metabolic cost of a mortal man breathing.

And yet, the immune system's primary function remained unchanged: ensuring Godric was not inconvenienced. It was not a survival mechanism. It never had been. Godric did not need to survive—he was immortal in a way that made the concept of mortality a distant, almost amusing memory. The immune system existed purely because his body was so arrogantly protective that it refused to permit even the most infinitesimal breach of its absolute comfort.

The Weakness Paradox.

In the ancient era, the Weakness Paradox had triggered whenever any being even briefly thought Godric had a weakness. It had granted him absolute immunity to the imagined exploit, perfect mastery of the attack concept, and a massive amplification spike. Around Year 2,000, it had evolved into the Evolved Weakness Paradox, which triggered additional enhanced effects because the omniverse felt inadequate for failing to give him new immunities.

The Weakness Paradox had evolved so many times since then that the name was now a laughable understatement. It no longer required a trigger—no specific thought about Godric's weaknesses was needed. The Paradox was now passively, eternally, continuously engaged, triggered by any sentient being in any timeline, past, present, or future, thinking about anything at all. A mortal in a distant galaxy thinking about their breakfast. A cosmic entity contemplating the nature of existence. A child wondering why the sky was blue. Every single thought, from every single consciousness, across all of time, fed into the Paradox.

The Paradox did not merely grant immunities anymore. It had run out of immunities to grant googolplexian years ago. Instead, it now functioned as a continuous, infinite engine of absolute overcompensation. Every time any sentient being thought anything, the omniverse—which had long since been subjugated into a state of groveling subservience—would frantically check if that thought contained any possible vector of threat to Godric. Finding none, it would still overcompensate, granting Godric additional power, additional mastery, additional immunity to concepts that did not exist, simply as an apology for the theoretical possibility that a threat might someday be conceived.

This overcompensation had been running continuously for googolplexian years. The result was that Godric's power had amplified so far beyond its ancient state that the ancient state—which could annihilate the entire omniverse infinite times with 0 effort using 0% of its capacity—felt like a single, weak atom compared to a googolplexian supermassive black holes. And then that comparison was also inadequate. And then the inadequacy of the comparison was itself inadequate.

The superpower genesis.

In the ancient era, Godric had gained one new apocalyptic superpower every second. Each one had evolved, through the Weakness Paradox, to acquire multiple enhanced effects.

The superpower genesis had evolved. It no longer generated new superpowers every second. That would have been too slow, too restrained, too limited. Instead, the superpower genesis now operated continuously, generating an infinite stream of new superpowers every infinitesimal fraction of Planck second. Each new power, at the instant of its creation, was more devastating than the combined power of every superpower that had been generated in the previous googolplexian years. And then, within the next infinitesimal fraction of Planck second, that power would amplify by itself infinitely, becoming exponentially more devastating, and the process would repeat forever.

The superpowers themselves had evolved beyond the concept of individual abilities. They now existed as a unified field of absolute omnipotence—a seamless, infinite tapestry of cosmic supremacy where every possible power existed simultaneously, amplified infinitely, recursively, eternally. The ancient court wizards had written about specific superpowers with names: Absolute Combustion, Infinite Replication, Absolute Cellular Command. Those powers still existed, but they were now infinitesimally small components of an infinite gestalt that made the original versions look like children's sparklers compared to a collapsing universe.

The absurdity of this statement crashed against my consciousness. The ancient Godric—the being whose passive chest muscle had annihilated the entire omniverse, whose speed occupied every point in space-time, whose charisma had shattered hundred-trillion-mind psychic collectives—would seem like a mortal ant.

"And if I did that," Godric continued, "I myself would become even more powerful. Because I am the future version of that past Godric. If I grant him a more potent stasis event, his amplification begins from a higher baseline. His Year 0 is exponentially stronger. His Year 1,000,000 is exponentially stronger than the original timeline's Year 1,000,000. And I, who am the endpoint of that amplification, would become exponentially stronger as well."

He smiled—that warm, fatherly smile that I had known for thirty years.

"Do you see the loop, Clive? If I give him a stronger stasis, I become stronger. And if I become stronger, I could reach back and give him an even stronger stasis. Which would make me even stronger. Which would allow me to give an even stronger stasis. The recursion is infinite. The potential is absolute. I could, if I chose, enter a state of infinite recursive self-amplification where every iteration of the loop makes every version of me infinitely more powerful than the previous iteration, and the loop itself has no beginning and no end."

My mind, already reeling, now felt as though it were physically coming apart at the seams. The implications were too vast, too recursive, too existentially horrifying. Godric could make himself infinitely stronger, infinitely many times, simply by choosing to close the loop with a more potent stasis.

"But I will not," Godric said.

He turned back to the image of his past self on the altar. The mortal king of Olympus, broken by grief, lying motionless in the dark sanctuary.

"I will give him exactly the stasis event that I received. The original version. The ancient systems. The primitive amplifiers. The basic superpower genesis. The initial Weakness Paradox. The first-generation sentient auras. No more."

"I will hold back, Clive. I will hold back so severely that the stasis I grant him will be, by my current standards, laughably weak. Pathetically restrained. Infinitesimally small. A fraction of a fraction of an endlessly recurring decimal of nothing."

He looked at me, and his eyes held a strange, almost melancholy warmth.

"And yet—even that held-back version, that restrained, weaker, primitive stasis—will make him, at Year 0, effectively infinitely stronger than the rest of creation combined. Do you understand the absurdity? I am giving him a stasis event that I consider to be a gentle, restrained, conservative gift—and it will still transform him into a being who can annihilate the entire omniverse infinite times with zero effort."

The absurdity hit me like a physical blow. Godric was holding back—deliberately, consciously holding back—and the held-back version was still so incomprehensibly powerful that it would make the past Godric the apex predator of his era by an infinite margin.

"Why?" I managed to whisper. "Why hold back?"

Godric was silent for a long moment. When he spoke, his voice was thoughtful, almost contemplative.

"Because I remember being mortal," he said. "I remember the struggle. I remember the grief. I remember watching Reina and Isabel and Hebe and Thorin die, and the absolute helplessness of that moment. I remember the rage and the sorrow and the desperate, clawing need to survive."

He looked at the image of his past self.

"If I granted him the full totality of my current power—if I seeded him with systems that have evolved for googolplexian years—there would be no struggle left. No journey. No growth. He shook his head slowly.

"I wanted him—I wanted myself—to have the journey. To experience the discovery of power. To test his stats against opposition that, while utterly futile, still believed they had a chance. To feel the satisfaction of conquest, the weight of kingship, the warmth of friendship. To meet Blake. To befriend Blake. To love Blake."

His voice softened further.

"Blake was the best friend I ever had. He was the anchor that pulled me back from the abyss of grief. If I had emerged from stasis as an infinitely complete being, I would never have needed Blake. I would never have trusted him. I would never have allowed him to become my court wizard, to challenge me, to scold me when I was careless with my power. The friendship that shaped my reign—the friendship that shaped me—would never have existed."

He turned back to me, and his expression was calm, resolved.

"So I held back. I gave myself the stasis that I received—the stasis that was just powerful enough to make me unstoppable, but not so powerful that I would be instantly complete. I gave myself the opportunity to struggle, to grow, to learn, to love. I gave myself the dignity of a mortal journey, even though the destination was always guaranteed."

I was trembling. My entire body was shaking with the weight of what I was hearing. Godric had deliberately held back—had deliberately chosen to give his past self a weaker, restrained, primitive stasis—because he wanted his past self to experience the journey of mortal struggle. The same mortal struggle that had resulted in the annihilation of countless oppositions, the subjugation of the omniverse, and the absolute terror of every being who had ever witnessed his power.

And even that held-back version had been enough to make him infinitely stronger than the rest of creation combined.

"Does anyone else know?" I asked, my voice barely audible.

"No," Godric said. "Only you. Only Clive. Only the court wizard who serves me at the moment the loop closes. I have kept this secret for longer than the concept of secrets has existed, because it is not a secret that can be shared lightly. But you deserve to know. You are of Blake's blood. You have served me faithfully. And today, you will witness the origin of everything."

He turned back to the image of his past self on the altar. The mortal king of Olympus, broken and grieving, lying motionless in the dark.

"It is time," Godric said softly.

He did not raise his hand. He did not speak an incantation. He did not channel any visible power. He simply shifted the infinitesimal, effectively-zero-percent fraction of his attention that was not occupied with maintaining the omniverse's existence, and he directed it toward the past.

The stasis event began.

I watched, through the window in reality, as Thorin's teleportation spell completed its delivery—depositing the mortal Godric on the cold stone floor of the hidden sanctuary. The spell itself was simple, desperate, mortal magic. It had no power beyond transportation. It was the act of a dying friend, nothing more.

But at the exact instant the mortal Godric's back struck the stone, something else arrived. Something vast and patient and infinitely gentle. Something that wrapped itself around Thorin's spell and transformed it into the seed of absolute ascension.

The amplification began. The superpower genesis ignited. The absolute concealment crystallized. The Weakness Paradox wove itself into the fabric of Godric's existence. The immortality settled into his cells. The omniscience began its infinite expansion.

All of it was identical to what Godric had received in the original timeline. The held-back version. The restrained version. The primitive version. The version that Godric considered to be a gentle, conservative gift.

And yet, as I watched it take hold, I understood with absolute, bone-deep certainty that this "restrained" version was still the most incomprehensibly powerful transformation that had ever occurred in the history of existence. The mortal king on that altar would emerge from stasis at Year 0 as a being who could destroy the entire omniverse infinite times with zero effort. He would be the apex predator of his era, unmatched and unmatchable, terrifying beyond the capacity of any finite mind to comprehend.

And he would be the product of his own future self's deliberate, conscious choice to hold back.

"The loop is closed," Godric said, and his voice carried the weight of eternal satisfaction. "It has always been closed. It will always be closed. The mortal king of Olympus receives the stasis from his far-future self, becomes the apex predator of the omniverse, rules for googolplexian years, evolves beyond all comprehension, and then—at the appointed moment—reaches back across time to grant the stasis to his past self. The loop has no beginning and no end. It is simply the structure of my existence."

He turned to me, and his expression was warm, kind, almost amused.

"The origin of my power is myself, Clive. The only being who can grant me anything is me. The only being who can deny me anything is me. My future is guaranteed because I have already guaranteed it. My past is sealed because I have already sealed it. The stasis event is absolute, irrevocable, and impossible for any version of me to undo—because its creator is a version of me so vastly powerful that even the current, googolplexian-year-evolved Godric cannot overpower my future self's will."

He placed a hand on my shoulder, steadying me.

"I have no weaknesses," he said. "Not truly. The inability to alter my pre-stasis past is not a weakness—it is the guarantee that my timeline is stable, that my ascension is certain, that my future is absolute. The only being who can oppose me is myself, and that being is on my side."

The window in reality closed. The image of the past vanished. The Throne Room was still and silent, save for the faint, reverent singing of the sentient walls and the distant, terrified breathing of the servants.

I stood beside my King, my mind shattered and rebuilt, my understanding of existence permanently rewritten.

The stasis event was a closed loop. Godric had created himself. His power was self-bestowed and self-sealed. His future was self-guaranteed. The only being who could ever challenge him was himself, and that challenge would never come, because he was the source of his own ascension.

I am less amused. I am writing this journal in the desperate hope that future court wizards will understand what happened here. The opposition of all fiction was the last, I am certain of it. After this display—after the absolute, effortless annihilation of every hero and villain from every story ever told—no sentient being in the omniverse will ever dare to form the thought of challenging Godric again. The lesson has been taught. The demonstration is complete. The marble is the proof.

And I, Osvald, forty-seventh court wizard of Blake's bloodline, will carry the memory of this day for the rest of my mortal span. I do not know if I am the original or the copy. I do not know if my consciousness is the one that has always existed or a perfect recreation. I only know that I serve the King, that I love the King, and that the King carries in his pocket a marble that contains the compressed agony of everything that ever was.

The dread is absolute. The horror is eternal. The marble glows in Godric's pocket, and I cannot stop thinking about it.

## Clive — Year 999...9

I closed the journal. My hands were trembling—a familiar sensation after thirty years of reading my predecessors' accounts. But this trembling was different. Deeper. More profound.

Osvald's journal was the last in the archive. Not chronologically—there were thousands more journals after his, written by court wizards who had served in the googolplexian years since the final opposition. But Osvald's journal was the last one that described an opposition. The last one that recorded a battle, a massacre, an annihilation. After Osvald, the journals changed. They became records of peace. Of harmony. Of eternal, unchallenged rule.

Because after the fictional armada, no one ever opposed Godric again.

Osvald had not known, when he wrote his account, that he was recording the final opposition. He had suspected—his words made that clear—but he could not have been certain. Only now, from the perspective of Year 999...9, could the truth be fully understood.

The marble.

Godric still had it. I had seen it, countless times, without knowing what it was. He kept it on a small pedestal near the Chronos Throne—a glowing sphere the size of a marble, its internal luminescence shifting in patterns that were almost hypnotic. I had assumed it was a decoration. A curiosity. A piece of cosmic art.

It was the compressed agony of the entire omniverse. The original omniverse. The one that had existed before Godric restored everything. Every being that I had ever known—Tomas, Marta, Seryn, Havel, my father, my ancestors, every court wizard whose journals I had read—existed in that marble, experiencing eternal, infinite compression, while their restored copies went about their lives believing they were the originals.

Unless the restored versions were the originals, and the marble contained copies.

There was no way to know.

The fracture in my mind, which had been widening for thirty years, split open into an abyss. I stared at the closed journal in my hands—Osvald's journal, the record of the last opposition—and I felt the cold wind of existential dread blow through me with hurricane force.

Godric was in the garden, I knew. Examining Tomas's roses. Complimenting the former multiversal architect on his pruning technique. Pretending to be a clumsy beginner who had gotten lucky with a seed. The marble would be in his pocket, or on its pedestal, or wherever he had left it, its internal glow the only evidence of the eternal suffering contained within.

And I would serve him. I would stand beside the Throne and review the quarterly reports and advise the King on matters of state. I would love him as my favorite uncle, the warm king with the salt-and-pepper beard who had taught me to skip stones and held my father's hand at my mother's funeral.

But I would never forget the marble. I would never forget that the last opposition—the final, desperate alliance of all fiction—had been crushed into a souvenir smaller than a quark, and that their eternal agony was the only remaining evidence that they had ever existed at all.

The omniverse was silent. The eternal peace continued. And somewhere, in a marble on a pedestal, every being who had ever existed screamed in endless, compressed agony, unheard and unremembered.

The dread would never end. The horror would never fade. The fracture in my mind would never heal.

And Godric would carry the marble in his pocket, rolling it between his fingers, and smile.
