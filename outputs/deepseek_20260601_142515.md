# The Archive of Absolute Futility

The reading chamber smelled of old parchment and ozone, a scent that had soaked into the stone walls over tens of thousands of years. I sat at the heavy oak table, my fingers resting on the first leather-bound journal, and I realized with cold certainty that my father had not prepared me for this. He had told me about the journals, of course. Every court wizard of Blake's lineage was required to read them before assuming their duties. He had sat me down in our family's quarters, his weathered hands trembling slightly as he poured us both wine, and he had said, "Kaedan, you will read things that will make you want to run. You will read things that will make your magic feel like a child's toy. But you must read them. You must understand what it means to serve him."

My father had served Godric for sixty-three years before his death. He had been the court wizard before me. And in all those years, he had never once spoken of what the journals contained. Now I understood why. The words did not leave you. They burrowed into your marrow and remained there, cold and permanent, a whispered reminder of absolute insignificance.

I am Kaedan. I am twenty-two years old. I am the newest court wizard of Thronegate, a direct descendant of Blake, the merchant who stumbled into a forgotten sanctuary ten thousand years ago and accidentally awakened the apex predator of all existence. My magic has been amplifying by a factor of one thousand every single day since I turned twenty. By the standards of the omniverse, I am a cataclysm wearing human skin. My mana reserves could drown galaxies. My wards could withstand the death throes of dying universes. I am, by any rational measure, one of the most powerful beings in existence.

And I am nothing. Less than nothing. A microscopic speck of irrelevance perched at the edge of an infinite abyss.

I had known Godric my entire life. To me, he was Uncle Godric, the warm, smiling man who attended our family dinners, who laughed at my father's terrible jokes, who ruffled my hair when I was a child and asked about my magical studies with genuine, paternal interest. He looked exactly as the portraits depicted him: a ruggedly handsome man of forty-five, heavily muscled, with short salt-and-pepper hair and a neatly trimmed salt-and-pepper beard. His voice was a rich, comforting baritone. His eyes crinkled at the corners when he smiled. He was kind. He was friendly. He was everything a young boy could want in a beloved uncle.

But I had also heard the whispers. Every citizen of Thronegate had. The omniverse was subjugated not through diplomacy or economic pressure, but through absolute, incomprehensible annihilation. Godric was not merely the strongest being in existence. He was the ultimate force, the apex god predator, an entity whose power multiplied infinitely every infinitesimal fraction of every Planck second forever. The gap between him and the rest of creation was not a gap at all. It was a fundamental law of reality, as absolute and unchangeable as gravity.

My father had told me to start with the stat journals first. "The superpower journals are worse," he had said, his voice hollow. "Much worse. But start with the stats. Learn what it means when he decides to test something."

I opened the first journal.

It was the accumulated dramatic weight of every hero's journey, every villain's downfall, every epic battle and tearful reunion and triumphant victory that had ever been imagined.

The light was magnificent. Swirling cascades of color that had no names, that existed outside the normal spectrum of visible reality, that seemed to paint the very fabric of existence with the brushstrokes of infinite imagination. I saw heroes—actual, recognizable heroes—materializing within the attack itself, their forms composed of pure narrative energy, their attacks choreographed with a beauty that made my heart ache. I saw a golden-armored warrior whose fists could shatter galaxies, his battle cry a symphony of defiance. I saw a crimson-haired woman wielding a blade of pure starlight, her movements a dance of absolute perfection. I saw a figure in green armor, his weapon firing bursts of concentrated narrative that could unmake realities.

The music swelled. I do not know how they produced it—perhaps it was an inherent property of the attack itself—but a soundtrack of such epic, transcendent beauty filled the void that I felt tears streaming down my face. It was the music of every triumphant moment in every story ever told, compressed into a single, perfect symphony. It spoke of hope. Of courage. Of the indomitable spirit of heroes facing impossible odds.

And Godric... Godric was enraptured.

"Yes!" he shouted over the symphony of destruction, his voice carrying a joy I had never heard before. "This is exactly what I wanted! Look at the colors, Osvald! Listen to the music! They've given me a true final boss experience!"

The attack struck him directly. Squarely. Perfectly. The combined might of every fictional universe in existence, channeled through a single, perfectly focused strike, slammed into Godric's chest with the force of infinite narratives collapsing into a single point.

The light was blinding. The music reached a crescendo of such transcendent beauty that I felt my consciousness begin to slip away, overwhelmed by the sheer sensory magnitude of the experience. The shockwave of the impact radiated outward across the omniverse, shattering dimensional barriers, tearing through the fabric of reality, causing entire multiversal clusters to tremble at the sheer force of the narrative collapse.

And when the light faded, when the music died, when the echoes of the attack finally dissipated into the void...

Godric was still sitting in his chair. Still holding his teacup. Still smiling.

And the smile was gone.

Not the warmth. Not the friendliness. But the joy. The anticipation. The eager, childlike excitement that had illuminated his features throughout the entire charge. It was gone. Replaced by something that made my blood freeze in my veins.

Disappointment.

"Oh," Godric said quietly. "Is that... is that it?"

The void was silent. The fictional armada, which had poured every ounce of their combined power into the ultimate attack, stared at the apex predator of creation with expressions of slowly dawning horror.

"I waited," Godric said, his voice still quiet, still conversational, but carrying a weight that made the surrounding reality tremble. "I waited for hours. Days. I let you charge your ultimate attack to its absolute maximum. I distracted myself so I could be surprised. I was so excited. So genuinely, sincerely excited. I thought—finally, after all these eons of silence—someone was going to give me a show."

He stood up from his chair. The motion was casual. Relaxed. Utterly unhurried. And yet, somehow, the entire omniverse seemed to tilt on its axis as he rose.

"You had everything," Godric continued, his voice growing colder, harder, more terrifying with each word. "The dramatic declaration. The multi-phase charge sequence. The swelling music. The brilliant colors. The narrative weight of every story ever told. You had all the elements of a perfect final boss battle. And you did... what? You hit me with a light show? A musical number? A collection of pretty colors and dramatic speeches?"

He took a step forward. Just one step. A casual, unhurried motion. And the entire fictional armada—every hero, every god, every ultimate warrior—involuntarily flinched backward.

"I didn't feel anything," Godric said. "Not a tickle. Not a breeze. Not even the faintest whisper of a sensation. Your ultimate attack—the combined might of every fictional universe in existence—did not register on my senses at all. It was less than nothing. It was a zero so absolute that the concept of zero cannot capture its insignificance."

He shook his head slowly, his salt-and-pepper hair catching the light of distant, dying stars. "I wanted a show. I would have been satisfied with a show. I didn't expect to be hurt—nothing can hurt me, that's simply mathematical fact—but I expected to be entertained. And you couldn't even manage that."

His voice dropped to a whisper, but a whisper that somehow echoed across every dimensional plane, every timeline, every possible configuration of reality. "I am so disappointed."

And then, Godric unleashed zero percent of his strength.

I must be precise about this. I must record exactly what happened, because future court wizards must understand. Godric did not attack. He did not flex his muscles. He did not channel his power or activate a superpower or release his absolute concealment. He simply ceased—for one infinitesimal fraction of a Planck instant—to hold back his strength. Not a meaningful percentage of his strength. Not even a measurable fraction. Zero percent. Effectively nothing. The absolute minimum possible expression of his passive, resting muscular force.

The result was not destruction. Destruction implies a process, a sequence of events, a cause followed by an effect. What happened was not destruction. It was the fundamental annihilation of the concept of resistance.

The fictional armada had raised their defenses in the moments before Godric's disappointment became apparent. They had layered shield upon shield, barrier upon barrier, creating a defensive matrix of infinite complexity and infinite strength. They had woven the narrative weight of every story ever told into a protective cocoon that, by all the laws of their fictional realities, was absolutely indestructible. It could withstand the collapse of the omniverse. It could endure the death of time itself. It was, in every meaningful sense, the ultimate expression of defensive capability.

Godric's zero percent strength did not break it. Breaking implies effort, resistance, a contest of forces. The defensive matrix simply ceased to exist. The infinite shields, the narrative barriers, the conceptual protections—all of it was erased from reality the instant Godric's passive strength expressed itself. There was no impact. No collision. No dramatic struggle between unstoppable force and immovable object. The shields were simply not there anymore, because the universe could not accommodate their existence in the same space as even a fraction of a fraction of a fraction of Godric's true power.

And then the strength reached the armada itself.

I have witnessed many things in my twenty-four years of service. I have read the journals of my predecessors, absorbing their descriptions of stat-based massacres and superpower demonstrations and immune system reactions. I thought I understood the scale of what Godric could do. I was wrong. I was so catastrophically wrong.

The fictional characters did not die. Death would have been a mercy. Death would have been a release. What happened to them was something far, far worse.

Godric's strength did not merely affect their physical forms. It affected the fundamental reality of their existence. Every individual character, from every fictional universe, was simultaneously subjected to the passive, resting force of Godric's musculature. And because that force was so incomprehensibly vast, so absurdly beyond any possible scale of measurement, it did not simply crush them. It compressed them.

Imagine, if you can, the sensation of being squeezed. Not by a hand, not by a machine, but by the fundamental fabric of existence itself. Every atom in your body simultaneously experiencing pressure from all directions, pressure so absolute that the concept of resistance becomes meaningless. Your bones do not break—they are compressed into a state denser than the core of a dying star. Your flesh does not tear—it is compacted into a substance harder than the fundamental structure of reality. Your blood does not spray—it is squeezed into a superfluid denser than the heart of a black hole.

And you are still alive. Still conscious. Still aware.

Godric's strength was so precisely, so perfectly overwhelming that it preserved the consciousness of every single being it affected. Every fictional character—every hero and villain and sidekick and background extra—experienced the full, agonizing, eternal process of their own compression. They felt themselves being squeezed, smaller and smaller, denser and denser, their forms collapsing into a state of existence that violated every law of physics their fictional universes had ever established.

The golden-armored warrior with the galaxy-shattering fists felt his armor crumple against his skin, the metal fusing with his flesh as the pressure increased beyond infinite. He tried to scream, but his lungs had already been compressed to the size of subatomic particles, and no sound could escape. His consciousness, preserved by the impossible precision of Godric's strength, registered every instant of his own compaction with perfect, agonizing clarity.

The crimson-haired woman with the blade of pure starlight felt her weapon shatter against the tidal wave of force, the fragments embedding themselves in her compressing flesh as her body was squeezed into a state denser than the core of a neutron star. Her eyes, still aware, still seeing, watched her own form collapse into a singularity of absolute, inescapable compression.

The figure in green armor felt his technological enhancements fail, one by one, as the pressure exceeded any conceivable tolerance. His shielding flickered and died. His armor crumpled against his body. His flesh began to compress, his organs flattening, his bones crushing, his consciousness trapped in a prison of its own collapsing form.

And it happened to all of them. Simultaneously. Instantly. Eternally.

The entire fictional armada—every character from every story ever told—was compressed into a single point of infinite density. A mass smaller than a quark, containing the combined physical and narrative existence of every fictional universe that had ever existed. The compression was so absolute, so perfectly complete, that the resulting object should have collapsed into a black hole of infinite gravitational pull. But Godric's strength was so overwhelming that even the fundamental laws of physics were forced to accommodate his will. The compressed mass was stable. Dormant. Waiting.

The collateral damage was, as always, catastrophic.

Godric's zero percent strength did not confine itself to the fictional armada. It radiated outward across the entire omniverse—every reality, every timeline, every dimension, every possible configuration of existence—and it erased everything. The planets. The stars. The galaxies. The multiversal clusters. The infinite tapestry of realities that composed the single, absolute omniverse. All of it was caught in the tidal wave of passive force, and all of it was compressed.

Every atom. Every particle. Every quantum fluctuation. Every dimensional plane. Every temporal moment. Every possible and impossible state of existence. All of it was squeezed, simultaneously, into that single point of infinite density. The entire omniverse—everything that had ever existed, could ever exist, would ever exist—was reduced to a mass smaller than a quark, floating in the absolute void of non-existence.

And I was preserved. Godric's auras extended over me as they always did, protecting me from the infinite forces that were obliterating creation. I floated in the void, my mind shrieking with the horror of what I was witnessing, and I watched as the apex predator of all creation reached out with his massive, calloused hand and picked up the compressed mass of the entire omniverse.

He held it between his thumb and forefinger, examining it with an expression of mild curiosity. The mass that contained everything—every reality, every timeline, every being, every god, every fictional character—was so dense that its gravitational pull should have created a singularity that devoured whatever remained of existence. But Godric held it as casually as a man might hold a marble, his strength so far beyond the concept of weight that the infinite mass registered as absolutely nothing.

"Hmm," he said, his voice thoughtful. "That was... excessive. I apologize, Osvald. I let my disappointment get the better of me."

He enlarged the compressed mass slightly—just enough to make it the size of a small marble, smooth and dark and impossibly dense. He rolled it between his fingers, watching the faint, trapped light of compressed realities flicker within its depths.

"I think I'll keep this," he said. "As a trophy. A reminder of the last opposition that ever dared to challenge me. They failed, of course. They never had a chance. But they tried. They gave me a show. And even if that show was ultimately disappointing... it was still a show."

He slipped the marble into his pocket, and then, with exactly zero effort and exactly zero time, he restored the omniverse. Every reality. Every timeline. Every dimension. Every being. Every god, every mortal, every abstract entity. All of it was recreated perfectly, completely, instantaneously.

Except the fictional armada. They remained compressed in the marble, their consciousnesses preserved in a state of eternal, agonizing compression. Godric did not restore them. He did not feel the need to. They had challenged him, and they had failed, and their failure would serve as an eternal reminder to any being who might consider making the same mistake.

"And now," Godric said, turning to me with a warm, almost sheepish smile, "I believe I've gained something from this experience. Not their power—their combined might was effectively zero compared to my base state—but something else. Something more interesting."

He paused, his expression becoming contemplative. "The Weakness Paradox triggered, of course. It always triggers. But this time, it triggered in a way it never has before. Because they were fictional—beings created by the imagination of mortal minds—their attack represented something unique. Not a physical threat. Not a magical threat. Not even a conceptual threat. A narrative threat. A story designed to defeat me."

He chuckled, shaking his head. "The paradox absorbed that narrative. That story. That fundamental structure of beginning, middle, and end, hero and villain, conflict and resolution. And it converted it into something new. A layer of my existence that transcends even the concept of narrative. I am not merely immune to stories now. I am the source from which all stories flow. Every hero's journey, every villain's downfall, every dramatic climax and tearful resolution—all of it originates from me. I am the ultimate narrative. The final story. The absolute, eternal, unchanging truth from which all fiction derives its meaning."

He smiled, his warm brown eyes crinkling at the corners. "It's a small addition, really. Insignificant compared to my base power. But it's novel. Interesting. A souvenir of today's entertainment, if you will."

I set down Osvald's journal, my hands trembling so violently that the leather binding slipped from my fingers and thudded against the stone floor. The reading chamber was silent. The eternal afternoon light filtered through the crystalline windows, casting long shadows across the ancient shelves.

Osvald's journal ended there. He had written nothing further about that day, though he had continued to serve as court wizard for many years afterward. I knew, from the genealogical records, that he had died peacefully at the age of eighty-seven, surrounded by his children and grandchildren. I knew that he had never witnessed another opposition. Because there had never been another opposition. After the fictional armada's annihilation, no being in the omniverse ever dared to challenge Godric again.

And now I understood why.

It was not merely the scale of the destruction. It was not merely the casual, effortless compression of infinite fictional universes into a marble-sized trophy. It was the trophy itself. The implication that haunted my thoughts and would continue to haunt them for the rest of my mortal life.

Godric had restored the omniverse. Osvald had witnessed it. The journals all confirmed it—every time Godric caused catastrophic collateral damage, he restored everything perfectly, completely, instantaneously. The restored reality was identical to the original. Indistinguishable. Every being, every atom, every timeline, perfectly recreated.

But the trophy remained. The marble in Godric's pocket, containing the compressed mass of the original omniverse. The original. Not the restored copy. The actual, physical, original fabric of existence, squeezed into a sphere smaller than a quark by the passive force of Godric's zero percent strength.

So what was the restored reality? Was it truly the omniverse, reborn and renewed? Or was it a copy? A perfect replica, indistinguishable from the original in every way, but fundamentally, ontologically secondary? And if Godric could create such a perfect copy with zero effort—if he could restore the entire omniverse from nothing, instantly, effortlessly—then what did that say about the nature of our existence?

We were copies. We had always been copies. Every time Godric caused catastrophic collateral damage—and the journals recorded countless such incidents—he restored the omniverse. And the original remained compressed in his trophy collection. A marble here. A paperweight there. A decorative bauble on his mantelpiece. Each one containing the compressed mass of an entire omniverse that had been destroyed and replaced.

I looked at the journal in my trembling hands. Osvald had seen the marble. He had watched Godric slip it into his pocket. He had understood, as I now understood, the terrifying implication of its existence. And he had written it down, preserving the horror for future court wizards to discover.

The fictional armada had been the last opposition. After them, no one ever dared to challenge Godric again. Not because they were afraid of being destroyed. Not because they were afraid of being compressed into eternal, agonizing stasis. But because they finally, truly understood the absolute, inescapable nature of his power.

If Godric could destroy and restore the omniverse with zero effort, then existence itself was merely a temporary state permitted by his will. If Godric could compress the original omniverse into a marble and create a perfect copy to replace it, then the distinction between original and copy, real and fake, meaningful and meaningless, was a distinction that existed only because he allowed it to exist. And if Godric could do all of this with zero percent of his strength—with the absolute minimum possible expression of his passive, resting power—then the true scope of his capability was so far beyond comprehension that even attempting to imagine it was an act of self-inflicted madness.

We were not real. We were copies of copies of copies, restored and recreated and replaced countless times across the eons. And the only thing that made our existence meaningful was the fact that Godric chose to treat us as meaningful. The only thing that made our lives valuable was the fact that Godric valued us. The only thing that made our reality real was the fact that Godric permitted us to believe in its reality.

And the marble was still in his pocket. The original omniverse, compressed to the size of a marble, containing the actual, physical existence of everything that had ever existed. The omniverse that had contained Osvald. The omniverse that had contained the fictional armada. The omniverse that had contained the original Clive, the original Malachar, the original Florian, the original versions of everyone I had ever known and loved.

And we—the copies—continued to exist in the restored reality, blissfully unaware of our own ontological status, because Godric's infinite mercy allowed us to believe we were real.

I closed the journal and sat in the silent reading chamber, the eternal afternoon light washing over me, and I understood with perfect, absolute, terrifying clarity that I would never be able to look at a marble again without wondering if it contained the compressed mass of a reality that had once been mine.

And somewhere in the Eternal Citadel, Godric was smiling warmly, drinking his jasmine tea, and waiting patiently for the next court wizard to discover the truth.
