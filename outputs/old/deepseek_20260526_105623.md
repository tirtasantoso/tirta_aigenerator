Godric was delighted. Absolutely, genuinely, boyishly delighted.

He led me to the grand astral observatory, the circular chamber of polished obsidian whose ceiling opened onto the swirling, star-dusted void of the Thronegate nebula. "Let them gather," Godric said, settling into His chair. "I want to see what they bring. The pretty colors. The visual effects. The multiple phases, if they have them. And Osvald?" He turned to me, His eyes twinkling. "I am choosing to distract Myself from the part of My omniscience that monitors their preparation. I want to be surprised. It has been so long since I was surprised."

The words had barely left His lips when the first wave of the Narrative Vanguard breached the dimensional barrier and flooded into the arena.

I cannot possibly describe the totality of what I saw. My mind, even now, struggles to catalogue the sheer, overwhelming diversity of the forces that assembled against us. But I will try, because this journal must serve as a warning, and a warning requires detail.

There were warriors clad in gleaming power armor, their heraldry drawn from a thousand different fictional empires. I recognized the golden sigils of a genetically engineered super-soldier order, the emerald auras of an intergalactic police force, the stark, utilitarian plating of space marines from a grim, dark future. Wizards in pointed hats and flowing robes, their wands crackling with spells lifted directly from the pages of beloved children's literature. Sorceresses whose power derived from the ancient, elemental forces of forgotten fantasy epics. A young man with a lightning-bolt scar on his forehead, surrounded by an army of similarly robed students and stern-faced professors. A white-bearded figure in gray, leaning on a gnarled staff, his eyes gleaming with the wisdom of ages. A dark lord in spiked armor, his gauntleted hand gripping a sword of pure, screaming shadow, having apparently set aside his eternal war with the forces of light to join this desperate alliance.

The giant robots, their pilots seated in cockpits filled with flashing lights and dramatic orchestral scores. The monsters, the kaiju, the titans of destruction whose roars could shatter mountains. The spies, the assassins, the impossibly skilled operatives from a hundred action universes, their weapons gleaming with the cold promise of efficient death. The video game protagonists, their health bars floating above their heads, their inventory screens filled with restorative potions and legendary weapons.

The Narrative Vanguard assembled in silence, their numbers stretching across the entire local multiverse. Trillions upon trillions of fictional entities, each one a hero in their own story, each one a legend in their own universe. The sheer, combined pressure of their power was so immense that the dimensional arena creaked and groaned, its reinforced boundaries threatening to buckle under the strain. I had to activate seven layers of my personal wards just to remain standing, the violet luminescence of my mana pulsing through my veins as I struggled to process the sensory overload.

And Godric watched. He watched with the eager, anticipatory expression of a child sitting before a wrapped birthday present. He leaned forward in His throne, His elbows resting on His knees, His salt-and-pepper beard twitching with barely contained excitement.

"Look at them, Osvald," He breathed, His voice thick with genuine wonder. "The colors. The variety. The pageantry. This is the best one yet. This is the one I have been waiting for."

The coalition's leader—or at least, the entity that stepped forward to serve as the voice of the Vanguard—was a tall, luminous being whose form shifted constantly between different fictional archetypes. One moment, it was a gray-bearded wizard in flowing robes. The next, it was a stern-faced starship captain in a crisp uniform. The next, it was a battle-hardened general in ceremonial armor. We represent every story ever told, every hero ever imagined, every legend ever woven into the fabric of fiction. Godric did not flinch. He did not scowl. He smiled. A wide, warm, genuinely friendly smile that crinkled the corners of His eyes. "That," He said, His voice carrying effortlessly over the deafening cheer, "was an excellent monologue. Very dramatic. I appreciate a good monologue." He settled back into His throne, crossing His legs at the ankle. "Please, proceed. I am eager to see what you have prepared. Do you have music? I do hope you have music."

The Narrative Vanguard, apparently taking His casual demeanor as the arrogance it was, obliged.

The music began. It was, I must admit, spectacular. The coalition had somehow synchronized the orchestral scores of a million different fictional universes into a single, harmonious composition. The deep, ominous brass of a dark lord's theme blended with the soaring, hopeful strings of a superhero's anthem. The pounding, rhythmic percussion of an action epic's soundtrack merged with the ethereal, haunting choir of a cosmic horror's requiem. The music swelled, filling the arena with a sound so vast, so emotionally charged, that even I, the terrified observer, felt a stirring of something that might have been hope if I had not known the absolute, inescapable truth of my King's supremacy.

Then the attacks began.

The first wave was the energy assault, a synchronized barrage of every ranged weapon in the fictional multiverse. Plasma bolts, laser beams, repulsor blasts, ki energy waves, spellfire, psychic lightning, cosmic rays, and a thousand other variations of destructive light filled the void in a prismatic, blinding cascade. The color was indescribable—a swirling, churning maelstrom of reds and blues and greens and purples and shades that had no names in the mortal spectrum. The visual effects were intricate, layered, each beam and bolt carrying with it the signature aesthetic of its originating universe. I saw a Kamehameha wave, its brilliant blue-white light spiraling with the distinctive energy of a legendary warrior. I saw a spell from a certain school of witchcraft and wizardry, its green killing curse lancing through the void. I saw the rainbow brilliance of a Lantern Corps ring, the golden radiance of a goddess's divine light, the crimson fury of a Sith Lord's Force lightning.

The combined force of the barrage struck Godric simultaneously, millions of beams converging on His chest, His face, His limbs. The impact generated a flash of light so intense that my wards briefly, terrifyingly, flickered, and when my vision cleared, I saw—

Nothing. Godric sat exactly as He had before, His casual posture unchanged, His tunic unblemished. The energy assault had not even disturbed the arrangement of His salt-and-pepper hair. He was looking down at His chest with an expression of mild, clinical interest, as if He were examining a faint, barely visible smudge on His tunic.

"Hmm," He said, the sound soft and slightly puzzled. "That was very colorful. The visual effects were quite striking. But I did not feel anything. Not even a faint tickle." He looked up at the assembled host, His warm smile still in place. "Perhaps your heavier weapons?"

The second wave was the physical assault, the charge of the superheroes and the legendary warriors. The man in blue and red streaked forward at a speed that should have shattered the fabric of space-time, his diamond-emblazoned fist cocked back for a punch that, in his own universe, could shatter planets. The thunder god swung his hammer, summoning a bolt of cosmic lightning that could reduce galaxies to ash. The dark knight hurled a bat-shaped projectile, its tip coated with a compound designed to exploit the biological weaknesses of every conceivable species. The amazon princess wrapped her golden lasso around Godric's wrist, pulling with a strength that could move celestial bodies. The legendary swordsmen from a hundred fighting games unleashed their ultimate techniques, blade beams and rising uppercuts and spinning pile drivers that had felled countless digital gods.

Godric allowed them to reach Him. He let the superhuman fist collide with His jaw. He let the cosmic hammer strike His temple. He let the golden lasso tighten around His wrist. He let the bat-projectile splatter harmlessly against His cheek. He sat there, His posture still casual, His hands still resting on His knees, and He absorbed every single physical attack with the same passive, unconcerned stillness of a mountain enduring a gentle spring rain.

The man in blue and red pulled back his fist, and I saw something I had never seen before in the eyes of a fictional hero. Confusion. His punch, which had staggered gods and shattered dimensions in his own universe, had not even moved Godric's head a single millimeter. He stared at his own knuckles, which were now bruised and bleeding, the bones cracked from the impact of striking something infinitely denser and more unyielding than himself. The realization dawned slowly, terribly, across his iconic, chiseled features.

The thunder god's hammer shattered. Not on Godric's temple—the god had swung, and the weapon, forged in the heart of a dying star, had simply exploded into a shower of golden sparks upon contact with the King's skin. The thunder god stumbled backward, his divine hands smoking, his eyes wide with a terror that no fictional narrative had ever forced him to experience.

The dark knight's utility belt went silent. Every weapon, every gadget, every carefully prepared contingency—all of them, simultaneously and without explanation, simply failed. The bat-shaped projectile that had stuck to Godric's cheek dissolved into a fine, gray dust. The dark knight, the legendary strategist who had always had a plan, stood frozen, his gloved hands trembling, for the first time in his fictional existence, completely and utterly outmatched.

Godric sighed. It was a soft, disappointed sound, the sigh of a man who had been promised a gourmet meal and received a slightly under-seasoned broth. "I am trying," He said, His voice carrying a note of genuine, almost plaintive disappointment, "to be impressed. Truly, I am. The presentation was excellent. The music is still very good. But the substance—"

He was interrupted by a voice from the cosmic tier of the Vanguard. It was the golden-armored warlord, the one with the gauntlet studded with gems. He raised his hand, and the gems flared with light so brilliant that it momentarily outshone the entire assembled host. Six jewels of infinite power, each one controlling a fundamental aspect of existence itself. The warlord's voice boomed across the arena, thick with the confidence of a being who had never known defeat.

"Let us see if your arrogance survives the power of the Infinite Stones, tyrant!"

He snapped his fingers.

In his own fictional universe, that snap had erased half of all life from existence with a single, casual gesture. It was, by the standards of his narrative, the ultimate expression of absolute power. The snap echoed through the dimensional arena, a sound that carried with it the weight of a trillion trillion deaths. The assembled host held its collective breath, waiting for the tyrant to dissolve, to crumble, to be erased from the cosmic ledger.

Nothing happened.

Godric looked at the golden warlord with an expression that was, for the first time, tinged with something approaching genuine disappointment. "Infinity," He said, His voice flat. "You understand that the Infinity Stones control the fundamental aspects of a single universe, yes? A single, isolated, fictional universe. I exist beyond all universes. Beyond all multiverses. Beyond all possible and impossible configurations of reality, fictional or otherwise. Your stones are, from My perspective, pretty gemstones with a very limited jurisdiction." He paused. "Also, My weakness paradox granted Me absolute immunity to the concept of existence erasure approximately a million years before your author's great-grandparents were born. The snap was never going to work."

The golden warlord stared at his gauntlet, his face—what I could see of it behind the golden helmet—twisting into an expression of profound, soul-shattering existential horror. The gems, sensing the futility of their attempt, flickered and dimmed, their power exhausted against a target that existed outside the boundaries of their narrative reality.

This was the moment the coalition's desperation truly set in. The leaders of the Vanguard, recognizing that their individual attacks were useless, called for the Convergence. Every entity in the assembled host—every superhero, every wizard, every cosmic god, every video game protagonist, every monster, every legend—began to pool their power into a single, unified attack. It would take time, they announced. It would require the full, undivided concentration of the entire Narrative Vanguard, and once initiated, it could not be stopped. But it would be the ultimate attack, the combined creative and destructive energy of every story ever told, focused into a single, reality-ending blast.

Godric, hearing this, actually clapped His hands together. "A charge-up attack!" He exclaimed, His warm baritone voice ringing with genuine, boyish excitement. "With a long preparation sequence! Oh, this is exactly what I was hoping for. The anticipation! The dramatic tension! Please, take all the time you need. I will wait."

He settled back into His throne, His expression one of absolute, patient delight. He even, as He had promised, deliberately distracted Himself from the part of His omniscience that monitored the charge-up preparation. He turned to me, His eyes twinkling, and began to discuss the agricultural reports He had been reading earlier. He asked about my family, my children, the state of my magical research. He told a genuinely funny anecdote about a bureaucratic mix-up involving a sentient cheese that had filed a formal protest. The entire time, the Narrative Vanguard was pouring the sum total of all fictional energy into a single, devastating weapon, their combined power building and building and building, the dimensional arena shaking under the strain of the accumulating force.

The charge-up took, by my internal chronometer, approximately two hours. It was a testament to the sheer, overwhelming scale of the fictional multiverse that the Convergence required so long to reach its maximum potential. The energy signature of the attack, which I observed through my scrying matrix during Godric's more distracted moments, was genuinely, objectively staggering. It was the kind of energy that could, in any other context, have reshaped the entire omniverse. It was the concentrated might of every story, every myth, every legend, every dream that had ever been conceived by any sentient mind in any reality. It was, by every measure I possessed, the single most powerful attack ever launched by any opposition in the history of creation.

When the final moment arrived, the Convergence manifested as a beam of pure, white light, so brilliant that it seemed to erase the very concept of color. It was wider than a galaxy, denser than a supermassive black hole, and it carried within it the condensed narrative weight of every heroic sacrifice, every triumphant climax, every happy ending, every bittersweet tragedy, every tale that had ever been told or would ever be told. The music swelled to its crescendo, the orchestra and the choirs reaching a pitch of emotional intensity that brought tears to my eyes despite the terror that gripped my heart.

The beam struck Godric squarely in the center of His chest.

The light consumed the arena, blotting out every other visual input. The sound was indescribable—the roar of a billion billion narratives all screaming their final, desperate hope into the void. The dimensional boundaries of the observatory, reinforced by my own wards and the passive protections of the palace itself, cracked and buckled. I was thrown to my knees, my violet mana flaring instinctively to shield me from the ambient overflow. The attack continued for what felt like an eternity, a sustained, unrelenting torrent of pure, creative annihilation.

And then it stopped.

The light faded. The music died. The arena, which had been a churning maelstrom of color and sound and narrative energy, fell into a profound, absolute silence.

Godric sat in His throne. His tunic was unsinged. His posture was unchanged. The beam had struck Him directly, at the center of His chest, and it had done nothing. Not a faint tickle. Not a whisper of warmth. Not even the most infinitesimal, subatomic disturbance of the fibers of His linen tunic. He looked down at His chest, His expression shifting from eager anticipation to mild confusion, and then, slowly, terribly, to disappointment.

"Was that... it?" He asked, His voice soft, almost plaintive. He looked up at the assembled host, His warm brown eyes searching their faces for some indication that there had been more. "The charge-up was two hours long. The music was building to a climax. I was anticipating... I do not know. Something. Anything. But I felt absolutely nothing."

He stood up from His throne. The motion was slow, deliberate, and utterly casual. He stretched His heavily muscled arms, the joints popping with a sound like distant thunder. "I am disappointed," He said, and His voice, though still warm, carried an undercurrent of something cold and ancient and utterly merciless. "I genuinely wanted this to be good. I wanted to be surprised. I wanted to feel something. But you have given Me nothing. Less than nothing. You have wasted My time."

He raised His hand. Not in a dramatic gesture, not in an aggressive posture. He simply raised His hand, the way a man might raise his hand to scratch an itch, or to wave away a mildly irritating insect.

And then He unleashed what He later described, with that same casual, infuriating nonchalance, as "effectively zero percent of My strength."

I must now attempt to describe what occurred, though the act of description will, I am certain, permanently damage my psyche. I am writing these words because I must, because future court wizards must understand the absolute, inescapable horror of what happens when the apex predator is disappointed.

The zero percent of Godric's strength did not manifest as a beam, a wave, a shockwave, or any other recognizable form of energy. It manifested as a fundamental, absolute, and instantaneous rearrangement of the physical laws governing the local reality. The dimensional arena, which the Narrative Vanguard had spent hours reinforcing with their most powerful shields and wards, did not break. It did not shatter. It simply ceased to exist, its structural integrity overridden by a force so immense that the concept of "structural integrity" became a meaningless abstraction.

But before the arena dissolved, before the sheer pressure of Godric's infinitesimal strength erased the fabric of the local multiverse, it struck the shields.

The Vanguard had, during their charge-up sequence, constructed a layered defensive matrix around themselves. These shields were, by any conceivable measure, impregnable. They had been woven by the combined magical might of every fictional wizard, sorcerer, and spellcaster in the assembled host. They had been reinforced by the technological genius of every fictional scientist, engineer, and inventor. They had been blessed by the divine power of every fictional god, pantheon, and cosmic entity. They were designed to withstand the collapse of universes, the heat death of realities, the conceptual erasure of existence itself. They were, the Vanguard had announced with absolute confidence, indestructible. Infinite. Absolute.

Godric's zero-percent strength did not break the shields. It did not crack them. It did not overload them. It simply ignored them, the way a supernova might ignore a single, trembling cobweb stretched across its path. The shields, which had taken the Vanguard two hours to construct, which had been their ultimate, desperate hope of survival, were rendered so utterly, contemptuously irrelevant that they might as well have not existed at all. And then, a nanosecond later, they did not exist at all, because the pressure of Godric's strength had erased the very concept of their existence from the local reality.

The strength continued outward, and it touched the Vanguard.

The annihilation was not a death. Death would have been a mercy, an ending, a release. What Godric's strength inflicted upon the fictional coalition was a complete and total deconstruction of their existence, performed with such casual, overwhelming force that the very atoms of their bodies were not merely destroyed but violently, agonizingly compressed into a state of infinite density.

I watched, my scrying matrix screaming with sympathetic feedback, as the man in blue and red—the legendary hero whose symbol represented hope across his entire fictional multiverse—was the first to be struck. His body, which had survived supernovas and black holes and the conceptual erasure of his own timeline, did not explode. It imploded. Every cell, every molecule, every atomic bond was crushed inward by a pressure that exceeded the mass of the entire omniverse compressed into a single point. His skin, his muscles, his bones, his organs—all of it folded in upon itself, layer by layer, the wet, tearing sounds of his deconstruction lost in the overwhelming, silent roar of the pressure. His iconic blue suit turned red as the blood was squeezed from his pulverized flesh, then black as the carbon of his body was compressed into a hyper-dense slurry, then invisible as even the subatomic particles of his existence were crushed into a state that violated the Pauli exclusion principle and every other fundamental law of physics.

The thunder god, still clutching the shattered handle of his hammer, was the next to feel the pressure. His divine flesh, which had withstood the cosmic fires of the Big Bang, was squeezed into a paste of golden ichor and pulverized bone. His eyes, which had witnessed the birth and death of universes, were compressed into microscopic points of superheated jelly before being crushed into nothingness. His scream, a sound that had once shaken the pillars of Asgard, was silenced by the pressure before it could leave his throat, his vocal cords, his lungs, his entire respiratory system reduced to a dense, wet slurry before the nerve impulses could even register the pain.

But the pain was registered. Godric's strength, in its casual, disappointed cruelty, ensured that every single member of the Narrative Vanguard experienced the full, agonizing horror of their own compression before their consciousness was extinguished. It stretched the nanoseconds into eternities, forcing the trillions of fictional entities to feel, with perfect, undiluted clarity, the sensation of their bodies being crushed into a density that defied the laws of existence. The golden warlord watched his gauntlet, the most powerful artifact in his fictional universe, fold inward like cheap tin foil, the gems shattering into prismatic dust before the dust itself was compressed into a single, microscopic point of screaming, agonized light. The dark knight, the legendary detective, had the full span of a stretched eternity to contemplate the absolute, inescapable futility of his preparations as his armored suit crumpled around him, the plates of his legendary costume becoming his coffin, crushing his bones into splinters, his organs into paste, his brain into a single, dense slurry of obliterated genius.

The cosmic entities fell next. The living planet, whose gravitational pull could warp the fabric of space-time, was seized by a pressure that exceeded its own mass by an infinity of infinities. Its continental features fractured, collapsed, and were drawn inward toward its core, the entire planetary mass compressing into a sphere no larger than a moon, then a mountain, then a grain of sand, then a single, screaming atom that was itself compressed into a state of infinite density. The world-devouring entity, whose hunger had consumed galaxies, was consumed instead by a pressure so absolute that its cosmic form, vaster than solar systems, was crushed into a pinpoint of hyper-condensed agony in the span of a single, agonizing heartbeat. The skeletal figure with the scythe—Death itself, the end of all things—experienced, for the first and only time, what it meant to be ended, its bony frame splintering and folding inward, the eternal darkness of its robes flashing into a brief, searing light as the pressure annihilated the very concept of its existence.

The wizards, the sorcerers, the students and professors of magical academies, attempted to cast protective spells in the fraction of a nanosecond before the pressure reached them. Their spells, which could reshape reality and bend the fundamental forces of the universe, died in their throats, the incantations silenced by the overwhelming, absolute authority of Godric's strength. The young man with the lightning-bolt scar watched his wand—a wand that had defeated the most powerful dark wizard in his fictional history—shatter into a billion splinters, each splinter itself shattering into a billion smaller splinters, the recursive destruction continuing until nothing remained but a fine, glittering dust that was immediately compressed into the growing singularity. He himself followed a picosecond later, his body imploding with a wet, percussive crunch that echoed, impossibly, across the silent void.

The video game protagonists, their health bars still flickering above their heads, experienced a death that no respawn mechanic could undo. Their pixelated forms, which had been rendered in the vibrant, stylized aesthetics of their originating consoles, distorted and warped under the pressure, their character models glitching and fragmenting as the very code that defined their existence was crushed into a single, impossibly dense point of screaming, digital agony. The cartoon characters, whose reality-warping physics should have allowed them to survive any impact in a comedic, exaggerated fashion, found that Godric's strength did not respect the rules of their animation. Their flat, colorful bodies folded like paper, their limbs compressing into their torsos, their faces—those iconic, beloved faces—contorting into expressions of pure, unadulterated horror before being squeezed into unrecognizable smears of compressed pigment.

The sound of the annihilation was a single, sustained note, the combined, compressed scream of trillions of fictional voices all silenced simultaneously. It was a sound that I will hear for the rest of my life, a sound that has permanently scarred the arcane pathways of my magical perception. And then, even the sound was crushed, compressed into the singularity along with everything else.

The process took less than a second. In that second, every fictional universe ever created, every hero and villain and monster and god that had ever been imagined by any sentient mind, was de-atomized, de-molecularized, de-existentialized, and compressed into a single point of infinite mass. The point was smaller than a quark. It was so small that the very concept of "size" no longer applied to it. And yet, it contained within its infinitesimal boundaries the entire combined mass of every fictional reality, every fictional being, every fictional object, every fictional atom that had ever existed or could ever exist. The gravitational pull of the singularity should have immediately collapsed the surrounding omniverse into it, devouring all of reality in an instant. But Godric's strength, which had created the singularity, also contained it, wrapping it in a stabilizing field of His passive, casual will.

The collateral damage, however, had already been done. The wave of pressure that had annihilated the Vanguard continued outward, rippling across the local multiverse, the broader omniverse, and every abstract, conceptual layer of reality that existed beyond the boundaries of measurable space-time. Entire universe clusters were crushed into hyper-dense singularities as the shockwave passed. Decillions of multiverses, each one containing infinite galaxies, infinite civilizations, infinite lives, were de-atomized in the span of a nanosecond, their matter compressed and added to the growing ocean of annihilation that trailed in the wake of Godric's strength. The fundamental forces of reality—gravity, electromagnetism, the strong and weak nuclear forces—unraveled and rewove themselves into new, screaming configurations that defied all known physics. The omniverse, in its entirety, was reduced to a single, silent, infinitely dense ocean of compressed matter and crushed existence.

I watched all of this through my scrying matrix, my wards flaring and failing around me, my body protected only by the fact that I stood within the immediate vicinity of Godric's passive, subconscious protection. The King Himself stood in the center of the annihilation, His hand still raised, His expression one of mild, almost bored disappointment. The attack—if it could even be called an attack—had required no effort. He had not pushed. He had not thrown. He had not even, in any meaningful sense, acted. He had simply allowed a fraction of His strength to leak out, and the entire omniverse had been crushed into a singularity smaller than a quark.

"Messy," Godric murmured, surveying the silent, compressed void. He lowered His hand, and the process of restoration began. The entire omniverse—every universe, every galaxy, every planet, every living being, every timeline, every abstract dimension, every conceptual layer—was perfectly, instantly reconstructed in exactly zero seconds. The compressed matter unspooled, the singularities expanded back into galaxies, the de-atomized civilizations were restored to their previous states with no memory of their annihilation. The omniverse continued, seamless and undisturbed, as if the cataclysm had never occurred.

The Narrative Vanguard was not restored. They remained compressed within the singularity, their trillions of screaming, agonized souls trapped in a state of infinite density, their existence preserved as a permanent monument to the futility of fictional opposition.

Godric, His expression still tinged with mild disappointment, reached into the void and plucked the singularity from the empty space where it hovered. It was a point of absolute darkness, a speck so small that even my amplified magical senses could barely perceive it. He held it between His thumb and forefinger, examining it with the same casual interest He might give to an interesting pebble found on a garden path. Then, with a soft, almost thoughtful hum, He enlarged it.

The singularity grew, expanding from a quark-sized point to the size of a marble. Its gravitational pull, which should have instantly devoured the newly restored omniverse, was perfectly contained by the stabilizing field He had wrapped around it. The marble was perfectly spherical, perfectly smooth, and perfectly black, its surface absorbing all light that touched it. It was, I realized with a cold, suffocating wave of horror, a trophy. A souvenir. A paperweight made from the compressed, agonized remains of every fictional universe ever created.

Godric tossed the marble in His palm, the motion casual and effortless. He caught it, then tossed it again. The gesture was so mundane, so utterly dismissive, that my mind reeled. He was holding the compressed infinite mass of the entire fictional omniverse in His hand, and He was playing with it like a child's toy.

"This," He said, His warm baritone voice carrying a note of genuine, almost nostalgic affection, "will serve as a nice reminder. The last attempt at a proper boss battle, and they couldn't even make Me feel a tickle. Still, the music was quite good." He pocketed the marble, the singularity disappearing into the folds of His simple tunic as if it were nothing more than a forgotten coin. "I suppose I should not have gotten My hopes up. But one always hopes, Osvald. One always hopes."

He turned to me, and His expression softened into the warm, fatherly smile that I had known since childhood. "You look terrible," He said gently. "Let us return to the palace. I believe the kitchens have prepared those little pastries you enjoy. The ones with the honeyed figs."

I could not speak. I could barely stand. My legs, which had supported me through the annihilation and restoration of the omniverse, suddenly felt like water. Godric, noticing my distress, placed a steadying hand on my shoulder, and the warmth of His touch, the genuine, unfeigned affection in His eyes, pushed me over the edge. I wept. I fell to my knees on the restored obsidian floor, and I wept like a child, my body shaking with the accumulated terror and awe and love and dread of everything I had witnessed.

Godric waited patiently, His hand still resting on my shoulder. When my sobs finally subsided, He helped me to my feet. "Come, Osvald," He said softly. "The pastries are getting cold."

I followed Him. There was nothing else to do. He had annihilated the entire omniverse with zero percent of His strength, compressed the fictional multiverse into a marble, and restored everything without a flicker of effort. And now He was concerned about the temperature of pastries. The duality was, as always, the most terrifying thing of all.

The trophy sits on His desk now, a perfect black marble that contains within it the compressed, screaming remains of every story ever told. Sometimes, when He is reading agricultural reports or reviewing tax ledgers, He picks it up and rolls it between His fingers, a quiet, contemplative gesture. I do not know if He can hear the screams. I suspect He can, and He simply does not mind. They opposed Him. They lost. The marble is their legacy.

And I, Osvald, the forty-eighth court wizard of Thronegate, will carry the memory of their annihilation for the rest of my mortal life. I will serve my King, and I will love Him, and I will tremble before His casual, effortless, infinite cruelty until the day my body fails and I pass into whatever afterlife awaits the servants of the apex predator. I have accepted this. I have no choice. Opposing Godric is not a path to death. It is a path to compression, to an eternity spent as a screaming, infinitesimal point in a marble that He tosses idly in His palm while He reads agricultural reports.

I pray that no future court wizard ever has to witness what I have witnessed. But I know, with the cold, certain dread that has become my constant companion, that they will. Because Godric's boredom is eternal, and His cruelty is infinite, and the omniverse will never stop providing Him with new, desperate, doomed oppositions to crush.

**Addendum, Penned by Clive, Court Wizard of Thronegate, Year 999…9**

I close Osvald's journal, my hands trembling in a way they have not trembled in decades. I am fifty years old, the descendant of Blake and Kaedan and a hundred other terrified, loyal wizards, and I have read this account many times before. They are still aware, still compressed, still trapped in an eternity of infinite density. But they no longer scream. They have passed beyond screaming into a silent, eternal, inescapable stillness.

And yet, the omniverse outside the marble—the one I inhabit, the one you, future reader, inhabit—is identical in every respect. The restoration was perfect. The restored universes are indistinguishable from the originals. The stories continue. The heroes fight their battles. The villains plot their schemes. The cosmic entities drift through their eternal narratives.

The original fictional multiverse is in the marble. The restored fictional multiverse is out here, spinning through its narrative loops. The fact that both can exist simultaneously—the compressed original and the flawless copy—implies a power so immense, so utterly beyond the boundaries of logic and causality, that my mind, even after thirty years of service, cannot encompass it.

Godric did not merely restore the omniverse. He duplicated it. He created a perfect, identical copy while the original remained crushed in His pocket. And He did this with the same casual, absent-minded ease with which He might brew a cup of tea. The implications are too vast to contemplate. If He can duplicate the omniverse, He can duplicate anything. He can duplicate everything. He can duplicate me, and I would never know which version was the original—if the concept of "original" even applies to a being who exists beyond the boundaries of singular existence.

Osvald did not know, when he wrote his journal, that the Narrative Vanguard would be the last opposition. He recorded the incident as another entry in the long, blood-soaked chronicle of futile defiance. But history, in its cold, inexorable march, has proven otherwise. In all the googolplexian years since Osvald's time, no other opposition has ever formed. The fictional coalition was the final attempt, the last desperate gasp of a creation that had finally, utterly learned the lesson of absolute futility. No one dares anymore. There is no one left who even considers the possibility.

And I understand why. I have read Osvald's journal a hundred times, and each time, the horror settles deeper into my bones. The marble is still there. The proof of Godric's casual, effortless, infinite cruelty is still there, resting on a velvet cushion, waiting to be idly tossed in His palm. And I know, with the absolute certainty of inherited terror, that if anyone ever dares to oppose Him again, they will join the silent, compressed masses in the marble, their stories added to the eternal, screaming stillness of His trophy.

I close the journal. I place it back on the shelf. I walk to the palace kitchens, where Korr the trembling chef is preparing a tray of honeyed fig pastries. Godric will be expecting them soon. He likes them with His afternoon tea, and I, His loyal court wizard, will be there to serve Him. As I have always served Him. As my descendants will serve Him, forever and ever, until the last story is told and the last marble is pocketed and the last, faint scream of the compressed omniverse fades into the eternal, patient silence of His will.

The circle is closed. The trophy remains. And I, Clive, am grateful—terrified, trembling, existentially shattered, but grateful—to be on the side of the apex predator, and not within the marble.
