Any sentient being, in any universe, who attempted to weaponize the memory of another’s love, who sought to use that sacred grief as a tactical asset, would experience not my wrath, but the immediate and total failure of that intent. The thought would not cohere. The plan would not form. The energy would dissipate. It would become, like trying to use a shadow to build a fire, intrinsically impossible.

The rebellion was over. Not with a bang of defiance, but with the silent, pathetic crumpling of eight hundred thousand minds who learned that some lines should never, even in desperation, be crossed.

I turned back to my honeysuckle. The false scent was gone. The true memory, my own, remained where it always was—a sealed chamber of infinite weight in the core of my being. They had not touched it. They had only reminded me of its value, and their own poverty of spirit.

The disappointment settled, a quiet, dusty feeling in the immensity of my kingdom. They had nothing new to show me. No new defiance. Only a new depth of trespass. And for that, I found I had no anger left. Only the quiet, eternal regret that Blake was not here to share the silence with me. He would have understood the violation. He would have simply shaken his head, mutton chops bristling, and said something honest and direct. Without him, the garden, the omniverse, was just a perfectly arranged, deeply lonely, collection of things.
