The air on Olympus never tasted of anything but honey and jasmine. That was the first truth every child learned, the second being that King Godric’s laughter could be heard from the highest spire to the lowest market stall. At forty-five, he stood as a monument to everything a king should be—broad shoulders that could carry a thousand burdens, a chest that had never known defeat in the wrestling rings of his youth, and arms that could still split oak for the Yule fire despite his station. His hair, cropped short and neat, had begun its salt-and-pepper transition with dignity, and his beard followed the same pattern, trimmed precisely along the jawline of a face that made maidens blush and warriors nod in respect. But it was his eyes that held the true warmth, the kind of blue that seemed to hold summer skies even in the deepest winter, and when he smiled, which was often, the lines around those eyes deepened into channels of genuine affection.

His daughter Isabel stood before him now, resplendent in white silk that had been spun by the mountain spinners of Thessaly, her dark hair woven with threads of gold and small white flowers. She was twenty-two, the same age her mother Reina had been when Godric had first seen her across the great hall and forgotten how to breathe. He looked at Isabel now and saw that same grace, that same impossible beauty, and felt his heart crack open with love for the hundred thousandth time since she had been placed in his arms, pink and squalling and perfect.

“You’re going to make me cry before I even reach the altar, Father,” Isabel said, her voice trembling with laughter and nerves.

Godric blinked rapidly and cleared his throat. “I’m not crying. I have something in my eye. Both eyes. It’s a condition.”

His younger daughter Hebe, fifteen and already possessing the sharp wit that would have made her a fearsome diplomat, snorted from behind her sister. “It’s called ‘fatherly sentimentality.’ There’s no cure.”

“Thank you for your medical assessment, Doctor Hebe,” Godric said dryly, and the three of them laughed together, the sound carrying through the marble corridors of the palace. She crossed to them and took his hand, squeezing it once, and he felt complete in a way that had nothing to do with crowns or kingdoms.

The ceremony was held in the Grand Courtyard, where the ancient pillars of Olympus rose toward a sky so blue it hurt to look at. Thousands had gathered—nobles from the outlying provinces, common folk from the city below, merchants from distant lands who had heard of the beauty of Olympian weddings and come to witness. Thorin stood at the altar, his white robes embroidered with the silver sigils of his ancient office, his long grey beard nearly touching his belt. He had been Godric’s friend since they were boys, the court wizard’s son and the king’s heir, sneaking out of lessons to fish in the river that ran through the valley. The music began, and Isabel appeared at the courtyard’s entrance.

She was sunlight made flesh. She was every promise Godric had ever made to himself about being a good father, walking toward him on a path of rose petals. She was hope and joy and the future, and Godric’s heart swelled until he thought it might burst from his chest.

The first arrow struck the high priest in the throat.

He felt himself pulled apart and reassembled across dimensions. He felt time become meaningless. He felt the sanctuary accept him, a cave of crystal and obsidian hidden in a pocket of reality that existed between the breaths of dying stars. He stopped aging.

He stopped needing to eat, to drink, to blink, to sleep, to exist in any way that mortals understood.

And in that exact moment of transition, when his mortal self was stripped away and something else was being born in its place, something in the fundamental architecture of the universe recognized what had been done. A spell of such forbidden power, broken mid-casting, combined with a soul that had been shattered into a billion pieces by grief, created a paradox that should have annihilated the omniverse.

Instead, it created Godric.

His strength did not merely increase. It began to multiply by a factor of one thousand, and then by that result, every Planck second. The smallest measurable unit of time, repeated forever, each iteration building on the last, a geometric progression toward infinity that had no upper bound. His endurance followed. His speed. His senses. His intelligence. His wisdom. All of them, caught in the same endless amplification, growing without limit, without pause, without mercy.

And then the superpowers began.

The second superpower arrived one second later. It was the ability to perceive all of time simultaneously, past and future and every branching possibility, laid out before him like a tapestry that had no beginning and no end. He saw the universe that had been created and the universe that would be. The fourth was the ability to create and destroy matter at will.

The fifth was the mastery of all forms of energy.

The sixth was the command of dimensional space.

On and on they came, each second birthing a new apocalypse, each new apocalypse immediately beginning its own multiplication toward infinity. And at the center of this storm of becoming, Godric slept the not-sleep of his stasis, his mind expanding, his power growing, his soul calcifying into something that the universe would learn to fear.

But the universe did not notice. The power that concealed him was already so immense that even the gods of other pantheons, if they passed through the pocket dimension, would see only a cave and a sleeping man and move on without a second thought. For ten thousand years, it continued.

Ten thousand years of Planck-second multiplications.

Ten thousand years of new superpowers every single second.

Ten thousand years of the concealment growing alongside the power it hid, maintaining the perfect balance, the flawless veil.

Ten thousand years of Godric becoming something that should not exist, something that could not exist, something that the omniverse had never conceived of and would have rejected if it had been given a choice.

# The Weight of a Fraction

I remember the day my husband became a ghost.

It was not the kind of ghost that haunts halls and rattles chains. It was the kind that sits at the dinner table and eats the food you place before him, that responds to your questions with monosyllables that do not reach his eyes, that lies beside you in the dark with his body present and his soul somewhere else entirely. Rogr became a ghost on the day he met King Godric, and I have spent the last ten years watching that ghost starve.

The memory of that morning is etched into me with the sharpness of a blade that does not rust. Rogr had been nervous, but excited. He had been chosen as one of the delegates from the newly subjugated universe of Qethara to present our formal surrender to the King of Thronegate. We had resisted, as all universes did, and we had been conquered in less than twelve minutes by five middle-aged men who looked like they would rather be at home with their families. But the stories that emerged from that conquest were not stories of cruelty. They were stories of kindness. The Thronegate men had apologized for the destruction, had helped rebuild the cities they had been forced to damage, had assured us that under King Godric, we would prosper.

And we did.

Godric's rule was perfect. There is no other word for it. Crime vanished. Poverty became a memory. Wars between our former nations ceased, not through force, but through the simple economic and social harmony that his decrees created. The Awakening did not apply to conquered universes—we remained mortal, ordinary—but we did not need superhuman power to thrive. We needed peace, and he gave us peace. We needed justice, and he gave us justice. We needed a king who cared, and he cared with a depth that seemed almost impossible for a being of such power.

So when Rogr was selected as a delegate, we celebrated. He was a good man, a patient man, a man who had raised our son Pete with the same gentle hand that he had used to build our marriage. He was not ambitious. He was not seeking favor. He simply wanted to represent our people with dignity.

I kissed him goodbye at the door, and I told him to be brave.

He was brave.

He was brave for exactly as long as it took for King Godric to look at him.

---

The delegate chamber was designed for comfort. I have read the official records. I have studied the architectural plans. The chamber was warm, softly lit, decorated with art from a thousand cultures, designed to put visitors at ease. Rogr had walked in with the other delegates, had taken his seat, had felt the gentle hum of the palace's life support systems, had smelled the faint fragrance of flowers that were bred to bloom in any atmosphere.

And then the door had opened, and Godric had entered.

The account that Rogr gave me, in the shattered fragments of the first few weeks after his return, was not coherent. It was not a narrative. It was a scream that had been compressed into words.

"He looked at me," Rogr had said, his hands shaking, his eyes fixed on a point that did not exist in our bedroom. "He looked at me, and I saw what he was holding back."

I asked him what he meant, and he could not explain.

But I have pieced it together over ten years, from the nightmares that wake him screaming, from the involuntary flinch he makes when anyone enters a room too quickly, from the way he sometimes freezes in the middle of a sentence, his face going slack, his breath catching, as if he is experiencing something that has already happened but will never end.

King Godric had been playing a prank.

That is what Blake told us, ten years later, standing in our modest home with his mutton chop and his honest eyes full of guilt. It was a prank. Blake had made an offhand comment before the delegation arrived, something about how Godric's reputation as a merciless tyrant could be fun to play with. And Godric, who loved Blake as a brother, who trusted him absolutely, had taken the suggestion and run with it.

He had decided to pretend to be displeased.

Not angry. Not cruel. Not threatening. Just... mildly displeased. A slight furrow of the brow. A hint of coldness in his voice. A fraction of a second where he allowed the delegates to feel that they had disappointed him.

Godric, who had spent ten thousand years accumulating power at a rate that defied mathematics, who had gained a new apocalypse every second of every day for a hundred centuries, who had held back that power with a concealment ability that grew at the same terrifying rate, had allowed a single, infinitesimal drop of his true presence to leak through.

He had held back ninety-nine point nine nine nine—the number of nines stretches into infinity—percent of his power. He had held back so much that he genuinely believed he was projecting nothing at all. He had held back so much that any rational being would have considered it complete suppression.

But Godric's concept of "holding back" was not the same as any other being's concept of holding back.

A fraction of a fraction of a fraction of his power, a sliver so small that it could not be measured by any instrument in any universe, had brushed against Rogr's consciousness for less than a second.

And that was enough.

---

The first year was the hardest.

Rogr came home from the delegation a different man. He did not speak for three days. He sat in his chair, staring at the wall, and when I tried to touch him, he flinched so violently that he fell off the chair and crawled into a corner, his arms wrapped around his knees, his breath coming in ragged gasps that sounded like a drowning man fighting for air.

Pete was seven at the time. He did not understand why his father would not play with him, why his father would not look at him, why his father would not even seem to hear his voice. I told Pete that Father was very tired, that the journey had been long, that he needed rest.

I lied.

The nightmares started in the second week. Rogr would wake up screaming, his body drenched in sweat, his eyes wide and unfocused, and he would grasp at my arms with a strength that I did not know he possessed, and he would say things that I could not understand.

"The edges," he would whisper. "The edges of it. It was there. It was all there. He held it back but I saw the edges. I saw the edges of the thing that should not be."

I held him. I rocked him. I told him it was just a dream.

But it was not just a dream. The dream was a memory. The memory was a truth that his mind could not contain, that kept leaking out in the dark hours when his defenses were down.

I tried everything.

I took him to the healers of Thronegate, who were the finest in the omniverse. They examined him with devices that could scan the soul, that could trace the pathways of neural energy, that could identify and repair any damage to the mind. They found nothing. His brain was perfect. His soul was intact. There was no physical or spiritual cause for his condition.

"Trauma," they said. "Severe psychological trauma. Time will heal it."

Time did not heal it.

I took him to the mages of the conquered universes, beings who had studied the mind for millennia, who had developed techniques for soothing the most troubled spirits. They performed rituals. They chanted incantations. They created calming elixirs that could pacify a raging storm elemental.

Rogr drank the elixirs, and his body relaxed, but his eyes remained hollow.

I took him to the ninjas of Thronegate, those impossibly fast beings who could move between moments, who could rethread the fabric of time itself. I begged them to help him forget. They tried. They reached into his memories, found the moment of the meeting, attempted to soften it, to blur it, to wrap it in layers of peaceful imagery that would dull its sharp edges.

The moment fought back.

The memory of Godric's displeasure, that fraction of a second of exposure, was so dense with meaning, so heavy with the weight of what it represented, that the ninjas could not touch it. They described it as trying to move a mountain with a spoon. The memory was too real, too present, too fundamentally true for any alteration to take hold.

I tried to appeal to Godric himself.

I wrote letters. I sent petitions. I requested an audience with the King, to explain what had happened to my husband, to ask for his help in healing the wound that his presence had caused.

The letters were received. They were read. They were answered by his staff, who assured me that King Godric was aware of the situation and that he had full confidence in Rogr's ability to recover.

Godric, I would later learn, genuinely believed that Rogr could "tough it up." He had absolute omniscience—he knew exactly what Rogr was experiencing—but he had underestimated the horror because his own frame of reference was so far beyond mortal comprehension. He had held back ninety-nine point nine nine nine... percent of his power? He had held back more than that. He had held back so much that he could not conceive of the damage that remained.

He thought that what Rogr experienced was a minor scare, nothing more. A momentary discomfort that any strong-willed individual could overcome with time and resilience.

He believed in mortal resilience.

He was wrong.

---

The years passed, and Rogr grew worse.

It was not a dramatic decline. There was no sudden collapse, no single event that marked the transition from wounded to broken. It was a slow erosion, a gradual wearing away of the man I had married.

He stopped going outside. The streets of our city were safe, beautiful, filled with laughter and prosperity, but Rogr could not bear to walk among them. He said that the light reminded him of something. He did not specify what.

He stopped eating with us. Pete and I would sit at the dinner table, and Rogr would sit in his chair by the window, staring at the horizon, his plate untouched. I would bring him food later, and he would eat mechanically, without tasting, without enjoyment.

He stopped speaking to Pete.

That was the worst. Pete was seventeen now, a young man who had grown up under Godric's perfect rule, who had never known war or famine or injustice. He loved King Godric with the fervor of a generation that had only ever known peace and prosperity. He had a portrait of the King in his room, a gift from the palace on his fifteenth birthday, signed by Godric himself. Pete would talk about the King's wisdom, his kindness, his vision for the omniverse, and Rogr would sit in his chair and stare at nothing.

I loved my son. I loved my husband. And I was being torn apart by the impossible contradiction of a world that was perfect and a husband who was destroyed.

I tried to explain to Pete, when he was old enough to understand. But how do you explain something that you do not understand yourself? How do you tell your son that the king he idolizes, the king who has given us everything, is also the source of his father's endless agony?

"He is a good king," I said to Pete one evening, after Rogr had gone to bed early, his footsteps dragging, his shoulders hunched. "He is the best king we could ever hope for. But he is also... he is so much more than us. He is so powerful that he cannot always control how his presence affects others."

Pete looked at me with the earnest confusion of youth. "But Father says he saw something terrible. He says he saw the edges of something that should not exist."

"Your father saw King Godric being displeased. For a moment. A fraction of a moment. And that fraction was so vast, so heavy, that it broke something inside him."

Pete was silent for a long time. Then he said, "I still love the King."

"I know, Pete. I love him too."

And I did. I loved him with a gratitude that was genuine and deep. He had given us utopia. He had ended suffering. He had created a society where I could raise my son without fear, where I could walk the streets at any hour without violence, where my neighbors were my friends and my community was my family. Godric's rule was not tyranny; it was salvation.

But I could not reconcile that love with the hollow shell of my husband.

---

The tenth year was the worst.

Rogr had stopped leaving his room entirely. He lay in bed, his eyes open, his breath shallow, his body growing thin. I fed him. I bathed him. I talked to him about the weather, about Pete's grades, about the new market that had opened in the southern district. He responded with nods, with grunts, with an occasional word that seemed to cost him more energy than he had.

The healers had given up. The mages had given up. The ninjas had given up.

I had not given up.

I tried rituals from ancient texts, chants that predated the empire, prayers to gods that had been dead for millennia. I tried remedies made from the tears of a star whale, from the pollen of a dimension that existed only in the imagination of a mad poet. I tried everything that any civilization in the omniverse had ever conceived of for healing the mind.

Everything failed.

The methods themselves seemed to recoil from the task. I once attempted a healing ceremony that involved placing crystals around Rogr's bed and reciting a series of harmonic frequencies. The crystals shattered. Every single one. Not violently—they simply turned to dust, as if they had been subjected to a pressure that they could not withstand.

I tried a meditative technique that required me to enter Rogr's mind and guide him toward a peaceful memory. I was thrown out of his mind so forcefully that I woke up in the next room, my nose bleeding, my ears ringing.

I tried a potion brewed from a recipe that had been used to cure warriors of the worst trauma. The potion boiled in its vial the moment I brought it near Rogr's mouth, and then it evaporated, leaving nothing behind but a faint scent of ozone.

The universe did not want Rogr to be healed.

No. That was not it. The universe was simply incapable of healing him because the wound had been inflicted by something that existed beyond the universe's own laws. Godric's power was so fundamental, so foundational to reality, that any attempt to undo its effects was like trying to rewrite the laws of physics by wishful thinking.

I stood in our bedroom, looking at my husband, feeling the weight of ten years of failure pressing down on me, and I wept.

---

Blake came to our door on a Tuesday.

I recognized him immediately. Everyone recognized Blake. He was the King's right hand, the merchant who had found him, the one person in the omniverse with whom Godric was always warm and kind. He was a legend, but he stood at my door with the awkward posture of a man who was about to deliver terrible news.

"Rosa," he said, and his voice was gentle. "May I come in?"

I let him in. I made him tea. I sat across from him in our living room, and I waited.

He took a long breath. "Ten years ago, King Godric met with the delegates from Qethara. I was there. I made a joke before the meeting. I told him to act like a tyrant, for fun. I didn't mean it seriously. I didn't think he would actually do it."

I stared at him.

"He only now mentioned it to me," Blake continued, his hands wrapped around the cup, his knuckles white. "He said, 'Do you remember that delegation? One of them was quite affected. A man named Rogr. He's still having trouble.' He said it like it was nothing. Like Rogr had a mild cold that was taking too long to clear up."

I felt something cold settle into my chest. "He knows."

"He knows everything. He always has. He just... underestimated. He thought he was holding back completely. He thought that what Rogr experienced was minor. He believed that mortal resilience would overcome it."

"He has been suffering for ten years," I said, and my voice did not break, though it wanted to. "He has been dying for ten years."

"I know." Blake's eyes were wet. "I'm sorry. I'm so sorry. I made the joke. I asked him to play a prank. I never imagined—"

"Neither did he."

Blake looked at me, and there was something in his expression that I had never seen before. It was guilt, yes, but it was also something else. Awe. Fear. The dawning realization of what his friend truly was.

"I want to fix this," Blake said. "I want to ask King Godric to heal your husband. He can do it. Instantly. Completely. Without any side effects. But I won't do anything without your permission. This is your family. Your decision."

I looked at the bedroom door, behind which my husband lay, hollow and fading.

"Yes," I said.

Pete came home from his studies, and we sat together, the three of us, and we talked. Pete was seventeen now, a young man with his father's eyes and his own kind heart. He loved King Godric. He revered him. And when Blake explained the situation, Pete's face cycled through emotions that I could not name.

"He caused this?" Pete asked. "The King?"

"It was an accident," Blake said. "A prank that went wrong. He didn't know how badly it would affect your father. He didn't understand."

"Can he fix it?"

"Yes."

Pete was silent for a long moment. Then he said, "Then let him fix it."

We agreed.

The moment the word left my mouth, Godric was in our living room.

He did not walk through the door. He did not appear in a flash of light. He was simply there, as if he had always been there, as if the space in front of our sofa had always contained him and we had only now noticed. He was massive, handsome, his salt-and-pepper beard perfectly trimmed, his blue eyes warm and kind. He was dressed in simple clothes, no crown, no regalia, a man who could have been any grandfather visiting his family.

But I had seen the edges.

I had never experienced them directly, but I had seen them in Rogr's nightmares, in his screams, in the hollow look that had consumed his eyes. And now, standing before the King, I felt a tremor of that same dread.

"Rosa," Godric said, and his voice was gentle. "I am sorry."

"I know," I said.

He looked at the bedroom door, and I understood that he could see through it, that he could see Rogr lying in bed, that he could see every cell of his body, every thread of his consciousness, every fiber of his trauma. And then he did something that I will never forget.

He frowned.

Not in anger. Not in displeasure. But in concentration. In focus. It was the look of a craftsman examining a piece of work that had a flaw, and deciding how to correct it.

"I will make this right," he said.

And then he did.

There was no gesture. No incantation. No visible sign of power. Godric simply decided, at the deepest level of his being, that Rogr should be healthy. And Rogr became healthy.

I heard a sound from the bedroom. A gasp. A sharp intake of breath. Then footsteps, steady and strong, as Rogr walked out of the room for the first time in months.

He looked ten years younger.

No, that was not right. He looked the same age, but the years had been lifted from him. His eyes were clear. His shoulders were straight. His face had color, life, presence. He looked at me, and he smiled, and it was the smile I had married, the smile I had thought I would never see again.

"Rosa," he said.

I ran to him. I held him. I wept into his chest, and he held me back, strong and warm and real.

Pete joined us, and we stood together, a family that had been broken and was now whole.

Godric watched us with a softness in his eyes that I had never seen in any portrait, any depiction, any story. He was not a tyrant. He was not a monster. He was a man who had made a mistake, who had caused harm without meaning to, who had fixed it the moment he understood.

"Cognitive dissonance," Rogr said later, after Blake had left, after Godric had vanished as silently as he had appeared, after we had eaten dinner together for the first time in a decade. "That's what the healers called it. The inability to reconcile two conflicting beliefs. I loved Godric, and I feared him. I was grateful for his rule, and I was destroyed by his presence. Both were true. Both were real. But now—"

"Now?"

He took my hand. "Now I only love him. The fear is gone. The trauma is gone. But I remember it. I remember what it felt like to be crushed by the weight of a god's attention. And I am still grateful. I am still loyal. It is a strange thing, to have been unmade and remade by the same hand."

I understood.

Godric offered me compensation.

He came to me privately, after the healing, and he said, "Rosa, you have suffered. Ten years of watching your husband deteriorate, of trying everything, of failing. I cannot undo that time. But I can offer you anything you desire. Wealth. Power. A new home. A new life. Name it, and it is yours."

I looked at him, and I felt the love that all Thronegate citizens felt for him, the gratitude that was as natural as breathing. But I also felt the lingering dread, the knowledge that this being who stood before me, so warm and kind and apologetic, was also the being whose fraction of a second of exposure had nearly destroyed my husband.

"No," I said. "Thank you. But no."

Godric tilted his head. "Why?"

"Because I cannot accept a gift from you without remembering what happened. And I do not want to spend the rest of my life in that shadow. I want to love you freely, without debt, without transaction."

He was silent for a long moment. Then he smiled, and it was a father's smile, a king's smile, a friend's smile.

"You are a remarkable woman, Rosa."

And then he was gone.

That evening, I stood at the window of our home, watching the sun set over the perfect city that Godric had built. Pete was in his room, studying, humming a song that praised the King. Rogr was in the kitchen, cooking dinner, singing under his breath.

I felt the happiness. I felt the gratitude. I felt the love.

And beneath it all, in the deepest part of me, I felt the echo of a scream that had never been screamed, the shadow of a presence that had never fully left, the knowledge that I lived in a universe where a being of infinite power could accidentally shatter a man's soul and then fix it with a thought.

I loved him.

I feared him.

I was grateful.

I was terrified.

All of it was true. All of it was real. And I would carry that contradiction for the rest of my life, because that was the price of living in Godric's universe.

But I would also carry this: my husband, whole and healthy. My son, safe and happy. My home, peaceful and warm.

It was enough.

It had to be enough.
