The air in the throne room of Olympus tasted of honeyed mead and blooming peonies. King Godric, at forty-five, stood before the sun-drenched altar, his muscular frame wrapped in ceremonial white and gold. His salt-and-pepper beard was trimmed close to a jaw that ached from smiling, and his eyes, the color of a summer sky, held a warmth that seemed to melt the very marble around them. He watched his daughter, Isabel, approach. At twenty-two, she was a vision of her mother, Queen Reina, who stood beside Godric, her hand finding his, their fingers intertwining like roots of an ancient, steadfast tree. Their younger daughter, Hebe, fifteen and bubbling with mischief, adjusted Isabel’s gossamer veil with solemn, uncharacteristic care. This was the pinnacle of Godric’s world—a kingdom of peace, a family of love, a day of union. He opened his mouth to begin the blessing, his voice a resonant baritone that carried to the farthest courtier.

The first explosion was not a sound, but a silence. A compression of reality that sucked the breath from the room before the western wall ceased to exist. Then came the screams, not of surprise, but of visceral disassembly. Through the smoke and shimmering debris strode the legions of Thronegate. They did not march; they manifested, their armor drinking the light, their weapons etching lines of void through the air. Godric’s world shattered not piece by piece, but all at once. He saw Reina turn towards Hebe, a mother’s protective arc, before a lance of condensed shadow pierced them both, pinning them to the remnant of the throne like terrible butterflies. Isabel’s wedding gown became a shroud of fire, her cry lost in the detonation that consumed her. Godric’s roar was not a king’s command, but the raw, animal sound of a soul being skinned alive.

“Godric! To me!” The voice was Thorin’s, his court wizard and oldest friend, his face a mask of ash and terror. Thorin’s hands were already moving, tracing sigils in the air that bled light. “The family spell! It is not for travel, it is for preservation! It has never been cast!” The forbidden teleportation incantation, a legacy of Thorin’s bloodline, unfolded in jagged, desperate syllables. A sphere of fractured light began to enclose Godric, a cocoon of last resort. A Thronegate executioner, a giant in obsidian plate, swung a blade that hummed with temporal decay. Thorin threw his body into its path. The blade passed through Thorin’s neck without resistance, but the wizard’s dying breath finished the final, fractured syllable. The spell, severed at its climax, malfunctioned catastrophically.

Godric did not vanish. He unraveled and was rewoven elsewhere, in a hidden sanctuary of quartz and silence deep within the world’s bone. The trauma was not just psychological; it was ontological. As he knelt on the cool stone, the images of his family’s ending etching themselves onto the inside of his skull, the incomplete spell’s backlash triggered. His aging stopped. The need for breath, for sleep, for sustenance, was erased from his biology. It was the least of the changes.

Inside him, a silent engine ignited. His physical strength, the endurance of his flesh, the speed of his nerves, the acuity of his senses, the depth of his wisdom, the breadth of his intelligence—each fundamental attribute began to multiply. Not by double, not by a thousandfold, but by one million times. And then that new, inconceivable sum multiplied by itself. And that new sum, a number already mocking mathematics, multiplied by itself again. This recursion did not tick like a clock; it was a continuous, exponential avalanche within every atom of his being, a chain reaction without a terminal point, compounding every single second.

Simultaneously, new superpowers awoke in his soul, one per second. The first was Absolute Concealment, the instinctual need to hold back the apocalyptic pressure of his own existence, and its potency began its own million-fold self-multiplication per second, forever keeping the lid on the boiling sun within. The second was Omniscient Perception, and instantly he knew the weight of every star and the dream of every mite. The third was Quantum Annihilation, a power that could unmake a galaxy by mere intention. The fourth, Reality Rewriting. The fifth, Conceptual Dominion. On and on, an endless arsenal of apocalypses, each one’s devastating potential itself multiplying at the same terrifying, infinite rate. The omniverse should have vaporized in a flash of metaphysical static. It did not, because the first power, already infinitely beyond godlike, held it all in perfect, effortless check.

And then, the final, cruelest law etched itself into the foundation of all things: the Weakness Paradox. Godric had no weaknesses. It was an absolute truth. But should any entity, in any corner of existence, ever entertain the arrogant thought that he did—a fleeting speculation, a desperate hypothesis—the universe itself would punish their insolence. The rate of his perpetual, infinite amplification would permanently increase to a new, even more unfathomable tier. Absolute immunity to the imagined exploit would be forged in the same instant. And, most terrifyingly, Godric would gain perfect, ultimate mastery over the very concept of that attack, becoming its living prime source, while retroactively denying its possibility to all others. This defense triggered not at the moment of attack, but at the moment of thought, long before plans were drawn, dooming the thinker from the inception of their own doomed ambition.

Ten thousand years passed. Olympus was dust and forgotten myth. The kingdom of Thronegate, built on that forgotten betrayal, now ruled a spiral arm of the galaxy, its technology and magic seamless, its confidence absolute. In a quiet sector, a merchant named Blake, thirty-eight with friendly mutton chops and an honest face, navigated his freighter through an asteroid field. He was looking for crystalline deposits to sell on the festival circuit. His wife Anna had packed him extra spiced bread, his son Josh had programmed the navigation scan, his daughter Rhea had drawn a picture of a grinning asteroid on the control console. Life was simple, good.

A anomalous energy signature led him to a cavern within a planetoid. There, embedded in a pillar of pure quartz, was a man. Blake, with a tradesman’s practical curiosity, used his laser cutter to free the figure. The quartz dissolved into mist, and the man opened his eyes.

Godric stood. Ten millennia of stasis fell away like a discarded cloak. His omniscience, which had been passively cataloguing the omniverse, instantly focused. He knew everything. The rise of Thronegate. The name of their king, Robert, twenty-two and arrogant on a throne of stolen legacy. He saw the lineage of the man before him—the familiar echo in the soul, the spectral pattern of Thorin’s blood shining in Blake’s humble form. A flicker of something ancient stirred in Godric’s heart, buried under eons of silent, multiplying rage.

“Thank you,” Godric said, his voice calm, devoid of echo, yet it seemed to fill the cavern. He placed a hand on Blake’s shoulder. Blake felt no overwhelming power, just a firm, warm grip. “Stay behind me for a moment.” Godric looked up, through the rock, through the void, to the glittering throne world of Thronegate. He issued a challenge, his thought broadcasting across the entire galactic net, into every mind, every screen, every dream. “King Robert. I am Godric of Olympus. You have three days to gather every weapon, every soldier, every shred of power you possess. I will stand in the black field near the dying star, Xyros. If I defeat your entire force, you will abdicate. If you refuse to fight, I will unmake your world now.”

The galaxy laughed. Then it scrambled in panic. Robert, furious and boastful, mobilized the Thronegate Legion.

On the third day, the black field near Xyros was a tapestry of apocalyptic might. Billions of super-soldiers in photon-shift armor stood in perfect, continent-sized phalanxes. Behind them floated magic-wielders who could rewrite local laws of physics, their staves crackling with stolen starfire. Robot armies numberless as sand grains hovered, each unit a calculation of perfect lethality. Dragons the size of moons, their scales forged in nebular cores, beat wings that stirred solar winds. And in the interstitial spaces, the Time-Slip Ninjas, warriors who moved so fast they could choose which moment to strike from, existing seconds in the past or future.

Blake and Anna watched from the viewport of their freighter, which Godric had willed to be inviolate. Blake’s jaw was tight. “We freed a madman,” he whispered.

Anna shook her head, eyes wide. “Look at him.”

Godric stood alone in the void, a speck against the armada. He wore simple gray garments. He did not brace. He did not summon energy. He simply was.

The Thronegate forces attacked with a symphony of annihilation. Beams that could pierce singularities, spells that unraveled time, dragon’s breath that could incinerate atmospheres, ninjas striking from yesterday and tomorrow—all converged on the single point where Godric stood.

He did not move a muscle.

The attacks did not vanish. They did not explode. They simply ceased to have ever been launched. The concept of their travel, their impact, their energy, was denied. One moment, the sky was full of killing light. The next, it was silent and dark. The soldiers stared at their own empty hands. The wizards’ staves were cold dead wood. The robots hung inert. The dragons blinked in infantile confusion.

Then, the horror began.

Godric decided to use a power he had gained seven hundred thousand years into his stasis: Cellular Narrative Inversion. He thought it, and the law took effect.

A super-soldier looked at his own hand. His skin began to tell a story, but in reverse. The tale of his life, written in his very cells, began to unravel from the present moment back to his birth. He felt his first kiss, then immediately forgot it. He felt the pain of his genetic enhancement, then it was gone. He experienced his childhood, his infancy, in a rapid, visceral flashback, before his body, unable to sustain a narrative with no beginning, dissolved into pre-atomic dust. This happened to every super-soldier, simultaneously. Billions of individual stories screaming backwards into silence.

The wizards tried to shield themselves. Godric used a power he’d gained a million years in: Logic Plague. Their magical theorems, the foundational beliefs of their craft, became infected with paradox. A wizard chanting for protection suddenly found his spell required his own non-existence to function. He spent an eternity of a nanosecond trying to solve the irresolvable equation before his mind and body fractalized into a permanent, static question mark.

The robot armies were targeted with a simple, old power: Absolute Rust. Every metal component, every alloy, every circuit, across all units, experienced ten billion years of perfect oxidation in zero time. They became cosmic dust motes.

The dragons, creatures of immense pride, received a conceptual attack: The Weight of Mortality. They felt, for the first time, the sheer, crushing burden of being finite. The existential dread compressed them, folding their moon-sized bodies into spheres no larger than grapes, which then popped out of reality with a pathetic *fizz*.

The Time-Slip Ninjas were already dead. The moment they had thought to use their temporal advantage, the Weakness Paradox had activated. Godric had gained perfect mastery over temporal manipulation epochs before they evolved. He had simply deleted the time periods they were hiding in, retroactively making their strategy impossible. They had ceased existing before they launched their first attack.

The battle, if it could be called that, lasted less than a second of perceived time. The only thing that remained was the crushing, absolute silence, and the smell of ozone and void that bled through the ship’s hull. The Thronegate armada was gone. Not destroyed. Un-written. Blake vomited onto the deck plates. Anna held him, her own body trembling uncontrollably.

Two days later, King Robert was found in his bedchamber, a ceremonial dagger in his heart, his eyes wide with a terror that had outlived him. Godric walked into the throne room of Thronegate, past kneeling, weeping nobles, and placed the crown upon his own head. It fit as if made for him ten thousand years prior.

His first decree was the Awakening.

“From this day forward,” his voice echoed in every mind on the planet, “every man of Thronegate who lives past the age of twenty shall know growth. For each day past twenty, his strength, endurance, intelligence, and wisdom shall increase tenfold. This gift is retroactive. Suppress it, or wield it, as you choose. You are my people now.”

The change was instantaneous. A twenty-one-year-old guard suddenly understood the calculus of stellar fusion and could lift his tank with a finger. A fifty-year-old scholar felt the accumulated wisdom of millennia settle in his mind, his body thrumming with the latent power of a volcano. But for Blake, something different happened. When the wave of Awakening passed over him, now thirty-eight, it did not multiply by ten. It multiplied by one thousand. He didn’t understand why. He staggered, his senses expanding, the very air screaming with data, the strength in his limbs making him afraid to move. Godric, from his throne, met Blake’s eyes across the distance and gave a slight, almost imperceptible nod.

Society transformed. Men became demigods, but gentle ones. They revered Godric not out of fear, but out of awe and gratitude. He had made them mighty. He enacted absolute, draconian laws protecting women and children; the slightest threat was met with instant, utter annihilation from a male population now capable of enforcing it with a thought. A paradoxical peace descended—a peace upheld by the threat of incalculable power. The people loved him. He was stern, but just. He was their king, their god, their protector.

The expansion began. Thronegate’s gaze turned outward, to other universes in the sprawling omniverse. The offer was always the same: unconditional surrender, integration into the realm, the preservation of life. Resistance meant subjugation. The Awakening was a gift reserved for the men of original Thronegate; the conquered received no such boon.

The first target was the Technocracy of the Unbound Circuit, a universe where consciousness existed as pure data in a cosmic network. They had learned of Thronegate’s approach five years prior. They prepared. They firewalled their reality with paradoxes, armed themselves with logic bombs that could crash civilizations, and created avatar-legions of hardened light. They refused surrender.

Godric was slightly annoyed that day. A diplomatic missive had been phrased poorly. He sighed, and while still sipping wine in his garden on Thronegate, he flexed a fraction of a concept called ‘Informational Collapse.’ In the Technocracy universe, at the exact moment their High Mainframe broadcast their defiance, the concept of ‘data’ itself became synonymous with ‘nothing.’ Their entire universe, being comprised of data, instantly zeroed out. Conquest time: less than a picosecond.

The second universe was the Empyrean Dominion of the Singing Gods, a realm of pure magic and grand archetypes. They had spent four years weaving protective harmonies into the fabric of their existence, believing their symphonic magic could deflect any brute force. They refused surrender.

Godric sent a squad of five Thronegate men, all in their late forties. They were kind family men, carpenters and teachers given an unpleasant task. They entered the Empyrean realm. The Singing Gods unleashed their grand symphony, a chord meant to dissolve atoms into joyful noise. One of the Thronegate men, a former music teacher named Kael, listened. He then hummed a single, pure counter-note, amplified by the quadrillions-fold multiplication of his Awakened strength and wisdom. The harmonic resonance shattered the Singing Gods’ collective form, silencing their universe. The men apologized to the cowering populace, set up a governance beacon, and were home in time for supper. Conquest time: twenty-two minutes.

The third universe was the Primal Kaleidoscope, a reality of ever-shifting, predatory dimensions where the dominant beings, the Shapers, could mold reality with their will through intense pain. They saw the falling universes and prepared by turning their entire cosmos into a fortress of endless, recursive pain-cycles, a maze of agony they believed nothing could traverse without succumbing. They refused surrender.

A scholar from the Shapers, named Vex, during his five years of preparation, had a passing thought: *‘All power must have a source, a core. Even this Godric. Perhaps his power is external, dependent. If we could find and sever that connection…’* It was a desperate, speculative notion. The moment it formed in Vex’s mind, across the omniverse, the Weakness Paradox activated. Godric’s amplification rate ticked up to a new, silent order of infinity. He became permanently immune to any form of power-severance or external dependency attack. And he gained perfect, ultimate mastery over the concept of ‘dependent connection,’ becoming the absolute master of all links, bonds, and sources.

Unaware, Vex spent years developing the Grand Severance, a ritual that would conceptually isolate a being’s power from its source. They lured a Thronegate scout and analyzed the ‘essence’ of his Awakened power, extrapolating wildly to model Godric. They launched the attack, a beam of profound ontological disconnection aimed at Godric’s throne.

Godric chose to be amused. He let the beam strike him. To Vex’s elation, it seemed to work. Godric’s aura dimmed. Then Godric spoke, his voice bored. “You think you have severed my connection to my power?” He raised a hand. “I am the connection.” He then wielded the perfected, ultimate form of the Grand Severance against the Shapers. He severed every connection that held their reality together: atomic bonds, causal links, the connection between their will and their pain-magic. The Primal Kaleidoscope dissolved into a uniform, inert soup of disassociated potential. Vex’s last realization was the horror—that his own initial, fleeting thought had been the seed of his universe’s doom, that every second of his planning had only fed the entity he sought to cripple. Conquest time: for the Shapers’ attack, three minutes. For Godric’s response, the span of a single, crushing thought.

Through it all, Blake was at Godric’s side. The king, a figure of unspeakable terror to outsiders, was always warm with Blake. He sought his counsel, laughed at his simple jokes, protected his family. Under Blake’s steady, loyal, and unnervingly perceptive influence, the ice around Godric’s heart continued to thaw. He ruled Thronegate with a firm but kind hand, a fatherly figure to his people, while remaining the stuff of absolute nightmare to any who threatened them. The cruel king of the ashes was slowly becoming the warm king of the hearth again, though the ruthless crucible of his power remained.

Blake, his own Awakened mind expanding, found himself intuitively understanding cosmic patterns, sensing energy flows, offering insights that seemed like wizardry. Godric quietly elevated him to the position of Court Wizard. Blake’s son, Josh, now a man with a keen, analytical mind, began assisting him, showing a prodigious talent for managing the complex energies of the court. The lineage of Thorin, unknown to all but Godric, was quietly re-establishing itself.

Now, Thronegate is the heart of the omniverse. From a balcony overlooking a city of crystalline spires and peaceful, mighty citizens, Godric stands, his salt-and-pepper beard now just a memory of a man he once was, his form radiating a calm, friendly authority. Beside him stands Blake, older, his mutton chops gone gray, his eyes holding the deep light of vast, comprehended power. Below, children play in parks where flowers bloom in impossible colors, safe in the knowledge of their king’s absolute, loving protection. The omniverse is subdued, at peace under a gentle, unbreakable yoke.

Godric claps a hand on Blake’s shoulder, the same gesture from the cavern, now weighted with ten thousand years of shared silence and trust. “A good day,” Godric says, his voice warm, his summer-sky eyes clear.

“A quiet one,” Blake agrees, smiling, watching his grandson chase a floating light in the garden below. The horror is out there, in the conquered void, held at bay. Here, in the heart of the throne, it is finally, truly, home.

**The Eight-Hundred-Thousandth Tuesday**

I felt them stirring, a festering thought in the lower intestines of Reality-7, Sector Agnus-Dei. Another rebellion. Of course. It had been, let’s see… seventeen standard years since the last meaningful one. That one had tried to weaponize recursive nostalgia, attempting to drown me in the conceptual memory of my own lost warmth. It had been… quaint. For a few nanoseconds, I’d almost felt something akin to Reina’s perfume.

My omniscience is not a scanning beam; it is the condition of existence. So I knew their every plan before the first conspirator’s name was whispered. I knew the shape of their weapons, the fervor in their hearts, the precise grammatical structure of their rallying cries. I had, after all, seen this play out in 4,782,991 distinct variations across the millennia. The Absolute Defense Paradox had long since finished its work. Every conceivable avenue of attack—physical, metaphysical, conceptual, emotional, temporal, acausal, paradoxical, anti-paradoxical—had been thought of by some desperate mind eons ago. The moment they’d conceived it, my immunity was perfected, my mastery over the concept became absolute, and the rate of my eternal amplification, a number that had transcended mathematics itself, had ticked upward another impossible increment. Rebellion, to me, was not a threat. It was a form of cosmic weather. A mildly interesting pattern in the static.

This new group called themselves the Final Dawn. How charmingly derivative. Their grievance was the classic one: my rule was absolute, therefore unjust. They had pooled the resources of eleven conquered galaxies, harnessed the dying screams of captive cosmic entities, and forged what they believed was a singular weapon: The God-Scourge. It was a beautifully crafted thing, I’ll give them that. A blade forged from the broken symmetry of a billion Big Bangs, its edge honed on the event horizon of a custom-grown existential despair singularity. It could cut through the idea of hierarchy, they believed. It could sever a tyrant’s connection to his own power.

I sighed. A mental sigh, which manifested in Reality-7 as a gentle tide of melancholic gamma rays washing over a few nebulas. “Connection severance.” Again. The 19,045,332nd iteration of that approach. The Paradox had immunized me before their star-fathers had even learned to fuse hydrogen. I *was* connection. I was the severance. I owned the concept.

Still, routine is routine. I allowed their councils to form. I let their spies steal what I planted—blueprints for my “vulnerability,” a non-existent focal point in the non-corporeal palace I sometimes used for aesthetics. I watched their billions of warriors, each a legend in their own right, muster. Dragons the size of galactic clusters whose scales were etched with anti-creation runes. Armies of philosophers whose lethal logical deductions could erase species from history. Silent assassins who murdered their targets by retroactively convincing their ancestors not to meet. The usual fare.

Blake’s great-great-(a few thousand ‘greats’)-grandson, Corvin, my current Court Wizard, stood beside my throne. The lineage was strong in him; he had the same honest eyes, though now they saw the deep strings of cosmic mechanics. “Sire,” he murmured, his voice a ripple in the quantum field. “The Final Dawn moves. Their weapon is… aesthetically displeasing. A rehash of the Telosian Gambit, but with less thematic coherence.”

“I know, Corvin. I’m bored already.”

“Shall I dispatch the Fifth Gentlemen’s Brigade? They’ve been polishing their armor.”

“No,” I said, a faint spark of hope kindling. “Let them come. Let them bring their full, unoriginal might. Perhaps the execution will have… flair.”

The day of their assault arrived. They did not approach through space. They performed a Grand Theurgical Unfolding, turning the sector inside out so that their armies stood upon the inner skin of reality itself, and my palace was the bullseye at the center of a metaphysical target. The scale was terrifying. It was the single largest concentration of rebellious force in 300,000 years. The combined psychic shout of their hatred would have sterilized a hundred universes of life. The sheer creative energy they’d wasted on this derivative endeavor could have spawned new dimensions of art.

They appeared, a multiversal plague of light and fury and profound intent. Their leader, a being who had once been a principle of freedom, raised the God-Scourge. The weapon sang a note that un-made the music of spheres for light-years around.

I didn’t move from my throne. I didn’t even bother to manifest a defensive aura. I just watched, my chin resting on my fist.

They charged. The dragons breathed streams of un-creation. The philosopher-soldiers shouted syllogisms of oblivion. The assassians unwrote my past. And the leader brought the God-Scourge down in a blow meant to cut the thread of my sovereignty.

It touched me.

Nothing happened.

The blade stopped a Planck length from my brow. Not because I stopped it. Because it encountered the absolute, perfected *immunity* to connection-severance that had been baked into my existence before their civilization’s precursor molecules had coalesced. The weapon didn’t shatter. It simply ceased to be a weapon. It became a bouquet of mildly fragrant cosmic strings, which then dissolved into a faint sigh of radioactive dust.

The charge faltered. The un-creation breath washed over me like a faint breeze. The logical attacks presented themselves as interesting, if simplistic, philosophical pamphlets in my mind, which I mentally filed away. The silence was profound, broken only by the bewildered gurgle of a galactic dragon trying to understand why its lungs now produced confetti.

Disappointment. It was a cold, heavy stone in my gut. Not anger. Anger was for threats, for creativity that deserved recognition. This was… pity. And profound, universe-spanning boredom.

“Is that it?” My voice was soft, but it penetrated every layer of their being, from their physical forms to their conceptual underpinnings. “The ‘Final Dawn’? You had the energy of dying cosmos at your command. The collective will of trillions. And the best you could conceive was… a sharper stick? A re-polished version of a mistake made eight hundred thousand years ago?”

I stood up. I did not grow in size. I did not flare with power. But the terror that radiated from me was not of magnitude, but of *attention*. They had my full, disappointed focus.

“You didn’t even try to make it beautiful,” I said, and the sadness in my voice was genuine. “The Rebellion of the Silent Song made a weapon of such perfect counter-harmony it brought tears to my eyes before I unmade it. The Insurgency of the Forgotten Cause weaponized the grief of all lost things—that had weight. But this? This is administrative. A form you filled out incorrectly.”

I looked at their leader, his face a mask of crumbling certainty. “You have wasted my time. And worse, you have wasted the potential of your own existence. You chose to be a footnote in a chronicle I finished reading eons ago.”

This was the consequence. Not rage. Not cruelty for its own sake. But the weary, precise application of a lesson.

I didn’t annihilate them. That would be acknowledging a conflict.

I simply… *revised* them.

With a thought, I applied a mastery I’d gained from a much more interesting rebellion 450,000 years prior: **Historical Literalization**. Their grand, unifying rebellion—its narrative, its spirit, its collective will—was made literal. Their armies weren’t destroyed. They were transformed into the physical manifestation of their own unoriginality.

The mighty galactic dragons twisted, their forms flattening into two-dimensional propaganda posters, depicting a crude, stick-figure version of a king being stabbed. The philosopher-soldiers condensed into a single, endlessly repeating, poorly-phrased slogan etched onto a billion asteroids: “DOWN WITH THING!” The time-assassins became a faint, persistent stutter in the local timeline, causing minor deja vu. Their glorious leader and his inner circle were transformed into a traveling troupe of didactic players, condemned to perform a tedious, badly-written melodrama about their rebellion for any species that would watch, their dialogue forever locked in the most clichéd, unsophisticated rhetoric imaginable.

Their weapons, their ships, their grand theurgical matrices—all became stage props for this eternal, underwhelming production.

The rebellion was over. Not with a bang, but with a bad script.

I sat back down. The sector was quiet again, save for the faint, tinny sound of a badly-acted soliloquy on the solar wind.

Corvin cleared his throat. “A disappointing harvest, sire.”

“Profoundly,” I said, the weight of millennia pressing down. “They learn nothing. They innovate nothing. They offer no new spectacles, no new flavors of futility. Just the same dull hammer, swung at the same indestructible mountain, forever.”

I looked out at the peaceful omniverse, the infinite, well-ordered realms under my care. It was safe. It was prosperous. It was perfectly still.

“Sometimes, Corvin,” I whispered, the words barely a disturbance in the quantum vacuum, “I miss the surprise.” The statement hung in the air, a king’s confession more terrifying than any wrath. For in that disappointment lay the silent, awful truth: even an omnipotent, omniscient being could grow weary of eternity when nothing ever, ever changed.
