We had even recovered a ghost of the old magic, a rhythm from the court wizard of Olympus, Godric. A bitter pill to swallow, using the Tyrant’s own stolen history against him, but poetry in its justice. It would be our key, our surprise.

“He will not see this coming,” I declared, the belief a fire in my core. “His gaze is on thrones, on galaxies, on the macro-scale of domination. He overlooks the microbe of the heart. That is his weakness.”

The word hung in the air. *Weakness.* We all clutched it, a shard of ice in a burning hand. It became our creed.

The translocation was perfect. One moment, the sterile silence of our planning sanctum. The next, the shocking, oppressive grandeur of *his* throne room. Marble that seemed carved from frozen light. The air itself tasted of absolute authority. And there he was. On a throne of simple stone, looking… ordinary. And beside him, the mortal Blake, older now, in wizard’s robes. They were speaking of crop rotations.

*See?* I thought, a vicious triumph leaping within me. *He is blind to us. He is mired in the mundane.*

I did not shout. I acted. I gestured, and reality in the room *locked*. Chronal energy, cold and final, speared toward Blake. The Resonance Bomb detonated—a silent, psychic supernova aimed at the Tyrant’s soul. And I, with the totality of my being, fired *Ingratitude*. It left me not as light, but as an absence, a moving hole in meaning, aimed at the space between the two figures.

It all happened in a timeless slice of a second. Our masterstroke.

Blake blinked. He shivered slightly, as if from a draft. The Tyrant… did not move. The psychic wave washed over him and vanished, leaving nothing. My conceptual bullet, the work of centuries, the focus of my hatred, simply stopped. Halted in the air, as if curious.

Then Zeus looked up. Not in alarm. Not in anger. He looked at us with an expression that, at first, I could not comprehend. It was patience. The infinite, weary patience of a mountain watching a trickle of water attempt to erode its base.

He spoke. His voice didn’t enter my ears; it unfolded inside my mind, a verdict written on the fabric of my consciousness.

“This is the culmination of two thousand years of preparation? I am… underwhelmed.”

My light-form stuttered. A flaw in the projection? No. A flaw in *me*.

He spoke of seeing the first flicker. In my ancestor. He spoke of watching our schematics drawn, hearing our vows. The words were not boasts; they were simple, dreadful statements of fact, as unassailable as gravity. *“I allowed it all.”*

*Allowed.* The meaning of the word expanded, swallowing my triumph, my purpose, my very self. Our hidden dimensions were not hidden. Our secret meetings were not secret. Our stolen magic was not stolen—it was *permitted*. We were not insurgents in the shadows. We were exhibits in a zoo, believing the glass was sky.

Then he explained the defense. The Paradox. My mind, a thing of refined logic and fierce emotion, recoiled. It sought a flaw in his logic and found only the abyss.

*Weakness.* The concept we had built our entire existence upon. The foundational stone of the Unbound. He explained that the moment we believed it—1,842 years ago by his count—we had triggered the very mechanism that made him stronger. Permanently. That we had gifted him immunity to our greatest weapon. That we had spent millennia not preparing a strike, but perfecting his invulnerability in the exact direction we aimed to attack.

The horror was not a wave; it was a glaciation. It started at the core of my being and spread outwards, freezing every thought, every memory. I saw it all recontextualized. The late-night strategy sessions, the forging sacrifices, the hopeful tears of our recruits—not acts of defiance, but of unwitting worship. We were priests building a temple to his omnipotence, using the bricks of our own suffering. Our resolve was the devotion. Our sacrifice was the offering.

*“You are not conquerors. You are supplicants.”*

The truth was an annihilating force. My form, once bright with righteous fury, dimmed. I wasn’t being defeated. I was being *erased* by understanding. The legendary strength of Kaelen? The infinite calculations of the Algorithm? Mora’s time-unraveling songs? They were the twitching of specks of dust in a cathedral whose scale we had never perceived. Our lives had meaning only as a subtle, millennia-long drama performed for an audience of one, to stave off his boredom.

The existential dread was absolute. It was the loss of narrative. Our story was not one of resistance. It was a footnote in his eternal biography, a paragraph he allowed to be written because he found the font amusing. The freedom we fought for was a stage prop. The future we dreamed of was a set piece he could dismantle with a thought.

When he raised his hand, I felt no fear of obliteration. I felt a profound, cosmic shame. We were not worthy of being destroyed.

His sentence was crueler than any death. To be returned, alone, with this knowledge. To carry the hollow truth forever, while the capacity for the collective hope that had defined us was surgically removed from our souls. We would be solitary monuments to our own futility.

He snapped his fingers.

There was no transition. I was simply… elsewhere. Back in the grey, static ruins of my home universe, the place where the light once flowed freely. The silence was no longer cultivated. It was absolute. The fire of rebellion was gone, not extinguished, but revealed as a mirage. I remembered my comrades, Kaelen, the Algorithm, Mora, not with camaraderie, but with a crushing pity. We had shared a delusion.

I looked at the dead sky. The Tyrant’s final words echoed in the hollow vault of my being, the only truth left: *“They thought your presence was his weakness. They never understood it is his foundation.”*

We had based our grand rebellion on a fundamental, catastrophic error in perception. And the only thing more terrifying than his power was his kindness in allowing us to discover it, long after any chance of true understanding could bring anything but despair. We were not Unbound. We had never been. We were puppets who spent two thousand years painstakingly weaving our own strings, believing all the while we were cutting them.
