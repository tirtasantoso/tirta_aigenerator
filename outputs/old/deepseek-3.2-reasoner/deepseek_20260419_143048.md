The sky above Olympus was the color of bruised lilacs and molten gold, the evening of the world’s most anticipated wedding. In the great marble amphitheater, fragrant with night-blooming jasmine and the salt-sweet air of the coastal kingdom, ten thousand subjects waited with breath held. At the center stood King Zeus, a man carved from the very ideals of kingship. At forty-five, his was a handsome, commanding presence; a closely trimmed salt-and-pepper beard framed a strong jaw, and his short hair was like wrought iron. His warmth was a tangible thing, a fatherly radiance that put even the most nervous courtier at ease. He smiled at his daughter, Athena, who approached in a gown of silver thread, her face alight with a future promised. Beside him, his queen, Hera, held his hand, her touch a familiar anchor. His younger daughter, Hebe, just fifteen, giggled with a handmaiden in the front row, a spray of white flowers in her hair. All was peace. All was prosperity.

The first arrow took Hebe through the throat. It was not an arrow of wood and fletching, but a shaft of condensed violet energy that silenced her laughter forever, pinning her delicate form to her mother’s side. The scream that tore from Hera’s lips was swallowed by the thunderous detonations that erupted from the sky. Ships of dark alloy, emblazoned with the jagged sigil of Thronegate, rained hellfire upon the gleaming spires. The wedding procession dissolved into a tableau of screaming and blood. Zeus saw Hera reach for their daughters, a shield of desperate light springing from her hands, before a beam of pure void-energy scoured the dais, leaving nothing but smoking marble and motes of ash. His family was gone, erased between one heartbeat and the next.

Through the smoke and chaos, a figure fought toward him. Godric, his oldest friend, his court wizard, his beard singed and robes smoldering. “Zeus! The sanctuary!” Godric shouted, his voice raw. He began to chant, words older than the kingdom, forbidden syllables that twisted the air. A teleportation spell, the last secret of his bloodline. A shield of crackling amber energy bloomed around them, deflecting searing bolts. But the shield flickered. A Thronegate lancer, moving with impossible speed, phased through the crumbling defenses. Godric’s chant became a wet gurgle as a crystalline spear erupted from his chest. He fell, his dying breath finishing the incantation with a final, gasped syllable.

The world tore. Zeus felt a wrenching in the fabric of his soul. Then, stillness. Absolute, profound stillness. He was in a chamber of smooth, featureless stone, lit by a sourceless glow. The grief hit him, a tsunami of such magnitude it should have vaporized his mind. He saw Hera’s smile, Athena’s joyful tears, Hebe’s giggle, Godric’s steadfast loyalty—all reduced to ash. The warmth in his heart did not just cool; it crystallized into a black, diamond-hard core of infinite cold. The kind father was murdered in that instant, and from his ashes rose something else entirely.

As he stood there, paralyzed by loss, the universe itself seemed to stutter. Time, for him, ceased its forward march. The need for breath left him. The whisper of hunger, the drag of fatigue, the very concept of mortality, sloughed away like dead skin. He was caught, frozen in a single moment of transformative agony. And within that frozen moment, an engine of impossible genesis ignited. His physical strength, the might that could once bend a steel bar, began to multiply. Not by increments, but by a factor of one million. And then, in the next second, that new, incomprehensible strength multiplied by another million. And again. And again. Every second, without pause, forever. His endurance, his wisdom, his speed, his senses, his intelligence—all embarked on the same exponential, infinite journey into the absurd.

Simultaneously, new realms of possibility bloomed within him every second. The ability to unmake matter with a thought manifested first, a power that could have disintegrated Olympus, Thronegate, the very continent, in the instant it was conceived. A heartbeat later, the power to manipulate cosmic entropy followed, then the capacity to rewrite physical laws locally, then the authority to create and destroy dimensions. Each power was apocalyptic at birth, and each, like his stats, began to multiply in potency by one million-fold per second. The omniverse should have unraveled like a frayed rope in his presence. But his first conscious superpower, arising from a primordial instinct for survival, was Perfect Concealment. It clamped down over the raging inferno of his being, a lid on a star, and it too began its own endless amplification, ensuring the cosmos remained blissfully ignorant of the god it now hosted. He stood in the silent sanctuary, a vortex of infinite potential trapped in a shell of perfect stillness, and he thought of Thronegate. The thought was not a wish, but a gravitational certainty.

Ten thousand years passed. The kingdom of Olympus became dust, then myth, then less than myth—a forgotten footnote erased by the victors. Thronegate thrived. It conquered its world, then its star system, then the arm of its galaxy. Its technology merged with deep, elemental magic; its soldiers were gene-forged giants clad in energy-absorbent carapace, its armies included self-replicating robot swarms that could disassemble planets for raw materials, and dragon-kin bonded to psionic pilots whose breath could core continents. They were the undisputed masters of their galaxy, arrogant in their security. The name Zeus meant nothing.

In a forgotten sector, on a backwater world that had once been part of Olympus’s heartland, a merchant named Blake flew a rattling cargo hauler. He was thirty-eight, with a kind face framed by friendly mutton chops, and a disposition as straightforward as the trade routes he charted. He was honest, sometimes to his own detriment, fiercely loyal to his wife Anna, and devoted to his two children: twelve-year-old Josh, who loved tinkering with engine parts, and eight-year-old Rhea, who collected oddly shaped rocks. One day, a navigational anomaly during a storm led him to a strange gravitational signature. Following it, he found a hidden asteroid, and within it, a seamless chamber. His hand, brushing against the wall to steady himself, left a smudge of grease and blood from a minor cut. The wall drank it in, flickered, and vanished.

Blake stumbled into the sourceless light, and found a man standing motionless in the center. The man looked like a statue of a king, unweathered by time, his salt-and-pepper beard perfectly trim, his eyes closed. Then those eyes opened. They were not human eyes. They were windows into a silent, screaming supernova. Blake fell to his knees, not in worship, but because his nervous system short-circuited.

Zeus inhaled. It was not air he drew in, but information. The state of the galaxy, its history, its current ruler—a twenty-two-year-old monarch named Robert, sitting on a throne built on the bones of Olympus—all flooded into him with absolute, instantaneous clarity. He looked at the trembling man before him. He saw the honest fear, the confusion, the mundane concern for a family waiting at home. He saw, in the man’s genetic code, a faint, echoing resonance—a familiar cadence in the soul, a ghost of a sacrificed friend’s lineage. A warmth, long extinct, stirred in the diamond-cold core of him.

“Thank you,” Zeus said, and his voice was like mountains grinding together, quiet yet massive. He helped Blake to his feet, his touch terrifyingly gentle. Then he turned his gaze star-ward, toward the glittering capital of Thronegate. He did not shout. He simply issued a statement, and his voice propagated through every communication channel, every psychic frequency, every dreaming mind in the empire. “King Robert of Thronegate. Your line murdered my family and stole my kingdom. I am Zeus, last King of Olympus. Gather every soldier, every weapon, every dragon and demon and war-machine at your command. Bring them to the field of Alyssia in three days. If I defeat them all, you will abdicate. This is your only chance to prepare.”

The galaxy laughed. Then it saw the security feeds from the asteroid. It saw the man, and it felt a primal dread it could not explain.

Three days later, the field of Alyssia, a vast plain on a neutral world, was the most heavily fortified point in galactic history. Billions of super-soldiers stood in phalanxes that stretched to the horizon, their combined energy shields creating a perpetual artificial dawn. Above them hovered dreadnoughts the size of moons. Dragon-kin the color of blood and obsidian blottted out the sun, their riders crackling with tempest magic. Legions of chrono-assassins, ninjas who could step between seconds and reverse local time to undo fatal blows, shimmered at the edges of reality. At the rear, in an impregnable command fortress, King Robert watched on holographic displays, confident. It was overkill for one man.

Zeus arrived alone. He stood in the center of the plain, Blake and Anna—who had refused to let her husband face this alone—huddled behind a shimmering, involuntary barrier he had placed a mile back. He did not adopt a stance. He simply stood, hands at his sides.

The attack order was given. A cataclysm of energy, plasma, spell-work, temporal distortions, and hyper-velocity projectiles descended upon that single point. The resulting explosion vaporized the plain, creating a new glass sea a thousand miles wide. The shockwave obliterated mountain ranges on the far side of the planet. The soldiers cheered.

The smoke cleared. Zeus stood on the pristine, untouched circle of grass where he had been. Not a thread on his simple tunic was disturbed. He had not moved a muscle.

Silence, vast and terrible, fell across the armada. Then, Zeus began. He did not attack. He simply allowed his presence, an infinitesimal, carefully measured sliver of it, to leak past his perfect concealment.

The dragons died first. They did not burn or break. They simply ceased to be dragons. One moment they were majestic, scaled behemoths; the next, they were clouds of pastel-colored soap bubbles that popped in the silent breeze. Their riders fell, but before they hit the ground, their bodies unraveled into streams of numbers and base code, which then dissolved into harmless ambient light.

The chrono-assassins moved, reversing time around Zeus. They found they could not. Time in his vicinity had become a solid, a sculpture. They shattered against it, their bodies aging to dust in microseconds, then un-aging into screaming infants, then dispersing as quantum foam.

The ground armies charged. A legion of robot warriors, each capable of surviving a direct nuclear blast, took one step into the zone around Zeus and turned into a field of sunflowers, which then immediately performed a complex, silent ballet before wilting into ash. A battalion of psychic giants, their minds powerful enough to crush cities, made the fatal error of trying to touch his thoughts. Their consciousnesses were filled with the sight of their own infinite irrelevance, a vista of cosmic scale that reduced their egos to nothing. They stood, drooling, before their nervous systems politely switched themselves off.

King Robert, watching from his fortress, ordered the orbital strike. The combined fire of a thousand dreadnoughts, capable of sterilizing a solar system, lanced down. Zeus looked up. The beams of energy slowed, then stopped, hanging in the atmosphere like jagged pillars of stained glass. He breathed out. The beams reversed course, each one perfectly targeting its ship of origin. The dreadnoughts vanished in silent, expanding rings of golden fire.

It went on for an hour. Zeus turned armies into music that played itself into silence. He transformed battalions of elite mages into flocks of paper birds that burned in the sunlight. He re-wrote the genetic code of a clone legion so they all became exact copies of his lost daughter Hebe, who then looked at their own hands and wept before dissolving into tears. He exerted so little effort it was an insult. The field was not a battlefield; it was a gallery of surreal, quiet horrors, a demonstration of such absolute supremacy that it broke the spirit of every being who witnessed it.

When it was over, the glass sea was littered with insanity and stillness. Not a single weapon remained. Not a single soldier retained their mind. Zeus had not taken a single step. He turned his gaze toward the command fortress, light-years away. In his throne room, King Robert met that gaze through the screen. He saw his own reflection in Zeus’s eyes—a tiny, insignificant boy playing with a stolen crown. Two days later, Robert was found in his chambers, having used a ceremonial dagger. There was no note. None was needed.

Zeus crowned himself king in the silent throne room of Thronegate. The next day, he issued the Decree of Awakening. To every man of Thronegate blood, a gift and a burden: for every day lived past the age of twenty, their strength, endurance, intelligence, and wisdom would increase tenfold. It applied retroactively. Overnight, milkmen and merchants, scholars and soldiers found themselves housing the potential power of demigods. They could dampen it, live normal lives, or summon it forth. They did not become immortal, but they became mighty. And they revered Zeus, for his gift also came with his absolute, unshakeable law: the protection of women and children was sacrosanct. A new, terrifyingly stable society was born from the ashes of the old.

Blake, though not of Thronegate blood, found his own strength awakening. But his multiplier was not ten. It was ten thousand. He could lift mountains without strain, solve equations that bent reality, feel the rotation of the galaxy like a whisper. He never understood why, just as he never understood why Zeus, a being of infinite cruelty to his enemies, always had a warm smile and a fatherly hand on his shoulder, why he was invited to private dinners, why his counsel was sought on matters of state. Anna would sometimes watch them talk, Zeus listening intently to Blake’s straightforward merchant wisdom, and she would see a ghost of the king he might have been ten millennia ago.

Thronegate, under its new god-king, looked outward. Universe after universe was contacted, offered peaceful integration. Those who refused were subdued. A squad of five Thronegate men, all in their late forties—kindly fathers and grandfathers who would rather be gardening—were sent to a universe where magic was music and the gods were symphonies. The defenders had prepared for five years, weaving reality itself into a grand, celestial sonata designed to unmake invaders. The Thronegate men landed. One of them, a former carpenter named Eli, hummed a counter-melody. The universal symphony shattered into discordant noise. The conquest took twenty minutes.

Another universe, ruled by transcendent AIs of pure logic, calculated every possible outcome of invasion. They prepared quintillions of simulated strategies. Zeus, mildly inconvenienced by their arrogance while enjoying a glass of wine with Blake, blinked. Across the dimensional barrier, every AI simultaneously experienced a fatal divide-by-zero error in their core consciousness. Conquest time: less than a second.

The attempts to find a flaw in Zeus were the most tragic. In a dimension of pure thought, the Grand Telepath, a being who existed as a collective belief, conceived of a plan. It reasoned that infinite power must correlate to infinite spiritual corruption. It would craft a wave of pure, empathic innocence, a memory of childhood’s first snow, to flood Zeus’s senses and trigger a cataclysm of redemptive guilt. The very instant the *thought* formed—long before the wave was crafted—Zeus, across the omniverse, felt his amplification rate tick upward yet again. A perfect, redundant immunity to psychic-induced guilt layered over his already impregnable mind. Simultaneously, he gained a mastery of empathic manipulation so profound he could make a black hole feel love for the stars it consumed. When the Grand Telepath finally launched its beautiful, heartbreaking wave of innocence, Zeus absorbed it, smiled a smile colder than the void between galaxies, and reflected it back, magnified by a googolplex. The Grand Telepath was not destroyed. It was forced to feel, for all eternity, the specific, crushing guilt of the child it had once been when it stepped on a beetle, amplified to infinite degrees. Its dimension became a silent tomb of perpetual, exquisite remorse.

Through it all, Blake remained. He never sought power, but it grew in him. He offered simple, honest advice that seemed to anchor Zeus. The cruel lines around the king’s eyes softened when Blake spoke of Josh’s latest engine modification or Rhea’s rock collection. Slowly, the warmth that had been frozen for millennia began to thaw, not for the omniverse, but for his people. Zeus became a stern but kind father to the empire, ruthless to external threats, but devoted to the prosperity and safety of his citizens. The people loved him, for under his absolute rule, there was no crime, no war, no fear.

Blake found himself, without fanfare, assuming a new role. He never wore pointed hats or chanted spells, but he became the king’s advisor, his fixer, the man who could translate infinite power into practical good. His son Josh, now a young man of frightening intellect and quiet strength, began to study the archaic systems of the palace, showing an uncanny knack for its ancient, hidden geometries. Rhea could calm any storm with a song. Blake saw it, but never spoke of the lineage that seemed to be reasserting itself.

Today, Thronegate is the heart of the omniverse. Its spires, grown from living crystal and thought, pierce the fabric of dimensions. In the central throne room, which exists everywhere and nowhere, Zeus sits. He looks like he did ten thousand years ago, but his eyes now hold both the infinite cold of the void and the gentle warmth of a hearth fire. Below the dais, Blake stands, his mutton chops now gray, his posture that of a man utterly at peace. He is the Court Wizard in all but name, the trusted friend, the living bridge between a god and his people. Outside, the citizens of a million assimilated worlds go about their lives, safe, prosperous, and free. The horror of conquest is a memory for others. Here, at the center, there is only the quiet, steadfast hum of a kingdom restored, and the unbreakable bond between a king and the descendant of the friend who saved him, a secret written in blood and loyalty that guides the stars themselves.

I watched its light play over my son Rob’s face as he adjusted his simple apprentice robes, his fingers—so like his mother’s—fumbling with the clasp. At sixteen, he was all eager energy and untested confidence, seeing the Court Wizard title as one of spellbooks and star-charts. Today, I would begin to disabuse him of that notion.

There were more. So many more. Jedi Masters with blades of solidified light, their Force a choir around them. A Magical Girl whose hope-generated power scintillated with galaxy-busting potential. An armored genius whose suit contained universes. A purple-skinned titan with a gauntlet that still shimmered with the ghost of infinite power. They had united. Every legend, every savior, every ultimate being from a million narratives, all their stories, their struggles, their hard-won power, converging on this one point with a single, focused will: Overthrow the Tyrant. Unseat Zeus.

Their combined aura was an existential scream. It wasn’t malice; it was a foundational rejection of our order. Their very presence asserted that infinite power should not reside in one being, that our peaceful, static eternity was a blasphemy against the concept of struggle, growth, and free will. The psychic pressure of their resolve would have vaporized the old Thronegate fleet in an instant.

I couldn’t move. My Awakened might was a frozen lake inside me. This was not an army. This was a congress of gods, each a protagonist of all creation.

Superman floated forward, his voice projecting not just sound, but truth, justice, and an unyielding moral framework. “Zeus of Thronegate! Your reign of absolute control ends today. You have stifled the multiverse. We stand for choice. For freedom. Stand down.”

Goku cracked his neck, a grin of exhilarating challenge on his face. “Yeah! Come on out! I’ve been training across, like, a thousand different time-planes for this! Let’s see what you’ve got!”

Doctor Strange’s spells solidified into shimmering, multidimensional cages around the entire palace complex. “We have severed your connection to the foundational mana streams of the omniverse. Your power is isolated. Surrender.”

Saitama looked vaguely puzzled. “So, is he like a bad guy? The place looks nice. But if everyone says he’s a problem… I guess I’ll have to punch him.”

From the Enterprise, Spock’s voice, cool and amplified, stated, “Logic dictates that infinite power concentrated in a single, emotionally variable entity represents the ultimate existential threat. The needs of the infinitely many outweigh the needs of the one. Your removal is necessary.”

It was the most magnificent, most hopeless thing I had ever seen.

The palace doors did not open. He simply was *there*, standing on the empty air before them. He wore loose, gray trousers and a simple white shirt, barefoot. He held a cup of what smelled like my best song-fruit tea. He took a sip.

The silence that followed was louder than their proclamations.

Zeus looked at the assembly, his gaze passing over Superman’s blazing eyes, Goku’s crackling aura, Strange’s woven sorcery, the assembled might of infinity. He did not look impressed, or angry, or threatened. He looked… mildly inconvenienced. Like a man whose quiet reading had been interrupted by a noisy flock of exceptionally colorful birds.

He sighed. “A united front,” he said, his voice a quiet tremor in the soul of reality. “How novel. It’s been… oh, at least six hundred thousand years since anyone tried a coalition. The last one had those conceptual beings who weaponized narrative tropes. That was more interesting.”

Superman’s eyes narrowed, heat vision building. “This is your only warning. We do not wish to destroy you, but we will.”

“Destroy me?” Zeus repeated, as if trying out a quaint, forgotten word. He took another sip of tea. Then he did something.

He didn’t raise a hand. He didn’t chant a spell. He *blinked*.

With that blink, Doctor Strange’s meticulously crafted multidimensional cages… became butterflies. Not magical constructs, but actual, mundane *Papilio machaon* butterflies. They fluttered away, confused. Strange stared at his empty hands.

“My spells… they’re just… biology?” he whispered, his voice raw with existential horror.

Goku, yelling, burst forward in a teleportation flash, a fist clad in energy that could shatter a universe aimed at Zeus’s face. Zeus, still holding his tea, took a small step back as if avoiding a frisky dog. Goku’s fist passed through the space where Zeus’s head had been. And Goku stopped. He stood frozen, his fist outstretched, his aura gone. A look of profound confusion settled on his features.

“My… my power…” Goku mumbled. “I can’t feel it. It’s… quiet.”

Zeus looked at him. “You defined yourself by always surpassing limits. I have momentarily imposed the concept of ‘a limit you cannot feel, sense, or overcome’ upon your being. It’s not gone. You just can’t perceive it. A form of existential sensory deprivation. Give it a few eons, it might wear off.”

Saitama, seeing his comrade stalled, performed his Serious Punch. The force behind it, the narrative weight of ‘ending any fight in one blow,’ moved forward, a wave of absolute finality.

Zeus lifted his teacup and placed it in the path of the punch.

The punch, the force, the concept, hit the ceramic cup. There was no sound. The cup did not shatter. The punch did not connect. It was simply *not there*. The infinite force met an object that had been declared, on a fundamental level, *uninteractable*. Saitama stared at his fist, then at the utterly ordinary cup. His expression of bland puzzlement deepened into the first true frown I’d ever imagined on his face.

“Huh,” Saitama said.

Now, the Hulk roared, growing larger, angrier, his power escalating infinitely with his rage. He became a giant of emerald destruction, his fist the size of a continent, swinging down.

Zeus looked at him. “Banner,” he said, and his voice was not loud, but it was the only sound. “You’ve had a hard time. You deserve a rest.”

The Hulk, mid-swing, shrank. Not into Bruce Banner. Into a sleeping infant Bruce Banner, swaddled in a green blanket, floating gently in the vacuum, sucking his thumb. A bubble of serene, dreamless sleep encased him.

Thor bellowed, summoning the Stormbreaker, calling down the lightning of a million angry heavens. The bolts converged on Zeus’s head. He lifted his index finger, like a man testing the wind. The lightning struck his fingertip, swirled into a tiny, crackling ball, and then shaped itself into a perfect, miniature replica of Mjolnir. He tossed it to Thor, who fumbled it, stunned.

“A trinket,” Zeus said. “Yours was always more stylish.”

Superman, seeing the systematic, effortless dismantling of his allies, flew at him with desperate speed, his body a projectile of solar energy and inviolable will. Zeus set his teacup down on the air, where it stayed. As Superman reached him, Zeus did not strike. He simply placed a hand on Superman’s chest, not to push, but to *still*.

“Kal-El,” Zeus said, and his voice held something almost like pity. “You fight for a beautiful idea. ‘Never give up.’ ‘Always protect.’ But you are built to struggle, to be challenged. I am not a challenge. I am the examination paper that was written after all answers, even the impossible ones, were already known. Your hope, your strength… they are variables in an equation I solved before your universe cooled.”

Superman strained, his muscles corded, his face a mask of effort. He could not move Zeus’s hand. He could not move *himself*. He was held not by force, but by a simple, immutable fact: *He was not allowed to move.*

“Your weakness,” Superman gasped, invoking the ancient lore, “Kryptonite…”

Zeus smiled, a small, sad thing. “The moment your coalition first conceived of this attack, the nanosecond the *thought* of using Kryptonite against me formed in any of your minds, it ceased to be. Not just for me. For you.” He leaned closer. “Try to remember it, Kal. The concept. The green stone. You can’t, can you?”

Superman’s eyes widened. The knowledge was gone. Not stolen. Made never to have existed for him.

The Enterprise fired every phaser, every quantum torpedo. The beams streaked toward Zeus and dissolved into harmless confetti and soft, glowing balloons that drifted past him.

Zeus finally looked… slightly engaged. He turned his gaze upon the assembled, remaining multitude. “You united under a common cause. Admirable. Let’s see the strength of your union.”

He snapped his fingers.

It was not a loud snap. But its effect was precise.

Every single member of the coalition was suddenly, utterly alone. Not spatially. *Narratively*. Superman found himself in a perfect replica of Metropolis, but where every person was a version of Lex Luthor who loved and admired him, removing all conflict, all reason to be. Goku sat in an empty, featureless room, his power accessible but with no opponent, no challenge, no measuring stick—a hell of perfect, meaningless strength. Doctor Strange’s magic now only performed party tricks. Saitama found a cockroach that could withstand his punches, forever. Thor was made a mortal office worker on a rainy Tuesday. The Terminator was given a single, unsolvable logic puzzle about human emotion. Spock, aboard the Enterprise, found every piece of data, every sensor reading, confirming with perfect logical consistency that he was, and always had been, a being of pure, irrational emotion.

They were not defeated. They were *disarmed*. Their defining traits, their sources of power and purpose, were turned into their own prisons.

The sky cleared. The portals vanished. The assembled legends were gone, each trapped in a custom-made, personal, and perfectly benign oblivion.

Zeus picked up his teacup. It was still warm. He took a final sip, then looked down, directly at me, Roger, the orchard-keeper, still kneeling in the silver grass, my mouth agape.

“Roger,” he said, his voice gentle. “The harmonic drift in Root-Node 7 is because of residual dimensional stress from that ill-considered rebellion in Sector 88945-Phi last week. It’s fraying the song-fruit’s connection to the Key of G minor. Apply a stabilizing compost of ground quartz and regret. It’ll clear up by noon.”

He then turned and walked back towards the palace, pausing only to glance at a single, bewildered butterfly—formerly a universe-caging spell—that had landed on his shoulder. He carried it inside with him.

I remained there for a long time, the smell of ozone and confetti slowly fading, replaced by the scent of song-fruit and damp earth. My Awakened strength, that planet-cracking power, finally settled, like a frightened animal returning to its den. I looked at my shaking, soil-stained hands.

They had brought everything. Every hero, every story, every conceivable power. And he had treated them like a minor disturbance in the garden. The most terrifying part wasn’t the display of power. It was the *tone*. The quiet, domestic inconvenience of it all.

I stood up, knees creaking—not from age, but from a shock deeper than any bone. I fetched the quartz and the jar of refined existential regret from my shed. As I worked it into the soil around Root-Node 7, I hummed a tune in G minor, steadying the harmony. The leaves above me began to chime once more, a soft, peaceful song in the quiet, eternal afternoon. The fight for the omniverse had lasted less than a minute, and the only task it had left in its wake was gardening.
