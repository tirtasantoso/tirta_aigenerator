The salt wind carried the scent of jasmine and sea-foam through the marble arches of Olympus as King Godric adjusted the ceremonial clasp at his shoulder, the golden sunburst catching the afternoon light. At forty-five, he was the image of benevolent majesty—his muscular frame draped in white and gold, his short-cropped hair the color of dark iron dusted with frost, his beard trimmed to a perfect point that accentuated the strong line of his jaw. Women sighed when he passed, men straightened their spines, and children ran to him without fear. He was the kind of king who remembered every servant’s name and knelt to speak to orphans at eye level. His daughter Isabel, twenty-two and radiant in her wedding gown of woven starlight, laughed as her father took her hands in his.

“You’re nervous,” Godric said, his voice a warm rumble. “I can feel your pulse through your gloves.”

“I’m about to marry the man I love, Father,” Isabel replied, her eyes bright with unshed tears. “Of course I’m nervous. What if I trip?”

“Then you trip. And you get up. And you laugh. And you live.” He pressed his forehead to hers. “That’s what marriage is. That’s what life is. Falling and rising together.”

Behind them, Queen Reina adjusted Hebe’s flower crown—the fifteen-year-old fidgeting with excitement, her hair a cascade of chestnut curls. Godric’s wife caught his gaze and smiled, and in that smile was twenty years of shared mornings, whispered conspiracies, the quiet miracle of watching their children bloom. The courtyard of Olympus blazed with color. Banners of sapphire and silver snapped in the breeze. Musicians played strings of spun light. The throne room, open to the sky, held three thousand guests—nobles from allied kingdoms, ambassadors from distant lands, farmers who had walked three days just to glimpse their princess’s happiness.

Thorin, the court wizard, stood beside the altar, his long grey robes embroidered with constellations that actually moved. His family had served Olympus for thirty generations, and his loyalty was carved into his bones. He caught Godric’s eye and winked, raising a crystal vial of ceremonial oil.

“Ready when you are, my king.”

The ceremony began. Godric took his place at the altar, his voice carrying across the silent crowd as he spoke of love, of duty, of the sacred covenant between two souls. Hebe sniffled into her mother’s shoulder. Reina’s hand found Godric’s and squeezed. The sun hung perfect and golden at its zenith, and for one suspended moment, the world was exactly as it should be.

Then the sky turned black.

Not with clouds. With arrows. Thousands upon thousands of arrows, their tips coated in a venom that hissed as it cut the air. They came from beyond the eastern wall, from siege towers that had been assembled in silence under cover of an illusion spell, from trebuchets that had been wheeled into position while Olympus celebrated. The first volley struck the crowd before anyone could scream. Godric saw a young knight take an arrow through the eye, the shaft emerging from the back of his skull, and then the screaming began.

“THORIN!” Godric’s roar tore his throat raw.

The wizard’s hands were already moving, tracing sigils of protection in the air, but the enchantment had been broken—somewhere, somehow, the ancient wards around Olympus had been bypassed. Thorin’s face went white.

“They had help,” he gasped. “The wards—they were unraveled from the inside. Someone—someone we trusted—”

The gates exploded inward. Through the splintered wood poured soldiers of Thronegate, their armor black as polished obsidian, their helms shaped into leering skulls. At their head rode King Aldric of Thronegate, a man Godric had called ally, had broken bread with, had saved from assassination ten years prior. Aldric’s sword was already wet.

“FOR THRONEGATE!” the invaders roared.

Godric grabbed Isabel and shoved her behind him. “Reina! Hebe! To the sanctuary, NOW!”

Reina scooped up their youngest daughter, but Hebe twisted free, reaching for her sister. The arrow took her in the throat. She made a sound—a small, wet gasp—and collapsed with her flower crown rolling across the blood-slicked marble.

Godric’s world stopped.

He saw Reina drop to her knees beside Hebe, her scream a raw animal thing. He saw Isabel running toward her mother, her wedding train dragging through crimson. He saw the Thronegate cavalry thundering through the courtyard, trampling the wounded, cutting down the fleeing. He saw Aldric’s sword swing in a lazy arc and take Reina’s head from her shoulders. He saw the blade reverse direction and bury itself in Isabel’s chest, the princess staring at her father with confusion as she fell.

Three heartbeats. Three deaths.

Godric’s own heart kept beating, which felt like a betrayal.

Thorin was beside him, blood streaming from a gash across his scalp, his hands shaking as he grabbed Godric’s arm. “I can send you away. There’s a spell—a forbidden teleportation—only my bloodline knows it. It will take you somewhere safe, but I need time—”

“I’m not leaving,” Godric snarled, reaching for his sword.

“You’re not dying here!” Thorin’s voice cracked. “Your people will need you. Someday. Please, Godric. Let me do this one thing.”

The wizard began the incantation. His voice rose above the carnage, speaking words that made the air shimmer and warp. The ground beneath them began to glow with a sickly blue light. Godric felt reality twisting around him, felt the fabric of space tearing to pull him elsewhere.

And then Aldric’s spear took Thorin through the back.

The wizard arched, the spell shattering mid-word. Half-formed energy detonated in a wave of blue fire that threw Godric across the courtyard. He crashed through a marble pillar, his ribs cracking, and landed in the ruined sanctuary beneath the throne room—a chamber that had not been opened in a thousand years. The incomplete teleportation latched onto him anyway, the broken magic desperate to complete its function. It dragged him down into the vault, sealed the entrance, and wrapped him in a stasis so absolute that time became meaningless.

But the spell was fractured. And fractures create consequences.

In that dark sanctuary, as Godric’s consciousness dimmed, something fundamental changed. The broken magic, desperate to preserve its target, reached into the raw fabric of existence and began to rewrite the rules of reality as they applied to him. Aging stopped. His heart stilled. His lungs ceased to draw breath. His need for sleep, for sustenance, for the very air that had sustained him since birth—all of it vanished in a single, silent instant.

And then the multiplication began.

One thousand times his strength. One thousand times his endurance. One thousand times every stat, every capacity, every metric of his being. And then that new value multiplied by itself. And then again. Every Planck second, the smallest conceivable unit of time, his power doubled in a way that defied mathematics. Exponential growth so extreme that after a single second, the numbers had ceased to have meaning. After a minute, they had exceeded the total particles in the known universe. After an hour, they had transcended the concept of measurement entirely.

But that was only the beginning.

Every second, a new superpower crystallized in his soul. The first was the ability to contain himself—a perfect, immutable seal on his aura that prevented his sheer presence from annihilating reality. It was necessary. Without it, the moment his power reached its second Planck-second of multiplication, the omniverse would have collapsed into a singularity of impossible energy. This containment power multiplied at the same rate as everything else, growing alongside his strength, ensuring that no matter how vast his might became, he would remain hidden, stable, contained.

The second power allowed him to perceive everything. Omniscience flooded into him like a river of fire. He saw every atom in the sanctuary. He saw the ruins of Olympus above, the Thronegate soldiers looting his palace, Aldric crowning himself on a throne of blood. He saw every star in every galaxy, every dimension folded between the cracks of reality, every thought that had ever been thought and every thought that would ever be thought. He saw the weight of existence, and it did not crush him, because he had become infinite.

The third power was the Paradox.

It manifested as a whisper in the fabric of causality, a defensive mechanism so absolute that it bordered on malicious. Godric possessed no weaknesses. This was not a boast. It was a fundamental truth of his existence, woven into his being by the broken spell. But the omniverse was vast, and sentient beings were arrogant. The moment any entity anywhere in existence even entertained the notion that Godric had a weakness—the moment the thought formed, before any plan was conceived, before any exploit was considered—the Paradox activated.

The rate of his amplification increased. Permanently. The entity’s false assumption became fuel. And the specific attack that the entity would eventually devise? Godric became immune to it before the thought fully crystallized. He gained perfect mastery over that attack—the ability to wield it with any magnitude, against anyone, at any time. And he gained the power to deny its existence to anyone else, to make the concept of that attack simply cease to function for anyone but himself.

The trap was perfect. The arrogance of existence would forever feed him.

Godric did not move. He did not breathe. He did not dream. He simply lay in the darkness, multiplying, growing, becoming the single most terrifying entity that had ever existed or would ever exist, while the world above him forgot his name.

Ten thousand years passed.

Thronegate evolved. It spread across the galaxy, its technology advancing to levels that would have seemed like sorcery to their ancestors. Their armies were legends. Their wizards could bend time. Their scientists had unlocked the secrets of dark matter, of quantum entanglement, of the fundamental threads that held reality together. They had conquered every civilization in the Milky Way, absorbed them, and molded them into a single, monolithic empire.

And they had forgotten Olympus entirely. The kingdom was a footnote in dusty archives, a myth told to children, a story that scholars debated as metaphor. Aldric’s original betrayal had been polished into glorious conquest. The name Godric meant nothing.

Blake Harper did not care about any of this. He was thirty-eight years old, a merchant of moderate success, with a mutton chop beard that his wife Anna said made him look like a dock worker and a temper that flared as quickly as it cooled. He was honest to a fault, loyal beyond reason, and cursed with a curiosity that had nearly gotten him killed a dozen times. His son Josh was twelve, already taller than his mother, already asking questions Blake could not answer. His daughter Rhea was eight, with her mother’s eyes and her father’s stubbornness.

His ship, the *Kindling Heart*, was a small freighter modified for long-haul cargo runs. On this particular run, he was carrying archaeological equipment to a backwater system on the edge of Thronegate space—a region so remote that the empire hadn’t bothered to fully colonize it. The pay was good. The job was simple. Drop the crates, collect the fee, return home in time for Rhea’s birthday.

But the archaeological site was not what it seemed.

Blake’s landing gear crunched against ancient stone. The ruins stretched across a barren valley, their architecture unfamiliar, their carvings worn smooth by millennia of wind. The research team was supposed to meet him here, but the site was empty. No ships. No tents. No signs of life.

He checked his communicator. Static.

“Typical,” he muttered, stepping out onto the cracked surface. The air was thin but breathable. The sky was a bruised purple, the system’s dying sun casting long shadows across the ruins. He decided to stretch his legs while he waited, wandering deeper into the complex.

That was when he found the sanctuary.

It was hidden beneath the ruins, accessible only through a collapsed passage that his curiosity—and his damned curiosity—compelled him to clear. The stairs descended into perfect darkness. His flashlight cut a beam through the black, illuminating walls covered in symbols that seemed to shift when he wasn’t looking directly at them.

At the bottom, in a chamber carved from obsidian, lay a body.

The man was enormous, his frame still muscular even in deathlike stillness, his beard as silver as starlight, his face frozen in an expression of profound grief. He wore armor that predated the Thronegate empire by ten thousand years, its design utterly alien to Blake’s eyes. He was not breathing. He had no pulse. He was cold to the touch.

And then his eyes opened.

Blake screamed. He scrambled backward, dropping his flashlight, which clattered and spun, casting wild shadows across the walls. The man sat up slowly, deliberately, as if relearning how to move his own limbs. His gaze swept the chamber, taking in everything, understanding everything, his omniscience flooding back into focus after ten thousand years of dormancy.

“You,” the man said, his voice a low rumble that vibrated in Blake’s chest. “You are Thorin’s blood.”

Blake stared at him, heart hammering. “Who—what—?”

But the man did not answer. He rose to his feet, his armor shifting with a sound like grinding stone, and walked past Blake as if he were not there. He ascended the stairs, emerged into the dying light of the alien sun, and stood silently, surveying the ruins of what had once been his kingdom.

Godric remembered everything. The arrows. The screams. The flower crown rolling across marble. He remembered Aldric’s betrayal, the death of his family, the fall of his people. He remembered Thorin’s sacrifice and the ten thousand years of unending multiplication that had turned him into something beyond comprehension.

And he remembered that Thronegate still existed.

He turned to Blake, who had followed him up, trembling, his mutton chop beard quivering. “What year is it?”

Blake told him. Godric assimilated the information, compared it to his omniscient knowledge of the current state of the galaxy, and nodded.

“I have a task for you,” Godric said. “You will take me to Thronegate Prime.”

Blake’s mouth opened and closed. “I—I have a family. I have a cargo to deliver. I can’t just—”

“Your family will be safe,” Godric said, and the certainty in his voice brooked no argument. “I will ensure it. And your cargo has already been delivered. I have seen to it.”

Blake wanted to argue. He wanted to run. But something in the ancient king’s eyes—something broken and vast and terrible—compelled him to obey. He led Godric back to the *Kindling Heart*, and as they lifted off, Blake’s communicator buzzed. The research team had arrived. His cargo was delivered. His payment was processed.

Godric had told the truth.

“How did you do that?” Blake whispered.

Godric looked at him, and for just a moment, the warmth that had once defined him flickered in his gaze. “I am not what I was, Blake Harper. But I am what I had to become. There is a difference. I hope you will help me remember it.”

Three days later, Godric stood before the throne of Thronegate Prime.

The palace was a monument to excess—spires of crystal, walls of liquid light, gardens that floated in defiance of gravity. The current king, Robert, was twenty-two years old, handsome in the way of inherited power, surrounded by advisors who whispered poison into his ears. He had been raised on stories of Thronegate’s invincibility, on the absolute superiority of their empire, on the myth that they had never truly been challenged.

The challenge came in the form of a single message, delivered by Godric himself, walking through the palace defenses as if they did not exist.

*King Robert. Abdicate your throne. I will face your entire army in three days. If I win, you surrender. If I lose, I die. These are your only options.*

Robert laughed. His advisors laughed. The entire court laughed. They had billions of super-soldiers, warriors trained from birth in combat that transcended mortal understanding. They had wizards who could unravel causality, robots that could reconstruct themselves from a single atom, dragons bred to level continents. They had ninjas so fast that they could reverse time, assassins who could kill a target before the target’s mother was born.

They had three days to assemble.

Godric spent those three days on a hill overlooking the capital, sitting cross-legged, his eyes closed, his face serene. Blake sat beside him, terrified, confused, and inexplicably loyal.

“Why are you doing this?” Blake asked on the second night. “You could destroy them all without moving. I saw what happened when that scout tried to shoot you. He didn’t even fire. He just—stopped. His heart stopped.”

“I am giving them a choice,” Godric said, without opening his eyes. “It is more than Aldric gave my family.”

“They’re not going to take it.”

“I know.”

The morning of the third day dawned bright and terrible. The Thronegate army assembled on the plains before the capital, a force so vast that it blotted out the horizon. Soldiers in powered armor that could survive a supernova wielding weapons that fired condensed singularities. Wizards floating on platforms of pure energy, their hands already crackling with apocalyptic spellcraft. Dragons the size of mountains, their scales gleaming like polished obsidian, their breath capable of melting continents. Robots so numerous that they formed a silver tide. Ninjas so fast that they existed in multiple moments simultaneously, their blades sharp enough to cut concepts.

Godric walked down the hill alone.

Blake watched from the summit, his hands shaking, his stomach churning. Anna was beside him—she had arrived the night before, summoned by Godric’s silent command, her confusion turning to horror when she saw the army.

“He’s going to die,” she whispered.

Blake shook his head. “No. No, he’s not.”

Godric stopped a hundred yards from the front line. He did not draw a weapon. He did not assume a combat stance. He simply stood there, his arms at his sides, his expression calm.

“This is your final chance,” he said, his voice carrying across the entire plain, reaching every ear, every mind. “Surrender. Lay down your arms. You will be treated fairly. I have no quarrel with the people of Thronegate, only with the legacy of their tyrant king.”

The commander of the Thronegate army laughed. It was a sound of pure, unshakable confidence. “You’re one man. One old man. Do you know what we have here? We have the might of a thousand conquered civilizations. We have weapons that would make your gods weep. We have w—”

Godric did not move a single muscle.

And the first billion soldiers died.

Not with violence. Not with fire or lightning or any visible force. They simply ceased to function. Their armor went dark. Their weapons powered down. Their hearts stopped beating. Their brains stopped firing. They collapsed in perfect unison, a wave of falling metal and flesh that swept across the battlefield in less time than it took to blink. The dragons screamed and dropped from the sky, their corpses cratering the earth. The robots shut down, their lights extinguishing. The ninjas—fast enough to reverse time—did not even have time to process what was happening before they joined the dead.

The wizards managed to cast one spell.

It was a reality deletion, a targeted annihilation that should have erased Godric from existence across all timelines, all dimensions, all possible pasts and futures. The spell completed. The magic took hold. And Godric stood there, unharmed, looking slightly bored.

The amplification had made him immune to reality deletion before the spell had even been conceptualized. He now possessed perfect mastery of reality deletion himself. He could wield it with the ease of breathing. And he could deny its existence to anyone else.

The wizards realized this in the fraction of a second before they died. The realization was the last thing they ever experienced.

The entire engagement lasted two seconds.

The Thronegate army, the most powerful military force in the galaxy, had been annihilated without Godric moving a single muscle. He had not blinked. He had not twitched. He had simply willed it, and they had ended.

Blake threw up.

Anna clung to him, weeping, her eyes locked on the figure standing alone in a sea of corpses. Godric turned and walked back up the hill, his steps measured, his face unreadable.

“It is done,” he said. “King Robert will be found dead in his chambers by morning. I will crown myself ruler of Thronegate. And then, Blake Harper, I will begin the work of redemption.”

Blake wiped his mouth, his hands trembling. “Redemption? You just killed billions of people.”

“I gave them a choice,” Godric replied, and for just a moment, the grief in his voice was so profound that Blake felt it like a physical weight. “They chose war. I will not apologize for surviving, Blake. I made that mistake once. I will not make it again.”

The next day, as predicted, King Robert was found in his private quarters, a dagger through his heart, a note on his desk that read simply: *I should have surrendered.*

Godric crowned himself in the Great Hall, the crown of Thronegate settling onto his brow like a dead weight. The surviving citizens—those who had not been part of the army—watched in terrified silence. They expected a monster. They expected slaughter and oppression and endless cruelty.

Instead, Godric made his first proclamation.

“The Awakening begins today.”

He explained it simply. Every man in Thronegate who had lived past the age of twenty would receive a gift. Every day they lived beyond that threshold, their strength, endurance, intelligence, and wisdom would increase tenfold. This applied retroactively—a man of forty would receive the compounding effect of twenty years of daily multiplication, making him powerful beyond mortal comprehension. A man of sixty would be a demigod. A man of eighty, a force of nature.

But they remained mortal. They would still die at the end of their natural lifespan. No immortality. No endless rule. Just power, earned through years, wielded with responsibility.

And Godric’s first law was absolute: women would be protected. Any man who raised a hand against a woman would face Godric’s personal judgment. Any man who abused his power would lose it. The Awakening was a gift, but it was also a test.

The people of Thronegate, who had expected a tyrant, found themselves under a king who walked among them, who listened to their complaints, who knelt to speak to children at eye level. It was disorienting. It was terrifying. And slowly, impossibly, it was beloved.

Blake received a modified gift. He did not know why. Godric did not explain. But when the Awakening touched him, the increase was not tenfold per day. It was a thousandfold. Every day past twenty, his power multiplied by a thousand, compounding endlessly. He would live to eighty-eight, and by the time he died, he would be one of the most powerful beings in existence—second only to Godric himself, whose power had long since transcended any scale of measurement.

“Why me?” Blake asked one evening, standing on the balcony of the palace, watching the twin moons of Thronegate rise.

Godric stood beside him, his hands resting on the railing. “Because you have a good heart, Blake Harper. And because you remind me of someone I lost.”

He did not explain further. He did not mention Thorin. He did not mention the bloodline that had served Olympus for thirty generations, the same blood that ran through Blake’s veins, the same loyalty that had survived ten thousand years of separation. He simply stood in silence, grateful for the company of a man who did not fear him.

And Blake, for his part, did not push. He had learned that Godric would share what he wanted to share, when he wanted to share it. And despite everything—despite the billions dead, despite the terror of that day on the plains—Blake found himself trusting this broken, ancient king.

The expansion began within a year.

Godric had no desire to rule a single galaxy. He had the power to claim everything, and more importantly, he had the responsibility. The omniverse was vast, filled with civilizations that preyed on the weak, with tyrants who enslaved their people, with horrors that lurked between dimensions. He would offer them mercy. He would offer them surrender. And if they refused, he would show them what true power looked like.

The first universe to face Thronegate’s expansion was a realm of crystalline entities, beings of pure thought who had transcended physical form. They existed in a state of perfect harmony, their society a symphony of shared consciousness. They had never known war. They had never known fear.

When the Thronegate envoy arrived—five men, all older than thirty-five, all carrying the Awakening’s weight—they offered the crystal beings a choice. Join Thronegate. Accept Godric’s protection. Live in peace.

The crystal beings refused. They could not comprehend the concept of surrender. They saw the men as primitive, flawed, limited by their physical forms. They attempted to absorb the men into their collective consciousness, to show them the error of their ways.

The five men looked at each other, sighed, and got to work.

The conquest lasted eighteen minutes.

The crystal beings had defenses that should have been impenetrable. They existed partly outside of time, their consciousness distributed across multiple dimensions. They could rewrite reality with a thought. They had prepared for five years—ever since they first detected the ripples of Godric’s awakening—building countermeasures, theoretical shields, weapons designed to destroy the concept of physical form.

None of it mattered.

The Thronegate men were kind. They did not enjoy what they had to do. But they were loyal to their king, and their king had given an order. They moved through the crystal consciousness like surgeons excising a tumor, efficient and precise. They did not kill. They simply ended. The collective consciousness was unraveled, thread by thread, until nothing remained but silence.

The entire universe was pacified in under half an hour. The crystal beings were not granted the Awakening. They were assimilated into Thronegate’s dominion, their worlds administered by Godric’s appointed governors, their surviving remnants left to rebuild under new rule.

The second universe was a nightmare made flesh. It was ruled by entities called the Feasters—beings of pure hunger that consumed realities as sustenance. They had devoured countless civilizations, growing fat on suffering, their power increasing with every world they destroyed. They were the apex predators of their cosmos, unchallenged for millions of years.

Godric himself was mildly inconvenienced when he learned of them.

He had been enjoying a quiet evening with Blake and his family—Josh was now fourteen, training to become a wizard under Godric’s sporadic tutelage; Rhea was ten, already displaying a sharp intellect that reminded Godric painfully of Hebe—when his omniscience alerted him to the Feasters’ existence. They were targeting a universe adjacent to Thronegate’s dominion, preparing to consume it.

Godric found this irritating.

He did not go to the Feasters’ universe. He did not need to. He simply reached out with his power, which had been multiplying for ten thousand years without pause, and—

The Feasters ceased to exist.

Not just them. Their entire universe. Every atom, every dimension, every timeline, every echo of possibility. Godric erased it all with the equivalent of a mental sigh. The universe was gone, replaced by a void that had never known anything else. The conquest was complete in a fraction of a second, executed without effort, without malice, without even a change in Godric’s expression as he continued his conversation with Josh about the fundamentals of dimensional folding.

Blake noticed. He always noticed. But he had learned not to comment.

The third universe was the most advanced. They were a Type-V civilization on the Kardashev scale, beings who had transcended the need for physical form, who lived as pure energy in realms beyond space and time. They had observed Godric’s rise. They had seen the pattern of his expansion. They had prepared for five years, constructing weapons that targeted the fundamental axioms of reality, defenses that operated in conceptual space, strategies designed to exploit the one perceived weakness they believed every being possessed.

They did not understand the Paradox.

Their lead strategist, a being of such intellectual magnitude that it could hold entire universes in its mind simultaneously, formed the first thought: *This Godric, this king of Thronegate, must have a weakness. No being can be truly invincible. There must be a flaw in his power, a crack in his armor, a failure point we can exploit.*

That thought was the beginning of the end.

The moment the strategist conceived of the weakness, the Paradox activated. Godric’s amplification rate increased. Permanently. The strategist’s next thought—*Perhaps his weakness is his attachment to the merchant, Blake*—became fuel. Godric gained immunity to any attack targeting his attachment to Blake. He gained perfect mastery over the concept of exploiting emotional bonds. And he gained the power to deny that concept to anyone else.

The strategist spent the next five years refining a plan to use Blake against Godric. They developed psychological weapons, reality-warping traps, contingencies that spanned infinite timelines. They were brilliant. They were thorough. They were utterly doomed.

When the Thronegate envoy arrived—five men, again, all older than forty—the Type-V civilization launched their attack. It was the most sophisticated assault in the history of existence, a multi-dimensional, multi-temporal, multi-conceptual strike designed to exploit the one weakness they believed they had identified.

The attack failed before it began.

Godric had been immune to it for five years, ever since the strategist’s first thought. He had perfect mastery of every aspect of the attack. He could wield it himself, against anyone, at any time. And he had denied the concept of the attack to his enemies, rendering their entire war effort pointless.

The Type-V beings realized their error in the moment of their defeat. They understood, with horrifying clarity, that the very thought of Godric having a weakness had doomed them. They had fed his power. They had granted him immunity. They had made themselves vulnerable.

The conquest lasted four minutes. The five Thronegate men, who had not even needed to fight, looked at each other with weary resignation and began the work of assimilation.

Forty-five minutes after Godric issued his first offer of surrender to the entire omniverse, he had received responses from one hundred and thirty-seven universes. One hundred and thirty-five had accepted. Two had refused and been subjugated. The rest of the omniverse, watching the pattern, made their decisions quickly.

Within a year, every universe had been offered the choice. Within two years, every universe had either joined Thronegate or been pacified. Godric ruled everything that existed, everything that had ever existed, and everything that could ever exist.

And he was still bored.

It was Blake who saved him.

Not through power. Not through strategy. Through simple, stubborn humanity. Blake treated Godric like a person. He told him when he was being unreasonable. He dragged him to family dinners, forced him to play games with Rhea, made him listen to Josh’s endlessly detailed explanations of magical theory. He introduced Godric to Anna’s cooking, to the simple pleasure of a good ale, to the joy of sitting in comfortable silence and watching the stars.

Slowly, impossibly, Godric changed.

It was not a dramatic transformation. There was no single moment of redemption, no tearful confession, no cathartic release. It was a thousand small moments, a million tiny acts of kindness, a billion shared breaths. The warmth that had defined him before the Fall of Olympus began to return. The fatherly gentleness, the genuine kindness, the capacity for joy—they had been buried under ten thousand years of grief and rage, but they had not been destroyed.

He still had no mercy for his enemies. He was still ruthless, cruel when necessary, capable of apocalyptic destruction without a second thought. But that was a tool now, not his entire identity. He was a king again, in the truest sense of the word.

And Blake Harper, the merchant who had stumbled into a ruined sanctuary and awakened a god, became his court wizard.

It was a natural progression. Josh had shown an aptitude for magic that bordered on genius, and Godric had taken him as an apprentice. The boy learned quickly, mastering arts that had been forgotten for millennia. Rhea, too, showed promise—she had her father’s stubbornness and her mother’s patience, a combination that made her formidable even at twelve.

Blake himself had grown into his power. The thousandfold Awakening had transformed him into a being of immense capability. He could reshape reality with a gesture, traverse dimensions with a thought, perceive the threads of fate that connected all things. But he used his power sparingly, preferring to solve problems with words rather than force, with kindness rather than strength.

“You remind me of Thorin,” Godric said one evening, as they stood on the balcony of the palace, watching the twin moons rise. It was the first time he had spoken the wizard’s name aloud in ten thousand years.

“Who was he?” Blake asked.

Godric was silent for a long moment. “He was my best friend. He was the most loyal man I ever knew. And he died saving me, using a spell that only his bloodline could cast.” He turned to look at Blake, his ancient eyes soft with an emotion that had been dormant for millennia. “You have his eyes. And his heart.”

# The Weight of a Joke

I remember the exact moment my husband died.

No, that's not right. Rogr did not die. But the man who came home from the audience with King Godric was not the man I had married. Something had been hollowed out of him, replaced with a void that breathed and wept and stared at walls for hours. The physicians said it was shock. The priests said it was a spiritual wound. The wizards said his mind had been touched by something too vast to contain.

They were all wrong.

The truth was far simpler and far more terrible: Rogr had seen the King's displeasure. Not even directed at him. Just present. Just existing in the same room for a fraction of a heartbeat. And that fraction had carved itself into his soul like a brand.

Ten years.

Ten years I had watched him deteriorate. Ten years I had tried every remedy, every therapy, every prayer to every god in every universe under Godric's dominion. And every single attempt had failed. Not just failed—collapsed. Exploded. Turned to ash in my hands as if the universe itself rejected the very notion that Rogr could be healed.

I remember the therapy chamber I built. It cost our entire savings, a device designed by the finest minds in twelve galaxies, meant to gently recalibrate the traumatized neural pathways. I powered it on. It hummed. It glowed with soft, healing light.

And then it screamed.

Not a mechanical scream. A living scream, as if the machine had suddenly become aware of what it was being asked to do and chose to destroy itself rather than proceed. It melted into a pool of molten metal and crystal, and the pool itself trembled, as if terrified.

I tried magical remediation. I found a sorceress who specialized in mending fractured souls. She performed the ritual, her voice rising in incantation, her hands tracing patterns of light around Rogr's trembling form.

She lasted six seconds before she began to weep. Not tears of failure. Tears of abject terror. She looked at me with eyes that had seen the edge of something she could not describe, and she whispered, "I cannot. The King's shadow is upon him. To touch it is to touch the sun."

She fled. I never saw her again.

I tried the simplest things. Herbal teas. Meditation. Long walks in the garden. Rogr would sit beside me, his hand in mine, and I would talk to him about our son Pete, about the weather, about the beautiful order that King Godric had brought to our lives. And Rogr would nod, and squeeze my hand, and I would see the terror flickering behind his eyes like a candle in a hurricane.

He never blamed the King. That was the strangest part. He loved Godric. We all loved Godric. The King's rule was perfect, his justice absolute, his kindness legendary. He had ended wars that had raged for millennia. He had fed the starving, cured the incurable, brought peace to a hundred thousand worlds. Rogr would speak of him with reverence, even as he shook.

But his body remembered what his mind could not forgive.

The nights were the worst. He would wake screaming, clutching at his chest, babbling about a pressure that had no weight, a cold that had no temperature, a presence that filled every corner of existence and found him wanting. I would hold him, rock him, whisper that he was safe. And slowly, the screams would subside into sobs, and the sobs into silence.

I loved him. I love him still. That love was the only thing that kept me sane.

And I loved King Godric. I loved him for the peace he brought, for the safety he guaranteed, for the smile he had given Pete when Pete had presented him with a drawing at the annual festival. The King had knelt—actually knelt—to accept a child's crayon scribble, and he had thanked Pete as if it were the most precious gift he had ever received. Pete had glowed for weeks.

How could I reconcile these two loves? How could I worship the hand that had cradled my son, when that same hand had, in a moment of casual displeasure, shattered my husband's mind?

I could not. So I simply existed in the contradiction, day after day, year after year.

Pete grew. He was twelve now, tall and strong, with his father's kind eyes and my stubborn jaw. He idolized the King. He wanted to join the Thronegate service when he came of age, to serve the man who had brought order to the omniverse. I did not have the heart to tell him what the King had done to his father. I did not have the words.

And then, on a quiet evening in the tenth year of our suffering, a merchant came to our door.

He was not a merchant. I knew that immediately. He wore fine robes, but he carried himself like a man who had seen too much. His mutton chop beard was streaked with grey, and his eyes held a sorrow that seemed ancient. He introduced himself as Blake Harper, Court Wizard to King Godric.

I almost shut the door.

"I know what you're thinking," he said, before I could. "You're thinking that I represent everything that broke your husband. And you're right. I do. But I also represent the only chance he has to be whole again."

I let him in.

Rogr was in his chair by the window, staring at the sunset. He did not turn when Blake entered. Pete was at his homework, a data slate glowing in his lap. He looked up with curiosity, then went back to his studies. He had learned not to hope.

Blake sat across from me in the kitchen. He did not make small talk. He simply told me the truth.

"Ten years ago, I made a joke to the King. It was an offhand remark, nothing serious. I said he should play the tyrant for the delegates from the newly subjugated universes. They expected a monster, so why not give them a show? It was meant to be a prank. A bit of fun between friends."

He paused, his hands gripping the edge of the table.

"I did not understand what I was asking. I knew the King was powerful, but I did not understand the scale. When he—" Blake swallowed. "When he pretended to be displeased, it was the mildest expression of annoyance he could manage. A fraction of a fraction of a fraction of what he truly feels. He thought he was holding back. He genuinely believed that no mortal would be harmed by such a tiny, insignificant flicker of displeasure."

He looked at me, and his eyes were wet.

"He underestimated himself. I underestimated him. And your husband was in the room."

I sat in silence. The kettle whistled. I poured tea with hands that did not shake, because I had no tears left to shed.

"Why are you telling me this now?" I asked.

"Because the King mentioned Rogr today. Casually. As if it were a trivial matter. He said, 'That delegate from the third subjugation—Rogr, was it? He seemed a bit shaken. I hope he recovered.' And I realized that the King had no idea. He had completely forgotten the incident. To him, it was a moment. To your husband, it was ten years of hell."

Blake's voice cracked.

"I asked the King if he knew what had happened. He said yes, of course—he has absolute omniscience, he has always known. But he did not interfere because he genuinely believed Rogr could 'tough it out.' He has faith in mortal resilience. He thought the effect was negligible. He was wrong."

I set down my tea. "So you came to apologize."

"I came to ask your permission to cure him."

The words hung in the air. Cure him. A single sentence that contained ten years of failed attempts, exploding machines, weeping sorceresses. A single sentence that promised what I had despaired of ever achieving.

"How?" I whispered.

"By asking the King to do it."

I laughed. It was not a happy sound. "You want me to trust the man who broke him to fix him?"

"I want you to trust the man who loves you both, despite his mistakes," Blake said. "Godric is not cruel, Rosa. He is not a tyrant. He is a father who lost his family and spent ten thousand years becoming something he never wanted to be. He is learning to be human again. And he made a mistake. A terrible mistake. But he can undo it. Completely. Instantly."

I looked at Rogr. He was still staring at the sunset. But his hand had moved to his chest, pressing against the spot where the terror lived. He did that a lot.

"Let me talk to him," I said. "And to Pete."

The family deliberation was not long. Rogr could barely form sentences about the event—he would start shaking, his eyes would go distant, and he would retreat into himself. But I held his hand, and I spoke for him. I told Pete what the King had done. I watched my son's face cycle through disbelief, anger, confusion, and finally, a heartbreaking acceptance.

"Can the King really fix him?" Pete asked.

"Yes," I said. "Blake believes so."

"Then we should let him."

Simple. Children understand mercy better than adults.

I told Blake our decision. He nodded, relief flooding his features, and opened his mouth to say something—

And Godric was there.

Not a dramatic entrance. Not a flash of light or a crack of thunder. He simply existed in the room, as if he had always been there, as if the space itself had been waiting for him to fill it. He was tall, silver-bearded, his eyes ancient and warm and terrible all at once. He wore simple clothes, not royal robes. He looked like a grandfather who had wandered in from the garden.

He did not move. He did not gesture. He did not speak a word of magic.

He simply decided that Rogr should be healthy.

I saw it happen. Rogr's shoulders straightened. The tremor in his hands ceased. The haunted look in his eyes—the look that had been there for ten years, etched into his very soul—simply vanished. He blinked. He looked around the room as if seeing it for the first time. He took a deep breath, the first deep breath I had heard him take in a decade, and he smiled.

Rogr smiled.

It was a real smile. Full and free and unburdened. He turned to me, and his eyes were clear, and he said my name. "Rosa."

I fell into his arms. We held each other and wept, and I felt his strength, his warmth, his solid presence. He was back. He was whole.

But even as joy flooded through me, even as gratitude overwhelmed my senses, a cold thread of dread coiled in my chest.

It was so easy.

Ten years. Ten thousand sleepless nights. Ten thousand failed cures. Ten thousand prayers to gods who could not hear because the King's shadow covered everything. And he had fixed it in an instant. Without effort. Without even visible effort. He had simply willed it, and it was done.

What did that make me? What did that make my long suffering, my desperate attempts, my shattered hopes? They were nothing. Less than nothing. The universe had been waiting for a single thought from the King, and my husband's suffering had been nothing but a pause button waiting to be unpressed.

I looked at Godric. He was watching me with kind eyes.

"I apologize for my oversight," he said. His voice was gentle, fatherly. "I did not understand the depth of the harm I caused. I was blind to my own scale. It will not happen again."

He paused.

"I offer you full compensation for your troubles. Whatever you desire. A new home. Wealth beyond measure. A position of honor. Name it, and it is yours."

I wanted to say yes. I wanted to demand something—anything—that would make the ten years mean something. But the dread in my chest held my tongue.

"If I accept," I said slowly, "will it make this moment less real? Will it mean that my pain was worth something?"

Godric's expression did not change, but something flickered in his eyes. Understanding, perhaps. Or sadness.

"No," he said. "Nothing can make pain meaningful. It simply is. You endured it. You survived. That is its own reward."

I shook my head. "I cannot accept. Not because I do not want compensation, but because... I am afraid. Afraid that if I take anything from you, I will be acknowledging that the last ten years were a transaction. And they were not. They were love. They were love for my husband, and nothing you can give me will make that love any greater or any less."

Godric inclined his head. "A wise answer. And a true one."

He turned to leave. At the door, he paused.

"Your husband is healthier now than he has ever been. I removed not only the trauma, but all the accumulated stress damage it caused. He will live a full, long life. And he will never remember the fear, only the moment of release."

Then he was gone.

Rogr hugged me again. Pete hugged his father. We laughed and cried and held each other, and the room was filled with a joy I had thought we would never feel again.

But that cold thread of dread remained.

Because I had seen it. I had seen the effortless nature of his power. I had seen the gap between his reality and mine, a gap so vast that my entire life's struggle had been, to him, a trifle that he could have fixed at any moment. He simply hadn't bothered.

And yet I loved him.

I loved him for curing Rogr. I loved him for the peace he had brought. I loved him for kneeling to my son. I loved him despite the horror, because of the horror, in a way that made no sense and yet was as real as the air I breathed.

That is the cognitive dissonance I will carry for the rest of my life. That is the scar that will never heal, even as my husband's mind is whole.

That evening, as the sun set on our small home, I watched Rogr and Pete play chess in the garden. Rogr was laughing. Actually laughing. Pete was grinning, delighted to have his father back. The scene was idyllic.

I sat on the porch, my tea growing cold in my hands, and I thought about the King. I thought about his gentle voice, his apologetic eyes, his instant, effortless cure. I thought about the ten years of pain that he could have erased with a thought, and chose not to, because he believed in mortal resilience.

And I realized that I would never understand him. I would never understand his scale, his perspective, his definition of mercy. But I understood his love. I had seen it in the way he looked at Blake. I had seen it in the way he knelt to Pete. I had seen it in the way he apologized, genuinely, for a mistake he had not even realized he was making.

He was not a monster. He was a man who had become a god, and was still learning what that meant.

I could accept that. I could love him despite it. I could even be grateful.

But the dread would stay. It would always stay.

Far away, in the palace of Thronegate Prime, Blake Harper stood on the balcony, staring at the twin moons. Godric stood beside him, his arms crossed, his expression thoughtful.

"That was a good thing you did," Godric said.

"It was a thing that should have been done years ago," Blake replied. "And it was your fault it wasn't."

"I know."

"Don't do it again."

Godric was silent for a moment. Then, a small smile crept across his face.

"Speaking of pranks," he said.

Blake turned to stare at him. "No."

"I was thinking something small. Inconsequential. A little misunderstanding with the ambassador from the Temporal Concord. I could pretend to be mildly confused about time zones. It would be hilarious."

"No."

"They would panic for maybe two seconds before I clarified. It's harmless."

"You said that last time."

"I underestimated. This time, I will hold back more. Much more. I have calibrated my understanding of mortal fragility."

Blake pinched the bridge of his nose. "You are going to give me an ulcer."

"Probably. But think of the story we'll tell."

They stood in silence, the weight of the omniverse resting easy on their shoulders. And somewhere, deep in the heart of a newly healed family, a woman felt a sudden chill, and didn't know why.

She held her husband closer, and tried to forget the sound of a god's displeasure.

She almost succeeded.
