Ten thousand years ago, the kingdom of Olympus was a jewel of the age, its spires of white stone catching the dawn light like spears of fire. King Zeus, at forty-five, was the heart of its warmth. He was a man carved from mountain stone, broad-shouldered and strong, his hair short and his beard a neat mix of dark and silver that only accentuated his handsome, fatherly face. On the day his eldest daughter, Athena, was to be wed, the air in the great courtyard was thick with the scent of blooming laurel and honeyed wine. Zeus moved through the crowd, clasping forearms, laughing, his deep voice a steady drum of comfort. He beamed at Athena, twenty-two and radiant in her silks, and at his younger daughter, Hebe, fifteen and giggling as she arranged flowers. Queen Hera stood beside him, her hand in his, a picture of serene joy. It was the last moment of peace the world would know.

The first fire-arrow struck the ceremonial canopy just as Zeus raised his hands to begin the blessing. The scream that followed was not from the flames, but from the walls as they shattered inward. Thronegate, the neighboring kingdom long envious of Olympus’s fertile valleys, had come not to raid, but to erase. The air dissolved into a storm of splintered stone, magical detonations, and the wet, terrible sounds of metal finding flesh. Zeus saw Hera fall, a spear of black iron piercing her chest before she could even turn. He saw Hebe crushed beneath a falling gargoyle. He saw Athena, her wedding dress aflame, reach for him before a Thronegate knight’s sword took her head in a single, casual swing. The world narrowed to a tunnel of red haze and screaming silence. Zeus fought, his own strength legendary, breaking men and monsters alike with his bare hands, but they were a flood, and he was a single rock. His best friend, the court wizard Godric, found him kneeling amidst the corpses of his family, his kingly robes sodden with their blood. Godric’s face was pale with terror and determination. “The line must not end!” he shouted over the din, his hands already weaving a pattern of light that hurt the air to behold. It was a forbidden spell, known only to the blood of his family, a teleportation incantation of last resort. As Godric chanted, a vortex of distorted space began to tear open behind Zeus. A Thronegate archer, high on a crumbling wall, loosed an arrow. It took Godric through the throat, his final syllable a wet gurgle. The spell, unleashed but incomplete, collapsed upon itself and then exploded outward.

Zeus did not feel the translocation. One moment he was in the hell of his courtyard, the next he was in a cool, dark silence. It was a hidden sanctuary, a vault of unknown making. The grief was a physical weight, a black sun in his chest, and as it consumed him, the botched magic of his dying friend began to interact with the raw, screaming injustice of his soul. He fell to his knees, and a profound stillness seized him. His tears froze on his cheeks. His breath halted in his lungs. His heart ceased its beat. He entered a stasis, but his mind did not sleep. It was trapped, aware, in that single, eternal moment of loss. And in that moment, something broke—or perhaps something was unlocked.

Deep within his frozen form, an engine of impossible genesis ignited. His strength, the mortal might that had once felled champions, began to multiply. Not by doubles, but by millions. And then that new, astronomical sum multiplied by itself. And then again. And again. It did this once every second. His endurance, his speed, his senses, his intellect—all followed, each parameter feeding into a recursive, exponential spiral that had no ceiling and acknowledged no law. Simultaneously, new capacities bloomed into being within him, one every heartbeat of a clock that no longer ticked for him. The first was the power to unmake a star by will alone. The second was the power to hear the thoughts of every being in a galaxy. The third was the power to dissolve time in a localized field. Each new power was apocalyptic at its birth, and each, like his stats, then began its own million-fold self-multiplication every second thereafter. The omniverse should have shuddered and died in that instant, a candle snuffed by his mere existence. But his fourth power was perfect, absolute concealment. It masked his aura, his destructive resonance, folding the infinite potential for annihilation into a flawless, internal singularity. And this power, too, multiplied at the same terrifying rate, ensuring the mask would never slip.

The stasis held for ten thousand years. His kingdom became dust, then myth, then forgotten sediment. Thronegate, enriched by its plunder, grew. It mastered magic, then technology, then the fusion of both. It tamed space, colonized star systems, and built an empire that spanned the galactic disk. It believed itself the pinnacle of existence. The name Olympus was not even a ghost in its historical records.

Twenty years before Zeus stirred, a merchant named Blake found himself lost. His cargo run had gone astray due to a faulty star-chart, and his small freighter had set down on a barren, rocky moon for repairs. Exploring a curious energy signature, he found a cave that was not a cave—its angles were too perfect, its darkness too absolute. Blake, a man of thirty-eight with a honest face and a direct manner, was motivated by curiosity, not treasure. He had a wife, Anna, who was his partner in all things, a twelve-year-old son, Josh, who loved engineering, and an eight-year-old daughter, Rhea, whose laughter was his favorite sound. Inside the vault, he saw only a strange, man-shaped statue of what looked like fused ash and pearl. His hand, brushing away dust, touched its chest.

The statue cracked. Then it dissolved.

Zeus stood before him. The salt-and-pepper beard was the same, but the face was a continent of hardened grief. The eyes that opened were not human; they were like windows into a silent, frozen supernova. He drew a breath—a habit, not a need—and in the time it took that air to fill lungs that no longer required it, he knew everything. Ten millennia of history, the rise of Thronegate, the location of every star, the secret name of every atom, the dreams of King Robert, age twenty-two, who sat upon a throne of stolen legacy. Omniscience was the 287,456,921st power he had acquired, and it was as natural to him as blinking.

He looked at Blake, the man who had freed him. Something in the merchant’s direct gaze, the uncalculated concern on his face, touched a chamber of memory long sealed. “Thank you,” Zeus said, and his voice was a glacier calving, deep and final. He did not explain. He simply turned his gaze starward, towards the glittering capital of Thronegate. His message was not sent; it simply *was*, appearing in the mind of King Robert and every one of his subjects simultaneously, a decree etched into consciousness: *I am Zeus of Olympus. You have three days to gather all your strength. If I can defeat it, you will abdicate. If not, I will destroy you.*

The Thronegate military assembled with the pride of a thousand victorious generations. The sky above the designated plain blackened with warships, each capable of sterilizing planets. The ground trembled under the march of billions of enhanced super-soldiers, their armor woven with spells of invulnerability. Legions of combat automatons, their logic engines sharp enough to predict causality, formed perfect, shimmering phalanxes. Dragons the size of mountains, scales harder than neutronium, filled the air with heat-shimmer and the scent of ozone. There were assassins who moved between seconds, and psionic choirs whose combined will could rewrite physical laws. King Robert watched from a command satellite, confident. This was the force that had unified a galaxy. What was one man?

Blake and Anna watched from a ridge, brought there by a glance from Zeus. They saw him walk onto the plain, a lone figure in simple, dark clothes. He did not assume a stance. He did not raise his hands. He simply stood, waiting.

The attack came as a single, cataclysmic wave—a symphony of annihilation meant to vaporize continents. Energy beams that could pierce stars, temporal stasis fields, entropy curses, relativistic projectiles, soul-rending psionic pulses—all converged on the spot where Zeus stood.

He did not move a muscle.

The violence did not touch him. It did not *reach* him. A foot from his body, it met a boundary of absolute negation. The energy beams inverted and shot back with perfect, amplified accuracy, each bolt finding the heart of a warship’s reactor. The temporal fields shattered, aging their caster-dragons to instant dust. The entropy curses rebounded, turning the robot legions into rust-flakes in the wind. The psionic pulses echoed back, overloading the minds of the psychic choirs, their skulls bursting like overripe fruit. The super-soldiers, mid-charge, simply disintegrated, their atoms politely deciding to cease cohering. The dragons, breathing fire that could melt time, found their own breath freezing in their throats, then exploding inward. The ninjas who reversed time found themselves reversing into their own mothers’ wombs and then ceasing to have ever been.

It was not a battle. It was a nullification. It was a man standing still while a universe of fury spent itself against the unchangeable fact of his existence. The confidence of Thronegate curdled into confusion, then into raw, primal terror. They threw everything, and he absorbed nothing. He simply was, and their everything was not enough. Morale did not collapse; it was erased. A billion minds, trained for war, were confronted with a truth so absolute it broke their sanity: they were not soldiers facing an enemy. They were paintings facing a fire. King Robert, in his satellite, vomited, his screen a tapestry of silent, expanding fireballs where his invincible armadas had been. Two days later, he was found in his chambers, a ceremonial dagger in his heart, the throne room empty before him. Zeus walked into that room and placed the simple crown upon his own head.

His first decree was the Awakening. To all men of Thronegate, he granted a boon: for every day past the age of twenty they lived, their strength, endurance, intelligence, and wisdom increased tenfold. A man of thirty had lived ten years past twenty—3,650 days. His power was multiplied tenfold 3,650 times. The numbers were incomprehensible, but the control was instinctive. They could suppress it, live normally, or call upon a sliver of it to shatter mountains. They remained mortal, living perhaps eighty years, but those years were now lived with the potential of demigods. The women were protected with absolute, ruthless edicts; harm to any woman brought a punishment from Zeus himself that made the galactic conquest look gentle. The people, especially the men, came to revere him. He had broken them, then remade them into something greater. Blake, however, noticed his own strength was… different. More profound. Where his neighbor could lift a ground-car, Blake, without strain, felt he could adjust the orbit of a moon. His multiplier was not ten. It was one thousand. He never questioned why.

Zeus’s rule turned outward. Thronegate, now a legion of awakened men, began to subjugate the omniverse. The offer was always mercy: surrender, integrate, enjoy peace under Thronegate’s aegis. Refusal meant conquest.

The universe of the Chronarchs had prepared for five years. They had learned of the “falling cosmos” from prophetic algorithms. They mastered time, could unmake events before they happened, and built fortresses outside causality. Their offer of surrender was delivered, and they laughed. A squad of five Thronegate men, all in their late thirties, kind-faced fathers and craftsmen, arrived. The Chronarchs unleashed temporal loops, paradox weapons, armies cloned from every possible timeline. The Thronegate men simply… moved. Their speed, a product of decades of tenfold compounding, was such that time became a viscous liquid they pushed through. They shattered the paradox engines with their fists. They gathered the infinite cloned armies into a single point and pressed them into a tiny, hot ball of nothing. The conquest took twenty-seven minutes. The Chronarchs died bewildered, their ultimate preparations revealed as child’s sandcastles against a tide.

The realm of the Dreaming Collective was next, a universe where thought shaped reality, and its masters were psychic gods of infinite creativity and will. They refused. Zeus, that day, was mildly inconvenienced by a malfunctioning ornamental fountain in his palace. Annoyed, he glanced in the general direction of the Dreaming Collective. In that fractional second of his attention, the entire universe, its infinite dimensions and all its dreaming gods, ceased to have ever existed. It was not destroyed; it was retroactively rendered a fictional footnote. The conquest was so effortless, so complete, that not even the Thronegate men sent as observers registered it had happened until they found only a void where a cosmos should be.

The third universe was the domain of the Unbound, entities of pure logic and energy who had transcended physical form. They analyzed the threat. They concluded Zeus’s power, while immense, must have a source, a core, a weakness. They theorized it was tied to his emotional state, to the trauma of his past. If they could simulate the exact emotional frequency of his loss and broadcast it at him, they could trigger a catatonic state. The moment the first Unbound thinker formulated this hypothesis—a stray, speculative thought that a flaw *might* exist—the paradox defense engaged. Across the omniverse, Zeus’s amplification rate, already beyond measure, received a permanent, significant boost. A perfect immunity to emotional-frequency attacks was woven into his passive shields, layered over immunities that already made him invulnerable to everything. Simultaneously, he gained a mastery of emotional-frequency manipulation that dwarfed the Unbound’s understanding to the degree a supernova dwarfs a match. He could now, if he wished, make a whole universe feel his grief, or joy, or nothing at all.

Unaware, the Unbound spent years building their weapon. They launched it, a wave of crafted sorrow designed to mimic the death of a family. It washed over Zeus as he sat in his garden with Blake, discussing trade routes. Zeus paused, feeling the touch of the simulation. He found it quaint. With a thought infinitesimally small, he denied the very concept of emotional-frequency manipulation to the Unbound. Their weapon vanished. Their knowledge of the field vanished. Their capacity to even conceive of such an attack vanished. Then, using their own now-forgotten science, but with potency multiplied by an infinity of infinities, he broadcast a single, pure emotion back to them: despair. Not a simulated one. The real, absolute, and final understanding of their hopelessness. Every Unbound entity, in the same instant, comprehended the totality of their error. They saw that the seed of their defeat was planted the very first nanosecond one of them had entertained the idea he was not absolute. Their civilization extinguished itself in a wave of perfect, logical surrender to the inevitable.

Through it all, Blake remained at Zeus’s side. The king who was a glacier to the omniverse was warm to the merchant. He smiled at Blake’s stories of his children, now grown. Josh had become a brilliant, if unconventional, engineer in the royal service. Rhea was a historian. Anna remained Blake’s anchor. When the position of Royal Advisor on Material Logistics (a humble title) became vacant, Zeus asked Blake to fill it. The role required a keen mind, absolute trust, and a certain… intuitive understanding of the flow of resources and energy. Some in the court began to quietly refer to Blake as the king’s wizard, though he wielded no magic they could see. He just had a way of making things work, of finding solutions in chaos, a legacy in his blood he never knew.

Today, Thronegate is the heart of the omniverse. Its spires, forged from the memories of conquered realms, pierce a sky where captured nebulae dance in permanent twilight. The awakened men walk its streets, their contained power humming like distant stars. Women and children live without fear. At the center of the greatest palace, in a quiet chamber open to the strange sky, Zeus stands. He is watching a fountain—now perfectly functional—play in a pool. Blake stands beside him, a sheaf of reports in his hand, speaking softly about supply lines to the newly integrated domains. Zeus listens, a faint, genuine smile on his face, the terrifying supernova in his eyes banked to a gentle glow. Outside, the omniverse turns, obedient and silent, under the rule of the king who cannot be challenged, who cannot be hurt, who cannot be understood. And beside him, always, is his friend.

The air in the Observatory tasted of ozone and cold stone, a scent that had not changed in one hundred thousand years. Ron, Court Wizard of the Omniversal Throne, felt the weight of every one of those millennia in his bones, a dull ache no magic could soothe. His title was a formality, a nod to a lineage he only vaguely understood—his ancestor, a merchant named Blake, had stood where he stood now. But where Blake had been a friend, Ron was a functionary. A glorified caretaker for a machine that required no care.

He watched the central display, a swirling map of the subjugated omniverse. A million million galaxies glowed with the soft, obedient blue of Thronegate integration. There were no red flashes of conflict. There hadn’t been for millennia. The peace was absolute, seamless, and to Ron, it was a tomb.

His eyes were not on the map, but on the single, isolated data-stream he had painstakingly carved from the periphery of the Omniversal Awareness Engine—the system that governed all. It was a trickle of information, a hidden ledger of resource allocations, personnel transfers, and energy diversions so vast they could fuel a billion rebellions. They were all tagged with a cryptographic signature that was a joke only two beings in existence could possibly get: a stylized, cartoonish thunderbolt with a smiley face.

Zeus was funding a rebellion. Against himself.

Ron had discovered it not through grand espionage, but through bureaucratic anomaly. A shipment of collapsed neutronium, enough to forge a billion unbreakable swords, had been misrouted to a derelict dimension officially listed as a ‘theoretical hazard.’ Ron had investigated, expecting a glitch. He found instead a forge-world of impossible scale, humming with automated industry, building armies of beings whose design philosophies were explicitly, violently anti-Thronegate. And the funding trail, once he knew where to look, was a river of wealth flowing from the throne’s own coffers, filtered through a thousand shell realities, all leading back to the smiley-faced thunderbolt.

The door to the Observatory hissed open, and the nature of the space changed. The air grew stiller, heavier, as if reality itself was holding its breath. Zeus entered.

He looked as he always had: the salt-and-pepper beard, the form that spoke of contained, catastrophic strength. But his eyes, usually windows to a frozen, indifferent cosmos, were alight with an emotion Ron had never seen in them: giddy, childlike anticipation.

“Ron!” Zeus boomed, his voice a friendly earthquake. He clapped a hand on Ron’s shoulder, a gesture that could have vaporized a planet but was calibrated to a firm, human warmth. “A beautiful day, isn’t it? The entropy levels in Quadrant Seven are particularly poetic today. A slow, elegant decay.”

“Sire,” Ron said, bowing his head slightly. He kept his voice neutral, his mind a shielded fortress. He had spent centuries learning to wall off even his subconscious, a necessary survival skill when your master could hear a stray thought like a shout.

Zeus drifted over to the main display, his hands clasped behind his back. He hummed a tuneless, happy little melody. “I’ve been thinking, Ron. About surprise. It’s the rarest commodity in all existence. The last time I was genuinely surprised… well, you know when that was.”

Ron did. The Fall of Olympus. The death of everything. The event that had birthed the monster-god before him.

“I miss it,” Zeus confessed, his tone shockingly conversational. “The clench in the gut. The widening of the eyes. The delicious, terrifying moment when the world reveals it is not as you knew it.” He turned, and his smile was terrifying in its genuine warmth. “So, I’ve decided to cultivate one.”

Ron’s heart was a trapped bird in his ribs. He forced himself to look at the god-king, to meet those supernova eyes. “A surprise, sire? From whom? There is no force left that can challenge the Throne.”

“Exactly!” Zeus laughed, a sound of pure delight. “That’s the problem! So, I’ve been gardening. A little pruning here, a little watering there. Ron’s blood ran cold. “A hero?”

“A construct,” Zeus said, waving a hand. “A magnificent one. I wove him from the archetypal threads of a thousand mythologies—the orphaned prince, the chosen one, the bearer of a sacred sword. I gave him a tragic past I invented myself. I even gave him a prophecy, Ron! A real, working prophecy that foretells my downfall. It’s my best work in ages.”

The horror of it was absolute. It was not just a rebellion; it was a bespoke, hand-crafted apocalypse, designed by its intended victim.

“But sire,” Ron said, his voice barely a whisper. “Your omniscience… you would see their every plan, their every move. Where is the surprise in that?”

Zeus’s grin turned conspiratorial. “Ah! The best part! I’ve blinded myself to it. Voluntarily, temporarily. I’ve partitioned my own awareness. The part of me that is ‘Zeus the King’ has no knowledge of the Crucible, its inhabitants, or their plans. That data-stream is firewalled from my primary consciousness. I came up with that, too.”

Chrono-Loop Traps (to freeze a moment of victory).* All of it, every weapon, every strategy, born in the mind of the being they were meant to destroy. The ultimate puppet show.

“When?” Ron managed to ask.

“Soon!” Zeus rubbed his hands together. “Any century now! The tension is exquisite. I’ve been dropping little hints to my ‘king’ self—anomalous energy readings, whispers from dead dimensions. I might even let them win a battle or two. For the drama.”

And he would never, ever let Zeus see the one true surprise that might have existed: the flicker of pity in the heart of his court wizard, for the lonely, omnipotent child who had to build his own toys, and for the toys who believed they were real.
