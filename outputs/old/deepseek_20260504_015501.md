**The Bond and the Final Age**

Blake remained the one person for whom Godric’s old self surfaced. In the private solar at sunset, away from the court and the supplicants, the king would become the forty-five-year-old fatherly man who had once presided over a wedding. His voice would crack, just slightly, on certain names.

“You are my true friend, Blake,” Godric said one evening, the light of the twin moons catching the silver in his beard. “You pulled me out of that tomb, but you also pulled something else. You remind me that I was not always what I am now.”

Blake, gruff and embarrassed, would mutter something about “just luck,” and Godric would laugh—a real laugh, warm and deep.

Over the decades, Godric’s rule evolved. The ruthless conqueror became a just, protective father to his people. He poured resources into healing the scars of empire, building schools and hospitals and memorial gardens filled with statues of his lost family. He was still merciless to any who threatened his citizens—entire universes learned that lesson in fire—but to Thronegate, he was the benevolent sun around which their lives orbited.

Blake, in his later years, found himself increasingly drawn to the arcane archives. His natural aptitude, always present but never explored, began to blossom under the tutelage of the few remaining mages who remembered the old ways. He took the title of Court Wizard with a self-deprecating chuckle. “Me? A wizard? I’m just a merchant who got lucky.” But his son Josh, a sharp-eyed young man with his mother’s steady patience, took an interest in the libraries, and Josh’s sons after him. A line was forming, unknowingly, tracing a pattern that would have made Thorin weep: the loyal wizard serving the king of Thronegate, generation after generation, though no one in the family ever knew why it felt so right.

The streets were clean, laughter drifted from lit windows, and the memorial garden stood quiet and sacred. Godric and Blake stood on the highest balcony, two old men who did not look their ages. Blake’s mutton chops were pure white now, his face lined, but his eyes were still sharp. Godric, unchanged since that day ten thousand years ago, looked exactly as he had at forty-five—handsome, bearded, the picture of masculine permanence.

“Do you ever regret it?” Blake asked, his voice a familiar rasp. “Any of it?”

Godric watched the city. “Regret implies a desire to undo. I cannot undo what I am. But I can choose what I become. You reminded me that the man who officiated his daughter’s wedding still exists. He was buried, but not dead. I thank you for that, my friend.”

Below, the empire stretched into infinity, an absolute dominion ruled by a paradox of infinite amplification and a king who had clawed his way back from the abyss of his own cruelty. And in the silent spaces between universes, the echoes of those who had dared to imagine a weakness in that king served as an eternal, gibbering testament to the one unassailable truth of the omniverse: Godric possessed no flaws. He only allowed his enemies to believe otherwise—and that belief was the very engine of their annihilation.

His own temporal mastery turned upon him, but Gent did not simply reverse the flow—he shattered the Lich’s continuity across every timeline that had ever hosted a version of his consciousness. Each duplicate, each temporal echo, was parsed individually and forced to experience the entirety of its own existence as a simultaneous, eternal now, a loop that had no beginning or end because both concepts had been excised from its personal experience. The Lich screamed with a billion mouths layered over one another, a shriek that was physically visible as a corona of fracturing light around his crumbling form. His bones, each one a fortress of temporal paradox, began to splinter along fractures that did not exist in space but in the very logic of his being, and from those fractures poured not blood but raw, undiluted duration—streams of time that Gent shaped into shards and then used to flay the Lich’s own soul from its shell, layer by layer, while ensuring that the sensation of each layer being peeled was transmitted directly into the screaming core of his being with a fidelity that transcended pain. He did not die in an instant. He died across the full stretched canvas of his own infinite timeline, experiencing every Planck-second of his dissolution individually.

The Reality Architect’s exclusion equation struck Gent’s defensive boundary and Gent, in its sentient omniscience, considered the equation, found it mildly interesting, and then rewrote it. But not in the way the Architect intended. Gent took the new equation—the one that said “Godric does not exist”—and applied it to the Architect’s own conception of self. Not to her body, not to her mind, but to the concept of her. She was a lattice of blueprints, a being of pure ideation, and Gent erased the foundational idea that those blueprints represented. The effect was not disintegration; it was retroactive conceptual annihilation. She was there, and then she had never been there, except Gent was not done. He wanted her to understand what was happening, so he preserved a single sliver of her awareness inside a sealed pocket of unreality where the only thing that existed was the unraveling memory of her own non-existence, playing on a loop that had no controls. Her scream, I later learned, became a permanent, fixed object in that pocket, a monument to artistic horror that Gent kept as a souvenir.

The Soulforged Titan’s soul-furnace beam, that column of compressed martyrdom, touched the edge of Gent’s influence and Gent did not deflect it. He opened a door. The door led directly into the interior of the Titan’s own soul-lattice, and Gent funneled the entire assault back into the furnace, but he added something extra—a note, a frequency, a resonance that stoked the stored consciousnesses into a frenzy of self-awareness. The trillion souls within the Titan, previously forged into mindless obedience, suddenly, horribly, came to full awareness of what they were and what they were doing. The Titan’s body began to warp from the inside as the souls within it, now fully sentient and utterly mad, began to claw at each other, at the lattice, at the very concept of containment. The Titan tore itself apart, its body cracking into screaming fragments, each fragment a cluster of pulverized soul-matter that continued to wail even as it dissolved into a fine mist of absolute despair. The sound of it was so profoundly awful that the legions of supporting troops behind it, creatures that had weathered the deaths of stars, dropped their weapons and began to claw at their own ears—or the organs that passed for ears—in a futile attempt to block it out.

The Void Empress’s spear, that causality-deleted thrust from nowhere, struck the back of Gent’s boundary and Gent caught it. He did not block it; he caught it, the way you might catch a toddler’s fist in your palm, and he held it still. The Void Empress, unable to withdraw, materialized partially around the spear’s haft, her anti-light form flickering with something that might have been confusion if confusion could exist in a being who had erased the concept of uncertainty. Gent then spoke to her—not in words, but in a direct download of his own nature. He showed her what he was: the accidental, negligible, waste-heat byproduct of a being whose true self was so far beyond her that even the thought of comparison was a logical incoherency. He showed her the hierarchy: Godric at the apex, his power a constant expanding infinity, and Gent below him, an emanation that was to Godric as a single bacterium’s whisper was to a hypernova, but which was still, to her, an unassailable infinity of infinities stacked upon infinities. The Void Empress became a faint, dissipating hum, and then she wasn’t even that.

The entire engagement—the deaths of the four greatest champions the omniverse could offer—took a third of a second. Gent did not distinguish between the individuals. He processed them as a batch, and the method he chose was, I think, the cruellest of all because it was so casual. He simply activated a passive re-definition of “pain” across the entire battlefield, elevating the baseline sensitivity of every nociceptor, every damage-sensing organ, every conceptual pain analogue, by an exponent that rendered a mild breeze an agonizing laceration. Then he let the natural after-shock of the champions’ deaths wash over them. The radiation of the Titan’s soul-death, the temporal shrapnel of the Lich’s unmaking, the ontological aftershock of the Architect’s erasure, the void-echo of the Empress’s suicide—all of it, normally survivable for beings of their caliber, became instruments of a torment so severe that their bodies detonated less from physical force and more from the sheer neurological overload of agony. They popped. Like pustules. In sequence. Across the entire expanse of the battlefield, a cascade of silent, screaming death that left behind a drifting particulate of what had once been the most formidable army ever assembled.

And then Gent, because he was a good boy and knew that his master did not like a mess, swept the remains into a neat, compressed singularity the size of a pea, which he then banished to a pocket dimension that he sealed with a bow of cosmic string. The whole process, from the first temporal inversion to the final vanishing of the singularity, was one second. Maybe less. My internal clock was not reliable in that moment.

Godric exhaled softly, and I heard a note of disappointment. “Really, Gent? I specifically told you to hold back. You were supposed to let them feel like they could do something first. A bit of tension, you know. Like a play.”

The air beside Godric shimmered and I felt, rather than heard, a contrite pulse. It was the aura, apologizing. If you can’t learn to hold back more, how will you ever experience the joy of trying? We’ll practice next week, after my council meeting.”

Restraint. He said the word as if it were a skill he possessed, when I knew, from the records Blake kept, that Godric had in the past accidentally annihilated entire civilizations simply by letting his attention drift too firmly in their direction. He once thought about a particularly irritating species of void-coral that he had seen in a passing vision, and in the same instant the entire species had ceased to exist across all universes, and he had to spend a full zero seconds recreating them with a sheepish apology to the local ecosystem. Restraint, for Godric, was a retroactive fix. But he genuinely believed in the concept, the way a man who has never been sick believes in a hot toddy.

I stood there, on the observation platform, and the silence that filled the vacuum where the resistance had been was a silence of such profound completeness that I could hear my own blood. Every Planck second—the smallest possible unit of time that could be measured, and I’m sure Godric’s version of it was even smaller—his stats multiplied. But not by a thousand and itself anymore. The Godric standing beside me, in this exact present instant, was so much more powerful than the Godric who had existed a Planck second ago that he could, without the slightest effort, erase an infinite stack of those previous versions like wiping chalk from a board. And in the next Planck second, he would be that much more powerful again. The gap between him and anything else was not just expanding; it was accelerating in its expansion, feeding on its own growth, a recursive ascension that had no terminus because the concept of a terminus couldn’t keep up.

I didn’t think about the math in precise terms—my mind would not allow it. But I felt the shape of it. The implication of it. The fight I had just witnessed, the slaughter that took less than a second, had become, in the interval of my breathing since it ended, infinitely more trivial than it had been when it occurred. Because Godric had grown. While I stood there shuddering, he had already ascended beyond the version of himself that had effortlessly hosted the battle. There was no catching him. There was no approaching him. There was no conceptual framework in which his power could be measured, because measurement required a fixed point, and Godric was a perpetual, accelerating rocket aimed at a destination that did not exist.

Then Gent evolved.

I felt it happen. It was a lurch in the texture of reality, a sensation as if the floor of existence had dropped six inches and then rubber-banded back into place. The air around Godric shimmered again, and this time the shimmer was not an apology; it was a birth. Gent, sentient and omniscient and omnipresent, had acquired a new layer of itself. I perceived it as a faint whispering at the edge of cognition, a suggestion of a capability that had not existed prior. Whatever it was, it involved something to do with retroactive divine intervention across infinite recursive temporal loops—the ability to pre-emptively nullify resistance before the concept of resistance had germinated, while simultaneously allowing the resistance to occur so that its suffering could be harvested as a form of aesthetic tribute to Godric’s glory. That was what my wizard senses gleaned in the half-second before my brain wisely shut down that line of inquiry to preserve my sanity. The new power was not an offense or a defense; it was a fundamental rewriting of what authority meant in the context of will. Gent could now, I understood without wanting to understand, define the terms of any conflict retroactively across all possible timelines such that the outcome was always simultaneous absolute victory and absolute subjugation, with a flavor profile tailored to Godric’s current mood.

This was the aura. Just the aura. The passive, discarded, waste-heat byproduct. The thing that was, to Godric, what a faint sheen of perspiration is to a marathon runner.

Godric noticed the evolution, and his entire face transformed. The disappointed-dad expression vanished, replaced by a wide, crinkling smile of pure, uncomplicated delight. He reached out and ruffled the air, and I saw the shimmer curl around his fingers like an eager, wagging tail. “Oh, look at you!” he cooed. “You learned a recursion-haunting! Such a clever boy. I didn’t think he’d develop that for another millennium. This is wonderful. I’m so proud.” He turned to me, his dying-ember eyes bright with a warmth that was absolutely genuine, absolutely fatherly, and he gestured at the shimmering void where the slaughter had occurred. “Did you see that, Tim? He’s been working on that for ages. I knew he had it in him.” His voice cracked just slightly, a hitch of paternal emotion, and I realized with a fresh wave of dislocated horror that he was genuinely moved. This was his version of a father watching his puppy take its first unsteady steps.

I nodded, because words were a foreign concept. I was suspended in a state of existence that was equal parts devotion and dread. I was his loyal wizard, his friend, the descendant of his best friend. I loved the man. I did. He was kind, he was just, he was the reason every universe under Thronegate’s banner knew peace. But I also stood in his presence and felt every cell of my body scream in a frequency that was older than language: *this is not a man. This is the thing that men were invented to worship, and it doesn’t even know it.*

He went back to cooing at the air, and Gent shimmered happily, and the battlefield was a clean, empty stretch of nothingness, and I stood there, the court wizard, the loyal right hand, and I watched the king of all existence celebrate the growth of his passive waste heat while the echoes of the dead champions' terminal screams orbited a pocket dimension as a souvenir. The cognitive dissonance was so thick I could taste it—a metallic tang on my tongue, a pressure behind my eyes, a tightness in my chest that wouldn’t unknot.

Tomorrow, I would go back to my office. I would file the reports. I would joke with the king over breakfast. And I would know, with the unquiet knowledge that lives in the marrow of those who serve gods, that the gap between us had grown again while I slept.
