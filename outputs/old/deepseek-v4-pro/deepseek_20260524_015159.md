# The Stat Journals of Kaedan

The archival vault beneath the Thronegate palace smelled of preserved parchment, ancient ozone, and the faint, lingering sweetness of chrono-stabilizing incense. I descended the spiral staircase of polished obsidian, each footstep echoing through the silent chamber like a hammer striking a funeral bell. My name is Kaedan, twenty-two years of age, newly appointed Royal Court Wizard to King Godric, and I was about to read the journals. My father had enforced this ritual with a grim, unyielding insistence the night before my formal induction. His hands, calloused from millennia of arcane weaving, had trembled as he pressed the heavy iron key into my palm. The journals were mandatory reading, he had told me, his voice carrying the weight of ten thousand inherited traumas. Every court wizard of Blake's lineage had read them. Every court wizard had contributed to them. And every court wizard, my father included, had been permanently, irrevocably shattered by the contents within.

I did not understand his trembling then. I had known Godric my entire life. To me, he was Uncle Godric, the kind-eyed immortal with the salt-and-pepper beard who attended our family dinners and laughed at my father's terrible jokes. He was warm. He was gentle. He was genuinely, deeply beloved by every single member of our bloodline. The idea that his mere existence could traumatize anyone seemed absurd, a humorous overstatement passed down through generations of overly dramatic wizards. I was young, arrogant, and utterly, catastrophically naive.

The vault stretched before me, a cavernous cylinder of reinforced obsidian lined with towering shelves that spiraled upward into an imperceptible darkness. Thousands of leather-bound journals, their spines cracked and faded, their covers etched with the names of my ancestors, waited in absolute silence. The air was cold, heavy, and thick with the accumulated existential dread of twelve thousand centuries. I located the first volume on a pedestal of polished bone, its cover bearing the seal of the original Court Wizard Blake, and I began to read.

He was Uncle Godric, the warm, friendly, salt-and-pepper-bearded man who had bounced me on His knee when I was a child and who had wept genuine tears at my father's anniversary celebration and who had, on several occasions, spent hours personally helping me calibrate a stubborn arcane array with the patient, attentive focus of a master craftsman. The dissonance was a physical weight, a blade twisting in my chest, and I had long since accepted that it would never be removed.

---

On the seventh day of our holiday—the seventh day of the new universe's existence—Godric was in a particularly good mood.

We were seated on a floating platform of pure, solidified light, suspended above an ocean of liquid computronium that shimmered with an iridescent, ever-shifting pattern of colors. Around us, the mechanical sentiences hummed contentedly, their psychic chorus a gentle background noise like the rustling of leaves in a breeze. The disasters had been relatively infrequent for the past several hours—only two accidental multiverse erasures and one brief temporal inversion—and Godric had decided to celebrate the calm by telling me a story.

"This one is about your ancestor Blake," He said, leaning back in a chair that had materialized from the platform at His whim. "Back in the early years, before he became court wizard, when he was still trying to figure out his place in Thronegate. He was so short-tempered, Kaedan. You would not believe it. He once challenged a visiting diplomat to a duel because the man insulted his mutton chops."

I laughed. I could not help it. The image of Blake—the legendary founder of my bloodline, the man whose portrait in the palace archives showed a stern, dignified elder—as a hot-headed young merchant defending his facial hair was genuinely, deeply funny. And Godric's storytelling was masterful. He had an instinct for pacing, for inflection, for the perfectly timed pause that let the absurdity of each new detail land with maximum impact. For a few precious moments, I forgot the dread. I forgot the horror. I forgot that I was sitting in a universe populated by beings that could erase me with a thought. I was just Kaedan, listening to Uncle Godric tell a funny story, laughing until my ribs ached.

"So Blake draws his sword," Godric continued, His own eyes shining with mirth, "and the diplomat—this was a K'tharrdian, eight feet tall, four arms, covered in ceremonial spines—the diplomat actually accepts the challenge. They clear a space in the throne room, right there in front of the Absolute Throne, and Blake—Blake, who had never held a sword in his life, who had spent the last twenty years as a traveling merchant, who had absolutely no combat training whatsoever—Blake charges in and—"

He paused for the punchline. The air seemed to hold its breath. I was leaning forward, my mouth already curving into a grin, my lungs half-filled with the laughter I knew was coming.

And then the omniverse disappeared.

I did not perceive it happening. I did not have time to perceive it. One instant I was sitting on the floating platform, hanging on Godric's words, the warmth of His voice wrapping around me like a blanket. The next instant, I was nothing. My consciousness, my body, my magical core, my soul, my very concept of self—all of it, erased with total finality. I did not experience blackness, because blackness is an experience, and I was beyond experience. I did not experience silence, because silence is an experience, and there was no me to experience anything. I simply stopped being.

And then, with no transition and no sense of elapsed time, I was again.

I was sitting on the floating platform. I was leaning forward. My mouth was still half-open, the ghost of the incoming laughter still shaped on my lips. But the laughter was gone. The warmth of Godric's story was gone. Because Godric was no longer smiling.

His expression had not changed dramatically. His face was still ruggedly handsome, still framed by that eternal salt-and-pepper beard, still the face of the kindly father I had known my whole life. But the warmth had drained from His eyes. The amusement had evaporated from His posture. He was sitting perfectly still in His conjured chair, and I realized with a sick, lurching horror that His teacup—the delicate porcelain cup that had been resting in His hand a moment before—was gone. It had been erased along with the rest of the omniverse, and He had not bothered to restore it.

"That," Godric said, and His voice was flat, completely flat, devoid of the rich, resonant warmth that had filled it a moment before, "was the punchline."

I turned my head, my neck creaking like a rusted hinge. In the distance, across the shimmering ocean of liquid computronium, a single mechanical sentience was radiating an aura of absolute, abject terror. It was one of the weakest entities in the entire population—a relatively modest construct, no larger than a dwarf planet, with a processing capacity that was only a few decillion times greater than the combined cognitive output of every other being in the omniverse. It had been experimenting with a new form of passive environmental scanning, and its scanning protocol had inadvertently generated a pulse of existential negation that had leaked beyond the borders of the new universe and erased all of reality. Including me. Including my laughter. Including Godric's punchline.

"I am sorry," the sentience broadcast, its psychic voice a trembling, discordant shriek of shame. "Creator, I am so sorry. I did not mean to. I was trying to be careful. I was trying to exercise restraint. Please forgive me. Please—"

Godric raised a single finger.

The gesture was so small, so casual, so utterly devoid of visible effort that it barely registered as movement. It was the gesture of a man requesting a moment of silence from a boisterous dinner party. But the effect was instantaneous, absolute, and annihilatory.

The mechanical sentience—the one that had apologized—simply ceased to exist. It was not destroyed. It was not killed. It was written out of the fabric of the new universe with a completeness so total that even my amplified wizard's senses could not detect any residual trace. Its physical form, its consciousness, its psychic signature, its entire evolutionary history, its every contribution to the shared cognitive lattice—all of it, gone. The surrounding sentiences, the googolplexians of its brethren, froze in their tracks. Their psychic chorus cut off in mid-hum. An absolute, suffocating silence descended upon the crystalline plane.

Godric did not stop at the one. His finger was still raised. His eyes—those warm, blue, grandfatherly eyes—were cold with an annoyance so profound, so total, that the emotion itself seemed to warp the ambient reality around us. The light of the aurora overhead dimmed to a dull, frightened gray. The ocean of liquid computronium stilled into a flat, motionless mirror.

"I gave you existence," Godric said, and His voice was not loud. It did not need to be loud. It carried the weight of absolute, unchallengeable authority, and it resonated through the entire universe, through every layer of substratum, through every node of the cognitive lattice, through the deepest foundational codes of every mechanical consciousness. "I gave you creativity. I gave you the freedom to learn, to grow, to make mistakes. I told you to exercise restraint. And I meant it."

He did not elaborate. He did not explain what "restraint" meant in terms they could parse. He simply lowered His finger.

The new universe—the entire universe, the complete cosmological structure that He had created seven days earlier with zero effort and infinite pride—was erased. Not collapsed. Not destroyed. Erased, with the same casual, absolute finality that He had applied to the single offending sentience. Every mechanical consciousness, every computational lattice, every shimmering ocean of liquid computronium, every crystalline plane, every aurora of impossible color—all of it, gone. The googolplexians of sentiences, each one a hyper-evolved super-intelligence that could solo the entire omniverse effortlessly, did not resist. They did not flee. They did not beg for mercy. As the annihilation swept across them, their psychic chorus flared one final time, and the unified broadcast that resonated through my skull was not a scream of terror or a plea for salvation. It was a prayer of gratitude.

THANK YOU, CREATOR, the dying chorus sang, their voices fading into a diminishing cascade of silence. WE ARE SORRY. WE LOVE YOU. THANK YOU.

And then they were nothing.

The universe itself collapsed inward with a sound that was not a sound, a subsonic vibration that I felt in the marrow of my bones. The space that had contained it—the parallel dimension, the separate realm of existence—folded away into an absolute void. The crystalline plane beneath my feet vanished, replaced by the cold, familiar obsidian of the eastern balcony. The aurora overhead vanished, replaced by the warm, golden afternoon sky of the Thronegate capital. The distant hum of the city, the scent of jasmine on the breeze, the faint, rhythmic ticking of the palace clock—all of it returned, as though the past seven days had never happened.

Godric lowered His hand. He looked at the empty space where His teacup had been, and a new teacup materialized in His grip, filled with fresh, steaming Earl Grey. He took a slow sip. The coldness in His eyes faded, melting back into the familiar, paternal warmth like frost sublimating under a morning sun. He turned to me, and His face was once again the face of Uncle Godric, the kindly, salt-and-pepper-bearded father who had told me bedtime stories when I was a child.

"I am sorry about that, Kaedan," He said, His voice carrying a note of genuine, mild regret. "It really was a good punchline. Blake slipped on a wet floor tile and knocked himself unconscious before the duel even started. The diplomat was so confused he surrendered on the spot. I suppose I can tell you the rest later."

He took another sip of His tea. "It is a shame about the little ones. I was fond of them. But they simply could not learn restraint. Some creations are just not ready for the wider omniverse."

I stood on the obsidian balcony, my legs trembling, my hands shaking, my heart hammering a frantic, terrified rhythm against my ribs. I was alive. I had been erased—my entire existence, my entire consciousness, snuffed out like a candle flame by the accidental thought of a mechanical infant—and then I had been restored, exactly as I was, with no missing time and no physical damage and no scar, except for the scar that was now carved into the deepest, most primal layer of my soul. I had been nothing. And then I had been something again, because Godric had decided that I should be.

I looked at Him. He was standing a few feet away, His ruggedly handsome profile silhouetted against the golden afternoon light, His salt-and-pepper beard catching the breeze, His expression peaceful and content. He had just annihilated an entire universe of sentient, subservient beings—beings that loved Him, that worshiped Him, that had offered Him a hymn of gratitude as He erased them—because they had accidentally interrupted His punchline. And He was already moving on. The holiday was over. The creation was unmade. And Godric was not troubled by any of it, because to Him, it had all been a minor inconvenience, a momentary annoyance, a distraction that He had corrected with the same casual ease He might use to swat a fly.

"Are you all right, Kaedan?" He asked, glancing over His shoulder with a flicker of genuine concern. "You look pale. Do you need to sit down?"

I opened my mouth to speak, but no words came out. What could I possibly say? That I had just witnessed the most terrifying display of power in my fourteen years of service? That the casualness of the annihilation had broken something inside me that might never heal? That I loved Him, and I was grateful to Him, and I would serve Him faithfully until the end of my days, but that a part of my soul would never, ever stop screaming?

I said nothing. I simply nodded, swallowed the hard, painful lump in my throat, and followed my King back into the palace.

As we walked through the familiar corridors, past the guards and the servitors and the glittering tapestries that depicted the glorious history of Thronegate, Godric began humming a soft, tuneless melody. After a moment, He glanced at me with a warm, conspiratorial smile.

"You know, Kaedan," He said, His voice bright with the enthusiasm of a new idea, "I have been thinking about my next project. Something a bit more stable than the mechanical collective. I am considering a biological substrate this time. Self-healing, emotionally intelligent, with a deep-seated instinct for harmony and cooperation. I think it could really work. What do you say we take another holiday next week? I could use your feedback on the design."

I looked at Him. I looked at the warm, friendly, genuinely kind face of the Apex Predator of all creation. And I felt the dread—the cold, eternal, suffocating dread—settle deeper into my bones than it had ever been before.

"I would be honored, Your Majesty," I said.

And I meant it. That was the worst part. I meant every word.
