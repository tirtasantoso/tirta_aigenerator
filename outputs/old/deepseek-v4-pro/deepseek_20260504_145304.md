Blake remained there as the sun climbed, painting the rooftops in honey and rose. From below, the sound of the first festival hymn rose—a choir of men and women, their voices twining together in a melody that had been sung for a hundred years. It spoke of the Immortal King’s mercy, of the Awakening that shielded the realm, and of the eternal peace that had been won on that terrible, necessary day. Blake’s own voice joined them softly, a baritone rumble in his chest that vibrated through the baby, and she slept.

When he finally turned back to the room, Lina was watching him with an expression of profound, unwavering pride. She looked at him and at their daughter, and in her gaze was the full weight of her journey—from the resentful girl taught to hate the patriarchy, to the woman who now rested in its embrace as one rests in a sunlit field. “I never thought I could feel this,” she admitted. “This wholeness. My grandmother would call it defeat, but I know better. It’s victory. The victory of love over hate.”

Blake brought Elara to her and laid the baby in her arms again. He sat beside them on the bed, his arm wrapping around Lina’s shoulders, and they three formed a tableau that could have been sculpted for the temple walls. Outside, the world stirred to life under the benevolent tyranny of Godric’s gaze—a gaze that saw everything, that had infinite power, and that chose, every Planck second, to hold it in check so that his children could grow and laugh and love in their own, small ways.

Later that day, as the festival reached its zenith, Blake carried his daughter into the Grand Square for the traditional blessing. Thousands had gathered, men in their finest tunics, women in gowns the colors of wildflowers, children darting between the legs of the crowd. At the base of the statue, an elder priest intoned the ancient words, and the people bowed their heads. Blake stepped forward with Lina at his side, and the priest touched Elara’s brow with a drop of consecrated water. “May the Immortal King ever watch over you,” the priest said. “May you grow in grace and strength, and may you honor his design all the days of your life.”

The crowd responded with a resonant “So be it,” and Blake felt the hum of the Awakening in his chest resonate with the collective faith around him. Lina took his hand, her grip firm and certain. She no longer cast any shadow at the name of Godric. She, like the kingdom, had been remade—not broken and rebuilt, but simply shown the truth that had always been there.

As the bells rang out across Leothrone, Blake closed his eyes and sent a silent prayer of thanks to the Immortal King. The prayer was not words but a feeling—a surge of devotion that he knew, with a strange certainty, would be registered and appreciated by the one to whom it was directed. And perhaps, in some dimension beyond mortal ken, Godric did indeed smile, that same warm, charismatic smile, and turn his attention back to his eternal vigil, satisfied that another soul had been saved from the poison of pride.

The morning brightened, and the kingdom woke to another day of peace. In the modest house against the city wall, a new life opened her eyes and, not yet knowing the language of her people, simply felt the safety of the arms that held her and the hum of a benevolent power that permeated every atom of her world. She would grow, and she would learn, and she would, in time, add her own voice to the hymns.

But that is a story for another age. For now, in the warm silence of the nursery, all was as Godric willed it: perfect.
