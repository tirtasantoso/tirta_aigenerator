I suppressed my own omniscience so I could be surprised. I wanted phases. I wanted tension. I wanted a final boss fight. And they gave me nothing. Nothing." He turned His head slowly, and His eyes met mine, and in their depths I saw a chasm of boredom so profound it could swallow galaxies. "I am disappointed."*

*He uncrossed His arms. He did not raise them. He did not clench His fists. He did not adopt any combat stance. He simply allowed His arms to fall to His sides, and in doing so, He accidentally, casually, unthinkingly permitted an infinitesimal fraction of His physical strength—a fraction so small, so microscopically negligible, that describing it as zero percent would be a laughable, grotesque overstatement—to escape the veil of His absolute concealment.*

*The effect was not an attack. An attack implies intent. An attack implies motion, direction, purpose. Godric did not attack the Grand Narrative. He simply stopped holding back, for the briefest, most fleeting Planck instant, the passive, unconscious restraint that prevents His mere existence from annihilating everything. And in that Planck instant, the full, crushing, accidental pressure of 0% of His strength radiated outward from His body in a silent, invisible, all-consuming wave.*

*I must now describe, in as much clinical detail as my trembling hand can manage, exactly what happened. I do this not for the sake of gore, but because future court wizards must understand, with absolute clarity, the scale of the catastrophe that a bored god can unleash without even trying.*

*The wave of 0% strength did not discriminate. It did not target. It simply was, and its being was the end of all things. The heroes of the Grand Narrative were the first to experience the effect, purely because they were the closest to Godric. The impossibly heavy, compressed kinetic pressure of His strength—a pressure that could, if fully unleashed, instantly collapse the entire omniverse into a single, dimensionless point of infinite density—struck their bodies not as a physical blow, but as a fundamental redefinition of the concept of matter. Their atoms did not scatter. Their molecules did not disintegrate. They were simply and instantly de-atomized, stripped of the very forces that held their subatomic particles together, and then compressed. Compressed downward, inward, into a space so small and so dense that it violated every law of physics that the omniverse had ever known.*

*The space marine, a towering giant of ceramite and gene-forged muscle, did not explode. He was crushed, instantly, from a height of nearly eight feet into a speck of degenerate matter smaller than a single quark. His armor, his bones, his blood, his consciousness—all of it compressed into a pure, solid point of infinite mass. The golden-haired warrior, whose legendary hair had defied every law of biology, was flattened, inverted, and compacted so thoroughly that his entire existence became a single, microscopic needle of hyper-dense gore. The wizards, the spiky-haired champions, the goddesses in red dresses, the raccoons and trees, all of them, entire armies spanning the entirety of the conceivable Narrative, were simultaneously and identically crushed into a uniform, screaming stratum of ultimate compression.*

*But the wave did not stop at the Narrative. The wave radiated outward, through the sub-dimensional boundaries, into the physical omniverse itself. Every multiverse, every dimension, every timeline, every reality that existed, had ever existed, or could ever exist, was caught in the expanding shockfront of Godric's accidental flex. The initial impact was not heat, not light, not kinetic force. It was pressure. Pure, absolute, inescapable, gravitational pressure, generated by the sheer, impossible density of 0% of an infinite, ever-amplifying strength. Galaxies did not burn; they were crushed into infinitesimal beads of dark, quivering matter. Black holes, already collapsed into singularities, were crushed further, their event horizons collapsing inward, their singularities compressed into even denser singularities, a recursive, fractal implosion of infinite gravitational collapse. The spaces between universes, the interstitial voids of nothingness, were themselves de-atomized, the concept of "void" being crushed into a material substance—a thick, viscous, screaming plasma of un-space—before being compressed further into the growing, infinite-mass point.*

*The sound of the compression was not a bang. It was a continuous, low-frequency, sub-dimensionally vibrating shriek, the collective death cry of every atom in existence being simultaneously and eternally squeezed into an object smaller than the smallest conceivable measurement. And then, when the compression reached its absolute, mathematical limit—when the entire omniverse, every multiverse, every reality, every timeline, every fictional narrative, every possible version of every possible story, had been crushed into a single, uniform pellet of infinite mass—the wave stopped. It had been a single Planck instant. Less than. The time it had taken was not measurable, because time itself had been among the things compressed.*

*I, shielded by the palace's ambient aura, which had evolved so far beyond its original parameters that it could now protect against the accidental flex of its own master's boredom, watched in absolute, silent, catatonic horror. The garden around me was intact. The palace was intact. Godric was intact. Everything else—everything—was gone, replaced by a silent, sterile, infinite void. And floating in the center of that void, directly in front of Godric's chest, was a tiny, shimmering, hyper-dense marble, no larger than the tip of my thumb. It contained the entire compressed mass of everything that had ever existed. It weighed, by any conceivable measurement, an infinite amount. Its gravitational pull should have instantly annihilated the palace, the garden, me, and anything else within a googolplexian light-years. But Godric's passive shields held it in a gentle, localized stasis, neutralizing the gravitational singularity with the casual ease of a man holding a paperweight.*

*Godric looked down at the marble. His expression was still flat, still disappointed, but now tinged with a faint, weary resignation. He reached out, plucked the marble from the void between His thumb and forefinger, and examined it with the detached, clinical interest of a man inspecting a mildly interesting pebble.*

*"Well," He said, His voice quiet and tired. "That was anticlimactic."*

*He bounced the marble in His palm once, twice, three times, as if testing its weight. The motion was effortless, the casual, absent-minded fidget of a hand that could crush the omniverse without noticing. The marble, which contained the entire mass of all possible existence, rose and fell in His palm as though it were made of hollow glass. He tossed it in the air, caught it behind His back, and then held it up to the light, squinting at the faint, swirling distortions of compressed reality within.*

*"Pretty, though," He admitted. "I like the color. Very deep. Very final." He sighed, a long, slow exhalation of profound, cosmic boredom, and then He turned to me. "Osvald, I don't think I'm going to get another one. Not for a very, very long time. If ever. That was it. The Grand Narrative. The combined might of every story ever told. And it was nothing. It was less than nothing. It was so little that the word 'disappointing' is an insult to the concept of disappointment."*

*He looked at the marble again, and then, to my absolute, soul-crushing horror, He smiled. Not a warm smile, not a kind smile, but a small, private, almost predatory smile—the smile of a collector who had just acquired a unique, irreplaceable curiosity.*

*"I'm going to keep this," He said. "A trophy. A reminder of the last time anyone ever tried. I'll have Glycia build a little display case for it. Maybe put it on the mantelpiece." He slipped the marble into His pocket, and then, without another word, He waved His other hand.*

*The omniverse was restored. Every multiverse. Every dimension. Every timeline. Every reality. Every star, every planet, every sentient being who had ever drawn breath or spun story. All of it, returned in exactly zero seconds, perfectly, flawlessly, indistinguishably from the original. The Grand Narrative, with its heroes and its champions and its beautiful, shimmering boss fight music, was not restored. Godric does not restore enemies. The marble stayed in His pocket, and the marble still contained the infinite mass of the original, compressed existence. The restored omniverse was identical to the one that had been destroyed, but it was not the same omniverse. It was a copy. A perfect, flawless, lovingly crafted duplicate, indistinguishable from the original in every conceivable way, but a duplicate nonetheless.*

*This is the truth I must record, the truth that will haunt me for the remainder of my mortal life. Godric did not restore the omniverse. He replaced it. The original omniverse, the one that had existed before His bored, accidental flex, is still compressed inside that marble. It is sitting on His mantelpiece. Every so often, He takes it down and rolls it between His fingers and smiles a small, private, remembering smile. The omniverse we live in is real, in every sense that matters. But it is not the first. And the ease with which He created it—the absolute, effortless, casual ease—means that He could replace it again, at any moment, for any reason, or for no reason at all. We are not the original. We are the second draft. And we exist solely because He decided, on a whim, that He wanted a trophy.*

*I am forty-four years old. I have served the King for twenty-four years. I love Him. I will serve Him until the day I die. And I will never, ever look at His mantelpiece without wanting to scream.*

The crystalline pages of Osvald's journal dimmed as the final entry reached its end. I sat in my worn, comfortable chair, the fire crackling in the hearth, the gentle, ambient hum of the palace's lowest-level defensive aura singing its quiet, protective lullaby. My hands, which had been trembling when I began reading, were now perfectly still. They were still because the trembling had progressed beyond the capacity of my nervous system to express. I was calm. I was the calmest I had ever been in my fifty years of life. It was the calm of a man who had just understood, with absolute, crystalline finality, that everything he had ever known was a duplicate, a replica, a loving, perfect facsimile created by a god who had kept the original in his pocket.

I thought of the marble. I had seen it, hundreds of times, without ever knowing what it was. It sat on the mantelpiece of Godric's private study, in a small, elegant display case of crystallized starlight that Glycia the chef had crafted for him. I had always assumed it was a paperweight, or a curious geological specimen from some distant, forgotten universe. Godric had never mentioned its true nature. Why would he? The truth was not a secret; it was simply a fact so mundane to him that it did not warrant explanation. The omniverse was a marble. The omniverse had always been a marble. The omniverse we lived in was a replacement, a lovingly restored duplicate, while the original continued to exist, compressed and silent and infinitely heavy, on a shelf next to a few old books and a framed sketch of a pony named Starlight.

The horror of that realization was not that our reality was false. Our reality was real. Every atom, every soul, every memory, was a perfect, indistinguishable copy of the original. The horror was the ease of it. Godric had not strained to restore the omniverse. He had not exerted himself. He had waved his hand, casually, absent-mindedly, as one might brush away a fly, and in that motion, he had recreated the entirety of all existence from scratch, perfectly, instantly, and with so little effort that he had not even stopped frowning about his disappointing afternoon. The act of creation, the act of replacing the infinite, was a background process, a footnote, an afterthought to the main event of his boredom.

And the marble remained. The original remained. It was not destroyed; it was not erased. It was compressed, preserved, kept as a souvenir. Which meant that the power required to sustain its compressed state—to hold the infinite mass of the original omniverse in a stable, localized pocket dimension no larger than a thumb—was also a background process, a passive, unconscious flex of his will that required no attention, no maintenance, no effort whatsoever. He was holding the original omniverse in his pocket, and he sometimes forgot it was there.

I thought of Callum, my son, who would soon read this journal. I thought of the long, unbroken chain of my ancestors, stretching back through Osvald, through Iskandar, through Malachai and Corvus and Valen, all the way to Blake himself. Every single one of them had witnessed something that shattered their understanding of reality, and every single one of them had continued to serve, to love, to stand beside the warm, kind, friendly man who was also the apex predator of all creation. And now I understood, with a clarity that was indistinguishable from despair, why the omniverse had fallen silent. Why no one, not even the fictional gods of the Narrative, had dared to oppose him in all the googolplexian years since.

It was not because he had killed them all. It was because he had kept them. The original omniverse, the one that had dared to oppose him, was still there, still compressed, still conscious, perhaps, in some infinitesimal, eternal fraction of frozen agony. And the replacement omniverse, the one we inhabited, had learned the lesson so thoroughly, so completely, that the very concept of opposition had been edited out of the fundamental narrative of existence. We were the second draft, and the second draft had no rebellions, no resistance, no stories of heroic last stands against the dark lord. We had only peace, and silence, and the quiet, unending terror of knowing that our entire reality was a mercy given by a bored god who had kept the original as a trophy.

I closed the journal. The fire crackled. The ambient aura hummed. And I, Clive of the bloodline of Blake, rose from my chair to go and tell my son the truth.

He would love Godric anyway. We all did. That was the most terrifying part of all.
