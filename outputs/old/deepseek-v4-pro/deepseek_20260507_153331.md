# The Weight of Eternity

The silence that preceded the end of all things was not a silence at all. It was the held breath of a universe that had not yet learned to scream.

High Chancellor Meridian of the Celestial Concordance stood in the war chamber of the Citadel of Final Light, his twenty-seven arms folded across his crystalline chest in what his species recognized as deepest contemplation. Around him, the holographic displays showed the death of everything they had ever known. Galaxy after galaxy. Dimension after dimension. Reality after reality. All falling. All bowing. All kneeling before a single name that had become synonymous with absolute, unchallenged dominion.

Godric.

The name itself felt wrong in the mind. Too simple. Too human. And yet the being who bore it had, in the span of two decades, done what no cosmic horror, no eldritch entity, no primordial force had ever accomplished. He had united the omniverse under a single throne. Not through negotiation. Not through alliance. Through the kind of overwhelming, incomprehensible power that made the Old Ones weep in their sleeping prisons and caused the gods themselves to invent new forms of prayer.

"Show me again," Meridian commanded, his voice a chime of breaking glass.

The displays shifted. They showed the feed from the outer perimeter sensors, capturing the approach vector of five small vessels. No. Not vessels. Figures. Five humanoid shapes moving through the void of space without ships, without suits, without any visible means of propulsion. They simply walked through the vacuum as though it were a pleasant meadow, their boots finding purchase on nothing at all.

"Five of them," whispered Sub-Strategist Vell, her bioluminescent skin pulsing with fear-colors. "They only ever send five."

"And they only ever need five," Meridian finished. The mathematics of their situation had been calculated seventeen trillion times by the Concordance's most advanced quantum intellects. The result never changed. Absolute zero probability of successful resistance. The numbers did not care about their trillion-year history. The numbers did not care about their accumulated wisdom, their technological singularity, their mastery over the fundamental forces of creation. The numbers only cared about the arithmetic of impossibility.

The five figures stopped at the exact boundary of the Concordance's defensive perimeter. One of them raised a hand in greeting. The gesture was almost apologetic.

Ten thousand years before this moment, in a kingdom whose name had been erased from every history, there had been a garden.

The garden belonged to the royal palace of Olympus, and in it, a forty-five-year-old king was failing miserably at braiding his daughter's hair.

"Father, you're pulling too hard," Isabel complained, though her tone carried more affection than genuine protest. She sat on a marble bench beneath an ancient olive tree, her wedding dress pooling around her like a cloud given physical form. Twenty-two years old, with her mother's dark eyes and her father's stubborn chin, she was the very image of youthful joy.

Godric's thick fingers, more accustomed to gripping sword hilts and signing decrees, fumbled with the intricate golden strands. His brow furrowed in concentration, the salt-and-pepper of his close-trimmed beard catching the afternoon sunlight. The muscles of his forearms, visible beneath the rolled sleeves of his ceremonial tunic, shifted as he worked—a physique maintained not through vanity but through the simple fact that a king who could not lift a boulder could not protect his people from the giants that still roamed the northern wastes.

"Hush now," he murmured, his deep voice carrying the particular gentleness that only his daughters ever heard. "Your mother braided her own hair on our wedding day, and I'll not have you looking less beautiful than she did."

"You're comparing me to Mother on her wedding day?" Isabel laughed. "Father, that's a battle you've already lost."

From across the garden, Reina looked up from where she was adjusting fifteen-year-old Hebe's flower crown. The queen's silver-streaked hair framed a face that time had touched with grace rather than cruelty. "I heard that, Isabel. And for the record, your father couldn't braid my hair either. I had to redo it myself before the ceremony."

"You're supposed to be on my side," Godric called to his wife, the mock indignation in his voice undermined by the warmth that softened every line of his face.

"The king has no allies on the battlefield of daughters," Reina replied serenely. "Thorin taught me that."

As if summoned by his name, the court wizard appeared at the garden's entrance. Thorin was a man of sixty-three winters, his long white beard tucked into his belt to prevent it from tangling in his spell components, his robes a deep blue embroidered with silver constellations. Behind him, the spires of Olympus rose against a sky so perfectly clear it seemed painted by divine hands.

"Your Majesty, the guests are assembled. The high priest is ready." Thorin's voice carried the particular cadence of a man who had served three generations of kings and found genuine joy in his service. His family had stood beside the throne of Olympus for longer than most civilizations had existed. His great-great-grandfather had served Godric's great-great-grandfather, and his son would one day serve Isabel's son. This was the way of things. This was the foundation upon which Olympus had stood for millennia.

Godric finished the braid—or what could charitably be called a braid—and stepped back to admire his work. It was lopsided. Several strands had already escaped. Hebe was trying very hard not to giggle.

"Perfect," Godric declared.

"It's terrible," Isabel said, but she was smiling, and when she stood to embrace her father, she held him tight enough that he could feel the rapid flutter of her heart against his chest. "Thank you, Father. For everything."

"For everything," Godric repeated softly, and for a moment, the king of Olympus was simply a father holding his daughter on her wedding day. "You deserve the world, my star. I'm sorry I could only give you a kingdom."

"You gave me a family," Isabel whispered. "That's more than any world."

He released her reluctantly, stepping back so Reina could work her maternal magic on the braid. Thorin caught his eye and nodded toward the palace, the message clear: it was time.

The kingdom of Olympus had known peace for three hundred years. Its borders were secure. Its people prospered. The neighboring kingdoms, including the ambitious Thronegate to the east, had long accepted Olympus's supremacy through a combination of diplomatic marriages, mutually beneficial trade agreements, and the unspoken acknowledgment that challenging the Olympian military was an exercise in creative suicide. Godric had spent his reign cultivating this peace, believing—perhaps naively—that the horrors of his grandfather's wars would never need to be repeated.

He believed this right up until the moment the first fireball struck the wedding hall.

The ceremony had been beautiful. Isabel had walked down the aisle on Godric's arm, her repaired braid gleaming, her groom—a young duke from the southern provinces—weeping openly at the sight of her. The vows had been spoken. The priest had raised his hands to pronounce them wed.

And then the sky had opened.

The Thronegate forces did not announce themselves. There was no declaration of war, no ultimatum, no opportunity for surrender. One moment, the wedding hall was filled with the soft glow of candlelight and the murmured blessings of four hundred guests. The next, the ceiling was gone, replaced by a maw of fire and screaming metal.

Godric's first instinct was to shield his family. His body moved before his mind could process, throwing himself across Isabel and Reina as burning debris rained down. Hebe was shrieking somewhere to his left. The groom was already dead, a shard of enchanted steel protruding from his chest.

"THORIN!" Godric roared. "THE QUEEN! GET THE QUEEN!"

But there was no reaching anyone. The attack was systematic, devastating, designed not for conquest but for annihilation. Dragons descended from the smoke-choked sky, their riders raining destruction on the palace grounds. Soldiers in Thronegate colors poured through breaches in the walls. The Olympian guard fought back with the desperate courage of men who knew they were already dead, buying seconds with their lives.

Godric drew the ceremonial sword at his hip—not his battle blade, just a decorative piece meant to look impressive during formal occasions—and killed the first Thronegate soldier to reach his family. Then the second. Then the third. His body remembered the wars of his youth. His muscles followed patterns drilled into them decades ago. But there were too many. There were always too many.

"Godric!" Reina's voice cut through the chaos. She had Hebe clutched against her chest, one hand pressed to a wound in her side where a piece of debris had struck her. Blood stained her silver dress black in the firelight. "Isabel—"

He turned. He saw his eldest daughter pinned beneath a fallen pillar, her wedding dress now more red than white, her eyes still open, still aware, still fixed on her father's face.

"Father," she said. Or tried to say. The word came out as a bubble of blood.

The world stopped.

Godric had known battle. He had watched men die. He had killed men. But this—this was not battle. This was the negation of everything he had ever loved, compressed into a single, endless moment of watching his daughter's eyes go still.

A hand grabbed his shoulder. Thorin. The wizard's face was streaked with soot and tears, his robes torn, blood flowing from a gash across his scalp. In his free hand, he was tracing symbols in the air that burned with azure fire.

"Your Majesty, I can save you. There's a spell—my bloodline—a sanctuary—"

"I'm not leaving them," Godric snarled, trying to pull away. Reina had collapsed now, her hand still reaching for Isabel even as her own life faded. Hebe was silent, her small body limp in her mother's arms.

"You're not leaving them. You're avenging them." Thorin's voice cracked with the weight of what he was about to do. "My ancestors forbid this spell for a reason, but they never foresaw this. They never—"

A blade erupted through Thorin's chest from behind. The wizard's eyes widened, his mouth forming a perfect O of surprise. The symbols he had been tracing flared once, brilliant white, and then everything collapsed into light.

The spell, incomplete. The intention, preserved. The caster, dying even as the magic took hold.

Godric felt reality tear around him. He felt himself being pulled sideways through dimensions that had no names, through spaces that existed between the spaces that existed. The last thing he saw of Olympus—of his kingdom, his family, his world—was Thronegate soldiers standing over the bodies of his wife and daughters, laughing.

Then there was darkness.

And then there was nothing.

And then, in the nothing, something began to change.

The hidden sanctuary had been built by Thorin's ancestors in an age so distant that even the oldest dragons had forgotten its existence. It existed in a pocket dimension, a fold in reality so subtle that no god or demon had ever detected it. Its walls were lined with the accumulated magical knowledge of a hundred generations of court wizards, and at its center, suspended in a column of light that had burned continuously for over forty millennia, was the stasis field.

Godric's body came to rest within that field. The incomplete teleportation spell merged with the sanctuary's ambient magic, creating a cascade reaction that no wizard had ever predicted because no wizard had ever been foolish enough to attempt what Thorin had done in his final moments.

The stasis took hold immediately. Cellular activity ceased. Neurological function paused. The biological clock that had ticked steadily for forty-five years simply... stopped. Godric's body would never age another second. His beard would never grow. His hair would never gray beyond its current salt-and-pepper. His muscles would never atrophy. He would remain, in physical terms, exactly as he was at the moment of his preservation: a forty-five-year-old king at the peak of masculine vitality, frozen in amber.

But the preservation was only the beginning.

Deep within the stasis field, at a level so fundamental that it predated the existence of physics itself, something was amplifying. The spell's incomplete nature had interacted with the sanctuary's protective wards in ways that defied explanation, creating what could only be described as a perpetual engine of enhancement. Every quality that defined Godric's existence began to multiply. Not by increments. Not by percentages. By factors of one thousand, and then by themselves, and then by the product of that multiplication, repeating endlessly, perpetually, at a rate measured in the smallest units of time that reality could express.

One Planck second. Strength multiplied by one thousand. Intelligence multiplied by itself. Speed multiplied by the new result. Wisdom multiplied by that. Then the process began again, with each new value serving as the baseline for the next cycle of amplification.

This had been happening for ten seconds when the first superpower manifested. It was a simple ability by the standards of what would follow: complete molecular control over any substance within the wielder's awareness. A civilization-ending power if weaponized. A footnote in the catalog of what was to come.

Ten seconds later, another power emerged. Then another. Then another. Each one would have been sufficient to establish a being as the supreme deity of any pantheon. Each one was merely a single note in a symphony of omnipotence that was composing itself in the silent dark of the sanctuary.

And yet the omniverse did not end. The stasis field did not collapse under the weight of the power it contained. The sanctuary did not detonate with the force of a billion supernovas. Because one of those first powers—manifesting in the very first second of the transformation—was a concealment ability so profound, so absolute, that it perfectly contained everything that would ever follow. The power to hold back. To mask. To exist without existing. And like every other ability, this concealment multiplied in potency with each passing Planck second, forever staying ahead of the infinite power it was designed to hide.

Time passed. Not in the sanctuary—there, time had become a suggestion rather than a law—but in the universe beyond the pocket dimension. Empires rose and fell. Gods were born and died. The kingdom of Olympus faded from memory, its ruins buried beneath newer ruins, which were themselves buried beneath still newer ruins. The kingdom of Thronegate, riding the momentum of its conquest, expanded across continents, then across oceans, then—as technology and magic merged into something unprecedented—across their galaxy.

Ten thousand years passed. And in all that time, the amplification never stopped. Never paused. Never even flickered.

The merchant ship was called the *Anna's Grace*, named for a woman who had told her husband that naming a cargo vessel after her was the least romantic thing he could possibly do, and who had then cried when she saw the name painted on the hull.

Blake had been a merchant for sixteen years, ever since he'd mustered out of the planetary defense force with a bum knee, a modest pension, and a burning desire to never take orders from anyone ever again. The *Anna's Grace* was his third ship, purchased with the profits from a particularly lucrative run of medical supplies to a quarantine zone, and she was the love of his mechanical life.

"Mutton chops are not a personality," his wife Anna had told him that morning, as she did most mornings.

"They're a statement," Blake had replied, stroking the impressive growth of reddish-brown facial hair that framed his jaw. At thirty-eight, he had the sort of weathered handsomeness that came from decades of hard work and the particular stubbornness of a man who refused to concede that his forties were approaching. "A statement that says 'I am a man of substance and character.'"

"It says 'I am a man who doesn't own a razor.'"

Their son Josh, twelve years old and already showing signs of his father's merchant instincts, had looked up from his breakfast. "Dad, why don't you just shave?"

"Because then your mother would have nothing to complain about, and she'd have to confront the existential void of a perfect marriage."

Anna had thrown a piece of toast at him. Their eight-year-old daughter Rhea had giggled. It was, by all accounts, a perfectly ordinary morning in the Blake household.

Now, three weeks later, Blake was standing in the cargo hold of his ship, staring at a wall that was not supposed to be there.

The *Anna's Grace* had been running a standard route through the Outer Arm systems, delivering terraforming equipment to a fledgling colony. A routine job. A boring job. Exactly the kind of job Blake preferred, because boring jobs didn't get you shot at by pirates or impounded by customs officials with delusions of grandeur. But a navigation glitch—or what he assumed was a navigation glitch—had pulled them out of hyperlight early, depositing them in an uncharted system with a damaged hyperdrive and a dwindling fuel supply.

The uncharted system contained exactly one planet. The planet contained exactly one structure, visible from orbit as a geometric anomaly in an otherwise featureless landscape. And that structure, upon closer inspection, proved to be a building of impossible architecture that radiated magical energy like heat from a dying star.

Blake was not a wizard. He had never been a wizard. His family, as far back as anyone could trace, had been merchants, soldiers, farmers—practical people doing practical things in a galaxy that rewarded practicality. But when he touched the wall of the structure and felt it pulse beneath his fingers like a living thing, something deep in his blood sang in recognition.

He did not know—would never know—that the song came from a lineage that stretched back ten thousand years, from a bloodline that had served the kings of a forgotten kingdom, from a wizard who had died casting a spell that should have been impossible. He only knew that when he pressed his palm against the cold stone, the wall opened, and a staircase spiraled down into darkness, and at the bottom of that staircase, something was waiting.

The inner chamber was vast, cathedral-like, its walls covered in glowing script that Blake's eyes could not quite focus on. And at its center, suspended in a pillar of light that hummed at a frequency felt in the bones rather than heard, was a man.

He appeared to be sleeping. Or dead. Or something in between. His physique was remarkable—the kind of musculature that belonged on ancient statues depicting forgotten gods. His hair was dark, short, with precise lines of silver at the temples. His beard, neatly trimmed, showed the same distinguished salt-and-pepper pattern. He looked, Blake thought, like a king. Not a modern king—the galaxy hadn't had true kings in millennia—but the sort of king that existed in old stories, the kind who led armies and wrestled monsters and fathered dynasties.

Blake had no reason to touch the man. Every instinct honed by sixteen years of merchant work screamed at him to back away, to seal the chamber, to pretend he had never found this place. But the song in his blood was louder than his instincts. And when his fingers brushed the man's shoulder, the pillar of light shattered.

The man opened his eyes.

And the universe, for the first time in ten thousand years, became aware that it had a king.

Godric's omniscience did not manifest as a flood of information. It manifested as a simple, immediate knowing—the way one knows their own name, their own reflection, their own heartbeat. One moment, he was suspended in the memory of fire and blood and his daughter's dying eyes. The next, he knew everything.

He knew the name of every star that had ever burned and every star that had yet to ignite. He knew the thoughts of every sentient being in every galaxy in every dimension that comprised the omniverse. He knew the history of Thronegate—how it had risen from the ashes of Olympus, how it had expanded across the galaxy, how it now held dominion over more worlds than there were grains of sand on the beaches of his lost kingdom. He knew the name of the current king: Robert, twenty-two years old, a boy raised in the shadow of ancestors who had turned regicide into a family tradition.

And he knew the man standing before him. Blake. A merchant. A father. A husband. The last living descendant of Thorin's bloodline, though the lineage had grown so diluted over ten millennia that no genealogist in the galaxy could have traced it. Blake himself had no idea of his heritage. He had grown up in a trading family on a backwater world, had never once suspected that his ancestors had once stood beside thrones and shaped the destinies of nations.

All of this, Godric knew.

All of this, and so much more.

"Where am I?" Blake asked, his voice steady despite the impossibility of what he was witnessing. This was a man, Godric understood, who had faced down pirates and customs agents and the occasional tax auditor. A glowing man appearing from a pillar of light was unusual, but the galaxy was full of unusual things. Merchants learned to adapt.

"You are in the sanctuary of the Olympian court wizards," Godric replied. His voice, after ten thousand years of silence, emerged as a resonant baritone that carried the weight of his amplification. "You are the one who woke me. What is your name?"

"Blake. Captain of the *Anna's Grace*." A pause. "Are you... okay? You've been in that light thing for a while, I'm guessing."

Godric almost smiled. Almost. The warmth that had once come so naturally to him flickered somewhere deep in his chest, but it could not reach the surface. The surface was still covered in the ashes of his family. "Longer than you can imagine, Blake. But I am... functional. And I have a message that needs to be delivered."

"A message?"

"Yes. To King Robert of Thronegate." The name tasted like poison on Godric's tongue, but his voice remained perfectly level. "Tell him that the true King of Olympus has returned. Tell him that I challenge his right to rule. Tell him that in three days, I will face his entire military—every soldier, every weapon, every dragon, every mage—and I will defeat them. And when I have done so, he will abdicate his throne, or I will take it from his cooling hands."

Blake stared at him. His merchant's mind, trained to calculate odds and assess risks, was clearly struggling with the sheer scale of what he was hearing. "That's... a big ask for someone who just woke up from a magic nap. You know Thronegate has the largest military in the galaxy, right? They've got super-soldiers. Robot armies. Dragons that can eat continents."

"I know."

"And you want to fight all of them? By yourself?"

"I will not fight them," Godric corrected. "I will simply... end them. There is a difference."

The casual certainty in his voice was more terrifying than any threat could have been. Blake, who had spent his adult life learning to read people, heard no boast in those words. No bluster. No false confidence. Just a statement of fact, delivered with the same tone one might use to announce that water was wet or space was cold.

"Okay," Blake said slowly. "Okay. I can... I can deliver a message, I guess. But how do I explain this to my wife?"

"She will believe you," Godric said, because he knew she would, because he knew everything. "And she will be frightened. That is acceptable. Fear is a rational response to what is about to happen."

King Robert of Thronegate received the message with the particular disdain that twenty-two-year-old monarchs who had never known true hardship reserved for anything they considered beneath their attention. His throne room was a monument to Thronegate's galactic supremacy—walls of living crystal that shifted through every color in the spectrum, floors of polished obsidian that reflected the constellations visible through the transparent ceiling, and a throne carved from the fused bones of the last great space dragon that had dared challenge Thronegate's dominion.

"A merchant brought this message?" Robert asked, holding the holographic recording that Blake had reluctantly agreed to deliver. "A merchant from some backwater trading route claims to speak for a king of a kingdom that doesn't exist?"

General Aldrich, commander of Thronegate's military forces, shifted his weight. At fifty-seven, he was old enough to remember a time before Thronegate's absolute supremacy, and that memory made him more cautious than his king. "The merchant's ship does have some unusual energy signatures. And the message itself... the language used is archaic. Pre-Thronegate. Possibly even pre-Expansion era."

"So?"

"So it might be worth investigating. Before we dismiss it entirely."

Robert waved a dismissive hand. His crown—a circlet of compressed neutronium that symbolized his absolute authority—glinted in the shifting light. "Fine. Investigate. But whatever this is, it's not a threat. No one can challenge the Thronegate military. We have billions of super-soldiers. We have the mightiest magical corps in galactic history. We have robot armies that can disassemble planets. We have dragons that can breathe novas. We have ninjas so fast they experience time backwards." He leaned forward, his young face hardening into an expression he had practiced in mirrors since childhood. "Whoever this 'Godric' thinks he is, he's about to learn what happens to those who challenge Thronegate."

But General Aldrich, whose instincts had kept him alive through three wars and seventeen assassination attempts, did not share his king's confidence. He ordered the military to maximum readiness. He deployed scouts to the coordinates where the *Anna's Grace* had been intercepted. He prepared for every contingency he could imagine.

It would not be enough.

It could never have been enough.

The plain of Calaxis was the largest uninhabited landmass on the Thronegate homeworld, a continent-sized stretch of volcanic rock and ancient impact craters that had been used for military exercises for thousands of years. On the morning of the confrontation, it held the entire assembled might of the Thronegate military.

The numbers defied comprehension. Three billion super-soldiers, each one genetically enhanced to the point where they could punch through starship hulls and survive in hard vacuum. Two billion combat robots, ranging from human-sized infiltrator units to continent-class annihilation platforms. Five hundred thousand battle mages, each capable of leveling cities with a gesture. Fifty thousand dragons, their scales shimmering with absorbed starlight, their breaths hot enough to ignite fusion reactions. And the ninjas—the legendary Shadow Corps, whose members had mastered temporal manipulation to such a degree that they experienced combat in reverse, seeing the effects of their strikes before they launched them.

Thronegate had never assembled this much force in one place. It had never needed to. The deployment alone had required a logistical effort that would have bankrupted a lesser civilization. But General Aldrich had insisted, and King Robert, despite his dismissive attitude, had ultimately agreed. Better, he had said, to make an example of this so-called king. Better to show the galaxy what happened to those who challenged Thronegate.

Godric walked onto the plain alone.

He wore no armor. He carried no weapons. He had not aged a day from the moment the stasis took him, and the king who faced the assembled might of a galactic empire was physically identical to the king who had failed to braid his daughter's hair ten thousand years ago. The same close-cropped hair with its distinguished silvering at the temples. The same neatly trimmed salt-and-pepper beard. The same powerful physique, muscles moving beneath his simple tunic with the fluid grace of restrained power.

Blake and Anna watched from a hillside three miles away, at what they believed was a safe distance. Anna was clutching her husband's arm hard enough to bruise. Her face was pale beneath her trader's tan, and she kept repeating a prayer she had learned as a child on her homeworld, a prayer to a god she had stopped believing in decades ago.

"This is insane," she whispered. "This is completely insane. He's going to die. We're going to die. The whole planet is going to—"

"Shh," Blake said. His eyes were fixed on the distant figure of Godric, standing alone against an ocean of military might. "Just watch."

"What am I watching for? A slaughter?"

"No," Blake said slowly. The song in his blood was louder now, a resonant hum that seemed to vibrate in time with something vast and ancient. "A demonstration."

The generals of Thronegate, observing from their command ships in low orbit, gave the order to attack. The robot armies moved first—a wave of metal and energy weapons that would have overwhelmed any conventional defense in seconds. Behind them came the super-soldiers, their enhanced bodies moving at speeds that created sonic booms with every step. Above them wheeled the dragons, their riders ready to unleash continent-shattering breath weapons. And weaving through the chaos, invisible to sensors and eyes alike, came the Shadow Corps, already striking in a time-frame that should have been impossible to counter.

Godric did not move.

He did not raise his hands. He did not speak a word of power. He did not even shift his stance. He simply stood there, his expression one of mild contemplation, as if he were considering what to have for lunch.

The first wave of robots reached him—and ceased to exist.

It was not that they were destroyed. Destruction implied a process, a transition from whole to broken, from functional to ruined. This was something else entirely. The robots simply stopped being. One moment they were there, their weapon arrays locking onto their target. The next, they were gone, erased so completely that not even subatomic particles remained to mark their passing.

The super-soldiers fared no better. Their enhanced bodies, capable of surviving atmospheric re-entry and weapons that could crack continental plates, unraveled like smoke in a hurricane. The energy that comprised their very existence—the chemical bonds of their molecules, the electrical impulses of their nervous systems, the quantum information that defined their consciousness—all of it simply... stopped. In perfect, absolute silence.

The dragons fell from the sky like extinguished stars, their immense bodies winking out of existence as if they had never been real at all. Their riders, still in their saddles, dissolved into nothing before they could even register their mounts' disappearance.

The Shadow Corps experienced something worse. Their temporal manipulation abilities, which allowed them to perceive and affect events in reverse, gave them a unique perspective on their own annihilation. They experienced the ending before the beginning. They felt themselves unmade in a direction that their minds had not evolved to process. Their final moments—or first moments, depending on one's temporal frame of reference—were filled with a horror so profound that it transcended language, that it registered as pure, undiluted existential terror.

And the mages—the most powerful magical beings in the galaxy, each one a walking arsenal of reality-altering power—felt their connection to magic itself sever. Not slowly. Not through any countering spell. The magic simply refused to acknowledge their existence anymore. Their carefully woven defensive wards collapsed not because they were broken, but because they forgot they had ever been cast. Their offensive spells fizzled into nothing because the universe no longer recognized their authority to command it.

Thirty seconds.

That was how long it took for the greatest military force ever assembled to cease to be.

Thirty seconds, and Godric had not moved a single muscle. Had not spoken. Had not even blinked.

From the command ships in orbit, General Aldrich watched his entire career, his entire civilization's military tradition, his entire understanding of how the universe worked, vanish in less time than it took to brew a cup of tea. His tactical displays showed nothing but empty space where billions of soldiers had stood moments before. His communication channels were silent, because there was no one left to communicate with. His sensor officers were weeping, and he did not have the presence of mind to reprimand them.

King Robert, watching from his palace through a holographic feed, soiled himself. He was twenty-two years old, and he had just watched his civilization's invincibility become a joke. He was twenty-two years old, and he knew, with absolute certainty, that he was going to die.

"Bring me more wine," he croaked to a servant who was no longer in the room, who had fled the moment the feed began showing the impossible. "Bring me... bring me..."

He did not finish the sentence. He would never finish another sentence. Two days later, his guards would find him in his bedchamber, a dagger in his heart and a note on his desk that read simply: "I abdicate."

Godric turned away from the empty plain and began the long walk back to the hillside where Blake and Anna waited. His expression had not changed. The mild contemplation remained, as if he had just completed a moderately interesting puzzle and was already moving on to the next.

When he reached them, Anna was crying. Blake was not, but his face had taken on the grey pallor of a man who had just witnessed something that his mind was desperately trying to reject.

"There," Godric said, his voice carrying no particular weight, as if he were commenting on the weather. "That's done."

"Done," Blake repeated. The word felt absurd in his mouth. "You... you just..."

"I held back, of course." Godric's tone was almost conversational. "The full extent would have been... excessive. And I have plans for Thronegate that require a planet to rule, not a debris field."

Anna made a sound that was half-laugh, half-sob. "'Excessive.' He calls that 'excessive.'"

Godric looked at her, and for just a moment, something flickered behind his eyes—a ghost of the warmth that had once defined him, the fatherly gentleness that had been burned away in the fire of Olympus's fall. "You are frightened. That's natural. But you need not be. You and your husband... you woke me. You freed me. I do not forget debts, Anna, and I do not harm those who have helped me." A pause. "Blake. Your ancestor would be proud of you."

"My ancestor?" Blake frowned. "I don't..."

But Godric was already walking again, toward the distant spires of the Thronegate capital, toward a throne that was waiting for its true king, toward a future that would reshape the omniverse itself.

The suicide of King Robert was, in Godric's assessment, a tidy solution to an awkward political problem. He attended the funeral—not because he felt any respect for the dead king, but because he understood the power of symbols. He stood. He did not draw a weapon. He did not summon power. He simply stood, and the air in the throne room changed. Not temperature. Not pressure. Something deeper. Something that made my wizard's blood—the blood of Blake—scream in recognition of an order of magnitude it had never been designed to perceive.

"Let me show you what you're trying to consume," Godric said. And he released the tiniest, most infinitesimal fraction of his magical presence.

The numbers first. I need you to understand the numbers, because I didn't, not until that moment. I had grown up believing that Godric kept court wizards because he lacked magical aptitude. I had assumed his power was physical, temporal, conceptual—not arcane. The court wizard's purpose, I had believed, was to provide the magical protection that the king himself could not.

What I learned, in that instant, was that Godric's magical traits—his mana, his offense, his defense, his control—had been multiplying every Planck second for eighty thousand years. Not by a thousand. That number had been left behind so long ago that the interval during which it had been a thousand was indistinguishable from zero on the cosmic scale. For age upon age, his magic had been multiplying at rates that exceeded rates that exceeded rates, an infinite recursive acceleration that had produced a quantity of magical power that could not be expressed in any notation that mortal minds could conceive.

And the fraction he released was zero-point-zero-zero—an infinite string of zeroes followed by a one so distant that the cosmic horizon would swallow it—percent of his total.

The Null Lich screamed.

It was not a scream of pain. It was a scream of comprehension. Because the Lich, as a being of magical negation, was uniquely equipped to perceive the magnitude of what it was facing. It saw the ocean—no, it saw the thing that contained all oceans, the concept of wetness, the idea of depth—and it understood that its own power, which had silenced the magic of gods, was to Godric's magic as a single grain of sand was to every beach on every world in every universe that had ever existed.

"The gap," the Lich choked out, its voice cracking into a wheeze, "the gap is—it's not a gap, it's a—it's not even—"

"I know," Godric said, and there was something almost like pity in his voice. "It's cruel, really. You came all this way, prepared your whole existence for this moment, and you never had a chance. But I want you to understand something before you go."

He stepped down from the throne dais. He walked past me—I was frozen, my staff hanging limp in my grip, my eyes wide, my mind a white void of disbelief—and he stopped directly in front of the Null Lich. The Lich was taller than him, skeletally vast, its robes of silenced spells swirling in an unfelt wind. And yet it cringed. It tried to step backward and found that it could not move.

"I do not keep court wizards because I need their magic," Godric said. "I keep them because they are my family. Thorin was my best friend before the stasis. Blake was my best friend after it. Their descendants—your ancestors, Gorm—have stood beside me for eighty thousand years. They provide counsel. They provide companionship. They provide the mortal perspective that keeps me grounded. But magic? Protection from magical threats?"

He reached out and, with a single fingertip, touched the Null Lich's skull.

The Lich did not die. Dying would have been merciful. Instead, the Lich experienced a complete, instantaneous inversion of its entire purpose. The magic it had consumed over eons—every spell, every enchantment, every scrap of arcane energy it had silenced—was restored to existence simultaneously. The Lich's body, which was designed to contain only absence, was suddenly filled with presence. Every silenced spell screamed back into being at once. Every consumed mage's last incantation replayed. Every dead enchantment bloomed.

And the Lich, a creature of void, was forced to become a vessel of infinite light.

It shattered. But the shattering was not an end—it was a recursion. The Lich shattered, and then the shards shattered, and then the shards of shards shattered, each iteration accompanied by a fresh wave of restored magic, a fresh agony of being, a fresh impossible contradiction between what the Lich was and what Godric's touch had forced it to hold. The cycle continued for exactly as long as the Lich could remain conscious, and because Godric had ensured it would remain conscious for the entirety of the process, it continued for a very, very long subjective time.

When it was over—when the final shard of the final shard had dissolved into light—there was nothing left of the Null Lich. Not a memory. Not an echo. Not a trace. The magic it had consumed was restored. The silence it had spread was revoked. Its existence was concluded with a thoroughness that bordered on the obsessive.

Godric turned to me. His expression was—Gods help me—concerned. "Gorm? You've gone pale."

I tried to speak. My mouth moved, but no sound came out. My staff clattered to the floor. My legs, which had carried me through years of training, through battles, through the proud assumption that I was the king's magical defender, folded beneath me.

"Ah," Godric said softly. "I think I understand. This is about the court wizard thing."

I managed a nod.

He knelt beside me—the King of the Omniverse, the being whose magic could erase infinite copies of the entire omniverse with an infinitesimal fraction of his power, knelt beside a twenty-year-old boy who had just realized his entire purpose was a comforting lie. His grey eyes were kind. His hand on my shoulder was warm.

"I should have told you earlier. I'm sorry, Gorm. I just... the court wizard tradition is important to me. Your family is important to me. I never wanted you to feel useless. You're not useless. You're my family. Your magic—your real magic, the magic you've trained so hard to master—is genuinely impressive. The thousandfold amplification has made you one of the most powerful wizards in the omniverse. I'm proud of you."

"But it's nothing," I whispered. "Compared to you. It's nothing."

Godric was quiet for a moment. Then he said, very gently, "Compared to me, everything is nothing. That's not a reflection on you. That's a reflection on... what happened to me. What the stasis did. What eighty thousand years of amplification have produced. I can't change it. I can't even fully comprehend it anymore—and I have absolute omniscience. That should tell you something about the scale we're dealing with."

He helped me to my feet. His strength was, as always, perfectly calibrated to not harm me.

"The court wizard's role," he continued, "is not to protect me from magic. It never was. It's to stand beside me. To advise me. To tell me when I'm being an idiot—which, according to your ancestors, is more often than I'd like to admit. And to remind me, every day, that I was mortal once. That I had friends. That I was loved. That is worth more than any spell, Gorm. That is worth everything."

I looked at him—this man who had been my favorite uncle, my king, my protector—and I saw, for the first time, what he truly was. Not a mask. Not a deception. He was genuinely warm, genuinely kind, genuinely fatherly. And he was also a being whose magic, if he ever stopped concealing it, would annihilate the omniverse so thoroughly that the concept of existence would become a myth.

The two truths did not cancel each other out. They coexisted. And I, Gorm the court wizard, had to learn to live with both.

That night, I stood on the balcony of my quarters and stared at the stars. The city of Thronegate sparkled below me, peaceful, protected, happy. The omniverse spun on its axis, attended by a king who loved his people. And somewhere, in some dark corner of some hostile universe, another being was already planning its attack on that king. It did not know that it was not a threat. It did not know that its plans had already been known, already been rendered obsolete, already been filed away in the infinite archives of Godric's omniscience. It did not know that the gap between its most powerful weapon and the being it targeted was so vast that the word "gap" was a cosmic joke.

I knew. And I would stand beside my king regardless. Not because I could protect him—that was a child's fantasy, and I was not a child anymore. But because he asked me to. Because he loved my family. Because he had knelt beside me when I fell.

Opposing Godric ends in agony. I saw it happen. I saw a being designed to consume magic be consumed instead, shattered across recursions until even its shards had shards. The Null Lich came to the throne room with purpose and power, and it left as less than nothing. That is the fate of every enemy. That is the certainty that underpins the peace we enjoy.

My staff still rests beside my bed. My robes still hang in my closet. Tomorrow, I will stand at Godric's right hand, and I will offer counsel, and I will laugh at his jokes, and I will be his family.

But I will never again believe that my magic protects him. I will know, with the absolute certainty of someone who has seen the truth, that the king protects me. And that knowledge is a weight I will carry for the rest of my life.
