# The Last King of Olympus

The sky above Olympus burned gold and amber, a canopy of silk banners rippling in the warm autumn breeze, and Godric stood at the altar with his hands clasped behind his back, watching his daughter Isabel practically vibrate with joy. She was twenty-two, radiant in her wedding gown, her dark hair woven with threads of pearl and myrtle. The courtyard of the royal palace had been transformed into a cathedral of flowers—roses and honeysuckle draped from every column, petals strewn across the marble in drifts of crimson and cream. Godric's chest swelled with something so pure it almost hurt.

He was forty-five years old, a king in his prime, his shoulders broad and his arms thick with the muscle of a man who still trained with his guards every morning. His beard, salt-and-pepper and trimmed neat against his jaw, caught the sunlight when he turned his head to find his wife Reina in the crowd. She was dabbing at her eyes with a handkerchief, and beside her, fifteen-year-old Hebe was trying very hard to look bored and failing entirely. Godric winked at his youngest daughter, and she broke into a grin.

"You're crying," Thorin muttered from behind him, close enough that only Godric could hear.

"I am not," Godric murmured back, not turning around.

"Your shoulders are shaking."

"It's the pollen."

Thorin snorted. The court wizard had been Godric's best friend since they were both boys scraping their knees on the training yard stones. His family had served the kings of Olympus for more generations than the histories could count, their bloodline carrying secrets of magic that had built and sustained the kingdom for millennia. Thorin was lean where Godric was broad, his fingers perpetually stained with ink and arcane residue, his robes smelling faintly of ozone and old parchment. He stepped up beside the king now, arms crossed, watching Isabel take her place before the altar.

"She looks happy," Thorin said quietly.

Godric nodded, not trusting his voice.

The groom was a good man—a knight of Olympus named Aldric who had served in Godric's personal guard for six years. He had kind eyes and steady hands, and when he looked at Isabel, his whole face softened like wax near a flame. Godric had given his blessing without hesitation. In his heart, he had already begun imagining grandchildren, imagining the halls of Olympus filled with the sound of small feet, imagining growing old beside Reina while their daughters built families of their own.

He stepped forward to begin the ceremony, raising his hands to quiet the assembled crowd. The music faded. The breeze stilled.

"My people," Godric began, his voice carrying easily across the courtyard, warm and fatherly and full of the easy authority that came from two decades of just rule. "Today we gather to witness the union of my daughter, Isabel, and the man who has proven himself worthy of her heart. In the traditions of Olympus, marriage is not merely a contract—it is a promise. A promise to stand together against the darkness. A promise to nurture light in one another. A promise that love, freely given, is the strongest foundation any kingdom can have."

He paused, looking out over the faces of his subjects. Farmers and merchants, soldiers and artisans, all gathered in their finest clothes, all wearing expressions of genuine joy. Olympus was not the largest kingdom, nor the wealthiest, but it was good. It was kind. Godric had spent his reign ensuring that.

Isabel caught his eye and mouthed, *I love you, Father*.

Godric's heart cracked open with tenderness. He smiled at his daughter, and for one perfect moment, the world was exactly as it should be.

Then the sky turned black.

---

The first fireball struck the palace's eastern tower with a sound like the world splitting in half. The shockwave threw Godric forward, and he caught himself on the altar, stone scraping his palms raw. Screams erupted from the crowd. He spun around, ears ringing, and saw the horizon devoured by flame.

Dragons. Six of them, scales gleaming obsidian and crimson, their wingspans wide enough to blot out the sun. They descended on Olympus with jaws agape, spewing liquid fire that turned marble to slag and flesh to ash. Behind them came the banners—the black and silver standard of Thronegate, the neighboring kingdom that had always watched Olympus with hungry eyes.

"No," Godric breathed. "No, no—"

The second volley hit the courtyard.

Godric never remembered the exact sequence of what happened next. His memory of those moments came in fragments, shards of horror that he would spend the next ten thousand years turning over and over in his mind like broken glass. Reina's face, her mouth open in a scream he couldn't hear. Hebe clutching her mother's sleeve. Isabel running toward him, her wedding dress catching fire from an ember, her beautiful dark hair alight.

He ran for her. He ran until his legs burned, until his lungs screamed, but the distance between them stretched and stretched like a nightmare. A soldier of Thronegate—one of their infamous super-soldiers, enhanced beyond human limits—appeared between them. Godric saw the blade descend. He saw his daughter fall.

The sound that tore from his throat was not human.

He fought. Gods, he fought. He seized a sword from a fallen guard and cut down three soldiers before a fourth drove a spear through his shoulder. He killed that one too, snapping the spear shaft and using the broken end to drive through the man's visor. Blood sheeted across the marble. Bodies piled around him. But there were always more, an endless tide of black and silver, and somewhere in the chaos he saw Thorin fall to his knees with a Thronegate blade through his chest.

"THORIN!"

The wizard's eyes found his. Blood bubbled on Thorin's lips, but his hands were moving, tracing patterns in the air that glowed with a sickly, forbidden light. Godric recognized the gestures. He had seen them once before, decades ago, when Thorin's father had explained the secret magic of their bloodline—a spell that could tear a man from the fabric of reality and deposit him elsewhere. A teleportation spell so dangerous it had been forbidden for generations.

"Don't," Godric choked out, crawling toward his friend. "Thorin, don't—"

"Olympus must survive," Thorin gasped. His fingers kept moving, the light growing brighter, more unstable. "The king must survive. You must—"

A Thronegate ninja materialized behind Thorin, moving so fast that time itself seemed to ripple. The blade entered the wizard's spine and exited through his chest.

The spell shattered.

Godric felt reality tear open around him—a wound in the fabric of existence, bleeding light and silence. The last thing he saw was Thorin's body dropping, the wizard's eyes already empty. The last thing he heard was the roar of dragon fire consuming everything he had ever loved.

Then the world folded in on itself, and Godric ceased to be.

---

He did not die.

The incomplete spell deposited him in a place that was not a place—a sanctuary hidden between the cracks of reality, a pocket dimension that Thorin's ancestors had built as a final refuge. It was a circular chamber of black stone, lit by a single eternal flame that burned at its center. The walls were carved with the history of Olympus, images of kings and wizards stretching back to the kingdom's founding.

Godric collapsed on the stone floor, blood pooling beneath him. His shoulder was ruined. His heart was worse.

"Reina," he whispered. "Isabel. Hebe. Thorin."

The names tasted like ashes.

Darkness crept into the edges of his vision. He welcomed it. Let it come. Let it take him. There was nothing left to live for. No family. No kingdom. No friend. Olympus was ash, and he was the king of nothing.

But the darkness did not take him.

Instead, something else did.

---

It began as a warmth spreading through his chest, entirely unlike anything he had ever felt. The pain in his shoulder vanished. The wound knitted itself closed with a sound like silk tearing. Godric's eyes snapped open, and he realized he was floating—rising from the stone floor as if gravity had forgotten him.

The eternal flame at the chamber's center flared white-hot, then cold, then every color Godric had ever seen and a thousand he had never imagined. His body locked rigid. A scream built in his throat but never escaped, because his lungs had stopped needing air, because his heart had stopped needing to beat, because every cell in his body was being rewritten by a force too vast and too ancient to comprehend.

Time fractured.

He felt his aging stop—a sensation like a clock shattering, like the gears of mortality grinding to a halt. Immortality settled into his bones, absolute and irrevocable. He would never need sleep again. Never need food, never need water, never need air. His body became a monument, a perfect vessel preserved at the moment of his deepest anguish.

Then the amplification began.

His strength multiplied by a thousand in the first Planck second—the smallest unit of time that existed, the time it took for light to travel a Planck length. Before that first unit of time could complete, his strength multiplied by a thousand again. And again. And again. Every Planck second, without exception, forever.

His endurance followed. His wisdom. His speed. His senses. His intelligence. Each attribute a thousand times greater with every passing Planck second, the growth compounding on itself, an exponential cascade that had no ceiling, no limit, no end.

But it did not stop there.

The superpowers came next.

In the first second after the transformation began, a new ability blossomed within him—the power to unmake matter at a fundamental level, to dissolve the bonds between atoms with a thought. This power alone, in the instant of its manifestation, could have caused the extinction of an entire civilization. But it did not end there. Like his physical attributes, the potency of this power multiplied by a thousand with every Planck second. By the time the first full second had passed, the power had amplified itself more times than there were stars in the universe.

The second second brought a new power. Then the third. Then the fourth. Every single second, a new apocalyptic superpower manifested within him, each one capable of instant civilizational extinction in its base form, each one then multiplying in potency at the same incomprehensible rate as all the others.

The chamber should have been destroyed. The planet should have been destroyed. The universe—the omniverse itself—should have been annihilated by the sheer pressure of his existence. His aura alone, uncontained, would have erased reality from the tapestry of creation.

But the first power that manifested had been the power of concealment.

He could hold back his presence. He could compress his aura into a perfect singularity, invisible and undetectable. And just like every other ability, this concealment grew stronger with every passing Planck second, flawlessly containing the ever-expanding ocean of his power. The omniverse continued to exist not because Godric was weak, but because he was strong enough to prevent its destruction.

And there was more. A deeper layer. A final, terrible perfection.

Godric possessed no weaknesses.

This was fundamental truth, woven into the very fabric of his transformed existence. He was absolute. Inviolable. Complete.

But the omniverse was vast, filled with arrogant beings who could not comprehend such perfection. And so the defense mechanism was woven into his transformation—a paradox of punishment. The moment any sentient being anywhere in creation dared to think that Godric had a weakness, even as a passing, idle thought, even before they could begin to plan or prepare or act, the mechanism would trigger. The rate of Godric's amplification would permanently increase to an even more unfathomable level. He would gain permanent immunity to whatever false weakness they had imagined. And he would gain perfect mastery over whatever method they might have used to exploit it, capable of wielding it at any magnitude, while simultaneously denying the very concept of its existence to all others in creation unless he chose otherwise.

The defense was passive, absolute, and utterly merciless. It did not require Godric's attention or awareness. It simply was—a law of existence as fundamental as gravity, as inescapable as entropy. The very act of challenging his perfection fed his power. The very thought of finding a flaw sealed the thinker's fate.

And Godric, suspended in his stasis, felt all of this happen. He felt the power cascade through him, felt his consciousness expand to encompass everything that was or ever could be. Omniscience opened before him like an endless book, every page visible at once, every word known in the instant of its writing. He saw the past in its entirety. He saw the future in its every branching path. He saw every atom, every soul, every thought that had ever been thought or ever would be.

And in that infinite knowing, he saw the faces of his family as they died.

Warmth, friendliness, kindness—these things burned away in the furnace of his grief. What emerged from that fire was something harder. Something colder. Something that had no room for mercy because mercy had been denied to him.

Ten thousand years passed in the space between his heartbeats.

When the merchant finally found him, Godric was ready.

The ruined sanctuary had been buried beneath a mountain on a forgotten world for so long that even the stones were ancient. Blake wiped sweat from his brow with a rag that had once been white and was now the color of old tea, his mutton chops bristling as he surveyed the collapsed tunnel ahead. His excavation crew had abandoned this site three days ago, claiming the readings were a fluke, but Blake's instincts said otherwise. His instincts had built his trading empire from nothing, and he trusted them.

Besides, Anna had told him to follow his gut. Anna was always right.

He was thirty-eight years old, a merchant of moderate success and immoderate stubbornness, married to the only woman who had ever been able to match his temper and blessed with two children who inherited the same gift. Josh was twelve and already trying to negotiate his allowance. Rhea was eight and had learned that batting her eyelashes could get her out of trouble. Blake loved them with a ferocity that sometimes surprised him, a devotion that made the long trading voyages worth every hardship.

The tunnel narrowed to a crack, and Blake squeezed through sideways, his lantern held ahead of him. The air on the other side was cold and dry, smelling of nothing at all—no rot, no dust, no life. The silence was absolute.

His lantern light fell on walls covered in carvings. Images of kings and wizards and something that looked like a flame. The artistry was unlike anything Blake had ever seen, flowing and organic, as if the stone had been shaped by hands that knew no limitation.

"Hello?" he called, his voice swallowed by the darkness.

The chamber opened before him. At its center, a single flame burned above a pedestal of black stone. And on that pedestal lay a man.

Blake's breath caught. The man was massive—taller than any human Blake had ever seen, with shoulders like a cliff face and arms corded with muscle that looked carved rather than grown. His beard was salt-and-pepper, neat against a jaw that could have been chiseled. His hair was short and dark, streaked with silver at the temples. His eyes were closed.

He looked like a king.

"Are you alive?" Blake whispered, stepping closer. "Can you hear me?"

He reached out, his fingers trembling, and touched the man's shoulder.

The eyes opened.

Blake stumbled backward with a cry. The man's eyes were not eyes—they were windows into something infinite, depths that contained galaxies and eons, that contained the death of stars and the birth of universes. Blake felt his mind reel, felt his sanity strain against the weight of what he was seeing, and then the man blinked, and the infinite receded.

The man sat up slowly, brushing ten thousand years of dust from his shoulders like it was nothing.

"You," he said, and his voice was a thousand voices speaking in harmony, "are a descendant of Thorin."

Blake's mouth worked soundlessly. "I—what? My name is Blake. I'm just a merchant. I was looking for salvage. Who are you?"

The man stood. The chamber seemed to shrink around him, unable to contain his presence. He looked at Blake with something that might have been recognition, might have been curiosity, might have been the first warmth he had felt in ten millennia.

"I am Godric," he said. "The last king of Olympus."

And then, because omniscience had already filled his mind with the knowledge of everything that had happened in his absence—the rise of Thronegate, the forgetting of Olympus, the galaxy of arrogance that had grown from the ashes of his home—Godric smiled. It was not a warm smile. It was not a kind smile. It was the smile of a man who had been waiting ten thousand years to settle a debt.

"Blake," he said, "I have a message for the king of Thronegate. And you are going to help me deliver it."

Three days later, the court of King Robert received a communication that should have been impossible. It materialized in the air above the throne room, written in letters of golden fire that burned without consuming:

*To Robert, King of Thronegate*

*Your ancestors destroyed my kingdom and murdered my family. I do not seek reparation. I seek conquest. In exactly three days, I will come to your world. I will face your entire military—every super-soldier, every mage, every machine, every dragon, every assassin. If I defeat them, you will abdicate your throne. If I fail, you may do with me as you wish.*

*This is the only mercy I will offer. Use your three days wisely.*

*—Godric, King of Olympus*

King Robert was twenty-two years old, handsome in the way of the young and untested, and he had never known a day of genuine fear in his life. He read the message three times, his brow furrowing, and then he laughed.

"Olympus," he said, turning to his generals. "Have any of you heard of such a place?"

Blank faces. Shaking heads.

"It's a fairy tale," Robert decided. "Some madman with a flare for theatrics. But very well. If he wants to face our military, let him. Gather every force we have. Make an example of this fool."

The generals bowed and departed to carry out his orders. And in the three days that followed, Thronegate assembled the mightiest military force the galaxy had ever seen.

---

The battlefield was a continent on Thronegate's homeworld, a barren expanse of black rock chosen specifically because there was nothing there to destroy. The Thronegate forces numbered in the billions—super-soldiers enhanced by genetic engineering and arcane rituals, their bodies capable of surviving orbital reentry and their fists capable of shattering mountains. Behind them stood the mage corps, wizards whose power had been refined over ten thousand years of magical advancement, their spells capable of unraveling reality itself. Above them hovered the robotic armies, machines of liquid metal and plasma weaponry, their intelligence networks so sophisticated that they processed battlefield data faster than light traveled. The skies were darkened by dragons—genuine dragons, bred for war, their scales capable of withstanding nuclear blasts and their breath hot enough to fuse silicon into glass. And scattered throughout the formation, nearly invisible, were the ninjas—assassins so fast that their movements bent time, so precise that they could sever a single neuron without disturbing its neighbors.

King Robert watched from a command ship in low orbit, surrounded by screens that showed him every angle of the battlefield. He wore his ceremonial armor, polished to a mirror shine. He was still smiling.

"No sign of the enemy yet," his tactical officer reported.

"Of course not," Robert said. "He's probably sobered up and fled."

The officers chuckled. The atmosphere in the command ship was relaxed, almost festive. Bets had been placed on how long the "king of Olympus" would last—the longest guess was thirty seconds.

Down on the surface, General Aldric Voss stood at the head of the army, his enhanced body humming with barely contained power. He had served Thronegate for forty years, had conquered seventeen systems, had personally killed more enemies than he could remember. He felt no fear. He felt nothing but mild curiosity and the faint annoyance of a man whose time was being wasted.

The wind picked up. A single figure appeared on the horizon.

---

Godric walked across the black rock at a leisurely pace, his boots making no sound. He wore simple clothing—a dark tunic, dark trousers, no armor, no weapons. His hands were at his sides. His expression was calm.

Behind him, at a safe distance, Blake and Anna huddled together on a ridge overlooking the battlefield. They had insisted on coming. Godric had not argued. He had simply told them to stay back and watch.

"He's going to die," Anna whispered, her hand gripping Blake's arm. "There are billions of them. He's one man."

Blake said nothing. He had seen Godric's eyes in that chamber. He had felt something in his blood stir when he touched the sleeping king—something ancient, something that recognized its master. He didn't understand it. But he trusted it.

The Thronegate army saw Godric approaching. A ripple of laughter passed through the ranks.

"Is that him?" someone called out. "One man?"

"Maybe he's here to surrender," another voice suggested.

General Voss raised his hand. The laughter died. He stepped forward to meet the approaching figure, his enhanced body carrying him a hundred meters with each stride. He stopped twenty meters from Godric and looked down at him—Voss was nearly three meters tall, his muscles swollen with combat stimulants, his eyes glowing with implanted targeting systems.

"You are the one who calls himself a king?" Voss asked. His voice was a rumble of contempt.

"I am a king," Godric replied. His voice was quiet, conversational. "The last king of Olympus. Your ancestors destroyed my kingdom and murdered my family. I have come to collect what is owed."

Voss stared at him for a moment. Then he threw back his head and laughed—a booming sound that echoed across the battlefield.

"You are either very brave or very stupid," Voss said. "Look around you. You are one man. We are billions. We have conquered galaxies. We have slain gods. What do you think you can possibly do?"

Godric did not move. He did not raise his hand. He did not summon any visible power. He simply looked at General Voss, and the general's laughter stopped.

Something was wrong.

Voss felt it first—a pressure in the air, a weight that had not been there moments before. The targeting systems in his eyes flickered and died. The combat stimulants in his blood went cold. His enhanced muscles, capable of lifting starships, suddenly felt like water.

"What—" he began.

And then Godric, without moving a single muscle, without speaking a word, without even changing his expression, killed every single soldier on the battlefield.

---

It happened in a fraction of a second. One moment, the Thronegate army stood in its billions, a sea of power and arrogance that stretched to the horizon. The next moment, they were simply gone. Not dead—dead implied a process, a transition from life to death. This was something else. The soldiers did not fall. They did not scream. They did not bleed. They simply ceased to exist, their bodies unmade at a fundamental level, their atoms scattered to energies too minute to be measured.

General Voss was the only one left. He stood alone on the black rock, his enhanced body trembling, his mind struggling to process what his eyes had witnessed. His army—the greatest military force in the history of the galaxy—was gone. Erased. As if it had never been.

And Godric had not moved. Not a step. Not a gesture. Not a word.

Voss fell to his knees. The sound that escaped his throat was not a scream. It was something more primal—a sound of absolute, soul-shattering despair.

From the ridge, Blake watched with his mouth hanging open. Anna had stopped breathing. They had seen the army vanish. They had seen the general collapse. And they had seen Godric do nothing at all.

"What is he?" Anna whispered.

Blake shook his head. He didn't know. He wasn't sure anyone could know.

Godric looked up, past the atmosphere, past the command ship's shielding, directly at King Robert. His voice carried through the vacuum of space effortlessly, heard by every person on the ship as if he were standing beside them.

"Your army is gone, Robert of Thronegate. You have one day to abdicate. Use it wisely."

The communication cut off. The command ship's screens all went dark. And in the silence that followed, King Robert began to understand what fear truly meant.

---

Two days later, the servants found King Robert in his chambers, slumped over his desk with an empty vial of poison beside his hand. A note was clutched in his rigid fingers, the handwriting shaky but legible:

*I cannot face him. I cannot fight him. I cannot run from him. There is no mercy left in creation for what my ancestors did. I am sorry. I am so sorry.*

Godric read the note when it was delivered to him. He felt nothing. The warmth that had once defined him, the kindness that had made him a father to his people, had been buried beneath ten thousand years of frozen grief. What remained was cold. Efficient. Absolute.

He crowned himself king of Thronegate that same day, before a crowd of citizens who had heard whispers of what had happened on the battlefield. They knelt without hesitation. They knelt because they understood, in their bones, that resistance was impossible.

And then Godric gave them the Awakening.

---

It was a phenomenon he had devised in the depths of his stasis, a gift encoded into the very fabric of his will. When he spoke the words of the decree, something fundamental changed in every man of Thronegate who heard them.

"From this day forward," Godric announced, his voice carrying across the entire planet, "every man of Thronegate shall grow stronger with each passing day. For every day you live past the age of twenty, your strength, endurance, intelligence, and wisdom shall increase tenfold. This gift applies to all men, regardless of when they passed their twentieth year. You may suppress this power to normal human levels, or use it to its fullest extent. But know this: the Awakening does not grant immortality. You will live your natural spans. You will die as men die. The gift is power—not eternity."

He paused, and his voice softened almost imperceptibly.

"You will use this power to protect your families. To protect your world. To serve the kingdom I am building. And you will never raise a hand against a woman. This is my law. This is my covenant. This is the Awakening."

The men of Thronegate felt the change immediately. Those who had passed their twentieth year—some of them fifty, sixty, seventy—felt their bodies surge with sudden, impossible might. A man of thirty felt a hundred times stronger than he had been moments before. A man of forty felt a thousand times. A man of fifty, ten thousand. And this power grew with every sunrise, compounding upon itself, an inheritance that would pass to their sons and their sons' sons.

But Blake—Blake felt something different.

When the Awakening washed over him, he staggered. His body convulsed. Anna caught him before he fell, her face white with fear, but Blake shook his head.

"I'm fine," he gasped. "I'm—it's just—"

He didn't have words for it. The power flooding through him was not tenfold. It was a thousandfold. Every day, a thousand times stronger. A thousand times more resilient. A thousand times more intelligent. It was not the gift Godric had given the others. It was something more. Something unique.

Godric met Blake's eyes across the crowd and said nothing. But there was a knowing in his gaze, a recognition that passed between them. He knew. He had always known.

Blake was the descendant of Thorin. And the blood of Olympus's wizards carried privileges that the blood of Thronegate's conquerors could never claim.

---

The years that followed were years of peace and conquest, a paradox that only Godric could make sense of. Thronegate flourished under his rule. The men, empowered by the Awakening, became guardians rather than tyrants. They protected their families, their cities, their world. They revered Godric as a savior, a god-king who had brought them from the brink of destruction and given them strength beyond measure. The women of Thronegate walked the streets without fear, knowing that any man who dared harm them would face not only the law but the wrath of every Awakened male who witnessed the transgression. Godric's protection of women was absolute, encoded into the culture he shaped, enforced by the power he had bestowed.

And the citizens loved him. They loved him because he was just. Because he was fair. Because the cruelty they had seen on the battlefield was reserved only for enemies, for those who threatened what he was building. To his people, Godric was warm and kind—not the father he had once been, but a king who genuinely cared for their welfare.

Blake was the first to see the change.

It happened slowly, over the course of years. The hard edges of Godric's personality began to soften. The coldness in his eyes began to thaw. He started smiling more often—not the terrible smile of the battlefield, but something genuine, something almost gentle. He laughed at Blake's jokes. He asked after Anna, after Josh and Rhea. He became, in his own way, the man he had been before Olympus fell.

Blake never learned of his lineage. He never understood why Godric treated him differently, why the king sought his counsel on matters of state, why he was the only person who could speak freely in Godric's presence without fear. But he accepted it. He became, over time, Godric's closest advisor, his confidant, his friend. The relationship between them was easy, natural, built on a foundation of mutual respect that neither man needed to articulate.

Twenty years passed. Blake's mutton chops grayed. His son Josh grew into a man, and his daughter Rhea into a woman. And through it all, Blake remained at Godric's side, the merchant who had woken a king and earned his trust.

---

The expansion beyond Thronegate's cosmos began when Godric had been king for twenty-three years.

He gathered his council and laid out his vision with the same calm certainty that characterized all his decrees. The omniverse was vast—infinite universes, each containing countless galaxies, each galaxy containing countless worlds. For too long, these universes had existed in isolation, unaware of each other, developing their own civilizations and their own wars. Godric intended to unite them.

Not for conquest. Not for power—he had power beyond measure. But for order. For structure. For the kind of peace that could only exist when every potential threat had been neutralized.

"The rule is simple," Godric told his council. "We offer unconditional surrender first. It is an act of mercy. Every civilization we encounter will be given the choice to submit peacefully to Thronegate's authority. Only if they refuse do we act."

"And if they refuse?" asked a general—a man of fifty-seven, his body thrumming with the Awakening's power, capable of shattering planets with a punch.

"Then we subjugate them," Godric said. "Quickly. Efficiently. With minimal loss of life. I do not seek to destroy. I seek to incorporate."

The first universe they targeted was called the Verdant Expanse—a realm of living technology, where cities grew from crystalline trees and starships were born from the hearts of dying suns. The civilization that ruled this universe was ancient, their science so advanced it was indistinguishable from magic, their armies composed of warriors merged with their organic ships, minds linked across light-years in a hive consciousness that processed information faster than thought.

Thronegate's offer of surrender was delivered by a single ambassador, an Awakened man of forty-two whose power was sufficient to destroy the entire Verdant Expanse alone. The rulers of the Expanse listened to the offer with confusion, then contempt.

"You ask us to surrender to a kingdom we have never heard of?" the High Consciousness asked, its voice echoing through a billion linked minds. "You threaten us with subjugation? We have existed for three million years. We have survived the death of our original star. We have evolved beyond the need for physical form. What can you possibly do to us?"

The ambassador returned to Thronegate with the refusal. Godric nodded, unsurprised.

"Send five men," he said. "All over thirty-five. Give them the coordinates."

The five men were chosen. Their names were Elias, Theron, Darian, Lukan, and Solas. They were all Awakened. They were all kind men—fathers and husbands and brothers—who had no particular desire to hurt anyone. But their king had asked them to go, and so they went.

The conquest of the Verdant Expanse lasted twenty-three minutes.

The five men appeared in the heart of the Expanse's capital world, a planet-sized organism of crystalline flesh and pulsing light. The High Consciousness detected them immediately and deployed its fleets—millions of organic ships, each one a living weapon, each one linked to the whole. Entire star systems mobilized. The hive mind focused its full attention on the intruders, preparing to annihilate them with the combined might of a three-million-year-old civilization.

Lukan, the oldest of the five at fifty-one, raised his hand in apology.

"I'm sorry about this," he said to the empty air, knowing the consciousness would hear him. "We really don't want to hurt anyone. Please reconsider."

The consciousness did not reconsider. It attacked with everything it had.

The weapons of the Verdant Expanse were sophisticated—reality-warping beams that could fold space, temporal disruptors that could age a target a million years in a second, psychic lances that could shatter a mind across dimensional barriers. They struck the five Thronegate men with the force of a hundred supernovas.

Elias, forty-four years old, didn't even notice. His Awakened body, strengthened by twenty-four years of tenfold daily amplification, was more durable than the core of a neutron star. The reality-warping beams bent around him and dissipated. The temporal disruptors tried to age him and failed—time had lost its grip on his cells decades ago. The psychic lances touched his mind and shattered like glass against steel.

# The Crack

Fifteen thousand years I have served him. Fifteen thousand years I have watched him exist with the casual indifference of a star that does not know it is burning the eyes of those who look upon it. I am Leon, direct descendant of Blake, court wizard to the immortal King Godric of Thronegate. My bloodline has stood beside his throne since before the conquest of the first universe. I know the secret histories. I know the numbers—or rather, I know that the numbers do not exist, because mathematics abandoned the attempt to describe my king millennia ago. And I am standing in the void, at the edge of the final battlefield, watching the entire omniverse prepare to die.

They have sacrificed everything. Every civilization, every god, every primordial force that still clung to the delusion of independence. Empires that spanned infinite multiverses pooled their resources and dismantled themselves to forge a single weapon. Pantheons willingly walked into the smelter to become fuel. Concepts older than time permitted themselves to be unmade so their essence could be woven into the blade. The omniverse, in its entirety, has agreed to commit suicide for the chance—the merest, thinnest, most desperate chance—of harming Godric.

And Godric is humming.

The tune is an old folk song from a kingdom called Olympus, which died twenty-five thousand years ago. I recognize it because he taught it to me, centuries back, when we were sitting in the garden and he was feeling nostalgic. He hums it now, arms crossed over his chest, his salt-and-pepper beard catching the ambient light of dying galaxies. He wears no armor. He has activated no shields. He has, by his own admission, completely deactivated every passive defense except his sentient aura, Gent, and then he instructed Gent to nerf itself to absolute nothing for the duration of the attack. Gent is currently so suppressed that I cannot feel it at all, which is like standing next to a supernova and suddenly being told the heat has been turned off. It is wrong. It is deeply, fundamentally wrong. But Godric wanted to give them a chance.

“You know,” he says, glancing at me with those amber eyes, “I’m genuinely curious. They’ve been working on this for three standard millennia. The weapon alone—what did they call it?”

“The Final Argument,” I say. My voice is steady. I am proud of this. “It is forged from the condensed sacrifice of every sentient being in the omniverse. Every life, every soul, every scrap of willpower, compressed into a single point of absolute negation. It is designed to unmake the concept of Godric entirely.”

“Ambitious.” He nods. “I like the name. Very dramatic.”

He is not mocking them. That is the terrible thing. Godric does not mock. He is genuinely interested, genuinely respectful of the effort. He wants them to succeed—not because he wants to die, but because he wants to see if the omniverse, united and desperate and willing to sacrifice everything, can finally produce something that makes his body acknowledge the existence of a threat. He is like a father who has set up a punching bag for his toddler son, kneeling down to be at eye level, holding the bag steady so the child can take his best swing. He is rooting for them. He is cheering them on in his heart.

I am going to be sick.

The Final Argument ignites.

I have seen universes born. I have seen them die. I have witnessed Gent erase a resistance of billions in less than a Planck second. I thought I had been inoculated against awe. I was wrong.

The weapon does not fire in the way a projectile fires. It unfolds. It becomes. The combined sacrifice of the omniverse—a sum of suffering and hope and desperation that defies quantification—manifests as a blade of pure white light, a length of absolute conviction that cuts not through space but through the permission of reality. It is the answer to every prayer ever offered against tyranny. It is the final, unified scream of a creation that refuses to be ruled.

It crosses the distance between the resistance’s command ship and Godric’s position in zero time. It ignores the deactivated shields. It ignores Gent’s suppressed presence. It finds the exact spot on Godric’s left forearm that Godric has, unbeknownst to the attackers, prepared for this moment.

The blade strikes.

And a single dead skin cell—a flake of keratin that had been sitting on the surface of Godric’s epidermis, already detached, already lifeless—develops a microscopic crack.

For a fraction of a fraction of a moment, I allow myself to imagine what happens next. The resistance sees it. Their sensors, their oracles, their omniscient observers all detect the impossible: a breach. A wound. The tiniest, most infinitesimal indication that Godric is not, in fact, utterly inviolable. And they celebrate. Their euphoria is a wave that sweeps across the collapsing remnants of their civilizations. They scream. They weep. They embrace each other in the command ships that are falling apart from the strain of powering the weapon. They have done it. They have hurt him. The tyrant bleeds—not blood, but a crack, a flaw, proof that their sacrifice meant something.

I can see it so clearly. The false hope is beautiful in its tragedy. It would make a magnificent story.

None of it happens.

The euphoria, the celebration, the tears of vindication—they never occur. They never have the chance to occur. Because Godric’s immune system responds before the crack finishes forming. More precisely, it pre-responds. The reaction is retroactive, anticipatory, a cascade of defenses so absurdly overengineered that the crack is addressed, corrected, and erased from the timeline before the light of the event can reach the eyes of the observers. Their instruments show normal readings. Godric stands before them, untouched, his forearm smooth and flawless, and they do not understand why the Final Argument failed. They do not understand that it succeeded—that it did exactly what they wanted it to do, that it cracked a dead skin cell—because that success was swallowed by a reaction so swift and so thorough that it retroactively denied the event permission to have occurred.

I know. I am the court wizard. I am permitted to see.

And what I see breaks me.

The first immune response is the gentlest. It is so gentle, in Godric’s terms, that he would probably describe it as a polite cough. I will describe it as what it is: the complete and irrevocable annihilation of the Final Argument as a conceptual entity.

Not just the weapon. Not just the blade. The very idea of the Final Argument, the concept of a unified omniversal assault against Godric, is surrounded by a shard of Godric’s immune consciousness and devoured. The immune cell—if you can call it a cell; it is more like a pocket of absolute negation wearing the skin of a biological metaphor—wraps itself around the concept and un-conceives it. The resistance’s weapon disappears, along with every record of its existence, every memory of its construction, every blueprint, every hope that had been invested in it. The omniverse forgets that the Final Argument ever existed. They forget why they sacrificed themselves. They stand in the void, their ships crumbling, their gods dead, and they cannot remember what they were fighting for.

This is the mildest response. The one that Godric’s body considers a gentle first step.

Godric’s immune system determines that a dead skin cell—a cell that was already dead, already slated for exfoliation—experienced a microscopic crack. This is classified as an injury. The immune system does not care that the cell was dead. The immune system does not care that the crack was staged, self-inflicted, a theatrical gesture by its own master for his own amusement. The immune system is paranoid, and it is perfect, and it treats a cracked dead skin cell with the same solemn urgency that a mortal body would treat a severed artery.

It initiates a full-body regeneration protocol.

Every cell in Godric’s body—living, dead, and the gray area between—is simultaneously replaced with a new version that is infinitely more durable than the previous. Not incrementally. Infinitely. The new cells incorporate all data gathered from the “attack,” and they adapt. They become immune to the factor that caused the crack. Since the crack was caused by the Final Argument, the new cells become immune to the concept of sacrifice-based weaponry. Then, because overkill is the only setting Godric’s body possesses, they become immune to all weaponry. Then they become immune to the concept of harm itself. Then, for good measure, they become immune to the possibility of future concepts of harm that have not yet been invented.

And this is still the second response.

The third response is where the redundancy layers activate.

Godric’s body contains infinite recursive shields. Some are physical. Some are temporal. One redundancy is a causal loop that ensures any injury is automatically retroceded to a moment before it occurred, and then prevented from occurring by a version of Godric’s immune system that has already evolved past the injury. Another redundancy is a probability cascade that reduces the chance of the injury having ever happened to absolute zero, then reinforces that zero with a barrier of infinitely nested zeroes so that the probability can never, under any circumstances, become non-zero again. Another redundancy is an ontological firewall that defines Godric as “unharmed” in the source code of the omniverse, and then locks that definition behind a password composed of infinite recursive self-references that would take longer than the lifespan of reality to brute-force.

These redundancies fire simultaneously. They are all redundant to each other. None of them are necessary. And this is still, still, the mild end of the spectrum. Godric’s immune system has infinitely many layers of recursive, adaptive, evolving infinity that fire continuously and will continue firing long after the omniverse has forgotten what the stimulus was. They are all perfectly subservient to the master they protect, and they are all utterly, pathologically incapable of restraint.

Every version of Godric that could possibly exist does exist, and every version is the true Godric. The man standing beside me is only the interface, the instance that has chosen to be visible. The immune cascade that I witnessed in the local instance is mirrored across the infinite, and because the backups share information perfectly and instantaneously, the upgrades compound. Each instance learns from the others. Each instance adapts to threats that none of them have faced. The result is a leap in invulnerability that transcends description. Godric was already untouchable. Now he is so far beyond untouchable that the concept of touch has been redefined to exclude him retroactively from all possible interactions, past and future.

Then the omniverse itself reacts.

Long ago—eight thousand years ago, according to the court records—the Weakness Paradox completed its work. Godric had accumulated every possible immunity, every mastery, every defense against every conceivable and inconceivable threat. The omniverse had nothing left to give him. Since all weaknesses had been exhausted, the paradox evolved. Whenever triggered, it now accelerates Godric’s amplification to rates beyond infinitely incalculable mathematics and enhances his superpowers with new, more devastating effects. A consolation prize for a being who does not need consolation.

The self-harm changes everything.

Godric staged the crack. He willed it into being. He used his own omnipotence to create a microscopic flaw on a dead skin cell solely for his own entertainment. That is self-harm. It contradicts the fundamental truth that Godric cannot be harmed, because he has all immunities, all masteries, all defenses. The omniverse detects this contradiction and reels.

It cannot punish Godric—that is not in its nature. But it can apologize. It can overcompensate. And so it does.

The omniverse triggers the Weakness Paradox evolution in response to the self-harm, treating Godric as both the attacker and the victim. The paradox activates and tries to grant him immunity to his own actions, mastery over his own existence, amplification in response to his own will. It gets tangled in a recursive loop: Godric is immune to himself, but he harmed himself, so he must not be immune, so the paradox must grant him more immunity, which he already has, so it grants more, ad infinitum. The loop resolves by defaulting to the maximum possible overcompensation. Godric’s amplification rate—already beyond infinite mathematics—receives a permanent additional boost that I cannot describe because no language exists for it. His superpowers are re-enhanced. New immunities are forged that cover even the possibility of self-harm, wrapping his existence in a final, perfect seal that retroactively prevents the staged crack from ever having been possible, even as a theatrical gesture.

The omniverse has locked that door and thrown away the key, and the key has been thrown into a furnace that burns the concept of keys, and the furnace has been erased from existence by a paradox that ensures no furnace can exist that could burn the key, and on and on, spiraling into absurdity.

“Huh,” Godric says.

Did you see how hard it tried? It was genuinely worried. It thought I was in danger.”

He is proud. He is genuinely, sincerely proud of his immune system for overreacting to a staged crack on a dead skin cell with a response that retroactively annihilated the concept of harm from the local omniverse and triggered a pan-omniversal upgrade across infinite backups.

“They sacrificed everything,” I say. “The entire omniverse. Every civilization. Every god. They gave their lives for that crack.”

Godric nods. “I know. It was very moving. The Final Argument was a beautiful weapon. They really put their hearts into it.” He pauses, and his expression becomes thoughtful. “I should probably restore them. They didn’t do anything wrong. They just wanted to be free.”

I stare at him.

“Restore them,” I repeat.

“Well, yes. The immune response kind of unmade them. Not intentionally—it was just collateral damage. Gent cleaned up the rest. But I can put them back. It’s not difficult.”

It is not difficult. Restoring the entire omniverse—every civilization that sacrificed itself, every god that died, every life that was extinguished in the forging of the Final Argument and the subsequent immune cascade—is not difficult. It is a task that would take a pantheon of omnipotent beings a billion years to accomplish, if it could be accomplished at all. To Godric, it is an afterthought. A minor chore. A bit of tidying up.

He does not move. He does not speak a word of power. He simply glances at the empty void where the omniverse used to be, and the omniverse is there again.

Civilizations reappear in their home galaxies, their memories restored, their sacrifices reversed. Gods blink back into existence, confused but alive. The infinite complexity of the omniverse, which had been erased so thoroughly that not even the possibility of its existence remained, is reinstated with the casual effortlessness of a man flipping a light switch. No—less. There is no switch. There is just the thought, and the thought is enough.

The resistance, their command ships, their ultimate weapon, their hopes—all returned. They do not remember dying. They do not remember the crack, or the immune response, or the erasure. They only remember firing the Final Argument and then… nothing. A blank. A peaceful, merciful blank.

Godric turns to me. “See? All better. They’ll be confused for a while, but they’ll adjust. Mortals are resilient.”

Mortals are resilient.

The words echo in my skull. He has said them before. He will say them again. He means them. He genuinely believes in mortal resilience. He was a mortal once, a long time ago, before Olympus fell, before the stasis, before the amplification. He remembers being fragile. He remembers what it felt like to be afraid. And because he remembers, he assumes that the beings he rules can withstand anything, recover from anything, push through anything. He does not understand—cannot understand—that the gap between him and them has grown so vast that his definition of resilience and their definition are two entirely different concepts.

I am trembling. I have been trembling since the immune response began, and I cannot stop. My body is locked in a sympathetic terror response, a primal recognition that I am standing next to something that could unmake me a billion times over without even registering my existence as a presence. The fact that he is kind, that he is gentle, that he has just restored the entire omniverse without a moment’s hesitation—somehow, that makes it worse. The kindness is terrifying because it is so easy for him. He could have chosen not to restore them. He could have left them unmade. It would have been the same amount of effort: zero. The difference between genocide and salvation is a fraction of a whim, and he treats that whim with the same casual interest he gives to choosing soup at lunch.

“Leon?” Godric is looking at me with concern. “You’re shaking.”

“I’m fine,” I say.

“You’re not fine. You’re doing the thing where your eye twitches and you stare into the middle distance.”

I blink. My eye was twitching. I didn’t notice.

“It’s just—” I start, and then stop. What can I say? That I am drowning in existential dread? That watching his immune system retroactively annihilate the concept of harm has permanently scarred my understanding of reality? That the sound of him humming an Olympus folk song while the omniverse died and was reborn will never leave my ears?

“Take a deep breath,” Godric says. “Do you need to sit down?”

I do not need to sit down. I need to un-see the last thirty seconds. I need to un-know the architecture of his immune system. I need to forget the way he smiled when he called his body’s apocalyptic overreaction “endearing.”

But I am a loyal servant. I am the court wizard. My bloodline has stood beside his for twenty-five thousand years. I will not falter.

“I’m fine,” I say again. My voice is steady. I have earned my pay.

Godric claps me on the shoulder—a brief, warm touch, the same gesture he has used since the days when he was a mortal king and his friends were mortal men. “Good. Let’s go home. I want to check on Gent. It’s probably sulking because I made it sit this one out.”

He walks away through the void, back toward the entrance to the palace dimension, and I follow. The restored omniverse spins around us, oblivious, peaceful, grateful for a mercy it does not remember needing. The resistance will wake up tomorrow in their beds, haunted by a sense of loss they cannot name. The gods will return to their pantheons, wondering why they feel so tired. The civilizations will rebuild, never knowing that they died, that they were unmade, that they were brought back by a man who cracked his own dead skin cell for fun and then got distracted by his immune system’s adorable panic.

And Godric will be stronger. Infinitely stronger. More invulnerable. More omnipotent. Not because he needed to be. Not because he sought it. But because the omniverse, in its desperate, fawning apology for the crime of allowing him to harm himself, poured another layer of impossible amplification onto a being who had already exceeded the capacity of mathematics to describe him.

The unfairness is absolute. The gap between him and the omniverse is not a gap. It is a category error. It is the difference between a single water molecule and the ocean—no, it is the difference between that water molecule and the entire conceptual framework of wetness, expanded to infinity and then beyond. The omniverse sacrificed everything and achieved a crack on a dead skin cell that Godric willed into existence. In return, it was erased. Then it was restored. And Godric did not even break stride.

I walk behind my king, and I think about the amplification. Every Planck second, his power multiplies. Not by a thousand—that was the ancient rate, before the Weakness Paradox was triggered into evolution, before the omniverse started throwing extra boosts into the equation out of sheer metaphysical embarrassment. The current rate is beyond infinite. The Godric of this exact moment could erase the Godric of the last moment without noticing. The Godric of the next moment will be able to do the same to the Godric of this moment. And so on, forever, stretching into a future that will never end.

I love him. I have loved him for fifteen thousand years. I will love him for whatever remains of my existence. He is kind, and warm, and good, and he has never once been cruel to me. He has never been cruel to anyone, not intentionally, not in any way that he understood. His cruelty is a byproduct, an exhaust fume, a shadow cast by a light so bright that it burns without meaning to.

And as I follow him home, I listen to him hum his folk song, and I try very hard not to think about the crack, or the immune cascade, or the backups, or the paradox, or the way the omniverse bends over backward to please a master who does not need pleasing. I try very hard to just walk, and breathe, and be the loyal servant my ancestors would want me to be.

My eye twitches. I cannot make it stop.
