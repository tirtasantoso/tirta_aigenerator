# The Fall of Olympus

The incense of burning myrrh drifted through the marble colonnades of Olympus, threading between the assembled nobles like the breath of sleeping gods. King Godric stood at the altar, his massive frame silhouetted against the setting sun that spilled through the cathedral's rose window. At forty-five, he remained a figure carved from the stuff of legends—shoulders broad enough to bear the weight of his kingdom, a salt-and-pepper beard trimmed with meticulous care, and eyes that held the warmth of a father regarding his children. His hands, calloused from decades of sword-work yet gentle as a healer's, rested on the ceremonial tome.

Isabel approached down the aisle, twenty-two and radiant, her mother's golden hair cascading beneath a veil of woven starlight. Godric felt his chest tighten. She was his firstborn, the child who had gripped his finger with impossible strength moments after her birth, who had demanded bedtime stories of dragons and distant stars until her voice grew hoarse from questions.

"You're weeping, Father," Isabel whispered as she reached the altar.

"Kings do not weep," Godric replied, though the tear tracking down his cheek betrayed him. "I merely have something in my eye."

Behind Isabel stood Hebe, fifteen and already possessing her mother's fierce intelligence, her arms full of white roses. And there, in the front pew, Reina—his wife of twenty-five years, her silver-streaked dark hair pinned with emerald combs, her smile the same one that had undone him when they were both impossibly young and foolish enough to believe in forever.

Thorin, his court wizard and dearest friend, stood to Godric's right. The wizard's crimson robes bore the ancient crest of his bloodline—a serpent consuming its own tail around a single unblinking eye. Thorin's family had served the kings of Olympus for generations beyond counting, their magical lineage woven into the kingdom's very foundations.

"Nervous?" Thorin murmured, his weathered face creasing with amusement.

"I've faced armies," Godric replied quietly. "Negotiated with beings that would drive lesser men mad. But this—" He gestured at his daughter, at the ceremony, at the fragile miracle of this moment. "This terrifies me."

"That's called being a father."

The ceremony commenced. Godric's voice rang through the cathedral as he spoke the ancient words of binding, his deep baritone carrying the weight of tradition, of love, of a thousand yesterdays that had led to this single, perfect now.

The first explosion ripped through the eastern wall before he finished the third verse.

Stone and glass erupted inward. The rose window shattered, its colored fragments becoming a horizontal rain of razor-edged beauty. Godric moved before thought, his warrior's instincts hurling Isabel behind him even as a chunk of marble the size of a warhorse crashed through the pews where nobles had been seated moments before.

Screaming. Fire. The cathedral's ceiling groaned, great cracks spider-webbing across its domed architecture.

"THRONEGATE!" The war cry came from everywhere and nowhere. Soldiers in black-enameled armor poured through the breaches, their swords already wet. Magic crackled—not the measured, disciplined arts of Thorin's lineage, but something crude and overwhelming, designed purely for annihilation.

Godric drew the ceremonial sword at his hip. It was ornamental, never meant for combat, but steel was steel. He cut down the first soldier with a single horizontal stroke, the blade singing through armor and flesh. The second died before the first hit the ground. The third—

"REINA!"

She had been running toward Hebe. A spear took her through the back, emerging from her chest in a spray of arterial crimson. Reina's body continued forward two more steps before collapsing, her hand still reaching for their youngest daughter.

Hebe screamed. A soldier's sword silenced her.

Godric's world contracted to a pinprick of impossible horror. He saw Isabel's face, frozen in terror. Saw Thorin's hands weaving desperate patterns in the air. Saw the black-armored tide surging toward them, endless and merciless.

"Father—"

Isabel's voice cut off. Godric turned. A blade had found her throat. She fell clutching her neck, blood pulsing between her fingers, her eyes fixed on him with an expression of bewildered betrayal, as if she couldn't understand why he hadn't protected her.

The sound that tore from Godric's throat was not human. It was the howl of a world ending.

"SIRE!" Thorin's hand clamped onto Godric's shoulder. The wizard's face was ashen, his eyes wild with desperate calculation. "The teleportation spell—my bloodline's forbidden art—I can save you, but you must hold still!"

"Save them!" Godric roared. "Save my family!"

"I cannot! They're—" Thorin's voice broke. "They're gone, Godric. They're gone. But you can survive this. You must survive this. Olympus must survive!"

The wizard began chanting. Ancient syllables, passed down through his family since before Olympus was founded, filled the air. The spell was incomplete—Thorin had never been meant to cast it without days of preparation, without the celestial alignments that would anchor its power. But there was no time. The cathedral was collapsing. The enemy was closing. His king was going to die.

Thorin's hands moved faster, his voice rising in desperate crescendo. The air around Godric began to shimmer, to twist, to—

The axe took Thorin in the spine.

The wizard's chant became a wet gurgle. He toppled forward, his spell unfinished, his life's blood pooling across the shattered marble. But his eyes met Godric's one final time, and in them was not fear, but fierce, defiant hope.

The incomplete spell detonated.

Reality folded. Godric felt himself being unmade, scattered across dimensions his mortal mind could not comprehend. The last thing he saw was Olympus burning—his kingdom, his family, his friend, his entire existence reduced to ash and memory. Then darkness claimed him, and he fell through the cracks between worlds.

# The Stasis Event

Godric materialized in a sanctuary hidden beyond the reach of time. It was a place Thorin's ancestors had prepared millennia ago, a failsafe for the royal bloodline should Olympus ever face annihilation. The chamber was carved from obsidian, its walls etched with preservation runes that pulsed with faint violet light. At its center, a raised dais awaited its occupant.

He collapsed onto the stone platform. Grief had hollowed him out, transformed him into a shell of the man who had stood at that altar moments before. The warmth that had defined him—the fatherly kindness, the ready smile, the patient wisdom—burned away in the fires of Olympia's fall. What remained was cold. Sharp. Merciless.

The preservation runes activated.

Godric felt his body seize. Cold unlike anything he had ever experienced flooded his veins, crystallizing his blood, stilling his heart, freezing every cell in perfect, eternal suspension. His eyes remained open but unseeing. His lungs ceased their rhythm. The biological machinery of life ground to an absolute halt.

But he did not die.

Something else happened instead. Something the runes had not been designed to trigger. Something that had no name in any language spoken by mortal tongues.

At the exact instant of his suspension, Godric's strength began to multiply. His endurance. His wisdom. His speed. His senses. His intelligence. Everything that constituted his being multiplied by one thousand—and then that product multiplied by itself, and that new product by itself, and on and on, each multiplication occurring not in seconds or milliseconds but in Planck time, the smallest possible unit of temporal measurement. Ten million trillion trillion trillion multiplications every single second, each one building upon the last in an exponential cascade that had no ceiling, no limit, no end.

Simultaneously, a new power bloomed within him. The ability to perceive all that existed in his immediate vicinity. Then another—the capacity to manipulate matter at its most fundamental level. Then another. And another. A new apocalyptic ability manifesting every second, each one sufficient to extinguish an entire civilization the moment it appeared. And like his base attributes, these powers did not remain static. They too multiplied by one thousand and by themselves, every Planck second, forever.

The sanctuary should have been annihilated. The planet should have shattered. The galaxy should have folded into a singularity around the impossibly dense concentration of power crystallizing within this single frozen form. But among those first manifestations came a power of concealment—the ability to perfectly contain his aura, his presence, the reality-warping magnitude of his existence. Like all his other abilities, it multiplied endlessly, flawlessly maintaining the balance even as the scales tilted toward infinity.

And then came the paradox. The final, absolute defense.

Godric possessed no weaknesses. This was not hyperbole. It was not aspiration. It was the fundamental truth of his transformed existence, as immutable as the multiplication that fueled him. But the multiverse was vast, and sentient minds were arrogant. They would look upon him and believe they saw flaws. They would scheme. They would prepare. They would attack.

The defense was passive. It required no thought, no awareness, no activation. The instant any consciousness anywhere formed the thought that Godric possessed a weakness—the very moment the concept crystallized in their mind—the multiplication rate of his attributes permanently increased. The falsely perceived vulnerability became immunity before the thought finished forming. And the being who dared imagine weakness in their superior found their intended attack perfected and claimed by Godric himself, its very concept denied to all others in creation unless he willed otherwise.

Every second an enemy spent planning to exploit his nonexistent weakness was another second they unknowingly fed his amplification, made him invulnerable to their pathetic gambit, and surrendered their greatest weapons into his arsenal. Their defeat was sealed at the moment of their first arrogant thought, long before any plan took shape. Godric simply allowed them to continue their futile scheming. Their despair when they realized the truth was, after all, deeply amusing.

The sanctuary held. Time passed. Olympus became legend, then myth, then less than memory. The universe turned. Civilizations rose and fell. Stars were born and died. And in the darkness between worlds, the King of Olympus slept, frozen in amber, growing beyond any measure that existence could contain.

Ten thousand years passed before anyone found him.

# The Merchant

Blake wiped sweat from his forehead and cursed the navigational computer for the seventeenth time that hour. Thirty-eight years old, with a friendly mutton chop that had begun showing flecks of gray and eyes that had seen too many close calls in the merchant trade, he was not supposed to be here. "Here" being an uncharted sector of the Thronegate periphery, his cargo hold full of perishable medical supplies that were growing less viable by the minute, and his fuel reserves dipping into numbers that made his stomach clench.

The distress beacon had been a fabrication. A clever one, rigged to mimic a civilian transport in need, but a trap nonetheless. Pirates. He'd barely escaped with his ship intact, but the emergency jump had spat him out in this gods-forsaken region of space where the star charts showed nothing but static.

"I'm going to miss Josh's graduation," he muttered. "Anna's going to kill me."

His wife would forgive him. She always did. Twenty years of marriage had taught Blake that he'd married far above his station—a woman with patience vast enough to weather his short temper and loyalty fierce enough to make his heart ache when he thought about it too long. Their son Josh, twelve and already showing an unsettling aptitude for mechanics. Their daughter Rhea, eight, who demanded he bring back a rock from every planet he visited.

The ship's sensors pinged.

Blake frowned. There was something out there—something that shouldn't exist. A structure, impossibly old, drifting in the void between star systems. The scans showed materials that predated any known civilization. Architecture that hurt his eyes when he tried to focus on it too long.

He should leave. Every instinct screamed at him to leave.

But the structure was broadcasting something. Not a distress signal. Not a beacon. Something older. Something that resonated in his bones, in his blood, in genetic memory he didn't know he possessed.

Blake guided his ship toward the structure.

The sanctuary opened for him. There was no security, no barrier, no test. The ancient doors simply recognized his presence and parted, as if they had been waiting for ten thousand years for a descendant of Thorin's bloodline to finally come home.

Blake, of course, knew nothing of this. He was a merchant. A simple man who loved his family, worked hard, and had never once suspected that his ancestors had served kings in an age before recorded history.

The chamber was dark. Violet light pulsed faintly along its walls, responding to his presence. And at its center, on a raised dais of obsidian—

A man. Frozen. Perfectly preserved. His eyes open but unseeing. His body massive, muscular, the physique of a warrior king. A salt-and-pepper beard, neatly trimmed. Short hair. Features that would have been called handsome in any era.

Blake approached slowly. He didn't know why, but his hand reached out. His fingers brushed the frozen man's cheek.

The ice cracked.

Godric's eyes moved.

And in the space between one heartbeat and the next, omniscience flooded into him. Every event of the past ten thousand years—the fall of Olympus, the rise of Thronegate, the fate of every soul who had perished in that cathedral—poured into his consciousness in an instant. He saw the current state of the galaxy. He saw the kingdom that had murdered his family, now ascendant, now dominant, now ruled by a twenty-two-year-old king named Robert who sat on a throne built from the bones of civilizations.

He saw the man standing before him. Blake. Merchant. Husband. Father. Descendant of Thorin, though he would never know it.

Something shifted in Godric's chest. Not warmth—not yet. But recognition. The first person to stand before him in ten millennia was the heir of his dearest friend.

"You," Godric said, his voice emerging rough from ten thousand years of silence, "have my gratitude."

Blake stumbled backward. "You're—you were frozen. How are you—"

"What is your name?"

"Blake. I'm just a merchant. I didn't mean to—I was lost, and this place—"

"Blake." Godric tested the name. "You freed me. That debt will not be forgotten." He rose from the dais, his movements fluid despite the eons of stasis. "I have work to do. A kingdom to address. But you—you will witness what happens next. You and your family."

Blake's face paled. "My family? How do you know about my family?"

Godric smiled. It was not a warm expression. Not yet. "I know everything, Blake. Everything that has happened. Everything that is happening. Everything that will happen." He placed a hand on the merchant's shoulder. "Take me to Thronegate. I have a message for King Robert."

# The Challenge

The throne room of Thronegate stretched for what seemed like miles, its obsidian columns carved with depictions of conquests both ancient and recent. King Robert lounged on his throne—a jagged construction of black crystal that was said to contain the compressed souls of defeated enemies—surrounded by advisors, generals, and courtiers who had spent their lives telling him exactly what he wanted to hear.

Robert was twenty-two, handsome in a sharp-featured way, with eyes that had never known doubt and a smile that had never been forced to acknowledge limits. He had inherited the throne at sixteen after his father's convenient assassination, and in six years of rule, he had expanded Thronegate's dominion across a dozen star systems. His advisors called him brilliant. His generals called him invincible. His propaganda ministers called him divine.

The doors of the throne room exploded inward.

Not opened. Exploded. The reinforced adamantine doors—rated to withstand orbital bombardment—simply ceased to exist, their molecules scattering across the electromagnetic spectrum in a display that would have been beautiful if it hadn't been so terrifying. Guards surged forward. Magic users raised their hands. Automated defense systems spun up.

Godric walked through the space where the doors had been.

He wore no armor. He carried no weapon. His clothing was the simple tunic he had worn to his daughter's wedding ten thousand years ago, preserved by the same stasis that had held his body. He looked like a relic. An anachronism. A ghost.

Every defense system in the throne room failed simultaneously. The guards found themselves frozen in place, their bodies refusing to obey their commands. The magic users screamed as their powers simply... vanished, cut off at the source by a will that regarded their arts as a child might regard a drawing in the sand.

"My name is Godric," he said, and his voice was calm. Conversational. "Ten thousand years ago, I was King of Olympus. Your ancestors, Robert of Thronegate, murdered my family. My wife, Reina. My daughters, Isabel and Hebe. My best friend, Thorin. Every man, woman, and child in my kingdom—slaughtered in an act of cowardice so profound that history itself chose to forget it ever happened."

Robert's face had gone the color of old milk. "Olympus is a myth," he whispered. "A fairy tale."

"The fairy tale," Godric said, "has come to collect its due."

He ascended the steps to the throne. No one stopped him. No one could stop him. The weight of his presence pressed down on the room like a physical force, and those closest to him felt their knees buckle, their bowels loosen, their minds scrabble at the edges of sanity as they glimpsed, for just a fraction of a fraction of an instant, the true magnitude of what stood before them.

Godric looked down at King Robert. The boy was trembling. Good.

"I am not here to kill you," Godric said. "Killing you would be mercy. And I am not feeling merciful." He smiled, and there was something terrible in it. "Here is what will happen. You have three days to assemble every soldier, every weapon, every scrap of military might that Thronegate possesses. In three days, I will meet your army in the Valley of Sorrows. If I defeat them—when I defeat them—you will abdicate your throne. You will confess to your people what your ancestors did. And you will spend the rest of your life in whatever quiet obscurity I permit."

"And if you lose?" Robert managed, some spark of defiance still flickering behind his terror.

Godric laughed. It was the first genuine laugh he had experienced in ten thousand years, and it was utterly devoid of humor. "Boy. I want you to listen very carefully to what I am about to say. In the time it took me to walk from your shattered doors to this throne, I could have annihilated this galaxy seven hundred trillion times. I could have unmade the fabric of this universe and woven it into something new. I could have reached into the past and ensured your ancestors never drew breath. I am not boasting. I am not threatening. I am informing you of the nature of your reality now. There is no scenario in which you win. There is no scenario in which you survive my displeasure if you refuse. There is only the choice I am giving you—the choice to face what you have created with some shred of dignity, or to die cowering on that throne like the coward your bloodline has always produced."

He turned and walked toward the shattered doorway. At the threshold, he paused.

"Three days, King Robert. I suggest you use them wisely."

# The Battle

The Valley of Sorrows stretched across the continent of Thronegate Prime like a scar. Once a lush grassland, centuries of industrialization and military testing had transformed it into a wasteland of gray soil and stagnant pools of chemical runoff. For three days, the valley had swarmed with activity as the full might of Thronegate's military assembled.

And what might it was.

The ground forces alone numbered in the billions—super-soldiers genetically engineered for combat, their bones laced with adamantine, their muscles threaded with synthetic fiber that could withstand impacts that would liquefy normal humans. They carried weapons that had conquered worlds: plasma rifles that could melt through starship hulls, gravity hammers that could create localized singularities, phase blades that existed partially in dimensions where armor simply didn't.

Above them, the sky was blotted out by warships. Dreadnoughts the size of small moons. Carriers that held millions of fighter craft. Orbital bombardment platforms whose primary weapons could crack continental plates. Every one of them had been recalled from their conquests, their garrisons, their patrols. Thronegate had stripped its empire bare to face this threat.

The magical corps had assembled in full. Archmages who had spent centuries mastering arts that could rewrite local reality. Battle-wizards whose destructive capacity was measured in solar masses. Summoners who had bound entities from dimensions where the laws of physics were suggestions rather than rules.

The dragons came next—ancient beasts, some older than Thronegate itself, their scales impervious to conventional weapons, their breath capable of reducing cities to glass. The smallest among them could level a mountain range. The largest could crack a continent.

And finally, the ninja corps. They moved so fast that time bent around them. The greatest among them could reverse causality itself, striking before they struck, killing enemies who had already died. They were Thronegate's ultimate assassins, deployed only against threats that conventional force could not address.

King Robert watched from a command bunker three hundred miles away, surrounded by his remaining advisors. They had tried to talk him out of this. They had pointed out that this Godric had walked through every defense the palace possessed as if it didn't exist. They had suggested negotiation, appeasement, flight.

Robert had refused. He was King of Thronegate. He did not flee. He did not negotiate with relics of dead civilizations. He crushed them, as his ancestors had crushed Olympus, as Thronegate had crushed everything that had ever dared oppose it.

"All forces report ready," a general said. "On your command, sire."

Robert leaned forward. Through the bunker's viewport, magnified by the finest optical systems ever constructed, he watched the valley. A single figure stood at its center. Godric. Arms at his sides. Eyes half-closed. Motionless.

"Begin," Robert said.

The first wave struck with enough force to vaporize a small planet.

Plasma bolts. Gravity distortions. Phase disruptions. Magical assaults that tore at the fabric of spacetime. Dragon fire hot enough to ignite fusion reactions. Ninja strikes that arrived before they were launched, striking at a Godric who existed several seconds in the past.

The combined assault continued for thirty seconds. When the light faded and the dust settled, the Valley of Sorrows had been transformed into a crater seventeen miles deep. The atmospheric shockwave had circled the planet three times. The energy release had been visible from neighboring star systems.

Godric stood at the crater's center, exactly where he had been standing before the assault began. He had not moved. He had not raised a hand. He had not acknowledged the attack in any way. His tunic was not even singed.

"Interesting," he said, and his voice carried to every soldier, every pilot, every mage, and every dragon in the valley. Not through technology. Not through magic. Simply because he willed it, and reality obeyed. "You've brought quite the collection. I'm almost impressed."

He tilted his head slightly. The motion was casual. Almost lazy.

"Allow me to demonstrate the difference between us."

Godric did not move. He did not gesture. He did not speak any words of power. He simply thought, and his thought became truth.

The warships in orbit—every dreadnought, every carrier, every bombardment platform—simultaneously lost power. Not weapon systems. Not shields. Power. The fundamental energy that allowed matter to hold together. Their atoms simply forgot how to bond. The fleet of Thronegate, the culmination of centuries of military engineering, dissolved into quantum foam and scattered on the solar wind.

The magical corps felt their powers vanish. Not suppressed. Not countered. Gone. As if the very concept of magic had been retroactively deleted from their personal histories. Archmages who had commanded the fundamental forces of creation found themselves standing in the mud, shivering, utterly mundane.

The dragons tried to flee. Godric's attention brushed against them, and their ancient minds—minds that had witnessed the birth of stars and the death of civilizations—simply shut down. Not killed. Not harmed. But convinced, with absolute and irreversible certainty, that resistance was not merely futile but inconceivable. They landed. They bowed their heads. They waited.

The ninjas came last. They moved beyond time, beyond causality, beyond any defense that a stationary target should have possessed. Their blades struck at a Godric who had existed before the attack was conceived. Their techniques targeted vulnerabilities that should have been present in any physical form.

Their blades shattered. Their bodies convulsed. And as they fell, their minds were filled with understanding—the terrible, crushing understanding that the moment their ancestors' ancestors' ancestors had first conceived of the martial arts that would eventually evolve into their techniques, Godric had already perfected counters to them. Had already claimed mastery over them. Had already rendered them obsolete.

The entire engagement lasted eleven seconds.

Eleven seconds from Robert's command to the complete, absolute, and unconditional annihilation of every military asset Thronegate possessed. Billions of super-soldiers, standing in the mud, their weapons dead. Hundreds of thousands of mages, stripped of their arts. Every dragon, pacified. Every ship, dissolved. Every ninja, broken.

And Godric had not moved a single muscle.

Blake watched from a hilltop three miles away, his wife Anna clutching his arm with fingers gone bloodless from pressure. They had come because Godric had asked them to come. Because he had wanted witnesses. Because, Blake suspected, someone needed to see what happened when ten thousand years of grief and rage and infinite, exponentially multiplying power decided to make a point.

"He didn't even blink," Anna whispered. "Blake, he didn't even blink."

Blake said nothing. What could he say? He had freed this being from stasis twenty years ago. He had watched Godric rebuild, reclaim, transform. He had become, against all probability, the one person Godric seemed to genuinely trust. But moments like this reminded him that the man he called friend was also something that defied all mortal comprehension.

The battle was over. The valley was silent. And somewhere in a bunker three hundred miles away, King Robert was learning what it meant to face a god who had no mercy left to give.

# The Aftermath

Two days later, King Robert was found dead in his private chambers.

The official cause was suicide. The royal physicians, those who remained, confirmed that he had ingested a poison so potent that his organs had liquefied before his body hit the floor. A note was discovered, though its contents were never made public. Those who read it—a handful of scribes and servants—spoke of rambling passages, tear-stained parchment, and a single phrase repeated over and over:

"He was right. He was always right. We never had a chance."

Godric received the news without visible emotion. He stood on the balcony of what had been Robert's palace, looking out over the capital city of Thronegate. The streets below were crowded with citizens—some terrified, some curious, some already beginning to whisper the first tentative words of hope. They had lived under Robert's rule. They knew what kind of king he had been. Perhaps this new ruler, this ancient being who had shattered their military with a thought, might be different.

"Will you address them?" Blake asked, standing at Godric's side.

"In time." Godric's voice was quiet. Not cold—not anymore. Something in it had shifted since the battle. Some ice had cracked. "First, I have something else to do. A gift. For the people of this kingdom who were not responsible for the sins of their ancestors."

He raised his hand.

The Awakening began.

# The Awakening

Across the entire planet, simultaneously, every male citizen of Thronegate felt something change within them. It was not painful. It was not frightening. It was like a deep breath drawn after a lifetime of shallow respiration—the sudden, startling realization that they had been operating at a fraction of their true capacity.

Godric's voice spoke in their minds. Not intrusively. Not commandingly. Simply informing.

"For ten thousand years, the rulers of Thronegate hoarded power. They built their empire on the backs of their citizens, reserving strength for themselves while giving their people nothing but obedience and fear. This ends now. From this day forward, every man of Thronegate will receive what I call the Awakening. For every year you live past the age of twenty, your strength shall increase tenfold. Your endurance, your intelligence, your wisdom—all multiplied. This applies to all men, regardless of their current age. Those of you who are eighty will find yourselves sixty times stronger than you were yesterday. Those of you who are forty will be twenty times enhanced."

He paused. Let the information settle.

"This power is yours to control. You may suppress it to normal human levels, or raise it to your full potential. You are not slaves to this gift—you are its masters. You remain mortal. You will age. You will eventually die, as all mortals must. But the years you have will be lived as something more than what Thronegate's kings allowed you to be."

The reaction was immediate and overwhelming. Across the planet, men tested their new limits. A dockworker who had spent forty years loading cargo discovered he could lift a starship with one hand. A scholar who had spent decades studying mathematics found he could perform calculations that quantum computers would need hours to process. A father who had always feared he couldn't protect his children realized he now possessed the strength of twenty men.

But it was more than just physical power. Godric wove subtle protections into the Awakening—safeguards against abuse, against tyranny, against the cruel impulses that unchecked strength so often awakened. The men of Thronegate would be powerful, yes. But they would also be just. They would be wise. They would be the protectors their families deserved.

And for the women—Godric ensured their absolute protection. Any man who attempted to use his awakened strength against a woman would find that strength deserting him. The Awakening was a gift, but it came with rules. With consequences. With the unspoken understanding that power without restraint was merely brutality, and brutality would not be tolerated.

Blake felt the Awakening differently. Where other men felt their strength multiply tenfold, he felt something else. Something deeper. His body surged with power that dwarfed what the other citizens received—a thousandfold increase where theirs was merely ten. He didn't understand why. He would never understand why. But somewhere in his blood, in the genetic legacy of a wizard who had sacrificed everything to save his king, the Awakening recognized its own.

"Godric," Blake said slowly, flexing his hands as light crackled between his fingers, "what did you do to me?"

Godric turned from the balcony. For the first time since his awakening, his smile was almost warm.

"I gave you what you needed to stand beside me, old friend. Nothing more."

# The Subjugation of the Omniverse

The expansion began within weeks. Godric had rebuilt Olympus once—now he would build something greater. Something that would ensure no tragedy like his family's murder could ever occur again. The Thronegate Empire would become an omniversal power, stretching across countless realities, but it would be an empire built on choice rather than conquest.

The rule was simple. Every universe targeted for assimilation would first receive an offer of unconditional surrender. They would join the empire willingly, retaining their cultures, their governments, their ways of life. The only requirement was acknowledgment of Godric's ultimate authority and acceptance of the empire's protection. Refusal meant subjugation. But refusal was always their choice.

Most refused.

# Universe 7-Theta: The Psionic Dominion

The Psionic Dominion had detected the approaching Thronegate expansion six months before the offer of surrender arrived.

For five centuries, the Dominion had ruled their universe through the power of collective consciousness. Every citizen was telepathically linked, their thoughts merged into a gestalt intelligence that spanned galaxies. The Dominion's ability to coordinate was absolute—entire civilizations of billions working in perfect harmony, their combined psionic might capable of tearing holes in reality itself.

They had watched the fall of neighboring universes with growing alarm. They had analyzed every scrap of data their far-seers could gather. They had spent months preparing defenses that drew upon the full psionic weight of two hundred trillion linked minds.

When the emissary arrived—an older Thronegate man named Marcus, fifty-seven years old, a grandfather of seven, a former librarian who had never fired a weapon in his life—the Dominion's response was swift and final.

"You will leave this universe," the Dominion's speaker declared, his voice resonating with the combined authority of two hundred trillion souls. "Or you will be unmade."

Marcus sighed. He had really hoped this one would surrender. His wife was making his favorite dinner tonight, and he'd hate to be late.

"I'm going to give you one more chance," he said. "Please. I'm not asking you to lose anything. You keep your worlds, your people, your way of life. You just acknowledge King Godric's sovereignty and accept the empire's protection. That's all."

"Your king," the speaker sneered, "is nothing to us. Our psionic might encompasses reality itself. We have seen the minds of gods and found them wanting. Your Godric will break against our collective will like water against stone."

"The thing about water and stone," Marcus said quietly, "is that water always wins. It just takes time." He looked at the four other men behind him—all over thirty-five, all awakened, all hoping this wouldn't come to violence. "We tried."

The Dominion struck first. Two hundred trillion psionically linked minds focused their collective power into a single attack—a wave of mental force designed to unmake the invaders at the conceptual level, erasing not just their bodies but their very existence from the timestream.

Marcus raised his hand. The Awakening surged within him, fifty-seven years of accumulated power—thirty-seven of those past twenty, multiplied tenfold for each year. The mathematics were staggering. The reality was more so.

He caught the psionic wave in his palm and crushed it.

“You've always known,” I whispered. “All this time. You never needed us.”

“Needed?” He tilted his head, genuinely puzzled. “No, I never needed your magic. But I wanted your company. Your ancestor Blake was my dearest friend, and through his line, I've kept a piece of him alive. I've watched each of you grow, learn, laugh, struggle. It's a gift to me, Gorm. Your magic, though small, is beautiful. It's the intent behind it that I treasure.”

Small. He called it small. He meant it as a kindness, a gentle acknowledgment of our effort. But the chasm between his definition of small and my entire existence was an abyss that swallowed every foundation I had built my identity upon. I understood, then, why he kept court wizards. Not for magical counsel—his omniscience rendered our advice laughable. Not for protection—his passive dandruff could undo creation. But for friendship. For the warmth of mortal connection, a reminder of the man he had been before the Stasis Event elevated him beyond godhood. And we, the proud line of Blake, had been honored housecats, purring at his feet while believing ourselves guard dogs.

The horror was not that he was powerful. The horror was that he was kind, and his kindness did not change the fact that we were less than atoms to him. He could, in a single unconscious twitch, erase my entire family from history, and he would be genuinely sorrowful afterward, the way a man might mourn stepping on an ant. He would restore us, of course—he always did—but the temporary annihilation would be real enough for us.

That night, I could not sleep. I sat in my chambers, staring at the shelves of grimoires my ancestors had compiled. Tomes of protective incantations. Volumes of counter-curses. Strategies for defending against arcane predation. All of it useless. Worse than useless—a self-deception maintained by a god's affectionate allowance. I reached out with my own magic, extending my senses across the palace, and felt the wards I had so painstakingly erected. They were intricate, potent, a lifetime's work crystallized. And they were nothing. They existed only because Godric's suppression permitted them to exist. If he withdrew that suppression for even a fraction of a fraction of a Planck second, the wards would dissolve like mist before a supernova, along with every other magical construct in the omniverse.

I recalled the texts on the Weakness Paradox. Long ago, any attempt to exploit Godric's perceived weakness fed his amplification and granted him permanent immunity and mastery. Now, after eternities of such feedings, the paradox had evolved: it not only boosted his already infinite statistics but also retroactively ensured that no harm could ever approach him. His magical aptitude multiplied every Planck second—not by a thousand anymore, but by values that had left mathematics screaming in the void. The Godric of this moment could erase an infinite number of copies of the Godric from a single Planck second ago. And yet, despite this, he still enjoys our company. He still calls me "my boy" and ruffles my hair.

I no longer believe in protection. I believe in the fragile grace of a deity who chooses not to blink. I serve him still, because what else can I do? I love him, and I fear him, and the two are indistinguishable. Every morning, I bow before the throne, and he greets me with a smile that could soothe galaxies, and I remember the Archon's scream, and the way the universe corrected itself without his conscious input.

One day, perhaps, his concealment will slip again. Not through malice, but through the simple, terrifying fact that holding back infinity is impossible even for the infinite. And on that day, I will not die. I will simply cease, along with everything I have ever known, and he will be briefly, gently sad, and then he will bring us back. That is his nature. That is my prayer. That is my nightmare. I am Gorm, Court Wizard of Thronegate, and I know now that my greatest accomplishment will be to never, ever make him frown.
