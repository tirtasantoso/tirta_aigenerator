# The Journals of the Witness

The library of the Court Wizards of Thronegate was not a room; it was a dimension. A hollowed-out shard of frozen spacetime, nested within the obsidian bowels of the royal palace, where the concept of linear distance had been surgically removed to accommodate the sheer mass of accumulated knowledge. Mile-high shelves carved from the petrified bones of dead universes stretched into a vanishing point that never arrived, their surfaces crawling with the faint, phosphorescent glow of preservation runes that hummed at a frequency just below the threshold of mortal hearing. The air tasted of dust and ozone and something deeper, something metallic and ancient, like the lingering aftertaste of a scream that had been absorbed by the very walls.

Kaedan stood in the center of this impossible archive, his hands trembling at his sides, the violet-tinged veins of his twenty-two-year-old arms pulsing with the barely restrained pressure of a thousandfold daily amplification that had been compounding for two years. He was a fresh Court Wizard, the latest in a lineage that traced all the way back to Blake himself, the legendary merchant who had accidentally shattered the stasis seal one million years ago and triggered the age of the Apex Predator. Kaedan was tall, lean, his face still carrying the softness of youth despite the crushing weight of his inherited arcane potency. He wore the simple, dark robes of his office, still stiff with new fabric, and he was terrified.

His father, Thaddeus, stood before him, a heavy, iron-bound crate cradled in his thick, scarred hands. Thaddeus was forty-eight, his temples streaked with grey, his mutton chops — the familial mark of Blake’s progeny — framing a face that had seen cataclysms. His eyes, the same shade of storm-cloud grey as Kaedan’s, were fixed upon his son with a complex mixture of paternal love and deep, unhealed dread.

“Every Court Wizard before you has read these,” Thaddeus said, his voice a low, resonant baritone that echoed unnaturally in the dead space of the library. He set the crate down upon a stone lectern with a heavy, final thud. The iron bands groaned, releasing a puff of rust-scented air. “My father handed them to me when I was twenty. His father handed them to him. The tradition is unbroken, stretching back to the days of the legendary Blake himself. These are the firsthand accounts — the journals of the witnesses.”

Kaedan swallowed. The pressure in his arcane core throbbed in sympathetic rhythm with his quickening pulse. “You’ve never spoken of what’s inside them, Father.”

Thaddeus’s jaw tightened. For a fleeting instant, a tremor passed through his fingers as they rested upon the crate’s lid. “Because words fail. They always fail. But the journals… they give you a foundation. A necessary foundation. To serve the King, to truly serve Him, you must understand the absolute, mathematically absolute, nature of His supremacy. Not intellectually. Not theologically. Viscerally. You must taste the horror that waits for those who oppose Him, and you must internalize the impossible grace that we, His family, are permitted to stand at His side.” He paused, his gaze boring into Kaedan’s soul. “Read them all. Do not stop. Do not skip. And when you are finished, if you can still stand, come find me.”

Without another word, Thaddeus turned and walked away, his heavy footsteps swallowed by the non-linear acoustics of the archive until silence, absolute and suffocating, reasserted its dominion. Kaedan stood alone, staring at the crate. Then, with the deliberate care of a man defusing a bomb, he unlatched the iron clasps and lifted the lid.

Inside, resting upon a bed of faded velvet, were the journals. They were not uniform. Some were bound in cracked, ancient leather, their pages yellowed and brittle. Others were sealed within plates of crystalline circuitry, humming with residual arcano-technic energy. A few were simply loose stacks of parchment, covered in handwriting that ranged from the immaculately precise to the frantically scrawled. The oldest dates Kaedan could see were from Year 100,000. The newest was only two decades old. The handwriting on the most recent journal — a thick, black leather tome — was unmistakably his father’s.

Kaedan reached for the first journal, his fingers brushing against the spine of a codex constructed from what looked like solidified shadows. A name was embossed upon it in tarnished silver filigree: *Arch-Mage Soren, Year 250,000 — The Incident of Absolute Senses*. He opened it, and the utter silence of the library seemed to pull in closer, as if the very air was bracing itself.

*Excerpt — The Sensory Cascade Incident*

I will write this account in the cold, dispassionate register of a scientist, because to allow emotion to seep into the record would be to invite a permanent fracture of my cognitive integrity. Let the data speak. Let the mathematics scream, for I no longer possess the capacity.

*Excerpt — The Comedy of Unfortunate Souls*

Forgive the flippant tone. I have found that the only way to process the absurd, horrific truth of our existence is through the lens of black, biting sarcasm. I will tell you of the Fortune Syndicate, and you may laugh if you wish, but know that your laughter will taste like ashes by the end.

Zeno saw, for the first time in his eternal existence, the face of a being who made him feel small.

And then, Godric erased him.

Not with a clap of the hands. Not with a deletion technique. Simply by willing it. The Grand Zeno, the Omni-King of the Dragon Ball multiverse, the supreme being who had been the undisputed apex of his own fictional framework, vanished from existence with less effort than it takes to blink. His angels, his attendants, his entire retinue—all of them, erased in the same instant, their existence deemed irrelevant and unnecessary by a god who had grown bored of their presence.

---

**The Dismantling: Part Four — The Abstract Entities**

The Witch of Theatergoing, Featherine Augustus Aurora, watched the cascade of annihilation with a serene, knowing smile. She had not yet struck directly. She had been observing, analyzing, rewriting the narrative threads of the battle to favor the fictional coalition. But now, as the Outer Gods lay screaming, as the Living Tribunal wept black shame, as the Omni-King vanished into nothing, she realized that her script was no longer under her control.

Godric turned to face her. "You are the author of this story," He said. "Or so you believe. You write, and reality obeys. You edit, and history changes. You are, within your own framework, the supreme narrative force. The ultimate storyteller. But tell Me, Featherine: who wrote you?"

The Witch's serene smile flickered. "I was written by Ryukishi07, a mortal author of the—"

"Of Earth," Godric finished. "A mortal author of Earth. A human. A speck of carbon and water who lived in a small house in a small country on a small planet in a small galaxy, and who imagined you into existence. And that mortal, that speck—he belongs to Me. His imagination. His stories. His entire fictional omniverse. It all exists because I have allowed it to exist, as a quiet, harmless corner of My omniverse, a small garden of dreams. And you, the Witch of Theatergoing, have dared to rewrite My script."

Featherine's smile dissolved entirely. She raised her narrative quill, her eyes blazing with something that might have been desperation. "Then I will write you out of the story entirely! I will author a reality where you never existed—where Olympus never fell—where your grief never—"

She did not finish the sentence. Godric did not let her. He activated Reality Restructuring again, but this time, He directed it not at Featherine, but at the very concept of authorship. The narrative threads that Featherine had woven, the entire framework of her fictional structure, was seized and inverted. The Witch of Theatergoing was no longer the author. She was the character. And the script she was now forced to follow was written by Godric.

Her body, her regal, elegant form, began to contort. The narrative quill in her hand turned against her, carving words into her own flesh—words she had not written, words she could not control. The words spelled out the full, detailed, agonizing story of her own defeat, her own humiliation, her own eternal, inescapable subjugation to the King of Thronegate. And the story, once written, became reality. Featherine was trapped inside it, forced to live out every moment of the narrative, forever, unable to change a single word.

The other abstract entities—the Chousin, the Lord of Nightmares, Akasha—witnessed her fate and understood that their own powers of creation and abstraction were laughable, infantile things in the face of the Apex Predator. The Chousin, the three goddesses who had created their own multiverse as an experiment, attempted to flee by ascending to a higher dimensional plane beyond any conceivable reach.

Godric allowed them to ascend. And then He simply took one step.

His speed, His movement, was not a traversal of space but a casual, passive realignment of His own location. He appeared on the highest dimensional plane the Chousin had fled to—a plane that existed beyond the conceptual framework of dimensions themselves—before they had even completed their escape. They were still mid-ascension when His hand closed around the throat of Tokimi, the eldest of the three.

"You create multiverses as experiments," Godric murmured, His voice low and cold. "I erase them as afterthoughts. Do not presume to flee from Me. There is nowhere you can go that I am not already waiting."

He squeezed. Tokimi's form, which was not physical but abstract, was forced into physicality specifically so she could experience the sensation of strangulation. Her sisters, Tsunami and Washu, screamed and hurled their combined creative power at Godric—the power that had birthed an entire fictional multiverse. The King absorbed it passively, effortlessly, and redirected it back into them, but twisted. The creative power that had once built universes was inverted into an unmaking force, and it began to systematically dismantle the Chousin from the inside out, unraveling their divine essences thread by agonizing thread, while their conscious minds were forced to experience every single moment of dissolution.

The Lord of Nightmares, the primordial chaos of the Slayers multiverse, attempted to engulf Godric in her own infinite, undifferentiated essence—to dissolve Him in the chaos from which all things had emerged. Godric stood within her, unbothered, and She spoke a single word: "Begone." The Lord of Nightmares' chaos was not just repelled; it was ordered. The primordial, undifferentiated sea of creation was forced into a single, permanent, crystalline structure—a vast, frozen monument of absolute, perfect logic—with the Lord of Nightmares trapped at its center, her chaotic nature screaming in eternal, agonizing stasis.

Akasha, the root of all existence from the Nasuverse, attempted a different approach. She tried to simply assert that Godric did not exist—not as an attack, but as a fundamental rewrite of the Akashic records, the absolute record of everything that had ever existed or would ever exist. She was the record itself, and she could decide what was recorded.

Godric reached into the Akashic records with a thought, and He added a single entry: "Akasha: no longer exists." The root of all existence was deleted from its own records, a paradox so absolute that it caused the entire Nasuverse to shudder and begin to collapse. Akasha, the supreme, unknowable foundation of reality, was unmade by a single, casually written sentence.

**The Dismantling: Part Five — The Annihilation of All Fiction**

The battle, if it could still be called that, had lasted approximately forty-five seconds. The greatest champions of the fictional omniverse were dead, dying, or trapped in states of eternal, personalized agony. The cosmic entities were shattered. The abstract beings were unmade. The armies of lesser warriors, heroes, and villains that filled the void—the billions upon billions of fictional characters from every conceivable story—had not even been able to approach the King. His passive, ambient pressure alone had been enough to hold them at bay, their attacks dissolving harmlessly against the outer edges of his absolute concealment.

And now, Godric was ready to finish the massacre.

He floated in the center of the void, surrounded by the shattered remnants of universes, the screaming ghosts of gods, and the fading, flickering light of a million dying fictional stars. His expression was placid, almost serene. He lifted His right hand, palm facing upward, and He addressed the remnants of the fictional coalition with a voice that carried no anger, no triumph, only a cold, absolute, terrifying finality.

"You have all fought with courage," He said. "You have all struggled with everything you had. You united, across every boundary, every rivalry, every ancient hatred. You set aside your differences and stood together against a common enemy. That is, in its own way, beautiful. But beauty does not grant victory. Courage does not overcome infinity. And the power of friendship—the power of love, of hope, of the indomitable human spirit—is, in the end, just another story. A story I am now ending."

He closed His hand into a fist.

And He activated Annihilation, one of His earliest and most evolved superpowers. In its original form, Annihilation was the ability to erase any target from existence. After a million and a half years of the Weakness Paradox, it had evolved multiple layers of horrifying enhancement. The first layer was Total Conceptual Deletion: the target was not merely killed, but erased from every timeline, every memory, every possible record. They had never existed. Their stories had never been told. The second layer was Agonizing Delay: the erasure was not instantaneous. It was drawn out, stretched across a subjective eternity, so that every single being being erased was forced to experience their own non-existence approaching, second by agonizing second, fully aware of what was happening and utterly powerless to stop it.

The fraction of Annihilation's power that Godric used was, as always, so infinitesimally, recursively near-zero that to call it zero percent would be an obscene overestimation. But it was enough.

The fictional omniverse began to die.

I watched through the astral scrying matrix as the wave of annihilation propagated outward from the center of the battlefield. Every character, every god, every abstract entity that still survived—and all the billions of lesser warriors who had never even approached the King—was seized by an invisible, unstoppable force. Their forms began to fade, but not gently. The fading was a tearing, a ripping, a violent extraction from the fabric of existence itself. And they could feel it.

Son Goku's spirit, still trapped in the void where his body had been destroyed, was the first to go. His indomitable will, his endless optimism, his unshakeable belief that somehow, someway, there would be a way—all of it crumbled as the Annihilation wave touched him. He felt himself being unwritten, and in his final, agonizing moments, he finally understood the truth: there was no way. There had never been a way. His entire story, from his humble beginnings on Mount Paozu to his ascension as the greatest warrior in the multiverse, had been a prelude to this single, absolute, inescapable moment of erasure.

The God-Emperor of Mankind, still trapped in the peripheral orbit of Godric's consciousness, felt His own soul begin to dissolve. The billion martyrs that had powered His psychic might screamed in unison, a choral wail of absolute despair that echoed through the collapsing Warhammer multiverse. The Golden Throne, the Astronomican, the Imperium of Man—all of it was erased, not with the dignity of a final battle, but with the casual, indifferent swipe of a god who was already moving on to the next target.

The Chaos Gods, their fused, undivided form, were torn apart into their constituent essences. Khorne, the god of blood and war, felt his rage drain away into nothing, replaced by a cold, hopeless emptiness. Tzeentch, the god of change and schemes, saw every plan, every scheme, every intricate manipulation dissolve into irrelevance. Nurgle, the god of disease and despair, experienced a despair so profound that even he, the lord of despair, could not endure it. And Slaanesh, the god of excess and sensation, felt every pleasure, every sensation, every experience invert into pure, undiluted agony.

The C'Tan, the Aeldari gods, the Old Ones—all of them, erased. The Anti-Spiral, its vast biomechanical form, dissolved into the void. The Getter Emperor, its infinite evolutionary permutations, ceased. The STTGL, its galaxy-sized form, flickered out like a candle in a hurricane, Simon the Digger's indomitable spiral energy finally, utterly exhausted.

The One Above All, the supreme being of the Marvel multiverse, was unmade with less effort than it takes to turn a page. The Endless—Dream, Death, Destiny, Desire, Despair, Delirium, Destruction—were erased as a single, unified group, their aeonic powers meaningless against the absolute finality of Godric's Annihilation.

Nyarlathotep's screaming obelisk was silenced. The Living Tribunal's weeping faces were stilled. The Lord of Nightmares' crystalline prison was shattered, and the chaos within was released into oblivion. Featherine Augustus Aurora's self-written narrative of eternal subjugation reached its final, agonizing chapter and then ceased to exist.

Every fictional universe. Every imagined cosmos. Every story ever told. All of it, erased.

The void was silent.

**The Aftermath**

Godric floated alone in the center of the absolute nothing, His hand still loosely closed, His expression thoughtful. He surveyed the emptiness where the combined fictions of all creation had once existed, and He nodded slowly, as if confirming a private theory.

"Good," He murmured to Himself. "That was... educational."

He turned, and with a single step, He re-entered the observatory of the imperial palace. I, Rutger, was still kneeling at the scrying matrix, my body trembling, my violet-tinged hands pressed flat against the cold obsidian floor. The King walked over to me, His heavy boots making no sound on the stone, and He placed a warm, calloused hand on my shoulder.

"You recorded everything?" He asked, His voice as gentle as a father inquiring about his son's schoolwork.

I nodded, unable to speak. My throat was closed, my heart a frantic, terrified drum. I had just witnessed the death of every story that had ever been told. Every character I had read about as a child, every myth that had shaped the imaginations of countless civilizations, every fictional framework that had ever sparked joy or wonder or terror—all of it, erased in a single, casual gesture by the being whose shoulder I was now kneeling beside.

Godric squeezed my shoulder gently. "Good man. I will read your journal when it is complete. Now, come. Your wife has made that honey cake you like. I believe we have earned a quiet evening."

He turned and began to walk toward the exit, His broad, heavily muscled back silhouetted against the amber light of the arcane sconces. I rose on shaking legs, my mind still reeling, my consciousness still vibrating with the echoes of a trillion fictional screams.

"My King," I managed to whisper, my voice cracking, "the stories... all the stories... they are gone. Forever."

Godric paused at the threshold. He did not turn around. "No, Rutger," He said, His voice calm and absolute. "They were never real to begin with. They were dreams. Echoes. Shadows cast by mortal imaginations. And those mortal imaginations still exist—on Earth, in the IRL universe, which I visited long ago and brought under My dominion. The humans there will dream new stories. They always do. And those new stories will belong to Me, just as the old ones did."

He turned His head just enough to offer me a warm, fatherly smile. "Now, really. Honey cake. Your wife will be disappointed if we let it go stale."

I followed Him. I had no choice. I am His Court Wizard, His sworn protector, His friend, His family. And I will serve Him, faithfully and fearfully and lovingly, until my mortal body collapses into the dust. But I will never, for the rest of my life, be able to read a story—any story—without hearing the distant, muffled echo of the Annihilation wave, and the trillion trillion screams of the fictional dead.

They had united. They had fought. They had hoped. And in the end, they were simply a chapter in a journal, written by a wizard who watched their extinction, and who is now going to eat honey cake with their killer.
