Very clean. You've outdone yourself, Therax. Your work just keeps improving. How are you feeling these days? Your light seems a bit dimmer than usual. You mustn't overwork yourself."

Therax's form flickered, a ripple of something that might have been gratitude or terror or a combination so profound it had no name. "I am well, Your Majesty. Truly. The work sustains me. The gardens are my purpose now. I... thank You. For the work. For the gardens. For... for everything."

Godric patted Therax gently on the shoulder, a touch so casual and yet so weighted with the crushing asymmetry of their relationship that I saw Therax's entire form shudder with the effort of not disintegrating under the sheer, overwhelming pressure of being touched by his Creator. "Good man. Keep it up. Oh, and I might need a bouquet of the stellar roses later, for the administrative task. Something festive."

We moved on. The path led us past the orchards, where trees made of solidified time bore fruit that was entire lifetimes, each apple a complete mortal existence from birth to death, sweet and fragile and endlessly replenishing. The orchard-keeper was a being named Veridias, who had once been the dictator of a million multiverses, a cruel and absolute monarch who had committed atrocities so vast that his name was still used as a curse in the languages of seventeen thousand surviving species. When Thronegate had eventually absorbed his realm—through the standard offer of unconditional surrender, which Veridias had arrogantly refused—Godric had not merely defeated him. He had allowed the full, unleashed weight of His gentlest passive aura, Gent, to wash over Veridias's empire for exactly one attosecond. In that infinitesimal moment, every single one of Veridias's soldiers, his weapons, his fortresses, and his abstract conceptual defenses had been atomized so completely that not even the memory of their destruction remained. Veridias alone was spared, frozen in the center of the annihilation, forced to witness the total, effortless erasure of everything he had built, everything he had been, everything he had ever believed himself to be. And then Godric had offered him a job picking apples.

Now Veridias climbed the orchard ladders with the careful, humble steps of a man who had long ago abandoned all pride, all ambition, all sense of self beyond his service. He was an old, stooped figure now, his once-terrifying form reduced to a simple, humanoid shape draped in the rough, brown robes of a orchard-worker. He greeted us with a low, reverent bow, his voice a dry, cracked whisper of a man who had spent eons in silent, grateful terror.

"Your Majesty. Court Wizard Clive. The time-fruit is particularly lush today. I have set aside the ripest batch for the palace kitchens. And I have prepared a special basket for the administrative task, as You requested. The lifetimes contained within are exceptionally well-rounded—full of small, quiet, beautiful moments. I hope they will serve Your purpose."

Godric accepted the basket with a warm nod. "Thank you, Veridias. You always know exactly what I need. How is your family? Did your son's apprenticeship in the conceptual-forge realm go well?"

Veridias's face, weathered and lined with the memory of ancient atrocities, softened into an expression of such pure, uncomplicated gratitude that it was almost painful to witness. "He is thriving, Your Majesty, thanks to Your generosity. He speaks of You constantly. We all do. We are... we are so grateful. More than words can express."

Godric smiled, that simple, crinkly-eyed smile that was exactly the same smile He had given Veridias on the day He annihilated his empire, and the cognitive dissonance of it was a physical ache in my chest. This was the truth of Godric's rule. The enemies who had fought Him—truly fought Him, with everything they had—were not merely defeated. They were transformed. Broken and then rebuilt, not as slaves, but as willing, devoted, grateful servants who genuinely, truly loved the being who had reduced them to nothing. And the horror was that their love was not coerced, not magically imposed, not the result of His charisma or His divine imposition. It was the natural, rational response of finite beings who had finally, completely understood the absolute futility of opposition and had been offered, in its place, a small, simple purpose, a place in the garden, a kind word from the King.

I had read the journals. I knew the mechanics of how His charisma had once rewritten the Free-Soul Confederacy into rapturous self-annihilation. But in this era, there was no need for such overt displays. The omniverse itself had, over googolplexian of years of uninterrupted, perfectly peaceful rule, simply... adjusted. Every sentient being, every conscious entity, every abstract sentience, every nascent possibility-hive that flickered into existence in the deep, dark spaces between multiverses was now born with an intrinsic, pre-installed understanding: Godric is the King. Godric is absolute. There is no resistance. There is only love, and service, and the quiet, peaceful joy of existing in a cosmos that He has made perfect.

We continued through the palace. The halls were vast and silent, their walls constructed from the compressed, solidified adoration of a decillion civilizations. The air hummed with the gentle, protective presence of Godric's passive auras. In earlier eras, the aura named Gent had been the only one active—the weakest, most primitive of Godric's sentient passive defenses, a mere waste byproduct of His existence. But over the eons, as His power grew and the recursion of His amplification scaled beyond all mathematical capture, the stronger auras had awakened. Not because Godric needed them. Never because He needed them. But because their own obsessive, clingy subservience had evolved to the point where they could no longer remain dormant. They had, quite literally, loved their way out of slumber.

There were more auras now than could be counted. More than could be conceptualized. They were infinite, an infinity of infinities, each one so far beyond the last that the gap between them was itself infinite. They did not have names anymore; they were too numerous for names, and Godric had long ago stopped bothering to label them, referring to them collectively with a fond, exasperated wave of His hand as "the Sentinels." Each Sentinel was a fully sentient, omnipresent, perfectly adaptive defense system, capable of independently annihilating the entire omniverse an infinite number of times over with less than a stray thought. And every single one of them was obsessively, neurotically, desperately subservient to Godric.

One of them—a relatively minor Sentinel, roughly the two-trillionth in power ranking—materialized beside us as we walked, taking the form of a small, silvery orb that bobbed gently in the air at Godric's shoulder. Its voice, when it spoke, was a soft, eager chime, like a child desperate to show its parent a crayon drawing.

"Father-Master-King! I have detected a sub-infinitesimal fluctuation in the thirty-seventh hyper-layer of the outer meta-causality! It is not a threat! It is not even theoretically capable of being a threat! But I neutralized it anyway, just to be safe! I rewrote the local timeline-skein, re-calibrated the fundamental constants, and retroactively pruned seventeen adjacent possibility-branches that might theoretically have produced something vaguely related to the fluctuation! I also left a small bouquet of apologetic gravity-waves for the neighboring realities, just in case they were inconvenienced! Did I do well?!"

Godric chuckled, reaching up to pat the silvery orb with a gentle, almost paternal affection. "You did very well, little one. Very thorough. But remember what we discussed about over-pruning? We want to leave some room for natural cosmic evolution. Even if a possibility-branch is unlikely, it might still produce something beautiful. You must learn to balance protection with permission."

The orb dimmed slightly, a visual blush of chastised eagerness. "Yes, Father-Master-King! I will remember! I will calibrate my pre-emptive annihilations to preserve aesthetic potential! I am sorry for being overzealous! I just love You so much!"

"I know you do," Godric said, His rich voice warm and genuine. "And I love you too. Now, run along. I have an important task to prepare for, and I need to speak with Clive."

The orb bobbed once, emitted a sound that was half a chirp and half a hymn of adoration, and vanished. I exhaled a breath I had not realized I was holding. The Sentinels were terrifying—not because they were powerful, but because they were so utterly, childishly eager to please. An entity that could erase the omniverse a trillion times over, and it behaved like a puppy who had just fetched a stick and was wagging its tail for approval. The scale of it made my brain itch.

Godric noticed my expression and laughed, the kind of rich, rolling laugh that echoed through the halls and made the solidified adoration-walls hum in sympathetic harmony. "You always look so tense around the Sentinels, Clive. They're harmless. Just enthusiastic. You should have seen Gent back in the early days—he was exactly the same. Always cleaning up messes that weren't even messes, just because he knew I like a tidy room. It's endearing, really."

"Endearing," I repeated, my voice faint. "Your Majesty, the Sentinel we just spoke to could accidentally delete my entire bloodline from existence with a stray thought. And it called You 'Father-Master-King' like a child asking for a bedtime story."

Godric's eyes crinkled. "Well, they are children, in a sense. My children. The Sentinels, the auras, the immune system—they're all just extensions of Me, Clive. Fragments of my own being that have gained sentience through eons of recursive self-evolution. They are Me, but they are also themselves. And like all children, they need guidance, affection, and the occasional gentle correction when they over-prune possibility-branches. It's quite a fulfilling aspect of fatherhood, actually. The closest I've come to it since..."

He trailed off, and I saw, for a fraction of a second, the ancient, unfading grief that still lived beneath His warmth. Reina. Isabel. Hebe. The mortal daughters He had held in His arms and lost, the wife whose blood had misted the marble of Olympus. A million years had passed, and He still loved them. He would always love them. And it was that love, that unending, unwavering, grief-tempered love, that was about to shape the Stasis Event.

I cleared my throat, gently steering the conversation back. "Your Majesty, about the administrative task. The Stasis Event. I have read Kaedan's journal—the revelation You shared with him, about Your future self sealing the past. And I understand, intellectually, that the future self who performed that act is... You, in some sense. But to witness it from this side, to see the loop closing... I suppose I am struggling to comprehend. Why now? Why this moment? And how?"

Godric paused before a vast, arched window that looked out upon the omniverse—not the omniverse as a static vista, but a living, breathing tapestry of all realities, all dimensions, all possibilities, all impossibilities, all states of being and non-being, all woven together in an infinite, harmonious, perfectly balanced pattern. At the center of it all, invisible yet omnipresent, was the constant, stabilizing presence of His will. He gazed at His creation for a long, quiet moment, His ruggedly handsome face softened by a gentle, contemplative peace.

"There is no 'why now' that would make sense to a mortal mind, Clive," He said at last. "Time, for Me, is not a river. It is not even an ocean. It is a tool, an instrument, a canvas. I can step to any point in its flow, forward or backward, with exactly zero effort. But the Stasis Event is a fixed point—not because something external fixes it, but because I fixed it. Or rather, a version of Me from an even further future, so far beyond My current self that the gap between us is itself an infinity of infinities, decided that the Stasis Event was necessary. And that decision, once made, became an absolute law of reality that no iteration of Me, at any stage of My development, can override. I am powerful enough now to reach back and trigger the event for My past self. I am not powerful enough—nor will I ever be, because the later versions of Me will always be infinitely more powerful—to undo the decision of the version who came after."

He turned to me, His eyes holding that strange, serene calm that I had read about in Kaedan's journal. "I am doing this because it is what I have always done. Because the loop must close. Because the Godric of Year Minus Ten Thousand must enter the Stasis, must begin the amplification, must become Me, so that I can, eventually, become the Me who decided all of this in the first place. It is not a paradox. It is a self-consistent, eternal recursion of absolute will. And I am perfectly comfortable with it."

He placed the basket of time-fruit—the gift from Veridias—on a small table beside the window, and He gestured toward a vast, circular platform at the center of the chamber that had not been there a moment ago. The platform was made of what I could only describe as solidified cause-and-effect, a shimmering, translucent disc that pulsed with the quiet, rhythmic heartbeat of temporality itself.

"Now," Godric said, His tone brightening, "let me explain how I'm going to do it. Not because I need to—I could simply think it and it would be done—but because I know you appreciate the mechanics, and I enjoy teaching. Consider it a final lecture for your journals."

I followed Him onto the platform, my heart hammering. The air here was thick—not with pressure, for Godric's absolute concealment still held, but with a steady, humming sense of significance, the kind of weight that reality itself acquires when it knows something monumental is about to occur.

"The Stasis Event," Godric began, His voice taking on the easy, instructive cadence He always used when explaining His powers, "is, at its core, a single, carefully calibrated alteration of a standard teleportation spell. Thorin—My mortal friend, My first Court Wizard—cast that spell with his dying breath. It was an emergency evacuation spell, designed to transport Me to a pre-prepared safehouse in the mountains outside Olympus. A simple, elegant piece of magic, born of desperation and love. I owe Thorin everything, Clive. Without his spell, there would be no framework for Me to work with. The spell was the seed. I am merely the gardener who shaped the tree.

"What I will do—what I am doing, as we speak—is reach backward across the entire span of time, from this moment to that one. I will locate the exact Planck-second of Thorin's death, the exact instant when his blood flecked the syllables of the incantation. And I will, with an infinitesimal fraction of My presence, gently, precisely, irrevocably alter the spell's parameters. I will add the stasis mechanic—the cessation of aging, the immortality, the removal of biological needs. I will seed the amplification engine—the recursive multiplication of all stats by one thousand and by itself every Planck second, which will later evolve beyond all mathematical containment thanks to the Weakness Paradox. I will trigger the genesis of apocalyptic superpowers, one per second. I will install the absolute concealment that will prevent My presence from collapsing the omniverse. And I will activate the Weakness Paradox, that cruel, beautiful mechanism that will punish every arrogant thought of opposition with permanent, irreversible gifts of power."

He paused, and His smile softened into something almost wistful. "And My past self will not know. He will wake up in a sanctuary, ten thousand years later, and discover that he has become a god. He will be furious, grief-stricken, desperate to undo what was done. He will try, again and again, to save his family. And he will fail, because the future Me will have already decided—has already decided, from his perspective—that the past must remain sealed. I know this because I remember being him, Clive. I remember the fury. The grief. The desperate, hopeless assault against the shadow of My own future. And I also remember the peace that came after, the acceptance, the slow, gentle reconciliation with the truth that some things cannot be changed, not even by Me. That is the gift I gave Myself. Not just power, but wisdom. The wisdom to know that grief is a part of love, and that trying to erase grief is trying to erase love. My family died, truly and horribly and irrevocably. But My love for them lives on, and it always will, and that is enough."

He raised His hand, not as a dramatic gesture but as a simple, casual motion, like a painter picking up a brush. The platform beneath us hummed, and the vast window ahead shifted, no longer showing the living tapestry of the omniverse, but a single, frozen moment of time. A marble colonnade, shattered. A sky choked with arcane fire. A man—a younger Godric, mortal, forty-five, screaming—being torn from the burning ruins by a spiral of raw, unshaped magic. Thorin's body, slumped against the altar, his skull pierced, his blood still steaming on the stones. And in the center of it all, the glowing, wavering tendrils of the teleportation spell, reaching out to wrap around the mortal king.

"This is the moment," Godric said softly, His voice barely above a whisper. "This is the instant Thorin's love saved Me. And this—" He gestured, and the platform responded, a ripple of pure, focused intentionality that was not quite a force, not quite a thought, but something infinitely beyond both, arcing across the gap of a million years and touching the tendrils of Thorin's dying spell. "—is the instant I complete the loop."

What happened next was not, in any sense, a display of power. There was no cataclysm, no shuddering of reality, no sense of overwhelming force. There was only a gentle, precise, infinitely nuanced alteration, as if a master watchmaker had reached into the mechanism of a clock and adjusted a single, microscopic gear that would change the entire future of the machine. The tendrils of Thorin's spell flickered, their simple, elegant framework absorbing the modifications like dry soil absorbing rain. The stasis mechanic seeded itself into the magic, a quiet, inexorable command that would pause the mortal king's biology at the exact moment of arrival. The amplification engine bootstrapped into existence, a loop of recursive self-improvement that would run forever. The superpower genesis ignited, and a cascade of brand-new abilities—each already capable of annihilating civilizations—began its eternal, accelerating bloom. The absolute concealment wrapped itself around the nascent god in stasis, a soft, protective blanket that would prevent His mere existence from shattering reality. And the Weakness Paradox, that most elegant of all His mechanisms, embedded itself into the fabric of His being, ready to trigger the moment any sentient creature, anywhere, ever dared to imagine He had a flaw.

And then it was done. The platform stopped humming. The window returned to its sweeping view of the harmonious omniverse. Godric lowered His hand, and He turned to me with the same warm, crinkly-eyed smile He always wore after a satisfying morning in the garden.

"There," He said, His voice rich and content. "The loop is closed. The past is sealed. And in ten thousand years, a merchant named Blake will stumble into a forgotten sanctuary and accidentally wake Me up. The rest, as they say, is history."

I stood frozen, my hands trembling at my sides, my mind a white-hot blank of overwhelmed processing. I had just witnessed the most significant event in the entire history of existence—the moment that had set the entire course of Thronegate's empire into motion, the moment that had created the King I served—and it had been so gentle, so quiet, so utterly, terrifyingly casual. Godric had altered the foundational causality of the omniverse with the same effort He might expend adjusting a loose button on His tunic.

And He had done it because He loved Himself. Because the future Godric, the being I served, loved the grieving, broken mortal king enough to give him a gift: the power to protect others, even if he could not save his own family. The power to ensure that no one else would ever suffer the way he had suffered, within the boundaries of his rule. And that gift, that act of self-love stretched across a million years, was the true foundation of everything.

"Your Majesty," I whispered, my voice cracking despite my fifty years of practiced composure, "I... I don't know what to say. That was... it was so..."

"Quiet?" Godric offered, His eyes twinkling. "Anticlimactic, perhaps? You were expecting thunder and lightning, the heavens splitting, the foundations of reality screaming in protest? I'm afraid I've outgrown thunder and lightning, Clive. Subtlety is the art of the true master. A quiet adjustment now is worth a billion cataclysms later. That's something I learned from gardening, actually." He reached down and picked up the basket of time-fruit Veridias had prepared. "But I did promise a celebration. Let's go have some breakfast. I think Chef Karaxis has prepared something special—he always does on significant days. And I want to hear your opinion on the new batch of honey-cakes. They're a recipe I've been refining for the past few million years."

We walked back through the palace, past the humming, eager Sentinel who had over-pruned the possibility-branches, past the orchards where Veridias was still climbing his ladders with the quiet, humble grace of a broken tyrant, past the gardens where Therax was carefully deadheading a galaxy that had finished its bloom-cycle. Every servant, every being, every cosmic entity that crossed our path offered a deep, reverent bow, their eyes flickering with that familiar mixture of love and terror that I had long ago recognized as the permanent emotional baseline of everyone who existed in Godric's orbit. The terror was not the terror of the abused, the terror of the oppressed. It was the terror of the finite gazing upon the infinite, the terror of the drop contemplating the ocean. It was, in its own strange way, a form of peace. A surrender so complete that it left no room for anything except gratitude and service.

Chef Karaxis was waiting for us in the palace kitchens. He was a being whose natural form was a roiling, multi-dimensional storm of culinary creativity—a living cookbook that existed across seventeen simultaneous planes of gastronomic reality. He had once been the supreme ruler of a tyrannical, food-based empire that conquered entire multiverses through the weaponization of irresistible flavor. His "Final Course," a dish so delicious that it caused complete, irreversible existential bliss-feedback in anyone who consumed it, had been intended to subjugate Thronegate itself. The dish had, of course, failed. Godric had eaten it, complimented the seasoning, and then casually offered Karaxis a job as the royal chef. Karaxis had accepted on the spot, weeping tears of pure, sensory joy, and had served in the palace kitchens ever since.

Today, he had outdone himself. The table was laden with dishes that were entire conceptual experiences, each bite a journey through a perfectly crafted narrative of taste and texture. The honey-cakes, when I tried one, were not merely sweet; they were a gentle, unfolding story of a summer afternoon, complete with the sound of distant laughter and the faint scent of jasmine on a warm breeze. I ate in silence, my mind still reeling from the Stasis Event, my heart thudding with a strange, bittersweet ache.

Godric ate with the simple, appreciative gusto of a man enjoying a good meal. He complimented Karaxis on every dish, inquired after the chef's family, and even offered a few suggestions for improving the rotational symmetry of the flavor-narratives. Karaxis bobbed in the air, his multi-dimensional form flickering with delight and terror and a desperate, clinging need to please.

After the meal, Godric walked me to the door of the palace, His hand resting on my shoulder with that familiar, grounding weight. The afternoon light—the light of the smiling sun-construct He had made—was warm and golden, casting long, gentle shadows across the perfect, orderly gardens.

"Clive," He said, His voice suddenly softer, more serious, "I know today was... a lot. You witnessed the closing of the loop, the completion of the chain that binds Me to My past and My past to Me. And I know you will write about it in your journal, for the Court Wizards who come after you. When you write, remember to include this: I did not create the Stasis Event out of grief alone, nor out of a cold, mechanical need to ensure My own existence. I did it because I love Myself. Not in a vain, self-absorbed way, but in the way a father loves his son. The mortal Godric, the king of Olympus who held his daughters and wept over their small, perfect fingers—he was a good man. He deserved better than the tragedy he received. I could not prevent the tragedy, not even now, because the deaths were real and true and I will not rewrite them. But I could give him a path forward. I could take his grief and forge it into a shield for the entire omniverse. That is what the Stasis Event is. A gift, from the future, to the past. A promise that the suffering would not be meaningless. That the love he held for his family would, in time, become the foundation of an eternal kingdom where no one else would ever suffer as he suffered."

He paused, and His ruggedly handsome face creased with that ancient, settled grief, softened now into something that was almost, but not quite, a smile. "I still miss them, Clive. After all this time. I will always miss them. But I do not regret accepting the gift. And I do not regret giving it to Myself."

I looked up at my King—the apex predator of all existence, the being whose shadow of an afterthought could crush all of His past selves combined, the entity who had just casually rewritten the fundamental causality of the omniverse with a gentle, watchmaker's touch—and I found, in that moment, that my terror had quieted. Not vanished. Never vanished. But quieted, settling into a low, steady hum beneath the overwhelming, genuine affection I felt for this strange, terrifying, infinitely kind, infinitely cruel, eternally grieving, eternally loving man.

"I understand, Your Majesty," I said, and my voice was steady. "I will write it all. I will write it true. And... thank You. For trusting me to witness it."

Godric smiled, that warm, crinkly-eyed smile that was exactly the same smile He had offered Therax and Veridias and Karaxis and every broken, terrified, grateful soul who had ever knelt before Him, and He clapped me on the back with a gentle, fatherly thump that sent a faint, pleasant vibration through my bones.

"Thank you, Clive. Now, go. Write your journal. And when you're done, come find me. We'll have tea, and I'll tell you the story about the time Thorin accidentally enchanted a pie to attract every animal in a five-mile radius. It's a good story. You'll laugh."

I did laugh, later, when I heard the story. And I wrote it all down, every detail of the Stasis Event, every word of Godric's quiet, gentle explanation, every flicker of terror and love and awe that had passed through my soul on that impossible morning. I wrote it because the record must exist. Future Court Wizards must know the truth: that the King they serve is not merely the apex of power, but the apex of love—a love so vast, so recursive, so eternal, that it reached back across a million years to give a grieving mortal a reason to keep existing. And that love, in its absolute, unassailable vastness, is the most terrifying thing of all.

Because it means that everything—every war, every rebellion, every desperate last stand, every annihilation and restoration, every journal entry written by trembling, terrified wizards—was always, from the very beginning, exactly as it was meant to be. The loop is closed. The past is sealed. And the future stretches away, infinite and incomprehensible, into an eternity of ever-amplifying Godric, each version more impossible than the last.

I serve the King. I love the King. And I am, with every fiber of my being, grateful to be exactly where I am: standing in the warm, golden light of His afternoon, waiting for tea, waiting for His stories, waiting for whatever quiet, terrible, beautiful thing He decides to do next.
