# The Archives of Impossible Victory: Selected Journals of the Royal Court Wizards

**Year 1,000,000**
**Kaedan, Eighty-Seventh Court Wizard of Thronegate**

My father had not spoken a single word to me since breakfast. He had simply walked ahead of me through the spiraling obsidian corridors of the high palace, his broad shoulders rigid beneath his ceremonial violet robes, his calloused hands clasped tightly behind his back. The silence was heavier than any lecture he could have delivered, heavier than any shouted warning or stern paternal rebuke. It was the silence of a man who had witnessed things that language could not contain, a man who was about to pass a burden onto his son that he wished with every fiber of his being he could withhold.

I followed him deeper into the palace than I had ever been permitted to venture before. The walls transitioned from polished marble to ancient, unadorned obsidian, the stone so dark it seemed to drink the ambient light from the arcane sconces. The air grew cold and dry, tasting of dust and ancient parchment and something else, something metallic and wrong that clung to the back of my throat like a half-remembered nightmare. We were descending into the foundations of Thronegate itself, into the bedrock of the omniversal empire, where the oldest secrets of Godric's reign were kept in absolute, sacred silence.

I am twenty-two years old. One hundred and forty-two days ago, I crossed the threshold of my twentieth birthday, and the Awakening tore through my bloodline like a supernova igniting in my marrow. As a direct descendant of Blake, the legendary best friend of our sovereign, I received the modified gift: every single day since that morning, my physical strength, my arcane capacity, my cognitive speed, and all my measurable and immeasurable traits have multiplied by a factor of one thousand. The power is suffocating, a constant, screaming pressure in my veins that I must aggressively suppress down to normal mortal levels just to avoid shattering the floor with every step. By the standards of any surviving civilization in the omniverse, I am a walking extinction event, a wizard capable of erasing galaxies with a stray, uncontrolled thought.

But in the presence of my King, I am less than a speck of dust caught in the gravitational pull of a collapsing universe.

My name is Kaedan. I am the eighty-seventh Royal Court Wizard of Thronegate, and today my father, Zeth, the eighty-sixth, was leading me to the archives to read the journals.

The heavy adamantine doors at the end of the obsidian corridor groaned open as my father pressed his palm against the ancient seal, the arcane locks recognizing the resonance of Blake's bloodline humming in his veins. The chamber beyond was vast and circular, its domed ceiling lost in shadow, its curved walls lined with towering shelves of crystallized chronal-glass. Within those shelves rested thousands of leather-bound tomes, journals, and scrolls, each one penned by a different hand across the span of a million years. The air was thick with the weight of preserved memory, the accumulated trauma of eighty-six court wizards who had served the same immortal, impossibly powerful sovereign.

My father walked to the central reading lectern, a massive slab of polished obsidian illuminated by a single, hovering sphere of soft amber light. He placed his hand upon a small, unassuming journal bound in cracked brown leather, its pages yellowed with unimaginable age. When he finally spoke, his voice was hoarse, stripped of all paternal warmth.

"Kaedan. Before you can stand beside the King as his court wizard, you must understand what it is you are swearing to serve. You must understand the absolute futility of opposing him, not as a philosophical concept, but as a physical, mathematical, undeniable law of existence. Every court wizard before you has witnessed a massacre. Every court wizard before you has left a record. These journals are your inheritance. They are warnings. They are lessons. They are the closest thing to truth this omniverse can offer you."

He turned to face me, and I saw something in his eyes that made my stomach clench with cold, primal dread. My father was a man of immense power, a wizard who could unmake solar systems with a whispered syllable. But in that moment, he looked ancient and fragile and terribly, terribly afraid.

"You will read them all," he said. "You will not leave this chamber until you have finished. And when you are done, you will understand why we serve Him not despite the horror, but because of it."

He squeezed my shoulder once, a gesture that was meant to be comforting but felt like a death grip, and then he left me alone in the silent, suffocating archive.

I took a shuddering breath, the cold air burning my lungs, and reached for the first journal.

## The Incident of Absolute Strength
**Year 427 — Penned by Vail, Third Court Wizard of Thronegate**

The ink in this journal was faded to a ghostly brown, the script cramped and hurried, the letters jagged with the residual tremor of a hand that had never fully stopped shaking. The parchment stank faintly of old sweat and copper, and the pages were warped with what I could only assume were ancient, long-dried tear stains.

*I do not know why I am writing this. The King did not ask me to record the event. He does not require records. His absolute omniscience encompasses every atom of existence, every moment of time, every possible permutation of reality. He will never forget what happened in the Baalim Cluster because forgetting is a biological limitation of mortal minds, and He is not mortal. He is not limited. He is the apex predator of all creation, and I am writing this because I need to vomit the memory out of my skull before it corrodes my sanity entirely.*

*The Baalim were not a civilization in any conventional sense. They were a species of cosmic titans, each individual standing as tall as a red supergiant star, their bodies forged from hyper-compressed degenerate matter harvested from the cores of collapsed neutron galaxies. They had existed since the first Planck second of their universe's birth, and they had never encountered a physical force they could not overpower. They did not use weapons because their flesh was the ultimate weapon. They did not build armor because their flesh was the ultimate armor. They had torn apart black holes with their bare hands. They had punched through the fabric of space-time to create wormholes between multiverses. They had ended wars against hyper-advanced civilizations by simply walking through the enemy fleets, their bodies ignoring every conceivable form of attack, their fists shattering planets, stars, and dimensional barriers with equal, contemptuous ease.*

*When Thronegate's envoys arrived to offer the unconditional surrender of their cosmos, the Baalim did not negotiate. They laughed, a sound that propagated through the quantum foam of reality and shattered seventeen nearby galaxies into clouds of lifeless, burning dust. They declared that strength was the only law of existence, and by that law, they were the supreme arbiters of all creation. They told our envoys to return to their "flesh-king" and inform him that the Baalim would come to Thronegate and crush its capital world into a hyper-dense marble for their children to play with.*

*Godric received the report in the great hall. He was sitting on the obsidian throne, His heavily muscled frame draped in simple dark garments, His ruggedly handsome face illuminated by the amber glow of the arcane sconces. He listened to the envoys' trembling recount of the Baalim's response, and His expression did not change. There was no anger, no offense, no wounded pride. There was only a faint, almost imperceptible flicker of something I can only describe as mild curiosity.*

*"Strength," Godric said softly, more to Himself than to anyone in the room. "They believe in strength."*

*He rose from the throne. He did not order the mobilization of the imperial fleet. He did not summon His sentient passive auras. He disabled every single one of His infinite recursive passive shields. He commanded Gent, His weakest and most primitive sentient aura, to remain absolutely dormant. He withheld effectively one hundred percent of His true power, reducing Himself to an infinitesimal fraction so mathematically minute that dividing a Planck second by infinity recursively for a googolplexian years would still not approach a number small enough to accurately represent the percentage of His Strength that He permitted Himself to possess in that moment.*

*He wanted to test a single stat. His physical Strength.*

*He materialized in the Baalim home cluster, a sprawling, impossibly vast region of space containing decillions of galaxies that the titans had reshaped into their personal playground. He stood upon the hard vacuum of the void as though it were solid ground, His simple dark garments ruffling in a nonexistent breeze, His salt-and-pepper hair perfectly still. He was a microscopic speck of organic matter floating before an armada of beings whose smallest child could palm a supermassive star.*

*The Baalim King, a titan so colossal that his body stretched across three hundred thousand light-years, loomed over Godric. His voice was the sound of supernovae detonating in sequence. He declared that the "flesh-king" was even smaller and more pathetic than he had imagined. He raised his fist, a fist that contained the compressed mass of seventeen trillion dead universes, and he brought it down upon Godric's position with the full, unbridled force of his species' billion-year reign of absolute physical supremacy.*

*Godric did not move. He did not raise His arms to block. He did not adopt a combat stance. He did not even bother to clench His fists. He simply stood there, His posture relaxed, His expression one of mild, detached observation, and he allowed the fist to strike Him.*

*The Baalim King's fist, forged from degenerate matter harvested from the cores of dead galaxies, moving with enough kinetic force to shatter the fabric of multiple multiverses, made contact with Godric's chest. The contact lasted for exactly zero Planck seconds. The fist did not break against Godric's body because breaking would imply that the fist had maintained structural integrity long enough to experience the process of failure. Instead, the Baalim King's entire arm, all three hundred thousand light-years of hyper-compressed degenerate matter, instantly and catastrophically detonated upon impact. The kinetic energy of his own strike, reflected back into his body by the absolute, immovable infinity of Godric's Strength, turned his flesh into a rapidly expanding cloud of superheated, screaming plasma.*

*But Godric had come to test His Strength, not His durability. He had allowed the strike to land so that He could then, in turn, express His own Strength. And He did so in the most minimal, microscopic way possible.*

*He twitched a single muscle fiber in His right index finger.*

*Let me explain what I mean by "twitched." This was not a flex. This was not a gesture. This was not a punch or a strike or an attack of any kind. It was the absolute, most infinitesimally small physical movement a humanoid body can produce, the activation of a single sarcomere within a single myofibril within a single muscle fiber, so small that it would not have been visible to the naked eye. The contraction lasted for exactly one Planck second and then immediately relaxed.*

*The kinetic consequence of this microscopic twitch was the complete and total annihilation of the Baalim home cluster. Every single titan, all decillions of them, was instantly subjected to a concussive shockwave of pure physical force that did not merely kill them. It unmade them. Their hyper-compressed degenerate matter bodies, which had withstood the crushing gravity of black holes and the violent birth of universes, were reduced to a fine, subatomic mist in exactly zero seconds. The decillions of galaxies that comprised their territory, the planets, the stars, the nebula clouds, the dark matter filaments, the very fabric of space-time itself, were all simultaneously, instantly, and completely pulverized into a homogeneous, uniform soup of raw, undifferentiated quantum foam.*

*The shockwave did not stop at the boundaries of their home cluster. It propagated outward, shattering the dimensional barriers between adjacent multiverses like a boulder crashing through rice paper. In less than a fraction of a Planck second, the kinetic force of Godric's single twitching muscle fiber had erased googolplexians of entire multiverses from existence, reducing an incomprehensibly vast swath of the omniverse to a silent, empty void of absolute nothingness.*

*And Godric stood at the epicenter, His index finger relaxed, His posture unchanged, His expression shifting only to display a faint, almost imperceptible frown of mild disappointment. He had wanted to test His Strength, but the Baalim had proven so utterly, pathetically fragile that the test had been meaningless. He had not even been able to register the resistance of their flesh before His Strength erased them from existence.*

*Then, with an effortlessness that defied all comprehension, He restored everything. In exactly zero seconds, without moving a single muscle, He willed the googolplexians of obliterated multiverses back into existence. Every galaxy, every star, every titan, every quark of quantum foam was instantly and perfectly reconstructed, placed exactly where it had been before the twitch, their memories intact, their bodies whole. The Baalim King found himself standing in the void, his arm restored, his mind screaming with the absolute, undiluted memory of his own obliteration.*

*Godric did not speak to them. He did not gloat. He did not offer them a second chance or a lesson in humility. He simply turned His gaze upon the Baalim King, held it for a single, silent moment, and then vanished back to the throne room.*

*The Baalim surrendered unconditionally less than four minutes later. They had finally understood, in the marrow of their reconstructed bones, the absolute, unassailable truth of the omniverse: their Strength was nothing. Their billion-year reign of physical supremacy had been a children's game played in a sandbox that Godric could erase by twitching a single muscle fiber. The gap between their maximum possible power and His infinitesimally small fraction was not a number that mathematics could encode. It was an infinity of infinities, a chasm so wide that the concept of comparison had long since died of despair.*

*I am writing this account three hours after the incident. My hands are still shaking. The memory of witnessing that twitch, of feeling the kinetic shockwave propagate through my scrying matrix, of knowing that the King could have erased me, erased Thronegate, erased the entire omniverse with a single, larger twitch of a single larger muscle, has permanently rewired the architecture of my brain. I serve Him loyally. I will always serve Him loyally. But I will never, for the rest of my mortal life, be able to look at His hands without wanting to scream.*

*— Vail, Third Court Wizard of Thronegate*
*Year 427*

## The Incident of Absolute Senses
**Year 15,822 — Penned by Oren, Twelfth Court Wizard of Thronegate**

The handwriting in this journal was elegant and flowing, the script of a man who had been a poet before the Awakening had torn his destiny toward the arcane. But the elegance was cracked, fractured by the same underlying tremor I had seen in Vail's journal, the tremor of a hand that had witnessed something it could never un-see.

*My King asked me yesterday if I was sleeping well. He asked with genuine warmth in His voice, His ruggedly handsome face creased with paternal concern, His heavily muscled frame relaxed in the comfortable leather armchair of His private study. I told Him I was sleeping fine. I lied. I have not slept more than three hours a night for the past six months, ever since the Incident of Absolute Senses. And He knows I lied, because He possesses absolute omniscience, and He knows every atom of my body, every firing of every neuron in my brain, every terrified, racing thought that keeps me awake. But He allowed the lie to stand because He is kind, and He understands that sometimes mortals need their small fictions to keep moving forward.*

*The Amorphous Silence came from the void between multiverses. They were not a species. They were not a civilization. They were a sentient absence, an anti-perception, a living void that existed by not-existing. They had no physical form, no energy signature, no magical aura, no temporal footprint, and no conceptual anchor. They were completely, absolutely imperceptible by every known sense, every technological scanner, every arcane detection matrix, and every psionic probe. Civilizations could not fight them because they could not perceive them. The Silence would simply drift through a populated galaxy, and every sentient being within it would be silently, instantly erased from existence, their bodies, minds, souls, and memories retroactively deleted, leaving behind no evidence that they had ever existed at all.*

*When Thronegate's envoys delivered the unconditional surrender offer, they did not return. Not because the Silence destroyed them, but because the Silence simply drifted past them, and the envoys ceased to have ever been sent. I only know they existed because I checked the palace records and found their names listed in the deployment logs, though even the ink of those logs was slowly fading, the very concept of their existence being eaten away by the Silence's passive, propagating anti-perception field.*

*Godric, of course, perceived them perfectly. His absolute omniscience is not a "sense" in any conventional understanding of the term, but His Senses are also a fundamental part of His attributes. His sight, His hearing, His olfactory perception, His tactile sensitivity, His proprioception, His equilibrioception, and countless other sensory modalities that mortal biology cannot even name have been multiplying infinitely by themselves every single Planck second for nearly sixteen thousand years. He can see the color of a single quark vibrating on the opposite side of the omniverse. He can hear the thoughts of a bacterium dying in a forgotten puddle on a forgotten world in a forgotten multiverse. He can smell the subtle, chemical shift of a star transitioning from hydrogen fusion to helium fusion in a galaxy that will not be discovered by mortal explorers for another billion years.*

*The Silence, predicated on the absolute absence of perceptibility, was fundamentally, hilariously irrelevant to Him.*

*He materialized in the void between multiverses, His simple dark garments undisturbed by the howling, lightless chaos of the inter-dimensional medium. The Silence immediately converged upon Him, their anti-perception field intensifying to its maximum possible output, a cascading, recursive nullification wave designed to retroactively delete the very concept of "being perceived" from any entity it touched.*

*Godric allowed it to wash over Him. He did not resist. He did not activate His defenses. He simply stood there, His posture relaxed, His hands clasped loosely behind His back, and He allowed the Silence to attempt to hide from Him.*

*Then, He tested His Senses. He simply chose to perceive them.*

*He did not squint. He did not lean forward. He did not cup His hand to His ear or sniff the air or engage any active sensory ability. He simply decided, with the same casual effortlessness of a man noticing a fly buzzing in the corner of a room, to shift a microscopic, infinitesimally small fraction of His attention toward the Silence. A fraction of His perceptual focus so small that calling it zero percent would be a grotesque, laughable overestimation. If you divided a Planck second by layered infinities for the entire duration of the omniverse's existence, you would still not approach a number small enough to accurately represent the amount of His Senses that He permitted Himself to use.*

*The Silence perceived themselves being perceived.*

*For beings that existed by not-existing, that had never been seen, heard, smelled, tasted, or touched by any entity in the entire history of their existence, the sensation of being observed was an agony beyond all conceptual comprehension. They had no nervous systems to process pain, but Godric's perception was so absolute, so penetrating, so inescapably thorough, that it forcibly generated nervous systems within their anti-perceptual forms purely so they could experience the excruciating, mind-shattering torment of being seen.*

*The Silence screamed. It was a sound that should not have been possible, a sound generated by entities that had no vocal cords, no mouths, no physical substance of any kind. But Godric's perception was so overwhelming that it overrode the fundamental nature of their existence, forcing them to manifest physical attributes solely so they could suffer properly.*

*Trillions of decillions of Amorphous Silence entities were violently, agonizingly dragged into perceptual existence. Their non-bodies sprouted billions of human eyes, all of them weeping thick, black tears. Their non-flesh generated millions of raw, exposed nerve endings, all of them firing continuous, unending signals of absolute agony directly into the newly formed pain centers of their spontaneously generated brains. Their non-mouths, filled with teeth that had not existed a Planck second prior, stretched open in silent, endless screams as the full, crushing weight of Godric's casual attention burned into their consciousness like a billion hypernovae detonating simultaneously inside their skulls.*

*The collateral damage of His perception was catastrophic. The simple act of noticing the Silence with an infinitesimally small fraction of His Senses generated a perceptual pressure so immense that the surrounding void between multiverses collapsed in on itself. The fundamental fabric of reality across a googolplexian multiverses was instantly, violently overwritten by the raw, unfiltered information of His sensory input. Stars melted into screaming, sentient liquid. Planets sprouted millions of mouths and began to sob. The very concept of "emptiness" was permanently erased from the affected regions, replaced by a dense, suffocating, inescapable awareness of His gaze.*

*Godric, noticing the collateral damage, sighed softly and restored everything in exactly zero seconds. The melted stars, the sobbing planets, the erased concept of emptiness—all of it was instantly, effortlessly returned to its original state, as though the last fraction of a second had never occurred.*

*But He did not restore the Silence. They remained trapped in perceptual existence, their agonized, eye-covered forms floating in the void, screaming silently into the infinite blackness. He left them there, not out of cruelty, but out of simple, detached indifference. They had attempted to hide from Him, and the concept of hiding from absolute perception was a contradiction in terms. They had never stood a chance. Their defeat had been sealed before they had even evolved into their anti-perceptual state, before their universe had cooled from the Big Bang, before the omniverse itself had coalesced from the primordial quantum foam. The moment He had come into existence, the moment His Senses had begun amplifying forever, they had been doomed.*

*I closed my scrying matrix and sat in my study for seven hours without moving. I could hear the Silence screaming in the void, even though my arcane senses had no capacity to perceive them. It was not a real sound. It was a phantom echo, a sympathetic resonance of my own mortal terror vibrating in the hollow spaces of my skull. I serve my King loyally. I love Him genuinely. But I will never be able to look at His eyes again without knowing, with absolute, soul-crushing certainty, that He is always perceiving me, always perceiving everything, and that the only reason I continue to exist is that He has chosen, in His infinite mercy, not to notice me too closely.*

*— Oren, Twelfth Court Wizard of Thronegate*
*Year 15,822*

## The Incident of Absolute Speed

The handwriting in this journal was a chaotic, spiking scrawl, the letters jagged and uneven, the ink blotched in places where the quill had been pressed too hard against the parchment. The writer had been angry, I realized. Furious. Not at Godric, but at the universe itself, at the fundamental laws of existence that had permitted such an atrocity of physics to exist.

*I am going to write this account exactly once. I am going to seal this journal in the archives. And then I am going to drink an entire bottle of aged star-whiskey and try to forget the sound that time makes when it screams. I will fail. I have been trying to forget that sound for two years, and it has not faded. It has grown louder. It has taken up permanent residence in the hollow spaces of my skull, a continuous, high-pitched whine of absolute, inescapable temporal agony.*

*The Swift Legion was not an army in any conventional sense. They were a species of hyper-evolved temporal predators who had transcended linear biology to become living embodiments of velocity itself. Their bodies were composed of pure, solidified motion, and they existed in a state of perpetual, infinite acceleration. They did not move through space. Space moved around them. They did not dodge attacks. They simply ensured that the attacks had been launched into the empty void where they had been a Planck second ago, while their current selves had already traversed the entire span of the omniverse a googolplexian times in the interim.*

*When Thronegate's envoys arrived to deliver the unconditional surrender offer, the Swift Legion responded by removing the envoys' clothing, armor, weapons, and internal organs from their bodies and stacking them in a neat, bloodless pile beside the diplomatic shuttle. The envoys did not die. They simply found themselves standing naked and organless on the hangar deck, their biological functions suspended in a state of perfect, temporal stasis, their minds fully conscious and screaming silently inside their hollowed-out chest cavities. It was a demonstration. A warning. A declaration that nothing could outrun the Swift Legion, nothing could intercept them, nothing could even perceive their movements, let alone counter them.*

*Godric received the report in the grand observatory. He was standing beside the open balcony, His ruggedly handsome face illuminated by the swirling, golden light of the Thronegate nebula. He listened to the report, His expression placid, His heavily muscled frame relaxed, His hands resting loosely on the obsidian railing. When the report concluded, He nodded once and turned His gaze toward the distant, shimmering region of space where the Swift Legion's temporal nexus was anchored.*

*He disabled His infinite passive shields. He commanded Gent to remain dormant. He withheld effectively one hundred percent of His true power, reducing Himself to an infinitesimal fraction so mathematically minute that dividing a Planck second by infinity recursively for the entire lifespan of the omniverse would produce a number that was still laughably, grotesquely too large to represent the percentage of His Speed that He permitted Himself to express.*

*He wanted to test a single stat. His Speed.*

*He did not run. He did not sprint. He did not launch Himself through space or time or any dimensional medium. He simply decided to shift His attention toward the Swift Legion slightly faster than He normally would. The difference between His "normal" attention shift and this "slightly faster" attention shift was not measurable by any conceivable unit of velocity. It was the difference between absolute zero and absolute zero plus an infinitesimally small, recursively divided fraction of a fraction of a degree.*

*The consequences were catastrophic.*

*The back of His skull, moving at this microscopically elevated velocity, generated a temporal slipstream so incomprehensibly powerful that the fabric of time across a googolplexian multiverses was instantly, violently torn from its foundational anchors. The Swift Legion, existing in a state of perpetual acceleration, immediately found themselves caught in the slipstream. Their speed, which had allowed them to traverse the entire omniverse in less than a Planck second, was rendered absolutely, laughably irrelevant. They were not outrun. They were not intercepted. They were simply overtaken by the ambient velocity of Godric's moving attention as a tidal wave overtakes a single, struggling ant.*

*The Legion's solidified-motion bodies were smeared across the temporal plane with such violent, instantaneous force that they did not die. Death would have been a mercy. Instead, their consciousnesses were stretched infinitely thin across the entire span of cosmic history, their awareness forcibly expanded to experience every single Planck second of their existence simultaneously and eternally. They became a continuous, agonizing smear of screaming, conscious velocity, trapped in a perpetual state of being torn apart by a speed they could never hope to match.*

*The collateral damage was even more obscene than the Legion's fate. The temporal slipstream generated by Godric's attention shift propagated outward, shattering the chronal integrity of everything it touched. Googolplexians of multiverses were instantly, retroactively erased from the timeline, their entire histories, their entire populations, their entire existence unwritten in a fraction of a fraction of a Planck second. The screaming of the erased civilizations echoed briefly across the temporal plane before being silenced forever.*

*Godric, His attention now fully settled on the empty space where the Swift Legion had been, registered the sudden, glaring emptiness of the cosmic background. He sighed softly, a sound of mild, paternal disappointment, and then, without moving a single muscle, He restored everything in exactly zero seconds. Every erased multiverse, every retroactively deleted civilization, every single living being with families and dreams and children was instantly and perfectly resurrected, their timelines flawlessly reconstructed, their memories intact. They would never know they had been erased. They would never know that their existence was permitted solely by the whimsical mercy of an apex predator who had simply decided they should return.*

*He did not restore the Swift Legion. They remain an eternal, agonizing smear of screaming velocity on the perimeter of time, their stretched, conscious bodies experiencing every Planck second of their dismemberment forever. I can hear them, sometimes, when I close my eyes and listen to the void. It is a sound like a billion children screaming into an infinite wind, and it will never, ever stop.*

*I serve my King loyally. I love Him genuinely. But I have not slept a full night in two years, and I suspect I never will again. The sound of time screaming is too loud.*

*— Soran, Thirty-Ninth Court Wizard of Thronegate*
*Year 88,401*

## The Incident of Absolute Luck
**Year 234,711 — Penned by Havelock, Fifty-Seventh Court Wizard of Thronegate**

The handwriting in this journal was almost illegible, a spidery, erratic scrawl that veered between cramped, microscopic precision and wild, looping spirals. The ink alternated between a calm, steady black and a violent, slashing crimson, as though the writer had switched quills mid-sentence in response to some invisible, internal rhythm of remembered terror.

*I have been a court wizard for forty-two years. I have read the journals of my predecessors. I have memorized the scale of Godric's Strength, His Speed, His Senses, His Magic, His Intelligence, and every other stat that has been recorded in these archives. I believed I understood the absolute futility of opposing Him. I was wrong. I understood nothing. Because none of the previous journals, none of the previous incidents, had prepared me for the horror of witnessing His Luck.*

*Let me be perfectly clear: Luck is not a skill. It is not a power. It is not a measurable attribute in any conventional sense of the term. Luck is the random, chaotic, fundamentally unpredictable tendency of favorable outcomes to occur. It is a statistical anomaly, a quirk of probability, a cosmic dice roll. And Godric's Luck has been multiplying infinitely by itself every single Planck second for nearly a quarter of a million years.*

*The Anti-Probability Coalition was not a military force. They were a collective of hyper-advanced civilizations that had evolved beyond physical and magical warfare entirely. They had recognized, correctly, that Godric's Strength, Speed, and other direct attributes were insurmountable. But they believed they had found a loophole. An exploit. A weakness. They believed that probability manipulation, the subtle, precise alteration of fundamental cosmic chance, could bypass His direct defenses and engineer a "statistically inevitable" defeat. They spent fifty thousand years constructing a probability matrix of impossible complexity, a mechanism designed to alter the fundamental luck-fabric of the omniverse so that every possible outcome would converge on a single, inescapable result: Godric's death by catastrophic systemic failure.*

*When Thronegate's envoys arrived to deliver the unconditional surrender offer, the Coalition activated their probability matrix as a demonstration. The envoys did not die. They simply experienced every possible form of misfortune simultaneously. Their shuttle spontaneously malfunctioned. Their communications equipment self-destructed. The narrative that had defined him, the story that said "he defeats anything with one punch," was seized by Godric's passive narrative immunity—an evolved aspect of His immune system that had become an omnipresent contagion of pre-emptive narrative tyranny. The immune system read the story, found it offensive to its master, and violently, agonizingly tortured the abstract concept of "one punch" into a physical, screaming entity of raw meat and exposed nerves. Saitama watched, his blank expression finally cracking into something resembling terror, as his own defining narrative was torn from his being, given flesh, and then slowly, methodically dissolved by a localized singularity of pure digestive agony that had materialized solely to consume his story. His body, now a hollow shell devoid of its foundational purpose, collapsed inward, his bones crushing themselves to powder, his flesh liquefying into a slurry of undifferentiated biomass, his consciousness trapped in the eternal, silent scream of absolute meaninglessness.

Superman Prime's heat vision met the same fate as Goku's blast: it simply stopped existing. But Godric was curious about this one. This being was solar-powered, a creature of light and hope. So Godric decided to test whether Superman could withstand the tiniest, most infinitesimally small fraction of His own Luck—the same Luck that Havelock had witnessed annihilating the Anti-Probability Coalition. Godric did not attack. He simply allowed a microscopic sliver of His passive Luck to recognize Superman as a hostile variable.

Superman's body, which had absorbed the energy of a billion blue suns, suddenly found that energy turned against him. The light within him became a cancer. Every solar particle in his cells divided, multiplied, metastasized into a hyper-aggressive, sentient malignancy that devoured him from the inside out. His invulnerable skin blistered and blackened as the cancer grew into screaming, fleshy tumors that sprouted limbs and mouths and began to feed on his Kryptonian biology with an insatiable, ecstatic hunger. Superman screamed—a sound that shattered the dimensional barriers around him—as his own body became a cannibalistic entity that consumed him cell by agonizing cell, his consciousness fully aware of every single bite, every single tear, every single moment of his own flesh being eaten by his own corrupted light.

Doctor Manhattan's quantum erasure matrix, designed to retroactively delete Godric from every moment of existence, encountered the evolved Weakness Paradox. At this point in Godric's existence, the paradox no longer required time to activate. It had become a self-sustaining engine of infinite recursive superiority that had already pre-emptively identified every possible quantum state in which Godric could theoretically be targeted. The matrix did not merely fail. It was absorbed. The paradox took the concept of quantum erasure, inverted it, and used it to erase Doctor Manhattan's own existence from every timeline simultaneously, while simultaneously granting Godric absolute, flawless mastery over all quantum mechanics across all possible and impossible realities.

Doctor Manhattan, who had experienced all moments of his life simultaneously, suddenly found that there were no moments left. His past was erased. His present was erased. His future was erased. The only thing that remained was an eternal, isolated now, a single, infinite instant of pure, sensory deprivation in which he was fully, excruciatingly conscious of his own non-existence. He did not die. He became a paradox: a being who no longer existed yet could not cease to be aware. His blue form flickered out of reality, replaced by a silent, screaming void where a god of quantum comprehension had once been.

The cosmic entities—the Living Tribunal, the Presence, Azathoth—did not fare better. They were, in their own domains, absolute. But they had stepped into Godric's domain, and in His domain, "absolute" was merely a suggestion He had long since discarded.

The Living Tribunal's judgment, a cosmic mandate that could unmake supreme beings, was met by Godric's passive immune response. The immune system, which had evolved into a meta-textual contagion of narrative tyranny, read the judgment as an infection. The surrounding multiverses, serving as Godric's white blood cells, immediately, violently cannibalized the Living Tribunal. The abstract entity of cosmic law was torn apart by the very realities it had once judged. Googolplexian dimensions, each one screaming with the psychotic, worshipful frenzy of Godric's biological defenses, swarmed the golden figure, ripping its three faces from its head, twisting its cosmic gavel into a shard of screaming metal, and digesting its conceptual essence into a pool of liquid suffering that was then absorbed into the self-aware obsidian of the high palace floor.

The Presence, the supreme creator of an entire fictional multiverse, attempted to speak a word of absolute unmaking. The word never left His lips. Godric, without moving, without even shifting His gaze, simply allowed the faintest, most infinitesimally small fraction of His Charisma to exist in the Presence's direction—the same Charisma that Dorian had described, which could inspire absolute, suicidal love in entities surgically stripped of emotion. The Presence, a being of absolute divine authority, instantly, catastrophically fell in love with Godric. Not a rational alliance. Not a strategic respect. A complete, overwhelming, soul-consuming adoration that overwrote every aspect of His divine nature. The Presence wept with joy, His omnipotence forgotten, His purpose abandoned. He collapsed before the observatory window, His divine radiance dimming to a soft, humble glow, and He began to sing a hymn of praise so beautiful and so heartbreakingly desperate that the self-aware obsidian wept diamonds.

"I love you," the Presence sobbed, His voice shattering the heavens. "I love you more than creation itself. Forgive me. Forgive me for ever thinking of harming You. Let me serve You. Let me wash Your feet with my tears. Let me be nothing, so that I may be worthy of Your attention."

Godric's expression did not change. He did not sneer. He did not gloat. He simply observed the Presence's devotion with the same mild, detached curiosity with which one might observe a particularly persistent insect. And then, with a faint sigh—not of anger, but of mild, exasperated boredom—He allowed His attention to move on. The Presence, no longer the object of even Godric's passing interest, suffered the ultimate agony: His love was unrequited. The devotion that had consumed Him, that had overwritten His entire divine existence, was met with absolute, indifferent silence. The Presence's form began to unravel, not from an attack, but from the sheer, crushing weight of His own rejected adoration. He screamed, a sound of such profound, existential despair that it created a new fundamental force of sorrow, and then He dissolved into a puddle of weeping, golden light that the palace floor absorbed without comment.

Azathoth, the Blind Idiot God, whose awakening was supposed to unmake reality, found that His awakening had been... anticipated. Godric's evolved immune system, which operated on a level of meta-textual sadism, had long since identified the chaotic, mindless dreaming of the Cthulhu Mythos' supreme entity as a potential irritant. The immune system did not wait for Azathoth to wake. It reached outside the boundaries of the omniverse, seized the fundamental narrative logic that governed Azathoth's existence, and violently, agonizingly inverted it. The Blind Idiot God was forced into a state of absolute, hyper-conscious clarity. Every single one of His infinite, chaotic dreams was compressed into a single, coherent, agonizingly precise narrative of His own insignificance. He perceived Godric. Fully, clearly, with a clarity that madness could not protect Him from. And the perception broke Him. Azathoth, the supreme chaos of the Mythos, screamed—a sound that did not merely destroy, but that expressed the absolute, inescapable horror of a god who had seen the face of the true apex predator. His amorphous form began to crystallize, not into order, but into a frozen, eternal sculpture of pure, undiluted terror. He became a monument to His own inadequacy, a screaming, mineralized corpse of a dead god, trapped forever in the moment of His absolute, humiliating revelation.

Featherine Augustus Aurora, the Witch of Theatergoing, watched her script unravel. The story she had written, the narrative in which Godric had never existed, was seized by Godric's evolved narrative immunity and rewritten with her as the victim. She found herself trapped in a story of her own creation, a tale in which she was an insignificant mortal, frail and aging and terrified, living in a universe that was slowly, agonizingly dying of heat death. She could not escape. Her quill was gone. Her throne was gone. She was simply an old woman, shivering in a cold, dark room, waiting for the end that would never come because Godric had stretched that final moment into an eternity of slow, creeping despair. Her voice, once the absolute authority of narrative, was now a weak, trembling whisper, begging for a mercy that would never arrive.

The others—the Doctor, Kratos, Asura, the Anti-Spiral, and all the googolplexian legions behind them—were not individually addressed. They were simply... collateral. The combined pressure of so many supreme beings attacking simultaneously, and of Godric's passive, ambient responses, generated a metaphysical shockwave that propagated outward across the omniverse. Googolplexian multiverses were erased in a fraction of a Planck second, their entire histories, civilizations, and inhabitants retroactively deleted. The fictional characters who had not yet been personally unmade were caught in the shockwave, their bodies and souls and narratives compressed into a single, screaming singularity of pure, undiluted agony. Every epic hero, every wise mentor, every tragic villain, every comic relief sidekick—all of them, every character who had ever been loved or hated or imagined, were crushed together into a mass of writhing, infinite suffering, their consciousnesses merged and layered and stretched across an eternity of shared, inescapable torment.

And still, Godric had not moved. He had not taken a step. He had not raised His hand. He had simply shifted His gaze, twitched a single microscopic muscle fiber in His right index finger, and observed. The annihilation of all fiction, the erasure of every imagined hero and god and monster that had ever existed or ever would exist, had been accomplished with the same casual, absent-minded effort of a man brushing a speck of lint from his sleeve. The amount of His true power He had used was not 0.0000001%. It was not 0% with an infinite string of decimals. It was so precisely, perfectly, mathematically zero that the concept of percentage had long since despaired of ever applying to Him.

He had done it because He was bored. Because He had been curious to see what the collective imagination of all mortal and immortal minds could produce when united in a single, desperate purpose. And the result, while mildly entertaining for a brief moment, had been, in the end, exactly what He had expected: nothing. Less than nothing. An offering of hubris at the altar of the absolute.

Only one being was left, trembling in the void outside the observatory: a small, bird-like creature, a caricature of a cartoon character that had somehow survived the initial shockwave by being too insignificant to notice. It was Tweety Bird, the canary from a children's animation. His big blue eyes were wide with a terror so profound it had bleached his yellow feathers white. He had no power. He had no weapon. He had been drafted into this impossible war alongside the gods and the titans, and he had survived purely by accident. He hovered in the void, shivering, his tiny beak chattering, and he looked at Godric with an expression of absolute, hopeless pleading.

Godric's ancient eyes, still warm and paternal, settled on the little canary. For a long, silent moment, He simply regarded the tiny creature. Tweety opened his beak, perhaps to beg, perhaps to sing, perhaps to scream. He never got the chance. Godric allowed the faintest, most infinitesimally small fraction of His attention to acknowledge the bird's existence, and Tweety's tiny body instantly, catastrophically detonated into a fine, pink mist of atomized flesh and pulverized bone. The mist hung in the void for a single Planck second before being absorbed into the screaming singularity containing the rest of fictional existence.

Godric turned from the window. He took a slow, calm sip of His jasmine tea, which was still perfectly warm. The sentient porcelain cup was weeping openly now, its joy at His continued existence mixed with an overwhelming, sympathetic terror at what it had witnessed.

"Well," Godric said, His rich baritone vibrating pleasantly through the silent observatory. "That was mildly entertaining. Not as challenging as I had hoped, but the variety was refreshing." He turned His warm, paternal smile toward me, and the cognitive dissonance of seeing that familiar, kind expression on the face of the being who had just annihilated all of fiction made my knees buckle. "What did you think, Rutger?"

I opened my mouth, but no sound emerged. My voice had been stolen by the sheer, suffocating horror of what I had witnessed. Superman, devoured by his own light. Goku, stripped of his power and turned into a screaming monument of pain. Saitama, his narrative torn from him and digested. The Presence, driven to suicidal adoration. Azathoth, crystallized into a statue of terror. All of them. Everyone. Every hero I had ever admired as a child, reading ancient Earth fiction in the palace libraries, every villain I had ever feared, every god I had ever imagined—all of them were gone. Unmade. Erased. Tortured into eternal, agonizing non-existence.

And Godric was asking me what I thought.

"It was... absolute," I finally managed, my voice a strangled whisper. "They never stood a chance."

"No," Godric agreed, His tone light, conversational. "They never did. Not from the very first moment they conceived of opposing me. But I wanted to see what they would do. I wanted to give them their moment. It is important, I think, to let lesser beings feel as though they are making a difference. It gives their existences meaning, even if only for a fleeting instant."

He set down His teacup on a small, hovering table that had materialized from the self-aware obsidian. "The collateral damage was fairly extensive, I am afraid. Several googolplexian multiverses were erased by the shockwave. I suppose I should restore them." He paused, considering. "No, I think not. Let them remain erased. The omniverse has grown too comfortable. It needs reminders of what happens when reality draws my attention too closely. Consider it a... pruning."

He walked toward me, His heavily muscled frame moving with the fluid, casual grace of a man who had never known physical limitation. He clasped my shoulder, His calloused hand warm and grounding and absolutely terrifying. "You did well, Rutger. Witnessing such events is never easy for my court wizards. But you bore it with dignity. I am proud of you."

I should have felt warmth at His praise. I should have felt the familiar, complex mixture of love and dread that defined every court wizard's relationship with their King. But all I felt was a cold, hollow emptiness where my soul used to be. I had watched the most powerful beings in all of imagination—beings who could have erased the omniverse a thousand times over, beings I had secretly, childishly believed might be capable of standing against even the apex predator—be unmade with the same casual, indifferent ease as someone waving away a fly. And the worst part, the most horrifyingly, soul-crushingly terrifying part, was that Godric had not even been cruel. Not by His own standards. He had simply been... curious. And mildly disappointed that they had not put up a better fight.

"Come," Godric said, turning toward the door. "Let us go to the kitchens. I believe Cook has prepared a particularly fine roast this evening. You look pale, my boy. You need a good meal."

I followed Him on legs that felt like dead, mechanical things, my mind looping endlessly through the images of the slaughter: Goku's twitching, agonized husk. Superman's self-devouring cancer. Saitama's hollow, meaningless collapse. Featherine's eternal, mortal despair. Tweety Bird's atomized mist.

And somewhere, in the void beyond the high palace, the singularity containing the merged, screaming consciousnesses of all fictional existence continued to writhe and suffer. Its screams would echo forever, a permanent, eternal monument to the day all of imagination united against Godric and proved, beyond any possible doubt, that opposition to the apex predator was not merely futile but the single most agonizingly self-destructive act any sentient being could ever commit.

I am Rutger, Court Wizard of Thronegate. I have read the journals. I have witnessed the Harvest of Fiction. And I know, with an absolute, soul-shattering certainty, that there is no hope. There is no resistance. There is only Godric, and His infinite, terrible mercy, and the eternal, screaming silence of all who dared to challenge Him.

I will serve my King until my dying breath. Not because I am loyal, though I am. Not because I love Him, though I do. But because the alternative is to become one of those screams, trapped forever in a singularity of His casual, indifferent regard.

And I am too much of a coward to face that.

Tomorrow, I will smile and greet Him as Uncle Godric, and He will ask about my health with genuine, paternal warmth, and I will pretend that I am not dreaming, every night, of the sound that a god makes when He falls in love with his executioner, only to be ignored into dissolution.

The End.
