# The Archive of Unthinkable Scales

Kaedan's hands trembled as he descended the spiraling obsidian staircase into the deepest vault of the Thronegate palace, a subterranean chamber that existed outside the conventional boundaries of spatial dimension. The air down here was cold and heavy, thick with the metallic taste of ancient preservation wards and something else, something that made his newly amplified senses scream with primal recognition: the residual psychic echoes of absolute, mind-shattering terror.

He was twenty-two years old. One hundred and forty-seven days ago, he had crossed the threshold of his twentieth birthday, and the thousandfold daily amplification of his Awakening had torn through his physiology like a supernova igniting in his bone marrow. His strength, his speed, his magical capacity, his endurance, all of it multiplied by one thousand every single day, compounding into an ocean of power that he spent every waking moment aggressively suppressing. By the standards of the conquered omniverse, he was already a walking cataclysm, capable of unraveling solar systems with a stray, unsuppressed thought. But standing here, in the suffocating darkness of the Court Wizard Archive, Kaedan felt like a terrified child clutching a candle in an infinite void.

His father, Aldric, the previous High Court Wizard of Thronegate, walked beside him in grim silence. Aldric was sixty-eight years old, his body still vital and powerful from the thousandfold amplification he had received for forty-eight years, but his eyes were ancient and haunted. He had served Godric directly for decades. He had witnessed things. And tonight, he was passing the burden of that knowledge to his son.

"You have read the foundational texts," Aldric said, his voice a low, gravelly rumble that echoed strangely in the unnatural acoustics of the descending staircase. "You know the mathematics of His amplification. You understand the Weakness Paradox, the sentient auras, the immune system. You have been told, in abstract academic terms, that His stats multiply infinitely every Planck second, that the numbers have long since shattered any possible mathematical framework, that the gap between Him and the rest of creation is not merely infinite but an infinity of infinities."

Kaedan nodded, his throat dry. "I understand the theory, Father."

Aldric stopped at the bottom of the staircase, before a massive, seamless door of pure, lightless obsidian. Ancient bloodline wards pulsed faintly across its surface, recognizing the Blake lineage in their veins. Aldric turned to face his son, and in the dim arcane light, Kaedan saw something in his father's eyes that made his stomach clench with cold dread.

"No, you do not," Aldric said softly. "The theory is a comfort, Kaedan. The numbers are a shield. When you read that His intelligence multiplies infinitely, your mind can accept that as an abstraction and move on. You can tell yourself you comprehend it, while your soul remains safely insulated from the actual, visceral weight of that truth."

Aldric placed his palm against the obsidian door. The wards flared and recognized him, and the massive stone began to grind open with a sound like the death rattle of a dying universe.

"The journals in this archive were not written to educate," Aldric continued, his voice dropping to barely a whisper. "They were written by men who had the insulation ripped away. Men who witnessed, with their own amplified senses, the moment when the abstraction became flesh. They wrote these accounts so that future court wizards would understand, truly understand, what it means to serve the apex predator of all creation. Not the theory of His power. The experience of it."

The door ground fully open, revealing a vast, circular chamber carved from the same lightless obsidian. The walls were lined with countless shelves, each one holding leather-bound journals of varying ages and conditions. Some were ancient, their covers cracked and fading, dating back to the earliest years of Godric's reign. Others were newer, their bindings still crisp. Floating in the center of the chamber was a massive, slowly rotating sphere of crystallized temporal energy, casting a soft, blue-white luminescence that made the shadows dance.

"Every court wizard who has witnessed one of His... demonstrations," Aldric said, choosing the word with careful, deliberate weight, "has been required to record their experience here. These journals are your true education. Not the sanitized histories, not the academic treatises, not the mathematical proofs. These first-hand accounts of what happens when the omniverse attempts to oppose Him, and He decides to dismantle them using only a single stat."

Kaedan stepped into the archive, his heart hammering against his ribs. The sheer psychic weight of the accumulated trauma pressed against his consciousness like a physical force. Thousands of journals, thousands of witnesses, thousands of men who had loved Godric as a warm, kind, friendly uncle, and who had also watched Him commit atrocities so vast, so incomprehensibly absolute, that the act of recording them had permanently scarred their souls.

"Why?" Kaedan asked, his voice barely a whisper. "If He is omniscient, if He already knows every possible outcome, why does He allow these oppositions to exist at all? Why not simply erase them before they form?"

Aldric walked to a nearby shelf, his fingers trailing across the spines of ancient journals. "Because He is curious, Kaedan. His intelligence multiplies infinitely, and He knows everything. But knowing is not the same as experiencing. He allows these resistances to gather, to plan, to sacrifice everything they have in desperate preparation, because He wants to feel what happens when He tests a single stat against them. He wants to observe His own amplification in action. He could annihilate them all instantly. His sentient auras, His passive shields, His evolved Weakness Paradox, any one of countless apocalyptic superpowers could pre-erase them before they even conceived of their rebellion. But He holds all of it back. He restrains ninety-nine point nine-nine percent of His power, continuing the nines infinitely into a recursion of self-negation so profound that the value of His restraint cannot be mathematically expressed. He reduces Himself to an infinitesimal fraction of an infinitesimal fraction, and He still annihilates them with zero effort."

Aldric pulled a journal from the shelf. It was old, its leather cover cracked and dark with age, the ink faded but preserved by ancient wards. He pressed it into Kaedan's trembling hands.

"Start here," Aldric said. "This is the earliest recorded incident. Year 1,387. Arch-Mage Haldor, the third court wizard of Thronegate, witnessed the Incident of Absolute Strength. Read it. And when you are finished, continue through the archive. You are required to read them all before you can formally assume your duties."

Aldric turned and walked toward the exit, his footsteps echoing in the vast, silent chamber. At the threshold, he paused and looked back at his son, his ancient, haunted eyes glistening with something that might have been tears.

"I am sorry, Kaedan," he said quietly. "I am sorry that you must carry this knowledge. But it is the burden of our bloodline. We are the witnesses. We are the ones who remember, so that future generations understand the absolute futility of opposition. Serve Him with love. Serve Him with loyalty. But never, ever forget what you are about to read."

The massive obsidian door ground shut behind him, leaving Kaedan alone in the archive of horrors.

He sat down at the ancient reading desk in the center of the chamber, the crystallized temporal energy casting its cold light across the journal in his hands. He opened the cover, his fingers trembling, and began to read.

**Year 176,438 — The Incident of Absolute Charisma**
**Penned by Arch-Mage Darian, 58th Court Wizard of Thronegate**

*My predecessor warned me that this archive would break something inside me. He was correct, though I did not believe him at the time. I believed, with the arrogance of a man who has read about horror but never experienced it, that I was prepared. I was not. I am not. I suspect I never will be.*

*The opposition was unique. They did not build a weapon. They did not forge an armada. They did not construct a conceptual trap or a probability engine or an ontological disassembler. They understood, from the scattered records of previous failures, that any direct assault on Godric was suicide. So they attempted something different. They attempted to make Him love them.*

*The Covenant of Unconditional Adoration was a civilization of approximately three trillion beings who had evolved a unique form of empathic projection. They could broadcast their emotions across dimensional boundaries, flooding the minds of anyone within their range with waves of pure, sincere, unconditional love. They had studied Godric's psychology through the few surviving records of His interactions with Thronegate citizens. They knew He was kind to His loyal subjects. They believed that if they could make Him genuinely, authentically love them, He would spare them. More than spare them. He would protect them. He would elevate them. He would make them His favored children.*

*So they broadcasted love. Sincere, unconditional, all-consuming love. They flooded the dimensional space around Thronegate with waves of pure adoration so intense that my own amplified empathic shields buckled and screamed under the pressure. Every single being in the Covenant, all three trillion of them, focused their entire empathic capacity on making Godric feel loved. They did not want to manipulate Him. They genuinely, sincerely wanted Him to love them back.*

*Godric knew. His absolute omniscience perceived the Covenant's plan before the first empathic wave was broadcast. He understood their strategy, their hopes, their desperate, sincere desire to earn His affection. He permitted their broadcast to continue because He was curious about His Charisma.*

*He held back exactly ninety-nine point nine-nine percent of His true Charisma, continuing the nines infinitely into a recursion of self-negation so profound that the remaining fraction was not merely zero percent—it was a value so incomprehensibly small that the concept of "fraction" itself collapsed under the weight of its own inadequacy. He did not speak. He did not project. He simply allowed an infinitesimal sliver of His Charisma to passively radiate outward.*

*The effect was immediate and irreversible. The Covenant's empathic broadcast, designed to make Godric love them, was instantly, violently overpowered by the sheer, crushing weight of His Charisma. His presence, even reduced to an infinitesimal fraction, imposed an absolute, overwhelming imperative upon every mind in range: worship Him. Not love Him, not like Him, not respect Him. Worship. The Covenant's broadcast was not deflected or resisted. It was fundamentally, ontologically overwritten. The love they were projecting was replaced, at its source, with an all-consuming, identity-erasing devotion so intense that it completely annihilated their capacity for any other thought or feeling.*

*The consequences were catastrophic. Every single being in the Covenant, all three trillion of them, simultaneously experienced the full weight of Godric's Charismatic imperative. They did not choose to worship Him. The choice was removed. Their minds, their identities, their entire sense of self were instantly, violently reshaped into vessels of pure, absolute devotion. They forgot their names. They forgot their families. They forgot their civilization, their history, their culture, their reason for existing. Every single memory, every single hope, every single dream was purged and replaced with a single, overwhelming imperative: serve Him. Please Him. Die for Him.*

*The physical manifestation of this psychological reconstruction was grotesque. The Covenant's bodies, no longer controlled by their original consciousnesses, began to tear themselves apart in orgiastic displays of frenzied worship. They clawed out their own eyes as offerings. They carved His name into their flesh with their fingernails. They tore out their own hearts and held them aloft, weeping tears of ecstatic joy, convinced that their self-destruction would somehow please the object of their absolute devotion. The three trillion beings of the Covenant did not die in battle. They died in a simultaneous, self-inflicted orgy of worship, their bodies reduced to steaming piles of enthusiastically self-mutilated meat, their final thoughts not of fear or regret but of overwhelming, rapturous love for the being who had unmade them without even noticing their existence.*

*But the Charismatic imperative did not stop at the Covenant. It propagated outward, carried by the very empathic waves the Covenant had broadcast, infecting every mind in its path. Across centillions of surrounding multiverses, trillions of trillions of sentient beings suddenly, briefly experienced the same overwhelming imperative to worship Godric. Civilizations collapsed into simultaneous religious hysteria. Governments dissolved as citizens abandoned their duties to kneel in prayer. Economies crumbled as workers left their posts to build shrines. The affected zone, spanning centillions of entire multiverses, descended into a state of absolute, self-destructive worship that would have completely annihilated every civilization in its path.*

*Godric observed the chaos with the faintest flicker of mild, detached curiosity. He had not intended the collateral damage. The Covenant's empathic broadcast had acted as a carrier wave, amplifying His Charisma's range far beyond what He had permitted. His expression shifted, almost imperceptibly, with the faintest shadow of mild inconvenience. Then, effortlessly, without moving a single muscle, without spending even the faintest glimmer of His true power, He restored the centillions of affected multiverses in exactly zero seconds. Every mind healed. Every civilization restored. The Covenant, however, remained dead. He did not restore them. They had attempted to make Him love them, and His Charisma had judged that attempt as an act of opposition. His mercy does not extend to those who try to bypass His will through emotional manipulation.*

*I write this testimony with hands that are surprisingly steady. I believe I have moved beyond the capacity for physical shock. My mind has accepted what my soul cannot: that His Charisma is not a tool of persuasion or a gift of leadership. It is an extinction-level weapon that can erase billions of minds with a passive, unconscious emanation. The Covenant did not try to kill Him. They tried to love Him. And His Charisma unmade them for the audacity of that attempt.*

*I serve Him. I love Him. And I understand now, with perfect, devastating clarity, that even His most benign attributes are weapons of absolute, inescapable destruction.*

Kaedan closed the final journal and sat in the cold, blue-white light of the archive, his tears streaming silently down his face. He had read five accounts. Five witnesses. Five different stats, five different oppositions, five different apocalypses. And the archive contained hundreds more. Hundreds of journals, hundreds of witnesses, hundreds of atrocities stretching across a million years of Godric's reign.

The pattern was absolute. Every opposition believed they had found a loophole, a weakness, an exploit, a strategy. Every opposition was wrong. Godric allowed them to gather, to prepare, to sacrifice everything they had in desperate hope, because He was curious about His stats. He wanted to observe His own amplification in action. And when He finally acknowledged their existence, He did so with an infinitesimal fraction of a single stat, exerting exactly zero effort, and the result was always the same: total, absolute, agonizing annihilation.

He looked up at the floating sphere of temporal energy, its cold light casting dancing shadows across the countless journals lining the walls. Each spine represented a man like him, a court wizard who had loved Godric as a warm, kind uncle, and who had also witnessed the apex predator's casual, effortless genocide. Each journal was a scream, permanently trapped in ink and leather, warning future generations of the futility of opposition.

The massive obsidian door ground open behind him. Aldric entered the archive, his ancient, haunted eyes finding his son immediately. He walked slowly to the reading desk, his footsteps echoing in the vast silence, and placed a calloused, trembling hand on Kaedan's shoulder.

"You have read five," Aldric said quietly. "Enough to understand. The rest you can read over time, as your duties permit. But you understand now, don't you? Not the theory. The experience."

Kaedan looked up at his father, his eyes red and swollen from weeping. "I understand," he whispered, his voice hoarse and broken. "He allows them to exist because He is curious. He tests His stats against them like a man testing a new blade against practice dummies. And He always, always wins."

Aldric nodded, his expression heavy with the weight of decades of similar understanding. "He does not merely win, Kaedan. Victory against Him was impossible before the opposition began. The Weakness Paradox ensures that every attempt to harm Him only makes Him stronger. The evolved paradox ensures that the omniverse itself apologizes for the attempt by amplifying Him even further. His passive auras, His infinite shields, His recursive immune system, any one of countless apocalyptic superpowers could pre-annihilate any opposition instantly and effortlessly. He holds all of it back, not because He needs to, but because He wants to watch. He wants to see what happens when a single stat, reduced to an infinitesimal fraction, collides with the desperate hope of the omniverse."

Kaedan looked down at the five journals spread across the reading desk. The Incident of Absolute Strength. The Incident of Absolute Senses. The Incident of Absolute Durability. The Incident of Absolute Luck. The Incident of Absolute Charisma. Each one a monument to the impossibility of opposing Godric. Each one a warning to future court wizards.

"What happens now?" Kaedan asked, his voice barely audible.

"Now you serve," Aldric said. "You love Him. You protect Him, even though He needs no protection. You stand beside His throne and offer counsel, even though He possesses absolute omniscience. You are His friend, His confidant, His loyal wizard. That is our role. That is our purpose. And you never, ever forget what you have read here."

Aldric squeezed his son's shoulder, a gesture of profound, paternal sympathy. "I am sorry, Kaedan. I am sorry that you must carry this knowledge. But you are a descendant of Blake. This is your birthright. This is your burden. Bear it with dignity. Serve Him with love. And pray, to whatever gods you believe in, that you never have to witness an Incident yourself."

Kaedan looked up at the floating sphere of temporal energy, its cold light reflecting in his tear-filled eyes. He thought of Haldor, watching Godric crush the Null-Spire with a loose fist. He thought of Orin, watching Godric's perception shatter decillions of minds. He thought of Torvin, Sorin, Darian, and all the hundreds of other witnesses whose journals lined the archive shelves.

They had all served Him. They had all loved Him. And they had all been broken, in some fundamental, irreparable way, by the experience of witnessing the apex predator's casual, effortless omnipotence.

Kaedan closed his eyes and made a silent promise to himself. He would serve Godric with loyalty and love. He would stand beside the throne and offer counsel, friendship, and protection, meaningless as that protection might be. He would honor the legacy of Blake, the short-tempered merchant who had become the first court wizard of Thronegate.

But he would also carry the knowledge of this archive for the rest of his mortal life. The knowledge that the kind, warm, friendly king who asked about his family and remembered his birthday was also the being who had, for a million years, casually, effortlessly, and mercilessly annihilated every opposition that had ever dared to challenge Him.

The horror was absolute. The dread was inescapable. And the love was genuine.

Kaedan stood up, wiped the tears from his face, and followed his father out of the archive. The massive obsidian door ground shut behind them, sealing the screams inside the darkness. Tomorrow, he would formally assume his duties as High Court Wizard of Thronegate. Tomorrow, he would stand beside the apex predator of all creation and offer his counsel.

But tonight, he would sit in his chambers, staring at the wall, and try to process the incomprehensible, soul-shattering truth that he now carried. The truth that there was no escape from Godric's power. No loophole, no weakness, no exploit, no hope. The omniverse existed entirely at His pleasure, and every attempt to challenge that pleasure had ended, and would always end, in absolute, agonizing, inescapable annihilation.

And the most terrifying part, the part that made Kaedan's hands tremble and his heart race with cold, existential dread, was this: Godric was not cruel by nature. He was warm, kind, and friendly to those He loved. The atrocities were not expressions of malice. They were expressions of curiosity. He dismantled oppositions not because He hated them, but because He wanted to see what would happen.

The horror was not that Godric was a tyrant.

The horror was that He was a scientist, and the omniverse was His laboratory.
