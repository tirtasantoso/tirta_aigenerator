The wound in the sky closed. The subsonic scream stopped. The mana that the Syllogism had absorbed was not returned to its casters; it was retroactively labeled as miscategorized and removed from the omniverse’s arcane ledger. The senior wizards, still standing at the spire with their hands raised, staggered as their magical senses registered the sudden, impossible absence of a threat that had, seconds before, been the most magically potent entity in existence.

Godric lowered his hand. He had not moved his feet. He had not spoken an incantation. He had not drawn a sigil. He had simply looked at the Final Syllogism and, with an infinitesimal fraction of his magical capacity—a fraction so small it might as well have been the difference between zero and zero—decided that the Syllogism did not belong in his reality.

He turned back to me, his expression mildly worried. “Gorm? Are you all right? You’ve gone pale.”

My legs gave out. I sat down on the sphere’s floor, my robes pooling around me, my mage-sight still burning with the afterimage of what I had witnessed. I had measured the output. My mage-sight had tried—tried and failed—to quantify the magic Godric had used, and in the fraction of a second before my analytical spells had shut down in self-protective failure, I had glimpsed the truth.

The magic Godric possessed was not large. It was not vast. It was not infinite. Those words were meaningless. His magical capacity was a category that did not exist anywhere else in the omniverse. All of his arcane traits—mana, spellcraft, magical offense, magical defense, magical comprehension, conceptual magic, anti-magic, and every dimension of magic I could name and a billion I could not—multiplied infinitely every Planck second, had been multiplying for forty thousand years, and would never stop. The combined magic of every wizard, every sorcerer, every god, every arcane entity, every magical artifact, every enchanted atom that had ever existed or could ever exist in the entire omniverse—the sum total of all that magic—was, compared to Godric’s magic, effectively zero. The infinitesimal fraction he had used to erase the Syllogism was not a fraction; it was a rounding error’s rounding error, a nothing so complete that calling it “practically zero percent” was an insult to the concept of percentages.

And I had thought he needed me. I had thought my shields, my wards, my counter-curses, my thousandfold-amplified lineage mattered. I had thought I was protecting him.

“Uncle,” I whispered, and it was the first time the word felt wrong in my mouth. “Your Majesty. The court wizards. All of us. We’re nothing.”

Godric knelt beside me, his knees pressing into the same floor, his face level with mine. He set his half-eaten apple aside and placed both hands on my shoulders. “You are not nothing, Gorm. You are my family. Your father is my family. Tim, who just exhausted himself trying to stop a thing he could never have stopped, is my family. Blake was my best friend, and I still miss him every single day. That is why you exist. Not to protect me. To be with me. To give Thronegate continuity, and warmth, and the knowledge that the King is not alone.”

His grey eyes held mine, and they were kind—genuinely, deeply kind—but behind that kindness I could now see something I had never been able to see before, because he had never let me. An abyss. An intelligence so vast that he could solve every problem in the omniverse in less than zero seconds. A magical capacity so absolute that the concept of magic itself continued to exist only because he allowed it to. If he wished, he could delete the very idea of magic from ever having been, and every wizard who had ever lived would simply have been a scholar of nothing. He did not do that, because he loved wizards. He loved me.

The horror of that love settled over me like a lead blanket. I was loved by a being who could unmake me with a thought so casual he wouldn’t even notice the thought happening. And I loved him back, fiercely, loyally, completely. The two truths were not contradictory. They were the same truth, viewed from different angles.

Godric helped me to my feet. “Now, about the mana-roses. The suppression field you wove is lovely, but you might want to reinforce the outer layer. There’s a slight resonance with the palace’s main ward that could cause petal bleaching over time. I can show you the adjustment after dinner—or I could just fix it myself, but I know you prefer to do your own work.” He smiled, a smile so warm and so normal that my heart cracked a little further. “I’ll be in the library if you need me. Your father wants tea.”

He walked out of the sphere, his sandals slapping softly against the stone, leaving me alone with the glass and the silent, unblemished sky.

I stood there for a long time. Long enough that the evening shift of guards changed, and the capital’s lights began to flicker on, and the nocturnal birds in the garden started their soft, chittering songs. My mind kept circling back to the Syllogism’s voice, its triumphant proclamation that it was the end of magic. It had been wrong. It had been so profoundly, catastrophically wrong that its wrongness was the most terrifying thing I had ever encountered. It had believed, right up until the instant of its deletion, that it was a threat. That it had a chance. That the King was afraid.

But the King had not been afraid. The King had been eating an apple.

I thought about Thorin, the first court wizard, who had died casting a forbidden teleportation spell to save a mortal king from a mortal army. Thorin, whose bloodline had produced Blake, whose bloodline had produced me. Thorin had been a wizard before Godric became what he was, when magic was still just a craft and not a footnote in the omniverse’s power structure. Thorin had given his life out of loyalty to a man he loved, not out of delusion that his magic could save Godric from anything Godric couldn’t handle himself. That was the difference, I realized. Thorin had known. Blake had known. My father knew. Tim knew. They all knew that they served not as protectors but as companions, and they had let me believe otherwise because some lessons cannot be taught; they can only be experienced.

I walked down the spiraling staircase, through the palace, and out into the garden. The mana-roses were glowing faintly in the dusk, their petals a deep, luminous indigo that my suppression field had nicely accentuated. I adjusted the field as Godric had suggested, my fingers tracing the sigils with a steady hand. The roses would thrive. Their petals would not bleach. And tomorrow, I would wake up, and attend the threat briefing, and study my counter-curses, and stand at the King’s side as his court wizard. Not his protector. His family.

That night, I sat in the library with my father and Tim and Godric, and we drank tea that the King had brewed himself, and we talked about nothing in particular—crop yields, a new composer in the capital, the migration patterns of subspace butterflies. The conversation was warm and easy and utterly mundane, the conversation of people who loved each other and enjoyed each other’s company. And beneath every word, every chuckle, every comfortable silence, I heard the echo of the Syllogism’s dying scream, a sound that had never happened because it had been retroactively prevented from existing, but that I would hear for the rest of my life.

Opposing Godric was impossible. Not difficult, not costly, not inadvisable—impossible. It was a fundamental constant, like the speed of light or the existence of magic itself. The only sane response was love. Not coerced love, not forced worship, but the genuine, freely given devotion of beings who understood that the hand that held them could close and crush them, and chose instead to offer tea and gardening advice. The choice to love him was the only freedom that mattered, and it was the most terrifying freedom I had ever exercised.

As I set down my empty cup and bid the King goodnight, he caught my eye. “Gorm. You did well today. The suppression field, the quick thinking when the breach happened—you’re going to be an excellent court wizard. Blake would be proud.” His voice was sincere, and his smile was the smile of a proud uncle, and the abyss behind his eyes was still there, ancient and patient and immeasurable.

I bowed, my hand over my heart. “Thank you, Uncle.”

I meant it. I meant it with every terrified, loyal, loving fiber of my being. And I knew, with a certainty that settled into my bones like the memory of a scream, that if anyone—anyone, anywhere, ever—tried to oppose him, tried to harm him, tried to exploit a weakness that did not and could not exist, they would learn what the Syllogism had learned. They would learn it in the last, agonizing instant before their existence was undone. The lesson was simple: Godric was not a king who needed wizards. He was a wizard before whom all other magic was a rounding error, a zero so close to nothing that the difference was meaningless. And if that sounds terrifying, it is. It is the most terrifying truth in the omniverse.

It is also the reason I slept soundly that night, and every night since. Because the most terrifying truth is also the safest truth. The predator is on our side. And he loves us. May the stars help anyone who forgets that.
