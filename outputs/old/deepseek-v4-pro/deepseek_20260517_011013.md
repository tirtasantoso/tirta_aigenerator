# The Archive of Absolute Futility

The archive chamber sat beneath the eastern wing of the imperial palace, accessible only through a descending spiral stairway carved from obsidian so dark it swallowed the amber glow of the arcane sconces lining the walls. Kaedan paused at the threshold, one hand pressed flat against the cold stone doorframe, feeling the ancient wards hum against his palm like the distant heartbeat of some buried leviathan. The air down here tasted stale, thick with the musty weight of countless millennia, and it carried a strange, coppery undertone that he could not identify but instinctively recoiled from.

He was twenty-two years old. Three days had passed since his formal induction as the newest Royal Court Wizard of Thronegate, a title inherited through the unbroken male lineage descending directly from Blake the Merchant, the legendary best friend of the sovereign. Three days since his father, Cedric, a man of seventy-one years with a perpetually furrowed brow and eyes that never quite seemed to focus on the present moment, had pressed a heavy iron key into his palm and told him, in a voice scraped raw by centuries of accumulated dread, that he was required to read every journal in the archive before assuming his duties.

"You will want to stop," Cedric had said, his grip tightening around Kaedan's wrist with the desperate strength of a man who had lived too long with knowledge he could never unlearn. "You will want to close the books and walk out and pretend you never read a single word. Every court wizard before you has felt the same. But you must finish. You must understand what He is before you stand beside His throne."

Kaedan had nodded, his young face still softened by the unearned confidence of his age, still carrying the bright, eager pride of a wizard who had just crossed the threshold of his twentieth birthday and felt the staggering rush of the thousandfold daily amplification flood his arcane pathways for the first time. He was powerful. He knew he was powerful. The daily multiplication of his mana reserves, his magical offense, his arcane comprehension, was a tidal wave of growth that made the mightiest sorcerers of the subjugated cosmos look like candle flames cowering before a supernova. And yet, standing at the entrance to this dark, silent archive, he felt the first cold tendril of genuine unease coil around his spine.

The archive was a circular chamber of modest proportions, its curved walls lined entirely with recessed stone shelves. Upon those shelves rested hundreds of leather-bound journals, their spines cracked and faded, their covers stained with the slow creep of ages. Some were thin, barely a dozen pages bound with fraying twine. Others were bloated and heavy, their parchment swollen with the obsessive documentation of horrors witnessed. Each bore a single name embossed in faded gold leaf—the name of the court wizard who had penned it, the name of a direct ancestor of Kaedan, a man whose blood flowed in his veins and whose trauma awaited him on these yellowed pages.

A heavy oak reading desk occupied the center of the chamber, scarred with the indentations of countless elbows, stained with the faded rings of ancient teacups. A single arcane lamp hovered above it, its soft blue-white luminescence casting stark, angular shadows across the room. Kaedan exhaled slowly, steeling himself, and stepped inside.

He selected the first journal at random, his fingers brushing over the cracked leather spine embossed with the name **Theron**. The year stamped beneath the name read **8,500**. Kaedan sat down at the desk, opened the brittle cover, and began to read.

**Journal of Theron, 9th Court Wizard of Thronegate**
**Year 8,500 — The Incident of Absolute Senses**

*I have attempted to begin this account seventeen times. Each previous draft lies crumpled in the waste receptacle beside my desk, their pages smeared with the sweat of my palms and the occasional drop of blood from my nose. The act of recalling what I witnessed triggers a sympathetic hemorrhage in my frontal cortex that my amplified physiology struggles to repair even as the damage occurs. I will write this once, cleanly, and then I will seal this journal in the archive and attempt to forget. I suspect I will fail.*

*The opposition called themselves the Aethereal Chorus. They were not a species, nor a civilization, nor an empire in any sense recognizable to the corporeal mind. They had no physical form, no energy signature, no arcane resonance, no psychic footprint. The fundamental law of their existence was absolute imperceptibility. *When Thronegate's expansion reached the Aetherial Veil, the region of non-space the Chorus inhabited, they did not fear. They had never been perceived. They believed—with the absolute certainty of a billion years of successful concealment—that no power in creation could locate them, let alone harm them. They declared war not with weapons, but with an orchestrated campaign of undetectable psychic erosion. Court officials began to lose their memories. *Godric received the reports during his afternoon meditation in the eastern gardens. He sat upon a simple stone bench beneath the flowering jacaranda trees, his ruggedly handsome face tilted slightly toward the warm sunlight, his heavily muscled frame perfectly relaxed. His salt-and-pepper hair stirred faintly in the breeze. I stood several paces behind him, trembling, clutching a datacrystal containing the latest casualty figures. He did not need the datacrystal. *He simply noticed them.*

*The Aethereal Chorus, dwelling in the imperceptible gaps between perception, experienced something that had never occurred in their billion-year existence. They were perceived. Not detected by instruments. Not sensed by magic. Perceived. The full, absolute weight of Godric's omni-perception crashed into their non-space like an infinite ocean of boiling light flooding a single, fragile bubble of vacuum. The Chorus had evolved to exist where perception could not reach. *The Chorus screamed. They had never possessed vocal cords, had never known the physical sensation of sound, had never even conceptualized the act of screaming. Godric's perception forced physical form upon them purely so they could experience the agony of being seen. Across the entire Aetherial Veil, a region of non-space spanning googolplexian conceptual layers, billions of undetectable thought-entities were violently, excruciatingly compressed into visible, tangible, bleeding forms. They manifested as writhing knots of translucent flesh, studded with millions of newly generated eyes that Godric's Senses had forced open, each eye weeping thick, black fluid as it perceived the perceiver looking back at it.*

*The psychic backlash of their forced visibility was absolute. Their minds, designed for eternal concealment, shattered against the unyielding wall of Godric's attention. Their conceptual bodies, now visible, now existing in a state of being-perceived, began to rapidly, agonizingly decay. The act of being seen was killing them. Their translucent flesh blackened, cracked, and sloughed away in wet, rotting sheets. Their newly formed eyes popped one by one, spraying hot, gelatinous fluid across the non-space that was rapidly collapsing into conventional reality under the pressure of Godric's perception.*

*But the true horror was the collateral damage. Godric's Senses, infinitesimally expressed, did not stop at the Chorus. His perception flooded outward, sweeping across the entire omniverse with the force of a googolplexian collapsing singularities. Every concealed thing, every hidden secret, every private thought, every buried memory, every forgotten god, every silent prayer in every dimension of every multiverse was instantly, involuntarily perceived. Every damaged reality was repaired. He did all of this without moving, without speaking, without expending any detectable effort. *The Aethereal Chorus was not restored. They remain in the archive of his memory, eternally perceived, eternally visible, eternally dying the death of being seen by eyes that should not exist. Their screaming continues in a dimension of pure perception that Godric created specifically to house them, a dimension where they will be seen forever, unable to hide, unable to die, unable to do anything but scream.*

*I closed my perception and vomited onto the garden path. Godric turned his head slightly, his warm, kind eyes crinkling with concern. "Are you unwell, Theron? Perhaps you should sit down." His voice was so gentle. So genuinely caring. I loved him in that moment, as I have always loved him, and the love made the horror so much worse.*

*I serve him. I will always serve him. But I will never again use the word "senses" without feeling the phantom pop of a billion eyes bursting in the back of my skull.*

**Journal of Orin, 12th Court Wizard of Thronegate**
