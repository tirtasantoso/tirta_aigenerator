Today would be the day his eldest daughter married.

Isabel, his beautiful, headstrong Isabel, had chosen a good man—a scholar from the northern provinces with gentle hands and a poet's heart. Godric had tested him, of course. That was the kind of son-in-law a king could be proud of.

"My love, you'll wear grooves into the stone if you keep gripping it like that."

Godric turned, and the smile that had been soft became radiant. Reina stood in the doorway of their chambers, her silver-threaded hair catching the morning light like a crown of starlight. She had aged alongside him, gracefully, beautifully, and the love that passed between them in that single glance was the kind that poets spent lifetimes trying to capture and never quite succeeded.

"I'm not gripping," Godric said, his voice carrying the warm, resonant tone of a man who had learned long ago that gentleness was not weakness. "I'm contemplating. There's a difference."

"Is there?" Reina crossed the room to stand beside him, her hand finding its familiar place in the crook of his arm. "And what profound truths has your contemplation uncovered?"

"That I am the most fortunate man in all of creation," he said, and meant it with every fiber of his being. "That our daughter is happy. That Hebe has finally agreed to wear the dress we picked out instead of that dreadful thing she found in the eastern markets."

"Do you understand now why your question made me laugh?"

I understood. God help me, I understood. I had asked if it was lonely to be the most powerful being in existence, and the answer was that it was the opposite of lonely—it was an eternal, escalating ecstasy that made every other form of satisfaction in the omniverse look like a pale imitation of a pale imitation of nothing. I had worried that my uncle was suffering in silence, burdened by his own supremacy, and the truth was that his supremacy was the most exquisitely pleasurable thing any being had ever experienced, and it was getting more pleasurable every Planck second, forever.

"But then," I said, latching onto something he had mentioned earlier, "you said there was one thing that made you bored or lonely. What did you mean by that?"

Godric's expression shifted slightly—not to sadness, not to regret, but to something that might have been a mild, distant frustration. The frustration of a man who owned the finest instrument ever crafted but had never been able to play it for an audience, because the audience would be vaporized by the first note.

"The only thing," he said, "that I find genuinely disappointing about my existence is that I have never had the chance to use my true power. Not even a fraction of it. Not even the faintest whisper of it. Every demonstration you've read about in the journals—the strength that crushed the Unmovable Spine, the speed that outran instantaneity, the endurance that absorbed the Quintessence Annihilator, the self-replication that filled the omniverse with infinite copies of myself, the cellular annihilation pulse, the chrono-freeze—all of it was achieved using effectively zero percent of my power. And I don't mean one percent. I don't mean zero point one percent. I mean an amount so infinitesimally small that the only accurate way to describe it is zero. Effectively zero. Practically zero. And even that, Kaedan—even that nearly-nothing expression of my power—was enough to instantly, effortlessly, completely annihilate the omniverse and everything in it."

He stood up from his chair, and I saw his body with new eyes. The muscular frame that had always seemed impressive but human—I now understood was anything but human. The salt-and-pepper beard and the kind eyes and the warm smile were not a disguise. They were a choice. A restraint. A deliberate holding-back of something so vast that even the barest fraction of its true expression would end everything.

"I have to keep my absolute concealment active at all times," Godric continued, walking to the window and looking out at the spires of Thronegate. "Not because I want to. Because I have to. If I let even a fraction more than zero percent of my power express itself—not my full power, not even one percent, not even zero point zero zero zero one percent, but just a slightly less infinitesimal fraction of nearly-nothing—the entire omniverse would cease to exist. Not be destroyed. Cease. The concept of existence itself would be overwritten by my presence. And I would have to put it all back together, which is effortless for me, but it's also tedious. I've done it before. I'm sure I'll do it again. But it's not exciting. It's not challenging. It's just... maintenance."

He turned back to me, and his smile had returned, but now I could see the edge of something beneath it—not cruelty, not malice, but a kind of restless energy. The energy of a being who contained infinite power and had never been able to express even the faintest shadow of it. "I have all this," he said, gesturing at himself, "and I can never use it. Not really. Not the way I want to. The omniverse is too fragile. Even with me holding back to the absolute limit of my capacity to hold back—even with absolute concealment active, even with my passive shields deactivated, even with Gent and all its siblings ordered into dormancy, even with every possible restraint fully engaged—the barest whisper of my true power would still annihilate everything. I am like a man who has trained his entire life to be the greatest warrior in history, but every time he tries to spar, his opponent dissolves from the heat of his body before he can even raise his hand."

He walked back toward me, and as he moved, I saw something I had never noticed before. His body, beneath the simple tunic, was not merely fit. It was perfect. Not the perfection of idealized art or divine sculpting—the perfection of something that had evolved beyond the concept of imperfection. The muscles of his arms shifted with a density that suggested infinite power compressed into human form. The veins that traced along his forearms carried not blood but something that glowed faintly, a golden luminescence that was barely visible, suppressed by his concealment. His chest, visible at the open collar of his tunic, was not merely muscular but sculpted with a definition that made classical statues look like rough clay models.

"Look," he said, and he rolled up his sleeve with the casual pride of a man showing off a new tattoo. His arm was thick with muscle, the bicep rising in a curve of dense, powerful tissue that seemed to hum with contained energy. "I used to train for hours every day when I was mortal. Sword drills. Wrestling. Riding. I built this body through decades of disciplined effort. And now—" he flexed, and the muscle swelled to a size that should not have been possible, the striations of individual fiber bundles visible beneath the skin, the golden glow intensifying until I had to squint, "—now it maintains itself. Improves itself. Without me doing anything. Every Planck second, my muscles become denser, stronger, more perfectly defined. I don't need to train. I don't need to maintain. My body simply becomes more perfect, eternally, and I can never show anyone. I can never use it. I can never test it against anything that wouldn't immediately cease to exist."

He raised the hem of his tunic, and I saw his abdomen—a wall of muscle so perfectly sculpted that it looked like something that should not exist outside of myth. The six-pack was not merely defined; it was carved, each abdominal muscle a distinct plate of dense tissue separated by grooves so sharp they could have been cut with a blade. The oblique muscles flanked them in sweeping curves, and the entire structure moved with his breathing in a rhythm that suggested power coiled and waiting, eternally patient, eternally restrained.

"Mortals work for years to achieve a fraction of this definition," Godric said, and there was something almost wistful in his voice. "I achieved it through training, originally. But now it just... is. And it keeps becoming more. Without effort. Without maintenance. Without limit. And I can never let anyone see it—not really see it, without the concealment—because the full expression of my physical form would unmake reality."

He dropped the hem of his tunic and held out his hand. Above his palm, something flickered into existence—a small, shimmering presence that I recognized from the journals as the birth of a new sentient passive aura. It was not Gent. Gent was, by Godric's own description, the weakest and most primitive. This was something else entirely, something that radiated a protective ferocity that made my wizard senses recoil even through the suppression.

"This one was born about three seconds ago," Godric said, looking at the aura with the fond pride of a father examining a newborn. "I'm calling it Ferin. It's... oh, about seventeen googolplex times more powerful than Gent at its maximum expression, and it has capabilities that make the cellular annihilation pulse look like a gentle suggestion. It can pre-annihilate hostile intent across infinite timelines. It can convert the concept of opposition into a self-consuming paradox that feeds back into my amplification. It can—" he paused, tilting his head as if listening to something I could not hear, "—apparently it can also restructure the fundamental constants of any reality that dares to threaten me, rewriting the laws of physics to make hostile action literally impossible. That's new. I didn't have one that did that before."

He looked at me, and his expression was almost apologetic. "I'll never get to see what it can really do. I'll order it into dormancy, like all its siblings. It will sleep until I command otherwise, and I will never command otherwise, because if I let it express even the barest fraction of its power, it would annihilate a googolplexian decillions of multiverses before I could tell it to stop. And the ones I'm birthing now—" he gestured vaguely, "—are so far beyond the ones from even a thousand years ago that the comparison is meaningless. There's one that's been forming for the past few minutes that I think might be capable of retroactively preventing the concept of harm from ever having existed. I'll have to suppress that one particularly carefully."

I stared at the shimmering presence above his palm. Ferin. Seventeen googolplex times more powerful than Gent at maximum expression. And Gent, even nerfed by an infinitely recurring ninety-nine point nine nine nine percent reduction, had slaughtered the largest resistance in omniversal history in less than a fraction of a Planck second. The implications of what seventeen googolplex times that power would look like were something my mind refused to process.

"But wait," Godric said, his face brightening with an idea, "you asked if I ever get bored or lonely. There is one thing that amuses me. One game I play with myself, since no one else can play it with me." He sat back down in his chair, leaning forward with the eager expression of a man about to share a secret. "I know the full extent of my power. Absolute omniscience means I perceive every aspect of my existence perfectly. I know exactly what would happen if I released my concealment. I know exactly what would happen if I expressed one percent of my power. I know exactly what would happen if I expressed one hundred percent. I have known these things since the moment I gained omniscience. But no one else knows. No one else can even guess, because the scale is beyond anything that any sentient mind has ever conceived."

His eyes met mine, and there was a playful glint in them that made my blood run cold. "So here's what I want you to do, Kaedan. Take a wild guess. Imagine what you think would happen if I released my absolute concealment—not all the way, just enough to express slightly more than the effectively-zero-percent I normally allow. What do you think would happen? Use everything you've learned from the journals. Everything you've seen. Everything you know about my stats and my superpowers and my immune system and my passive auras. And then tell me: what do you think would happen if I let out just a little bit more?"

I stared at him. The question felt like a trap, though I knew it wasn't—Godric did not set traps for his court wizards. He genuinely wanted to know what I thought, because my thinking was, to him, a novelty. A mortal perspective on something that no mortal could truly comprehend.

I closed my eyes and tried to imagine. The journals described annihilation on omniversal scales—googolplexian galaxies erased, decillions of multiverses collapsed, the entire omniverse destroyed and restored in zero seconds. And all of it was achieved with effectively zero percent of Godric's power. So what would slightly more than zero percent look like?

"If you released slightly more than zero percent," I began, my voice unsteady, "I imagine the entire omniverse would be destroyed instantly. Not just the physical structures—the galaxies and the universes and the multiverses—but the conceptual frameworks that underpin them. The laws of physics would cease to function. The structure of causality would collapse. The distinction between existence and non-existence would become meaningless. Every being in every dimension would be simultaneously annihilated, their consciousnesses erased so completely that they would never have existed at all. The omniverse would not merely die—it would be retroactively prevented from ever having been born, across all timelines and all possible realities, leaving nothing but void. An absolute, eternal void with no memory of what had been lost, because the loss itself would be so total that even the concept of loss would be destroyed."

I opened my eyes. Godric was watching me with an expression of polite interest, like a teacher listening to a student's answer on an exam.

"And if you released one percent of your power," I continued, my voice growing faster, more desperate, "that destruction would not be limited to our omniverse. It would propagate outward—no, not outward, because there is no 'outward' beyond the omniverse—it would propagate into the conceptual spaces where the omniverse's existence is defined. It would annihilate the possibility of any existence, ever, anywhere. It would erase the framework that allows reality to be real. It would unmake the very concept of 'something' and leave behind an absence so complete that even the absence would be absent. And that would happen not in zero seconds, but in negative time—the destruction would precede its own cause, would have always already occurred, would be the fundamental state of what remained, which would be nothing, forever, with no possibility of restoration because the concept of restoration would have been among the first things destroyed."

I was breathing hard now. The images in my mind were spiraling beyond my control—infinite recursion of annihilation, layers of destruction folding into layers, each one more absolute than the last. "And if you released one hundred percent—" I stopped. I could not continue. The scale of what I was trying to imagine was too vast for my mind to hold.

"If I released one hundred percent," Godric finished for me, his voice almost gentle, "you would need to be able to conceive of something that language cannot describe. Not 'annihilation.' Not 'destruction.' Not 'ending.' Those are temporal concepts, and what would happen would transcend temporality. What would happen is beyond anything that any sentient mind has ever imagined, because sentient minds exist within the framework of existence, and what would happen would be the cessation of that framework, and of all possible frameworks, and of the concept of frameworks, and of the concept of concepts, and of the idea that anything could ever have been, and of the possibility that anything could ever be, and of the space where those ideas once lived. And even that description—even everything I just said—is not remotely close to the truth."

He chuckled—a warm, genuine chuckle that made the hairs on my arms stand up. "Your guesses were very creative, Kaedan. And absolutely, completely, laughably wrong. Not remotely close. Not even in the same conceptual neighborhood. What you described—the retroactive annihilation of the omniverse, the destruction of all possibility, the erasure of existence itself—that is what would happen if I expressed an amount of power that is to my weakest aura what a single Planck second is to a googolplex of eternities. What you described is not even close to zero point zero zero zero—" he waved his hand, "—an infinite string of zeroes, and then a one, percent of my weakest aura's power. The weakest aura, Kaedan. Not my stats. Not my superpowers. Not my immune system. The weakest, most primitive, most pathetically inadequate expression of my power that exists. And what you described for one hundred percent—" he shook his head slowly, still smiling, "—that is so far from reality that I cannot even begin to correct it. Because the correction would require concepts that do not exist, expressed in a language that has never been spoken, using a frame of reference that no mind has ever possessed."

He leaned back in his chair, and his expression was one of profound, almost paternal amusement. "That's the game I play with myself. I know what would happen. I have always known. And I know that no one else will ever know, because no one else can know, because the knowledge itself would unmake them. So I sit here, containing all of this—" he gestured at himself, at his body, at the shimmering aura still hovering above his palm, at the invisible presences of countless other auras and shields and systems that surrounded him like an invisible court, "—and I can never show anyone. I can never use any of it. I can never express even the faintest whisper of what I truly am. Because even the faintest whisper would end everything. And I would have to put it all back together, and that's just tedious."

The silence that followed was the loudest thing I have ever heard.

I looked at my uncle—at the warm eyes, the salt-and-pepper beard, the relaxed posture, the muscular body that was becoming more perfect every Planck second—and I understood something that no journal had ever been able to convey. The journals described what Godric could do. They described the annihilation and the massacre and the absolute futility of opposition. But they could not describe what it felt like to sit across from him, in his private study, and listen to him explain that the most extreme destruction I could imagine was not even close to the barest whisper of his weakest expression. They could not capture the particular horror of seeing him flex his arm with the pride of a man who had built his body through hard work, while knowing that the same body, if not perfectly suppressed, would unmake all of reality without him even noticing.

They could not convey what it meant to realize that Godric was not suffering under the weight of his power. He was enjoying it. He was being pleasured by it, nourished by it, rewarded by it, every instant, forever. And the only thing that disappointed him—the only thing—was that he could never share it, never use it, never express even the faintest shadow of what he truly was, because the omniverse was too fragile to survive the revelation.

"Do you understand now?" Godric asked, and his voice was gentle. "Why your question made me laugh?"

I nodded. I could not speak.

"It's not lonely," he said. "It's ecstasy. It's satisfaction beyond anything you can imagine. It's constant, eternal, increasing pleasure that makes every other joy in existence look like nothing. And the only sadness—the only tiny, insignificant disappointment—is that I have all this, and I can never show anyone. I can never share it. I can never let anyone see what I really am. Except in moments like this, when someone I love asks the right question, and I get to give them a glimpse. A tiny, filtered, heavily-suppressed glimpse of the truth."

He stood up, and Ferin vanished—sent into dormancy, I assumed, to join its countless siblings in the silent, waiting dark. Godric stretched, a casual movement that made the muscles of his shoulders and back shift beneath his tunic with a grace that was almost hypnotic. "Now, I believe we were going to have lunch. I'm famished—not literally, of course, I don't need to eat, but I enjoy the taste of food and the pleasure of company. Will you join me?"

I followed him out of the study, my legs moving automatically, my mind still reeling. I had asked a question born of concern—the concern of a nephew for an uncle who had lived for a million years and would live for countless millions more, who had watched everyone he loved grow old and die, who had endured the Fall of Olympus and the loss of his mortal family. I had worried that he was lonely. That he was suffering. That his power was a burden he carried in silence.

The truth was the opposite of everything I had assumed. Godric was not enduring his power. He was reveling in it. He was being pleasured by it, nourished by it, rewarded by it, every instant, forever, without cost, without effort, without limit. He was the happiest being in existence—infinitely happier than any mortal could ever be, because his happiness was built into the fundamental structure of what he was. He grew more powerful, and the growth felt like ecstasy. He gained new abilities, and the gain felt like pleasure. He became infinitely stronger every Planck second, and the strengthening felt like being loved by the entire omniverse simultaneously.

And the only thing that could even remotely be called a sadness—the only tiny shadow on an otherwise infinite brightness—was that he could never share it. The omniverse was too fragile. Even the faintest whisper of his true power would end everything. So he kept it contained. Suppressed. Hidden behind absolute concealment and passive shields and the deliberate choice to appear as nothing more than a handsome, muscular, friendly middle-aged king with a warm smile.

I thought about what he had said—that my wildest guess had not been remotely close. I had imagined retroactive annihilation, the erasure of existence, the collapse of all possible frameworks. And that, according to Godric, was an infinitesimal fraction of his weakest aura's power. Not his power. His weakest aura's power. The metaphysical equivalent of dead skin cells. The waste byproduct.

And I had not even come close to describing what that waste byproduct could do.

The horror of that realization settled into me like a permanent resident, a tenant that would live in my mind for the rest of my life. I would serve Godric loyally. I would love him as the uncle who had taught me constellations and attended my naming ceremony and always asked about my studies with genuine interest. And I would remember, every moment of every day, that the warm, kind, friendly man I served was containing something so vast, so powerful, so absolutely beyond comprehension that its full expression would do things to reality that no language could describe.

And he found it amusing. He found it satisfying. He found it the opposite of lonely.

We reached the dining hall, and Godric took his seat at the head of the table with the casual ease of long habit. The servants brought out the honey-glazed roast he had mentioned—a dish I knew he enjoyed, not because he needed sustenance, but because the taste pleased him. The pleasure of food was another drop in the ocean of pleasure that was his existence.

"Kaedan," he said, as I took my seat beside him, "I want you to know that I appreciate you asking. It's rare that anyone asks about my experience, rather than my capabilities. The journals focus on what I can do. The stories focus on what I have done. But you asked how I feel. That means something to me."

He raised his glass—water, I noticed, not wine, though he could drink an ocean of wine and feel nothing—and his warm smile was the same smile he had always given me, the smile of the uncle who loved me.

"It is the greatest pleasure any being has ever known," he said. "And I carry it with me, every instant, forever. And I carry it alone, because no one else can survive knowing it. Except, perhaps, in tiny glimpses. In moments like this." He took a sip of water. "So thank you. For asking. For wanting to know. For being brave enough to hear the answer."

I raised my own glass. My hand was steady. My father's training, and my own thousandfold daily amplification, gave me that much. But inside, in the place where my deepest self lived, I was still falling. Falling through the chasm between what I had imagined and what was real. And I knew, with absolute certainty, that I would never hit the bottom.

Because there was no bottom. There was only Godric, and the infinite pleasure of his infinite growth, and the terrible, wonderful, impossible knowledge that I would never, ever truly understand what he was. And that not understanding was itself a mercy, because full understanding would unmake me as thoroughly as his unshielded presence would unmake the omniverse.

I am Kaedan. I am afraid in ways that transcend fear. And I serve my king with all my heart, because he is my uncle, and he loves me, and his love is the only thing that keeps the infinite at bay.
