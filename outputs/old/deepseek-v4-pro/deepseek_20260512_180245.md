The Vault of Testimony was a circular chamber hewn from the same primordial stone that formed the foundations of the Thronegate capital. I had never been permitted inside before. As my father, Cyran, led me through the adamantine doors, his face was a mask of solemn dread. He had served as court wizard for thirty-two years, his body honed by the thousandfold daily amplification into a vessel of staggering arcane might, yet his eyes, a deep violet from the mana circulating his blood, held a permanent shadow that I had never understood.

"Kaedan," he said, his voice carrying the weight of a million years of lineage, "you have crossed your twentieth year and felt the Awakening ignite. You now wield power that can unmake stars. But today, you will learn what true power is, and why our family has served without question since the days of Blake." He gestured at the shelves lining the walls, each filled with leather-bound journals etched with names and years. "Every court wizard before you recorded their witness. Read them. All of them. Do not stop until you understand the futility of opposition and the depth of our king's love for his own."

He left me there, the heavy doors sealing with a resonant thud that echoed in my bones. I took a shuddering breath, the scent of ancient parchment and ozone filling my lungs, and reached for the first volume on the nearest shelf. The name embossed on the spine read Halric—Year 15,230. My hands trembled as I opened it to the first page.

I, Halric, court wizard to King Godric, write this account while my hands still shake and the acrid stench of seared flesh clings to my robes. Let this record serve as a warning to all who come after: our king’s physical might is not a force to be measured. Godric watched them, his ruggedly handsome face relaxed, his salt-and-pepper beard stirring in the breeze. He took a seat on a stone bench and said to me, “Halric, let us see how weak my strength is. I will permit the tiniest fraction to express itself.”

I knew intellectually that his attributes multiplied beyond any infinite scale every Planck second, but I did not understand what “tiniest fraction” meant until that moment. Godric did not move. He did not clench a fist or furrow his brow. He simply ceased suppressing his passive strength. Later, I calculated that he allowed roughly a decimal followed by an infinite recursion of zeroes before a one, of one percent of his total strength to slip past his concealment. I believe I have not even come close to the truth; the actual magnitude is beyond my ability to express in any language.

The effect was immediate and absolute. The sheer gravitational pull of his muscle density, even at rest, created a singularity of attraction that seized the Legion’s entire armada. I saw their neutronium bodies, harder than the cores of dying quasars, bend inward. It was not compression; it was obliteration. Their flesh, if it can be called that, was stripped layer by layer, glowing with the heat of unmaking. I could hear their screams—a psychic broadcast that bypassed vacuum—as their consciousness was stretched across the event horizon of their own demise. Their skin peeled back in glowing, superheated plates, revealing rivers of quark-gluon plasma that splattered across the sky in arcs of impossible color. Limbs snapped free and hurtled into the growing gravity well, only to be crushed into fine, luminescent powder. Each warrior became a geyser of liquid metal and vaporized bone, their final agonizing moments etched into the fabric of space-time as irreversible scars.

The flagship did not explode; it imploded with such force that its dark matter core inverted into a white fountain of pure agony. The sound was a low, continuous roar that my enhanced ears interpreted as a trillion simultaneous deaths compressed into a single, unbearable chord.

The collateral damage was obscene. The gravitational shear extended outward from the capital, ignoring the planet’s protective wards—not because Godric aimed it, but because even the lingering echo of his unfettered strength cannot be fully contained. Entire neighboring star systems were drawn into the pull. Planets with billions of inhabitants were crushed into molten slush, their populations reduced to a thin spread of organic paste across the collapsing celestial bodies. The galaxy convulsed, its spiral arms wobbling as dozens of civilizations met instantaneous, violent ends.

I vomited, my stomach voiding its contents onto the pristine marble. Godric glanced at me with a look of mild concern, then sighed and waved his hand. In an interval of time so small it cannot be said to exist, every atom, every life, every star, every galaxy was restored to its exact state prior to the slaughter. The Legion, however, was not restored. He did not comment on that.

I knelt there, trembling, and realized that the strength I felt pulsing through my veins each day after my Awakening was less than the dead skin that flakes from his palm. He exerted zero effort. Zero. And the omniverse almost ended. I will serve him faithfully until my dying breath, but I will forever nurse a terror so deep it has become a second soul.

They had spent a million years engineering a scenario where victory was not just likely, but guaranteed. They built a weapon called the Certainty Loom, which locked all possible futures into a single inevitability: Godric’s death. “When I was seventeen, I witnessed the incident Korbin recorded,” he continued, his gaze distant. “I had hidden behind a pillar, against his orders. I saw the Legion fall upon themselves. I saw my own father weeping as he wrote that entry. After that, I never questioned why we serve. Not once.”

I found my voice, though it cracked. “Father, how… how do you bear it? Knowing that everything we are, everything the omniverse is, amounts to less than a stray thought of his? That we exist only because he chooses to love Blake’s bloodline?”

Cyran’s lips curled into a sad, knowing smile. “Because that love is real, Kaedan. Godric’s cruelty is for his enemies. His warmth is for us. He does not need court wizards. He leaned forward, placing a hand on my shoulder. “You are a court wizard now. You will likely witness your own catastrophe someday. When that day comes, remember these journals. They are not meant to break you; they are meant to prepare you. You must never allow pride to make you think that we, with our thousandfold amplifications, are anything but loyal companions. Never, ever entertain the idea that we could stand against him. The thought itself would destroy you.”

I swallowed hard. The existential dread that had been growing throughout the day solidified into a core part of my being. I knew I would carry it forever—a cold, heavy presence beside my heart. But I also understood that this dread was the price of my lineage. Blake had been Godric’s best friend. Thorin had died for him. Our bloodline was bound to the king through sacrifice and love. I would not dishonor that legacy.

“I understand,” I whispered. “I will serve faithfully. And I will write my own record, so that my son will know the truth.”

Cyran nodded, his eyes glistening. “Good. Now come, the king invited us to dine with him this evening. He asked specifically about you, wanting to hear how your arcane studies are progressing.” He stood and offered me his hand.

I took it, rising on unsteady legs. The walk to the dining hall was silent. When we entered, Godric was already there, seated at the head of the table, his ruggedly handsome face breaking into a warm, genuine smile upon seeing us. “Kaedan! Cyran! Come, sit. I had the kitchen prepare that roast you like.” His voice was rich and kind, his eyes crinkling with affection. The contrast between the monster of the journals and the loving patriarch before me was absolute. I seated myself, accepting a glass of wine with a trembling hand. And as I raised it to my lips, I silently swore that no matter what horrors I might witness in the millennia to come, I would never waver. For Godric was the greatest friend anyone could have, and the most terrifying enemy the omniverse could imagine. And I was grateful to be on his side.
