**From the Private Journals of Kaedan, 47th Court Wizard of Thronegate**

The air inside the ancestral archive chambers of the Thronegate palace tasted like old parchment and cold stone, a heavy, suffocating dryness that coated my tongue the moment I stepped past the obsidian threshold. My name is Kaedan, and at twenty-two years of age, I am the newest Royal Court Wizard to the eternal sovereign, a title I inherited through the unbroken, ancient bloodline of the legendary Blake. My father, a stern man of sixty-seven years whose hands could conjure localized supernovas, had spent the entirety of my adolescence preparing me for this moment. Not the magical training, though that had been brutal and unrelenting. Not the political education, though that had consumed entire decades of my youth. No, my father had spent years hardening my psyche for the single, unavoidable requirement of my ascension: the reading of the inherited journals.

I had heard of them my entire life, spoken of in hushed, trembling whispers by my uncles and cousins whenever the wine flowed too freely at family gatherings. The journals were the official record of our lineage, the accumulated trauma of forty-six court wizards who had served Godric before me. Each volume documented the incidents, the tests, the massacres where our sovereign's curiosity about his own impossibly amplifying attributes had resulted in the absolute, catastrophic annihilation of whatever opposition had been foolish enough to draw his attention. Reading them was not a suggestion. It was a mandatory rite of passage, a grim inheritance enforced by the previous court wizard—my father—who had himself been traumatized by the very volumes he now demanded I consume.

I had asked him once, when I was fifteen and still naive enough to believe my own magical amplification made me something more than a speck of dust, why the journals were necessary. He had looked at me with eyes that had witnessed horrors beyond my comprehension and said, "Because you must understand, my son, that our King does not merely win. Victory against him was impossible before opposition began. You must carry that knowledge in your bones, or you will break when you witness it firsthand."

The archive chamber was a massive, cylindrical room carved deep into the bedrock beneath the palace, its curved walls lined with obsidian shelves that stretched upward into a darkness so profound my magically amplified eyes could not pierce it. Each shelf held a single tome, bound in leather that ranged from ancient, cracked hide to gleaming, freshly treated skin. The older volumes radiated a faint, psychic pressure that made my teeth ache, the accumulated existential dread of their authors bleeding through the pages like a physical stain upon reality.

My father had placed a heavy oak reading table in the center of the chamber, upon which rested a single, flickering arcane lantern. The lantern's light was carefully calibrated to illuminate only the immediate reading surface, leaving the rest of the vast archive in oppressive, suffocating shadow. It was a deliberate choice, my father had explained. The darkness reminded the reader that they were surrounded by horrors they had not yet witnessed, horrors that would soon become permanent residents of their psyche.

I sat down at the table, my hands trembling slightly as I reached for the first volume. The leather was ancient, cracked, and impossibly cold to the touch, as if the book itself had absorbed the chill of the void. The cover was embossed with a single name: Vane. The eighth court wizard, who had served in the twelfth millennium. My father had spoken of Vane with a strange, distant reverence, as if the man's writings had permanently altered the trajectory of his sanity.
