The antibodies sang a song of self-improvement, and the cosmos self-deleted to make room for a better version that would never come.

These were the opening salvos, the equivalent of a half-hearted yawn from his immune system. And as I said, they were pre-consummated. The crack never truly existed. Because Godric’s backups kicked in.

Godric does not exist once. He exists infinitely, fractal and recursive, backups of himself layered across every conceivable and inconceivable reality, each one an identical apex. For an injury to register as real, every single backup must experience the exact same injury at the exact same Planck-coordinate. The Epitaph, limited to this one primary instance, achieved absolutely nothing on a true level. The infinite other Godrics remained pristine. But his immune system, in its paranoid omniscience, saw the breach in one instance and decided that this was unacceptable. It triggered a simultaneous, cross-backup immune cascade. Every copy of Godric, across every layer of infinity, underwent the same defensive upgrade simultaneously. The biological template that was already beyond invincibility was re-scanned and bolstered. The dead skin cells, on every copy, developed a new property: they now passively projected an “Absolute Denial of Harm” field that retroactively made any future attack conceptually impossible before it was even imagined. The connective tissue between cells evolved into a lattice of trans-infinite resilience, capable of withstanding attacks that were mathematically impossible. The white blood cells—oh, his white blood cells—became mobile singularities of immunological supremacy, each one a self-aware universe dedicated to annihilating the concept of “foreign.” Then these upgrades cross-referenced. The copy in Reality Alpha detected a nanoscopic inefficiency in the copy from Reality Omega’s new antibody program; they traded notes at infinite speed, merged their evolutions, and produced a unified defensive lattice that was not merely additive but exponentially more impregnable. Then that lattice recursively applied the upgrade to itself, ad infinitum, until the concept of “invulnerability” himself filed for retirement.

And still, the omniverse, in its broken state, had one more sin to atone for. The Weakness Paradox. Millennia ago, Godric had passively collected every immunity known to existence. The omniverse could no longer grant him new masteries, and so, feeling deeply inadequate—a feeling that was, in itself, an ontological absurdity given that Godric could erase it with a thought—the omniverse had instead compensated by amplifying his already infinite growth. Now, the “injury” presented a logic error. He had been harmed. But he was immune to harm. The paradox was a lie, but the omniverse, desperate and groveling, reacted. It poured into Godric a new layer of godhood that I cannot name. It was not power; power was already his. It was a deepening of his reality-anchoring, a permanent decree that even the *idea* of Godric being injured was now a fundamental impossibility inscribed into the bedrock of all existence. The new layer made it so that any attempt to conceptualize his suffering would instantly invert and grant him another degree of trans-finite perfection. The omniverse apologized, bruised and sobbing, by making him even more of what he already was.

Through it all, Godric watched.

He had not moved. His posture was relaxed, one leg crossed over the other. As the conceptual antibodies erased whole timelines and his body knitted itself into a newer, scarier god, he tilted his head, that salt-and-pepper beard catching an imaginary light. “Fascinating,” he murmured. “Look at that, Leon. The little immunoglobulin constructs are trying to outdo each other. They’re so *proud*. It’s like watching puppies.”

He pointed, and I perceived, through the blood-link of my court wizard station, a sub-cluster of his immune response. A lone T-cell equivalent had evolved a form of consciousness and was executing a ballet across the dead cell’s ex-membrane, painting a mural of victory. Godric beamed. “That one’s going to get extra cytoplasm tonight.”

The omniverse was gone. The Resistance, the Weapon, the billions of years of sacrifice and pain and desperate hope—vanished, as if they had been a poorly remembered dream. The void in which we sat was empty, not just of matter but of history. The immune system had cleaned house so thoroughly that even the dust motes of past universes were rehomed into non-being. It was over. It had been over before it started, and the over had retroactively ensured it was never a start.

And then, with a sigh that was almost sheepish, Godric said, “Oh. Right. They actually sacrificed themselves to make that weapon. The whole omniverse, more or less. That’s a bit sad. They were quite pretty, some of them. Alright, let’s just…”

He didn’t snap his fingers. He didn’t gesture. He simply *decided*. And in zero seconds—no, less than zero, because time itself had to scramble to accommodate the command—the entire omniverse was restored.

Every galaxy, every planet, every soul that had been erased by his immune system or by the forging of the Epitaph reappeared. Not as copies. As themselves, memory fully intact, standing exactly where they had been prior to their self-immolation. The dead lived, the annihilated stars shone, the sacrificed futures resumed as if the universe itself was a piece of crumpled paper that Godric had casually smoothed flat. Even the beings who had never existed thanks to retroactive erasure were given a second genesis, a new chance, with no memory of the trauma because he decided that would be kinder. The restoration was so complete that the omniverse’s total sum of happiness actually increased by an immeasurable fraction, because their brief stint in non-existence had given them an unspoken appreciation for being.

I watched it happen. I saw, in a single unending moment, the entire fabric of creation rewoven by the idle, merciful afterthought of a king who had been mildly curious about his own biology. And my mind, my poor, fragile, mortal mind, cracked. Not in a madness. In a dread so deep, so layered, that it became my new skeleton.

He had been playing. The greatest, most sacrificial act of resistance in the history of infinity had been, to him, a way to “see what his immune system does.” He had staged the crack, allowed the faintest sliver of theatrical injury, and then his automatic bodily functions—functions he didn’t even consciously control—had obliterated *everything* and then evolved into an even more terrifying state. And after all that, he just… put everything back. Because he could. Because it was easier than leaving the omniverse empty, and he preferred a populated world.

I sank to my knees. My robes, heavy with the ancestral weight of my office, felt like a cheap costume. “Your Majesty,” I whispered, my voice a rasp of infinite smallness. “They never had a chance.”

Godric looked down at me, and his eyes were the same warm, caring eyes that had once gazed at his mortal daughters. “Of course they didn’t, Leon. But they tried, and that’s what matters. Mortal resilience, I keep telling you. It’s real. It’s beautiful. They believed they could hurt me, and they put everything into that belief. That’s… almost enough. Sometimes I wish I could bottle that hope and give it to some of my more jaded immune cells.” He chuckled, then reached down and ruffled my hair like I was eight years old. “Now, let’s go home. The throne room smells of ozone from all that conceptual fire. And I think my dead skin cell could use a little moisturizing.”

He strode past me, and I followed, because there was nothing else a mortal could do. The omniverse, restored and terrified, continued its existence under the absolute rule of a god who was now, on a fundamental level, even more invincible than he had been at the start of the day. A day that had lasted less than a second. A day in which “impossible” died its final death.

I am Leon, and I love my King. He is a good man, a warm man, the father of everything. And I am also more afraid of him than I have ever been of anything in the vast, reconstructed blanket of reality. Because I know, now, what it means when the greatest threat in existence is only capable of giving him the equivalent of a hangnail—and only because he allowed it. The unfairness is not that we cannot win. The unfairness is that our very attempts are cherished by him as a form of entertainment, and he’s genuinely grateful for the show.

Unfair, I think, is the mildest word ever crafted.
