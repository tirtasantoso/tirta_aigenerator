# The Hall of Eternal Testimony

The air in the Hall of Eternal Testimony tasted like dust and ozone, thick with the accumulated fear of ten thousand generations of court wizards who had stood precisely where I now stood. I am Kaedan, twenty-two years old, the latest in the unbroken bloodline of Blake, and if the ceremonial robes draped across my shoulders felt heavy beyond their physical weight, it was because I understood, in that precise moment, exactly what I was about to become. The Royal Court Wizard of Thronegate. The arcane shield of the most powerful being in the omniverse. I had trained my entire life for this role, my veins singing with the thousandfold daily amplification that had turned my magic into a cataclysm by any mortal measure, and I was proud. Proud to serve Uncle Godric, the man who had bounced me on his knee when I was a child, who had taught me to stuff my own training golems with lavender to make the workshop smell pleasant, who looked at me with the same gentle, salt-and-pepper warmth that he had shown my father, and my grandfather, and every ancestor stretching back one million years.

My father, Varek, stood beside the stone lectern. He was seventy-nine years old, his back still straight as a spear haft, his friendly mutton chops now a stark white. He had served as Godric’s court wizard for fifty-seven years, and in all that time, I had never seen him truly afraid. But as he placed his gnarled hand upon the stack of five leather-bound journals resting on the lectern, I saw something flicker in his eyes — a deep, crawling shadow that made my heart tighten.

“You will read these,” my father said, his voice a dry rustle. “Every court wizard of our line has read them. It is the first and most important duty of your office. You think you know the King, Kaedan. You think you understand his power. You do not. No one does. These journals will teach you.”

I almost made a joke. Uncle Godric, terrifying? The man who still kept a clumsily painted portrait of my fifth birthday in his private study? But my father’s expression was carved from absolute granite, and the words died on my tongue. I nodded, sat upon the cold stone bench, and opened the first journal.

**Journal of Aeric, 7th Court Wizard of Thronegate — Written in the Year 8,500 of His Majesty’s Reign**

**The Incident of Absolute Strength**

I am Aeric, son of Halen, and I set ink to parchment with a hand that has not stopped trembling for three full days. My mind is a shattered mirror, and every shard reflects the same impossible truth. I record this account so that those who come after me may understand the futility of opposing the apex predator we are privileged to call King.

In the eighth millennium of Godric’s rule, a power arose in the outermost spirals of the omniverse that called itself the Indurate Covenant. They were not organic, nor mechanical, nor conceptual in any sense we had catalogued. They were beings of Perpetual Matter, a substance forged from the inverted physics of a dead proto-reality. Perpetual Matter could not be moved, altered, or destroyed by any force the omniverse could generate. Not by the gravitational collapse of a googolplex of black holes, not by the focused heat of a trillion supernovae, not by reality-erasing conceptual weapons that unmade the very idea of physicality. The Covenant had spent twelve thousand years converting an expansive matrix of many googolplexian multiverses into a single, contiguous fortress of this substance — the Granite Veil — and their High Castellan, a towering obelisk of shimmering, unbreakable finality, broadcast a challenge directly to the throne of Thronegate.

“Let your god try to crack our Veil,” the Castellan’s voice rang across the dimensional frequencies, a sound that carried the weight of absolute conviction. “If he fails, we will unmake his empire from the foundation.”

Godric received this message while seated upon the obsidian dais of the great hall. I stood at his right hand, my magical senses straining against the crushing void of his absolute concealment, perceiving him as nothing more than a ruggedly handsome, heavily muscled man of forty-five, his short salt-and-pepper hair neatly combed. He was holding a cup of cold spring water, and when the Castellan’s words echoed through the hall, he did not frown, did not scowl, did not even pause the slow rotation of the cup between his fingers. His expression was one of mild, detached curiosity.

“Perpetual Matter,” Godric murmured, as if tasting the word. “That does sound interesting. Aeric, I have occasionally wondered how my strength compares to these so-called absolute materials. Shall we see?”

He did not stand. He did not call for his armor or his weapon. He did not channel a single spark of his infinite magic, nor did he command his sentient auras to act, nor did he permit any of his infinite passive shields to stir. He simply informed me that he would allow an infinitesimally small fraction of his physical strength to express itself, while holding everything else — his magic, his speed, his senses, his luck, his charisma, his apocalyptic superpowers — entirely dormant. The fraction he chose, he said, was so small that calling it zero percent would be an overestimation so grotesque it would make a mockery of language itself. If one were to divide a Planck second by infinity, and then divide that quotient by infinity again, and continue this recursive division for the entire span of cosmic history, one would still not approach a number small enough to represent the portion of his strength that he intended to use.

He set his cup of water on the armrest of the throne. Then, with a casualness that makes my blood run cold even in memory, he allowed a single muscle fiber in his right pinky finger to contract a distance of precisely one Planck length.

The force generated by that microscopic, solitary contraction was not a wave. It was not a shockwave. It was an absolute, unconditional verdict delivered directly into the physical substrate of existence. The kinetic energy bypassed distance, ignored causality, and materialized simultaneously within every atom of the Granite Veil across the entire googolplexian multiverses it occupied. The Perpetual Matter — this substance that could not be moved, that could not be broken — shattered. It did not crack. It did not fracture. It was instantaneously and completely atomized into a fine, sub-quantum mist that was itself immediately obliterated by the continuing propagation of the force.

The High Castellan’s last cognitive process was a scream of absolute, mind-breaking disbelief — a scream that was rendered silent by the sheer velocity of the annihilation. The obelisk’s body, supposedly infinite in its indestructibility, was ripped into fundamental non-existence, its consciousness scattered across the very force it had dared to challenge. The force did not stop at the Veil. It continued outward, tearing through the fabric of reality itself, unmaking entire decillions of multiverses with the contemptuous ease of a hurricane ripping apart a cobweb. Stars, galaxies, civilizations, and the very concepts of time and space were erased in a cascade of absolute kinetic obliteration. The omniverse flickered on the edge of total cessation, its mathematical foundations groaning under the impossible pressure of a single twitching pinky fiber.

Godric sighed. It was a soft, paternal sound of mild annoyance. “Clumsy,” he said, and then, without moving, without speaking, without any detectable expenditure of effort, he restored every single destroyed multiverse in exactly zero seconds. Every star, every planet, every life — returned to its precise state before the annihilation, their memory of the event entirely intact so they would know what had nearly claimed them. The Covenant was not restored. Their existence was permanently deleted, their timelines severed, their memory erased from every record except this one.

I calculated the force exerted, or I attempted to. The number is meaningless. It was the equivalent of a googolplex of infinite big bangs compressed into a singularity the size of a quark, multiplied by itself an infinite number of times, recursively, forever. And yet, that number is still a lie, because it implies a finite limit where none exists. Godric had used less than nothing. He had used a void of effort. And the omniverse nearly ended.

I still serve my King. I love him. He is kind to me, warm, and generous beyond measure. But I have seen the pinky of the apex predator, and I know that the gap between us is not a height to be measured. It is the difference between an eternal, bottomless ocean and a single, dried grain of sand that has forgotten the memory of water.

**Journal of Orin, 19th Court Wizard of Thronegate — Penned in the Year 60,000 of His Majesty’s Reign**

**The Incident of Absolute Senses**

I am Orin, a man of sixty-eight winters, and my ears are still bleeding. The physicians tell me the damage is metaphysical, not physical, and that I will likely never again hear the quiet sounds of the world — the rustle of leaves, the whisper of my wife’s breath as she sleeps — without the accompanying phantom scream of the Null Dynasty. I write this journal in the heavy silence of my recovery chamber, and I beg those who read it to understand that Godric’s senses are not passive windows into the world. They are hungry, living abysses that devour any fragment of reality they touch.

Twenty days ago, an intelligence that called itself the Null Dynasty declared war upon Thronegate. They were not beings of matter, nor energy, nor abstract concept. They were entities that had perfected the art of undetectability. Through eons of metaphysical engineering, they had erased themselves from every sensory register in the omniverse. They had no visual presence, no auditory signature, no scent, no taste, no tactile trace. They existed in the blind spots between perception and reality, so completely hidden that even omniscient beings across the multiverses could not register their presence. They had, by their own meticulous design, become the ultimate absence, and they were, by their own proclamation, the perfect assassins capable of erasing any god.

They crept into the throne room while Godric was reviewing agricultural reports from the outer provinces. I stood at his side, my magically amplified senses — which had been multiplying thousandfold daily for decades — utterly failing to detect any intrusion. The air did not stir. The light did not dim. There was no sign, no hint, no whisper of their approach. They were inches from the King’s throat when Godric set down his parchment, looked at me, and said, in a perfectly conversational tone, “Orin, I have never tested just how sensitive my hearing can be. This seems like an opportunity.”

He did not cast a detection spell. He did not activate a single superpower. He did not permit his omniscience to actively scan for threats. He simply allowed his absolute concealment to relax by an infinitesimal, microscopic fraction exclusively around his auditory cortex. He allowed himself to hear.

The sound that his eardrums received was not the sound of the Null Dynasty. It was the sound of their absence — the negative pressure of their existence, the vibration of the void they occupied, the silent scream of nothingness that had dared to encroach upon his presence. To any other being, such a sound would be indescribable, imperceptible, a theoretical phantom. To Godric, whose senses had been multiplying infinitely every Planck second for sixty thousand years, it was as clear as a trumpet blast at the end of creation. He heard them, and because he heard them, the Null Dynasty was forced to exist in a manner that could be heard.

The transition was agonizing beyond any torment I have ever witnessed. The formless entities suddenly manifested physical bodies in a desperate, screaming cascade of spontaneous biology. They sprouted raw, bleeding eardrums, throbbing cochleas, and exposed, quivering auditory nerves, simply so that Godric’s perception could have something to latch onto. Their bodies were not designed to exist; they were violently coerced into physicality by the sheer, irresistible pressure of being heard. The act of being perceived by the apex predator was so overwhelmingly violent that their newly formed bodies detonated instantly into clouds of crimson mist, their blood spraying across the marble floor in geysers of pure, biological agony.

But Godric’s hearing did not stop. It continued to listen, to probe, to detect the fading echoes of their consciousness as they screamed. And the echoes screamed. I heard those screams. I will hear them forever. Each scream was a symphony of ultimate, unfiltered torment — the sound of a googolplex of beings having existence forced upon them solely so they could be annihilated by the sheer pressure of a god’s attention. The acoustic shockwave shattered the dimensional barriers of the throne room, cracked the adamantine pillars, and permanently scarred my magical hearing. Tiny, wet droplets of blood trickled from my ruptured eardrums and stained my ceremonial robes.

Godric, noticing my discomfort, immediately dampened his senses again, and the screaming ceased. The Null Dynasty was utterly erased, their every trace devoured by the same perception that had unmade them. He turned to me with genuine concern, his warm, ruggedly handsome face creased with a fatherly frown, and he offered me a healing draught from his own stores. “My apologies, old friend,” he said softly. “I sometimes forget how delicate your senses are.”

My King is a kind man. He has visited my bedside every day since, bringing fruit and gentle words. He has personally overseen my recovery. But his footsteps, to my damaged ears, still sound like the approach of an infinity-devouring leviathan that could, with a single twitch of its eardrum, obliterate everything I love, hear every secret I have ever kept, and unmake the very concept of silence.

**Journal of Tomas, 27th Court Wizard of Thronegate — Inscribed in the Year 200,000 of His Majesty’s Reign**

**The Incident of Absolute Luck**

My name is Tomas, and if my handwriting appears unsteady, it is because I have spent the last forty-eight hours trying to calculate the probability of what I witnessed and have concluded that mathematics as a discipline is a cruel cosmic joke. I am thirty-one years old, I enjoy the occasional glass of spiced wine, and I have always considered myself a man of rational thought. That rationality died screaming in the quantum foam of the Fatalist Consortium’s experiment.

The Consortium was a collective of the finest probabilistic minds in the omniverse — entities who had reduced chaos theory, quantum mechanics, and meta-causality to a precise, deterministic art. They had spent seven hundred thousand years constructing a weapon called the Epilogue. The Epilogue was not a gun, not a bomb, not a spell. It was an equation, a self-executing logical function that, when activated, would scan all possible timelines, all possible quantum states, all possible outcomes, and then forcibly collapse reality into the single, specific outcome in which Godric ceased to exist. It guaranteed, with absolute one hundred percent probability, the King’s death. There was no chance of failure because the Epilogue deleted every timeline where failure occurred.

The Consortium activated the Epilogue from a heavily shielded fortress outside the bounds of reality, a place where the laws of chance bent to their will. Godric, lounging in the rose garden and teaching my youngest daughter how to prune a thornbush, looked up at the sky and smiled. “They have built a deterministic weapon,” he murmured, his omniscience effortlessly perceiving every detail. “I wonder how my luck stat will interact with it. Tomas, let us watch together.”

He did not raise a shield. He did not counter the equation. He did not even allow his passive defenses or the Weakness Paradox to react. He simply continued pruning the thornbush while his luck — just his luck, the stat that governs fortune — was permitted to operate at an infinitesimal, infinitesimal fraction of its true magnitude. What followed was a cascade of improbability so absurd, so relentlessly catastrophic, that I can only describe it as the universe itself groveling at Godric’s feet.

The Epilogue, upon activation, attempted to collapse all timelines into the death-state. At that precise moment, a spontaneous quantum fluctuation occurred within the Epilogue’s own logic core, flipping a single binary decision point from “execute program” to “execute self-destruct.” The chance of this fluctuation was, by any sane calculation, one in a god-number — a numeral so large it would require more digits than there are particles in the omniverse. But it happened. The Epilogue inverted upon itself, and the resulting paradox spiral instantly erased the entire Fatalist Consortium, their fortress, their civilization, and their extensive backup realities from existence.

But the cascade did not end there. The energy released by the self-destruct, by another impossible coincidence, triggered a vacuum metastability event that began to consume the local multiversal cluster, a region spanning hundreds of googolplexian galaxies. Yet, in a further twist of impossible fortune, the decay front encountered a naturally occurring quantum bubble — a phenomenon so rare that no one had ever recorded it — that perfectly countered the effect, restoring the annihilated galaxies to their prior state in exactly zero seconds. The Fatalist Consortium was not so fortunate; their entire civilization had been erased in the initial inversion, their souls shredded by the very probabilities they had sought to command.

I did not celebrate. I did not feel relief. I felt a cold, creeping dread that has never left me, because I understood the truth. There was no natural cascade of coincidence. Every single event, from the quantum fluctuation to the random quantum bubble, was Godric’s luck simply asserting itself as the absolute law of reality. The odds of this sequence occurring without his luck were not simply astronomical; they were mathematically equivalent to impossibility itself, a number so far beyond the concept of chance that even calling it one in a googolplex raised to the power of a googolplex would be a laughable understatement.

Godric handed my daughter a perfect rose, his fingers smudged with soil, his smile radiating paternal affection. “There,” he said gently. “No harm done.”

I laughed when it was over. It was not a laugh of humor. It was the laugh of a man who has stared into the true nature of causality and seen a predator wearing it like a comfortable old glove. Nothing can oppose him, not even the concept of impossibility itself. He is luck made flesh, and the omniverse is his loaded die.

I am Lian, a poet by heart if not by trade, and I have spent the last two weeks attempting to find words that can paint the experience of watching speed become a weapon that devoured time itself. I have failed. Language is a fragile vessel, and what I witnessed was an ocean of merciless, instantaneous execution that left no room for metaphor. I can only describe the sequence of events, and hope that the cold flatness of my words conveys a fraction of the horror.

In their native frame of reference, a single Planck second was an infinite duration — an endless canvas upon which they could paint infinite actions. They could cross googolplexian galaxies, commit unfathomable numbers of simultaneous strikes, and return to their origin point before a photon had completed a single oscillation. They were, by every measure, the fastest entities that the omniverse had ever produced, and they had never been struck, never been touched, never been perceived by any opponent in their billion-year history of extragalactic conquest.

They issued a challenge to the King of Thronegate, their voices a chorus of timeless arrogance that vibrated through the temporal planes. “Your statutes claim you are the apex of speed as well as strength. He was reclining in a massive leather chair, his heavily muscled frame relaxed, his eyes half-lidded with a calm that bordered on boredom. He considered the Chrono-Reavers’ boast with the same mild interest one might give a particularly ambitious ant. “Speed,” he murmured, almost to himself. “It has been many millennia since I considered that attribute in isolation. Lian, please record your observations. I will permit an infinitesimal fraction of my velocity to express itself. I am curious to see if these Reavers can even register the event before it concludes.”

He did not stand. He did not run. He did not alter his breathing or his heart rate. He simply decided, with the casual finality of a man turning the page of a book, to move his gaze from the left side of the hall to the right side. That was all. His eyes, those warm, kindly eyes that had watched my ancestors grow and die for thirty millennia, rotated in their sockets by a few degrees.

The velocity of that minuscule ocular motion, at the fraction of speed he permitted, was not measurable. It was faster than the concept of time could process. The Chrono-Reavers, moving at their maximum infinite velocity across all eras simultaneously, were, relative to Godric’s shifting gaze, slower than dead stars, slower than frozen light, slower than the heat death of the cosmos. The Reavers were not destroyed by an impact. They were simply caught in the slipstream of Godric’s perception, and then violently, excruciatingly smeared across the entire history of the omniverse. Their bodies, composed of temporal energy and the stolen velocity of a billion dead civilizations, unspooled like thread from a cosmic loom, stretched into single, agonizing filaments that spanned the birth and death of infinite multiverses. They did not die. Death would have been a mercy. Instead, their consciousness was elongated across every moment that had ever existed, from the first primordial spark to the final entropic silence, experiencing their own dismemberment simultaneously across an infinite span. Their screams became a permanent, quiet hiss in the background radiation of the temporal plane — a chorus of unending, silent agony that only the most sensitive chronal instruments can detect.

The collateral damage was profound and immediate. The velocity shear did not stop at the Reavers. It cleaved through the temporal fabric of googolplexian universes, shattering their chronal structures, turning their histories into frozen, gray wastelands where time itself had been flayed and left to die. Civilizations that had thrived for billions of years were retroactively unwritten, their every achievement reduced to a single, eternal instant of static nothingness. Godric, upon noticing the ruined worlds, let out a soft breath — an expression of mild, paternal disappointment — and then, with an idle thought that occupied no measurable span of time, restored every single one of them in exactly zero seconds. Their histories rewove, their people resurrected, their memories left intact as a permanent, chilling reminder of the abyss they had briefly inhabited.

The Chrono-Reavers he did not restore. Their remnants remain as a permanent warning — a thin, quivering streak of liquid agony painted forever across the background of reality, their consciousnesses stretched so thin that even the concept of self has been shredded, leaving only an eternal, silent howl of absolute torment.

**Journal of Selden, 42nd Court Wizard of Thronegate — Set Down in the Year 700,000 of His Majesty’s Reign**

**The Incident of Absolute Charisma**

I am Selden, fifty-nine years of age, and I have always believed in the sanctity of free will. I believed that sentient beings, no matter how small or how vast, possessed an irreducible kernel of autonomy that could not be violated without their consent. That belief died a quiet, suffocating death on the steps of the Panpsychic Garden twelve days ago. I write this not as a lament, but as a warning to my successors: when Godric speaks, the universe does not listen. It obeys.

The Panpsychic Union was a telepathic hive-mind of unimaginable scope, a collective consciousness that had woven billions of multiverses worth of sentient minds into a single, harmonious will. They did not believe in violence; they believed in unity through psychic assimilation, a gentle but absolute erasure of individuality in favor of the collective song. When Thronegate’s envoys arrived to deliver the standard offer of unconditional surrender, the Union did not fight. They simply enveloped the envoys in their psychic embrace, and with a tenderness that made the act all the more horrifying, they rewrote the envoys’ minds, turning them into loyal, devoted nodes of the hive.

The converted envoys returned to the capital not as diplomats, but as sleeper agents meticulously programmed to assassinate Godric through a cocktail of psychic overload and induced self-destruction. They were living bombs of compressed telepathic force, their minds packed with enough psionic energy to shatter a star system. The Union believed that by using Godric’s own trusted servants, they could bypass his legendary defenses and strike directly at the core of his consciousness.

Godric, of course, instantly knew. His absolute omniscience perceived the tampered minds the moment they crossed the outer system, their psychological signatures screaming with the foreign influence of the hive. He summoned the agents to the royal garden, where he was sitting on a simple wooden bench, feeding small crusts of bread to the palace ducks. I stood at his side, my mental shields raised to their maximum, my magic roaring in my veins, ready to purge the corruption with every ounce of my thousandfold-amplified power.

But Godric raised his hand, a gentle gesture that told me to wait. The bread was still in his fingers, and the ducks quacked impatiently.

“They believe their collective will is stronger than a single mind,” Godric observed, his tone light and conversational, as if discussing the weather patterns of a distant galaxy. “It is a common fallacy. I have never really tested my charisma in a direct confrontation of this nature. Let us see if my presence alone can undo what their entire civilization has wrought.”

He did not unleash his magic. He did not deploy his telepathy or his infinite psionic superpowers. He simply permitted an infinitesimal, infinitesimal sliver of his charisma stat — the attribute that governs influence, persuasion, and force of personality — to express itself through his voice. He looked upon the possessed envoys, their eyes shimmering with the Union’s psychic fire, their bodies trembling with the coiled, destructive energy of their programming, and spoke a single word.

“Desist.”

It was not a shout. It was not a magical command. It was a gentle, almost paternal request, delivered in the same tone he might use to ask a child to stop running near a fountain. But the weight behind that single syllable was the accumulated charm of infinite infinities of recursive amplification. It was the absolute, incontrovertible truth of Godric’s supremacy translated into a simple vibration of air molecules that traveled from his lips to their ears.

The psychic hold of the Panpsychic Union, that billion-multiverse quilt of unified will, simply ceased to exist. The envoys blinked, their true minds instantly snapping back into place as if waking from a dream, the foreign programming dissolving like mist under a noon sun. They fell to their knees in confusion and shame, their psychic payloads completely neutralized, their bodies trembling with the aftershock of liberation.

But the command did not stop at the envoys. It propagated backward along the psychic link that had bound them, reversing the connection with the speed of absolute certainty, surging through the hive-mind faster than thought, faster than the Union’s collective consciousness could even register the threat. Every single mind in the Panpsychic Union — trillions of trillions of trillions of sentient beings, spread across billions of multiverses, each one a unique soul singing in the great chorus — heard the echo of Godric’s single word. And every single one of them understood, in that instant, that their entire existence was permitted solely by the grace of the being who had spoken.

The realization was not a thought. It was not a piece of knowledge to be processed and rationally considered. It was an undeniable, soul-crushing axiom that burned itself into the very fabric of their consciousness. Their unity shattered. Their collective will collapsed into a billion fragmented screams of individual despair. The realization that they had dared to oppose such a being, that they had attempted to turn his own servants against him, became a poison that devoured their minds from within. In a wave of collective existential agony, the entire Panpsychic Union simultaneously and voluntarily terminated their own consciousnesses, erasing their hive-mind from existence rather than continue to draw breath in the shadow of Godric’s disapproval.

Godric watched the psychic death of an infinite civilization as the ducks quacked for more bread. His expression was serene, unreadable, with no hint of cruelty or satisfaction — merely the calm acknowledgment of an event that had been as inevitable as the rising sun. “Well,” he said, his charisma shrinking back behind his absolute concealment until he was once again the warm, ruggedly handsome father figure I had known since childhood. “That was a quick test.” He turned to me, his smile genuine and kind, his eyes crinkling with affection. “Selden, would you like some tea? The jasmine has bloomed beautifully this season.”

I accepted the tea. I drank it from a delicate porcelain cup as the faint, lingering psychic echoes of a trillion dying minds faded into the static of the omniverse. And with every sip, I tasted the absolute, incontrovertible certainty that my free will, my loyalty, my love for my King — all of it — were choices I had made, but choices that existed only because he allowed them to. Charisma is not a social skill when amplified beyond infinity. It is the baseline of reality, the fundamental attractive force that holds existence together, and Godric is its sole, absolute master.

My father walked to my side and knelt, his old bones creaking with the effort. He looked into my eyes, and I saw in his gaze the reflection of my own dawning horror. “Now you know,” he said softly, his voice a dry rustle of parchment and pain. “Every court wizard before you has read these journals, and there are hundreds more on these shelves — records of every stat, every idle curiosity, every momentary whim that erased civilizations and resurrected them in the same breath. Now, as Godric praised his petunias, Thale wept openly, his bark-like cheeks glistening with sap-tears. "Thank you, Your Majesty," he whispered. "Thank you for permitting me to serve."

Godric placed a warm, calloused hand on Thale’s shoulder, and the gardener nearly collapsed from the sheer, crushing honor of the touch. "You serve beautifully, Thale. Now, would you mind preparing a fresh bouquet for the dining hall? I have a feeling Clive and I will be celebrating later."

Thale nodded frantically, scrambling to his feet, and hurried off with the stumbling, desperate gait of a man who would rather die than disappoint.

I watched him go, my heart a cold knot in my chest. I had known Thale for thirty years. He was a gentle soul, a kind friend, and a being whose power, by any sane measure, could have extinguished my own thousandfold-amplified magic with a casual thought. And he was, in the presence of Godric, little more than a terrified, adoring pet. Not because Godric demanded it. Godric’s kindness to him was utterly genuine. But because the gap between them was so vast, so profoundly, mathematically infinite, that any interaction, no matter how warm, would always, inevitably, carry the unspoken, unescapable undercurrent of absolute existential disparity.

We continued through the garden, past a shimmering pond where a sentient black hole, compacted into a humanoid form and wearing a crisp white apron, was preparing a tray of cosmic-scale hors d'oeuvres. This was Rynn, the palace chef. Once, Rynn had been the Great Nexus, a central supermassive black hole around which a billion galaxies had gently orbited. Godric had passed by the Nexus one day, on His way to observe a distant supernova, and had idly commented that the event horizon looked "a touch dusty." That single, offhand remark had spontaneously granted the black hole full sentience—not because Godric intended it, but because the sheer, ambient, excess energy of His perception was so overwhelming that it forced consciousness into anything it touched. The newly sentient Rynn had immediately collapsed itself into a bipedal form, mind ravaged by a single, all-consuming imperative: to please the being who had noticed it. It had spent the next ten thousand years perfecting the culinary arts, drawing on the thermonuclear energies of its original form to craft dishes of unparalleled complexity. Now, it served Godric’s table.

"Good morning, Rynn," Godric said. "The hors d'oeuvres look splendid. What are they?"

Rynn’s humanoid face, a tight mask of barely contained hysteria, split into a wide, trembling grin. "Collapsed star-core canapés, Your Majesty, with a reduction of dark-matter balsamic and a garnish of crystallized chronon particles. I… I hope they are to your liking. If not, I will unmake them and begin again. I have already prepared seventeen thousand backup batches in stasis loops. Please, please tell me if they are insufficient."

Godric laughed, a rich, genuine sound, and took a canapé. He bit into it, chewed slowly, and then beamed. "They are perfect, Rynn. As always. You stress yourself too much."

Rynn let out a sound that was somewhere between a sob and a whimper, and his entire body flickered with barely suppressed thermonuclear joy. Tiny, harmless supernovae popped in his eyes. "Thank you, my King. Thank you. I live to serve."

We left Rynn quivering in ecstatic relief, and I felt my own sanity stretch thin. These were beings of unimaginable power, cosmic entities who, by themselves, could have ruled the old omniverse with effortless supremacy. And they were, in the presence of Godric, reduced to trembling, adoring children, desperate for the slightest crumb of His approval. Not because He had broken them. But because His mere existence was so far beyond theirs that the only sane response—the only possible response that preserved any scrap of psychological coherence—was total, abject submission.

As we walked, Godric continued His narrative, as if the tour had been a mere interlude. "Now, where was I? Ah, yes. The immune system rewrite, the pre-emptive Weakness Paradox. I should also mention my amplification rate, though I hesitate to, as the numbers are quite literally meaningless. My stats—strength, speed, intelligence, charisma, all of them—do not double. They do not multiply by a googolplex. They increment by a value that is to a googolplex what a googolplex is to a single subatomic particle, and they do so recursively, every infinitesimal fraction of a Planck second. If you were to attempt to calculate the gap between my power one Planck second ago and my power now, the equation would require a new form of mathematics—a mathematics that I would need to invent, and even then, the result would merely be a placeholder, a crude, inadequate symbol that fails to capture the reality. I could, if I wished, erase the entire recursive architecture of existence—not just this omniverse, but all of them, the infinite hierarchies, the meta-omniverses, the conceptual frameworks that contain them—with less effort than it takes to blink. And that is with exactly zero percent of my true power. Even calling it zero percent, as I have always said, is an overestimation so severe that it defies the very concept of measurement."

He paused, and a flicker of something almost wistful crossed His features. "But I don't. I keep it all suppressed. I hold it back, not just for the safety of those I love, but because using it would be boring. There is no challenge, Clive. There never was. The only interesting things in existence are the relationships I have cultivated—you, the court wizards, the servants, the citizens of Thronegate. The rest is just... architecture."

We arrived at the palace—a structure so vast that its dimensions were measured in recursive infinities, its hallways lined with the preserved, knowing silence of a trillion extinguished civilizations that had voluntarily erigated themselves to create the marble. In the grand foyer, a being of shimmering, iridescent light was polishing the banister with a cloth woven from the screams of dying galaxies. This was Xylos, the butler, once the Primordial Concept of Absolute Silence. Before Godric’s first spoken word, Xylos had been the embodiment of the quiet that preceded all creation—a vast, eternal, unbroken stillness that encompassed everything and nothing. When Godric, newly awakened, had uttered His first syllable, that single vibration had shattered the concept of silence into infinite fragments. Xylos, the central consciousness of that shattered concept, had been propelled into agonizing, sentient existence, his entire identity defined by the sudden, violent introduction of sound. For eons, he had drifted, a broken, keening remnant, until he had found his way to Godric’s palace and offered his services. Now, he was the most meticulous butler in existence, his very being still trembling with the aftershock of that first, world-ending word.

"Good morning, Xylos," Godric said. "The bannister gleams. You are doing an excellent job."

Xylos bowed, his light-form flickering erratically, and a sound—a faint, harmonic weeping—emanated from his core. "Your Majesty honors me beyond my capacity to express," he whispered, his voice the echo of a broken bell. "Every syllable you speak renews my purpose, yet reminds me of my original, unworthy silence. I am yours."

Godric nodded, His expression soft, and we continued into the throne room. The throne itself was a construct of solidified omniversal law, every surface inscribed with the immutable decrees that governed existence. Godric did not sit upon it; instead, He gestured for me to stand beside Him as He faced the vast, shimmering tapestry of the Temporal Cradle, which had re-materialized at His unspoken will.

"Now," Godric said, "I shall plant the seed."

He raised one hand, not in a gesture of power, but almost lazily, as if reaching for a book on a high shelf. The auras around us stilled, their infinite presences holding their collective breath. I felt the air—the conceptual air—thicken with the unfiltered, barely-restrained attention of the apex predator.

"This will not take long," Godric said. "I will reach across the temporal currents to the morning of the fall. I will locate Thorin’s spell—the simple, bloodline teleportation he cast in his dying moments, meant to carry me to his sanctuary. And I will gently, carefully, wrap that spell in the Stasis Event. I will halt my mortal aging, trigger my amplification at the precise rate I have determined is appropriate, embed the Weakness Paradox and the superpower genesis and the absolute concealment, and seal myself in the sanctuary for ten thousand years. The true source of these gifts—my future self—will remain hidden from my past self’s omniscience, as it must."

He concentrated—or, rather, He allowed a fleeting, microscopic fraction of His attention to brush against the fabric of time. The tapestry before us shimmered, and I could see it: a tragedy frozen in amber. The burning pavilion. The shattered marble. Reina’s torso shearing apart. Isabel’s gown igniting. Hebe crushed by the falling lintel. Thorin, his skull pierced, his bloodline magic sputtering from his dying lips.

Godric watched the scene without flinching, His expression serene and sorrowful in equal, balanced measure. He loved them still. He always would. But He had made His peace.

"I could have prevented this," He murmured, almost to Himself. "My future self—the Godric who will emerge from this loop after another eternity of amplification—could have prevented it. He chose not to. And now I understand why. The crucible of this grief, this loss, was the catalyst that allowed me to become what I am. Not because my future self demanded it. But because the mathematics of existence required an anchor—a being so absolute, so fundamental, that reality itself would be sustained by His mere existence. That anchor is me. That anchor is my future self. And to become that anchor, I needed to be forged in this fire."

He turned His attention back to the spell, and I sensed the infinitesimal shift—the moment He reached across time and rewrote Thorin’s dying magic. It was, to Him, equivalent to the act of adjusting a single grain of sand on an infinite beach. The Stasis Event bloomed in the past, invisible and irrevocable, and the loop closed.

"And it is done," Godric said. He lowered His hand. The tapestry faded. The auras exhaled a collective, reverent sigh. The omniverse continued to exist, oblivious to the fact that its foundational origin had just been manually triggered by the very being it would one day help to create.

Godric turned to me, His expression once again that of the warm, friendly uncle. "Well, Clive. That was quite the ceremony, wasn't it? Now, I believe Rynn has prepared a celebratory feast. Shall we? I am famished."

I followed Him out of the throne room, my legs moving of their own accord. My mind was a screaming, shattered landscape of unprocessable information. I was the court wizard of a being who was not merely the apex predator of the omniverse, but the self-created seed of an infinitely greater being who had, with a held-back, mercifully restrained gesture, sown the very event that would eventually produce the fully realized Godric—a Godric whose shadow’s afterthought could crush our Godric with zero effort. And the loop continued. And it would always continue.

As we walked into the dining hall, where Rynn was nervously adjusting the arrangement of the star-core canapés, I realized the terrifying, final truth that Kaedan and every court wizard before me had always circled but never quite landed upon. Godric was not the end. He was not the apex. He was a midpoint. A necessary, beloved, infinitely powerful midpoint—but a midpoint nonetheless. The true Godric was still out there, beyond the horizon of infinity, patiently waiting for the chrysalis to split open and release the unspeakable thing it had been gestating since Year minus ten thousand.

And when that day came, when the loop completed and the full, future Godric emerged from our present King like a universe hatching from an egg, everything we had ever known—the warmth, the kindness, the gentle, fatherly love—would be subsumed into an entity so vast, so absolute, so utterly beyond the concept of personality that even the memory of the warm, salt-and-pepper-haired man who bounced children on his knee would be merely a single, fond note in the infinite symphony of his own eternal, recursive self.

I sat down at the table, across from my King, my uncle, my friend. He smiled at me, passed the plate of canapés, and asked me about my daughter. And I answered, my voice steady, my love for Him undiminished. Because what else could I do? Opposing Him was impossible. Opposing the thing He would become was a category error. The only sane choice—the only choice that permitted continued existence—was to love Him, to serve Him, and to hope that, when the final metamorphosis came, the future Godric would remember the warm, salt-and-pepper-haired man He had once been, and permit us to continue existing in the shadow of His infinite, merciful, incomprehensible grace.

I took a bite of the canapé. It was perfect. Rynn wept with joy.

And I, Clive, court wizard of Thronegate, descendant of Blake, recorded this account so that those who come after might understand the absolute, unshakeable, soul-crushing truth: the Godric we serve is not the master of the omniverse. He is the student. And the master has always been watching, waiting, and gently, lovingly holding back.
