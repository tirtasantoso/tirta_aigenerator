The air inside the Court Wizard’s private archive was thick with the scent of aged parchment, arcane preservation oils, and something far more subtle—a faint, lingering tang of pure, unadulterated dread. I, Kaedan, at twenty-two years of age, stood in the heart of the palace library on the capital world of Thronegate, my new ceremonial robes already feeling like a funeral shroud. My father, Marius, had served as King Godric’s court wizard for the past forty-one years. He was a man of quiet dignity and profound arcane might, his daily thousandfold amplification making him a living god by any reasonable cosmic standard. Yet as he handed me the heavy, leather-bound stack of inherited journals, his hands trembled, and his eyes held the hollow, glassy quality of a soldier who had seen too much and could never unsee it.

“You are the newest Court Wizard of Thronegate, Kaedan,” my father said, his voice stripped of all warmth. “Before you stand at His Majesty’s side in any official capacity, you will read every one of these journals. This is not a request. It is the law of our lineage, passed from Blake to me, and now to you.” He set the stack down on the heavy oak table with a thud that seemed to shake the room. “When you have finished, you will understand why we serve, why we love our King, and why we will never, ever speak of what we have written.”

I watched him leave, the great brass-bound doors closing behind him with a resonant finality. I had grown up in the palace halls. His ruggedly handsome forty-five-year-old face, with its neatly trimmed salt-and-pepper beard and twinkling eyes, was the face of a protective, doting father. To me, he was not the Apex Predator of the Omniverse; he was simply the man who always laughed at my terrible jokes. And yet, the terror in my father’s eyes was a testament to a different truth.

I sat down, the smooth wood of the table cool beneath my suddenly clammy palms. I took a long, steadying breath, selected the oldest journal from the pile, and began to read. My hands were already shaking, though I did not yet fully comprehend why.

**Journal of High Court Wizard Aldric — Year 5,000 — The Incident of Absolute Senses**

The handwriting in this journal was elegant but erratic, the letters slanting sharply as if the author had been writing at a precarious angle, barely keeping his composure. The ink was a deep, rusty brown, smelling faintly of iron and old fear.

*I am Aldric, fourth court wizard of the lineage of Blake. I write this not for glory, but as a desperate attempt to externalize the horror before it curdles my sanity permanently. Yesterday, I witnessed the death of a multiversal coalition not by blade or spell, but by a single, casual inhalation. I must record the truth, even if my quill shakes with every stroke.*

*The Scentless Ones were a species of pure conceptual absence. They inhabited the negative spaces between thoughts, the vacuums that exist between moments. They had no physical form, no odor, no sensory footprint whatsoever. For millennia, they had silently unmade Thronegate’s outer territories, erasing whole star clusters and leaving behind only a growing, maddening quiet. Our finest scrying arrays found nothing. Our most advanced detection wards registered zero. They were, by all known metrics, perfectly imperceptible.*

*King Godric, may the stars preserve His mercy, sat upon the observatory balcony overlooking the nebula. He wore a simple gray tunic, His immortal forty-five-year-old frame relaxed and at ease. He was stroking His short salt-and-pepper beard thoughtfully, His ruggedly handsome face creased with mild curiosity. “They certainly know how to keep a low profile, Aldric,” He remarked, His deep, rich voice carrying no hint of concern. “I’ve always found that a truly unique civilization carries a distinct aroma. Let us sample theirs.”*

*I did not understand His meaning until He tilted His head back, closed His eyes, and inhaled. It was a soft, leisurely breath, the kind a man might take while leaning over a blooming rosebush. He was not drawing mana. He was not activating an arcane technique. He was simply, gently, sniffing the air. That was the entire preparatory action.*

*The effect, however, was a cataclysm that my daily thousandfold-amplified mind still struggles to reconstruct. Godric’s sense of smell, like all His traits, has been amplifying infinitely by itself every Planck second since the dawn of the Stasis Event. By the Year 5,000, the baseline sensitivity of His olfactory perception was not merely superhuman; it was a physical law of the omniverse, a fundamental force that could isolate and identify a single specific molecule across an endlessly recursive hierarchy of multiverses. And He was deliberately testing it, holding back, by His own later admission, to an infinitesimal fraction so pitifully small that calling it zero percent is a laughable, grotesque overestimation.*

*When He inhaled, He did not simply draw in air. He drew in the very concept of scent from every corner of the local omniversal cluster. The Scentless Ones, those arrogant entities of pure nothingness, were abruptly and violently forced into corporeal existence purely because His divine nose demanded something to smell. The vacuum created by His diaphragmatic contraction was so cataclysmically intense that it tore their non-dimensional forms out of the void and condensed them into screaming, bleeding physical bodies, crafted spontaneously and excruciatingly from the fabric of reality itself, solely so that they might exude an odor. They materialized as towering, pulsating columns of raw, exposed nerve endings and weeping, translucent flesh, trillions of beings suddenly thrust into physical agony, their newly minted vocal cords emitting a deafening, continuous shriek of absolute biological terror.*

*The inhalation’s suction did not stop there. It continued, a relentless, omniversal vacuum that ripped entire galaxies from their orbits and dragged them toward His nostrils. The Scentless Ones, now a writhing carpet of incarnated meat, were peeled apart molecule by molecule as the pressure differential exceeded any known force. The smell of their destruction—a complex bouquet of burnt ozone, liquefied logic, and screaming—was immediately cataloged, analyzed, and dismissed as “mildly earthy” by the King. The air around Him filled with a thick, black mist of disintegrated consciousness, which He then exhaled in a sigh that smelled faintly of lavender.*

*The collateral damage was absolute. The inhalation’s shockwave had not merely cleansed the enemy; it had swept through three hundred billion multiverses, each containing countless googolplexian civilizations. I felt them die through my scrying link—a sudden, dreadful silence where the hum of life had been. Trillions of trillions of histories, cultures, and souls were inhaled, paused on the edge of His olfactory receptors, and then, with a casual, effortless thought, instantly restored. He exhaled, and the multiverses were recreated in exactly zero seconds, every atom perfectly replaced, every being resurrected with no memory of the erasure. I vomited onto the obsidian floor before I even knew my stomach had turned.*

*The Scentless Ones, however, were not restored. They remain now as a permanent, microscopic trace element in the atmosphere of the royal observatory. Their consciousness, eternally trapped in the state of being perpetually smelled, contributes a subtle, salty note that Godric occasionally remarks upon, finding it “pleasantly maritime.” I have not been able to breathe through my nose since without tasting their endless, silent screams.*

*Godric later found me trembling in the corridor. He placed a warm, calloused hand on my shoulder and offered me a cup of chamomile tea. “You look pale, my boy. Was it something you ate?” I told Him it was just the altitude. He nodded sympathetically, His friendly smile holding no awareness of the atrocity He had just casually committed. I love my King. I must love my King. But the smell of that tea will haunt me for the rest of my mortal life.*

I closed the journal, my fingers stiff and cold. The image of Godric—my Uncle Godric—sniffling a cosmos out of existence was so violently incongruous with the man who had taught me to skip stones across a pond that my mind physically recoiled. I set the journal aside and reached for the next one, my heart hammering a heavy, uneven rhythm.

**Journal of High Court Wizard Seraphin — Year 300,000 — The Incident of Absolute Strength**

The script in this journal was immaculately precise, almost mechanical, as if written by a scholar of pure logic. Yet the pages were crumpled at the corners, and in several places the ink had bled through where the quill had been pressed down with far too much force, betraying the terror behind the clinical facade.

*I am Seraphin, thirty-third court wizard of Blake’s lineage. I have served Godric for one hundred and nineteen years. My thousandfold daily amplification makes me a colossus of arcane might by any sane measurement. Yesterday, I observed the King’s demonstration of His physical strength, and I now understand that the word “strength” has no meaning in His presence. It should be retired from the lexicon.*

*The opposition styled themselves the Adamant Concordance. They were a fusion of hyper-advanced machine empires and transcendent energy beings who had surgically grafted their existence into a hypersolid pocket dimension forged from the condensed, immutable constants of reality itself. Their soldiers were literally unbreakable—their bodies woven from frozen time and absolute mathematical certainty. They issued a declaration of war that was essentially a treatise on why they could not be physically harmed, complete with supporting proofs and peer-reviewed data.*

*Godric received the document during His afternoon tea. He read through it with the patient indulgence of a scholar reviewing a student’s flawed thesis. “They have invested so much faith in the concept of unyieldingness,” He mused, setting aside His porcelain cup. “I haven’t examined my own physical strength in isolation for several millennia. Let us see if a tiny pinch can provide some novelty.” He specifically excluded Gent, His passive shields, and all other autonomous defensive systems from the engagement zone.*

*He then raised His right hand and brought His thumb and forefinger together, as if preparing to pick up a stray spec of dust. He did not clench a fist. He did not swing a blow. He simply performed that infinitesimally small pinch, squeezing the air between His digits. The fraction of His absolute strength that He allowed to express itself, by His own later offhand assessment, was so stupendously minuscule that if you were to write a zero after a decimal point and continue writing zeros until the heat death of every timeline, you would not have approached the beginning of the number’s actual smallness. It was, in every meaningful sense, exactly zero percent.*

*Yet that pinch did not merely compress the space between His fingers. It compressed the entire geometric substructure of the local omniversal manifold. The pressure exerted by those two digits exceeded the integrated structural integrity of reality itself. The Adamant Concordance’s pocket dimension, anchored as it was in a hyper-stabilized sub-quantum fold, was instantly crushed into a density that surpassed any black hole—a density that broke the concept of density. Their invincible soldiers of frozen time did not die; they were reduced to a hyper-compact slurry of shattered physical law and screaming sentience, forced into a volume smaller than a Planck length but containing the entire mass-energy of seven hundred trillion multiverses. The sound of the compression was not audible; it was a metaphysical shriek that caused my amplified mage-senses to hemorrhage and my teeth to crack in my gums.*

*The collateral damage was simultaneously instantaneous and absolute. The pressure differential radiated outward from the pinch point, collapsing the dimensional boundaries of every adjacent reality in an expanding sphere of destruction. Entire googolplexian galaxies across a hundred billion multiverses were squashed flat, their entire histories extruded into a two-dimensional film before being torn apart by the tidal forces. I perceived the extinction of an uncountable infinitude of sentient life, all snuffed out in the fraction of a nanosecond that His pinch endured.*

*Godric, observing the sudden, profound silence of the newly formed void, clicked His tongue in mild irritation. “Ah. A bit more dramatic than I intended.” Without the faintest glimmer of effort, without moving a muscle, He simply willed the omniverse to reverse. Instantly, every destroyed astrophysical structure was perfectly restored. Every sentient being was resurrected, their timelines reknit, their memories utterly unblemished. The entire cataclysm was undone so completely that, from the perspective of the victims, it had never occurred. I alone bore the memory, a burden He did not seem to recognize.*

*The Adamant Concordance was not restored. Their compressed, eternally agonized remains now hang from a silver chain that Godric affixed to the ceiling of the throne room as a “conversation piece.” I have heard that if you focus your magical senses on the microscopic pendant, you can still discern the faint, endless screams of trillions of souls, crushed into unending, conscious torment. Godric occasionally glances at it and smiles, remarking that it “adds a certain sparkle.” After the demonstration, He flexed His hand casually and said, “My joints feel a bit stiff, Seraphin. I should stretch more often.” I knelt and could not speak. I will serve Him until I die, but I know now that my strength, my mighty thousandfold amplification, is to His what a whisper is to a supernova. I am a fiction of power in the presence of the only truth.*

I set the journal down, my stomach churning. I had seen the silver pendant many times, had even thought it a pretty ornament. Now I knew it was a genocide compressed into jewelry. My hands moved mechanically to the next volume, the pages feeling heavier with each turn.

**Journal of High Court Wizard Iskar — Year 600,000 — The Incident of Absolute Speed**

This journal was a mess of jagged, scrawled text, the handwriting fractured and inconsistent. Words were violently crossed out, paragraphs began and ended without reason, and the pages themselves were torn and dog-eared, as if the writer had been struggling against a breakdown with every sentence.

*They were called the Instantaneous Legion. They were not fast. They were speed given consciousness, beings who had transcended linear time and existed only in the negative intervals between Planck moments. They could strike before the thought of a strike was formed, could kill you in a past that had already been revised. They declared that Godric’s power was meaningless because He would never perceive the attack that killed Him. They were correct about one thing: He never did perceive it. But only because it never happened.*

*Godric stood in the palace courtyard under the eternal twilight of the capital, wearing His oldest, most comfortable boots. He listened to their declaration with that patient, warm half-smile I have known since childhood. “Speed,” He said, and He chuckled, a genuine, deep laugh that shook His heavily muscled shoulders. “They think speed means going fast. Iskar, my boy, let me show you what speed actually means.” He told me to observe closely. He promised He would use only the most minimal possible motion.*

*He then, with an exaggerated, almost theatrical slowness, took a breath. His chest rose and fell normally. And then He blinked. A single, ordinary blink. His eyelashes, those same lashes that had brushed His cheeks during countless naps, descended and rose again. The duration of that blink was, to any conventional measurement, instant. But to call it “instant” is an insult to the word. Because Godric’s Speed stat has been multiplying infinitely by itself every Planck second for six hundred thousand years. The kinetic value of even the most infinitesimal movement of His body is not a quantity; it is an axiom. It does not obey time; time attempts to obey it.*

*When He blinked, His eyelashes did not move through space. Space, causality, and the entire framework of temporal mechanics rearranged themselves to permit the motion. The blink generated a chronal shear wave that propagated backward through the full extent of history, a wave of pure, ineluctable velocity that struck the Instantaneous Legion before they had even conceived of their invasion, before their species had first crawled from their primordial ooze, before the stars that would birth them had kindled. It did not kill them; it retroactively annihilated the possibility of their existence. Their entire timeline—past, present, and all potential futures—was severed from reality like a frayed thread and discarded. They never were. There was no battle, no struggle, no final defiant scream. There was only the blink, and then the eternal, immaculate silence of a hole in causality where a mighty empire used to be.*

*But the blink did not stop at erasure. The friction of His eyelid dragging across His eye at that velocity ignited the chronal fabric of reality itself. Time caught fire. A decillion light-year radius of the omniversal manifold was instantly consumed by a raging, unstoppable temporal inferno. Billions of multiverses, each with their own grand histories and teeming civilizations, were incinerated in a single, agonizing conflagration. I saw through my scrying matrix entire species screaming as their moments were devoured, their pasts and futures collapsing into a singularity of pure, searing chronological pain.*

*Godric, His blink completed, glanced at the chronal firestorm and sighed. “Ah, messy.” He then, without a thought, without a whisper of effort, instantly restored everything. The burned timelines were reknit, the erased civilizations reinstated, the temporal fires extinguished. He did not restore the Legion. He left them as a permanent lacuna, a demonstration of what happens when you pit velocity against something that already finished before the start.*

*I am Iskar. I write this because I must. But I cannot remember the name of the enemy. His speed blinked them out of existence so thoroughly that even my memory of them is fading. I only know they existed because these words prove it. Godric later found me huddled in a corner, and He asked, with genuine, bewildered concern, why I was shaking. “It was merely a blink, my boy,” He said, patting my shoulder. “I barely moved at all.” I laughed, and the laugh turned into a sob. He still does not understand.*

I pressed my palms against my eyes, trying to stem the dizziness. A blink. The same gesture I had seen Godric make a thousand times when he was reading a book or watching a sunset. Every casual motion of his body was a latent apocalypse. I reached for the fourth journal with a sense of inevitability, my dread now a cold, permanent resident in my chest.

**Journal of High Court Wizard Bram — Year 10,000 — The Incident of Absolute Luck**

This journal was written in a deceptively casual tone, rife with dark humor and almost jovial sarcasm. The margins, however, were filled with tiny, frantic scribbles that told a different story entirely.

*Alright, listen up, future wizard. I’m Bram. Fifth court wizard, tenth millennium, and I’m writing this because my predecessor, the poor sod who still smells screaming in his tea, told me it’s mandatory. Today’s topic: luck. Pure, dumb, physics-defying, reality-warping luck. The kind of luck that makes you suspect the omniverse itself is a cosmic stagehand and we’re all just props in a play starring one man.*

*The Hiss’tek Dominion was a mechanical hivemind that had eradicated randomness from their entire civilization. They operated on absolute determinism, modeling every possible variable to a certainty of one hundred percent. They sent us a fifty-thousand-page document mathematically proving why their invasion would succeed. Probability of failure: zero. They were smug, logical, and insufferably correct—on paper.*

*Godric was relaxing in the royal garden, sipping lemonade. He read the first page, grinned, and handed it to me. “Bram, they’ve forgotten to account for one variable. My luck stat. I’ve never really isolated it to see what happens. I think today’s the day.” He then leaned back in his wicker chair, folded His hands over His stomach, and did precisely nothing. Not a gesture, not a word, not a glance. He just sat there, smiling at the clouds, while I stood by, confused and increasingly nervous.*

*The Hiss’tek armada, numbering trillions of perfect, invincible warships, commenced its attack. And then, what transpired was a cascade of absurd, impossible, humiliating accidents that I still have trouble believing. First, a microscopic grain of exomatter, drifting in the interstellar void, collided with the fleet’s forward flagship. That grain, composed of a rare isotope found only in one specific nebula ten billion light-years away, somehow passed directly through the ship’s impervious shields—a one-in-an-infinity chance—and struck the central command core, causing a feedback loop that disabled the entire armada’s synchronization matrix. No explosion, just a quiet, polite shutdown of all defensive systems. The Hiss’tek, being logical, switched to backups, but the momentary delay caused their chrono-synchronized weapons to fire exactly one minuscule fraction of a Planck second out of phase. The resulting temporal dissonance reflected their own world-ending blasts back onto themselves. They meticulously, efficiently bombarded themselves into scrap.*

*Then, their supreme commander, a continent-sized logic engine, spontaneously experienced a critical failure because a stray gamma ray from a dying star, emitted seventeen billion years ago and traveling across the universe, struck a single specific transistor in its infinite array. That transistor jammed, causing the commander to issue an emergency override ordering all ships to retreat into a nearby “safe” spatial anomaly. That anomaly, by sheer coincidence, had just recently collapsed into a supermassive black hole. The entire fleet obediently dove headlong into the event horizon, where they were stretched into atomic pasta and slowly, agonizingly torn apart across fifty thousand years of relativistic time dilation.*

*And the final, crowning insult: the Hiss’tek homeworld, located in a completely separate multiverse, was annihilated by a sudden vacuum decay event triggered by a parallel version of their own supreme commander who, in a fit of despair over a minor bureaucratic error, turned on a doomsday device. The weapon misfired across dimensional barriers—an impossibility—and erased the prime Hiss’tek universe, retroactively deleting the entire invasion’s origin point. The armada, having no cause to exist, simply winked out of reality, their pseudo-destiny overwritten by the sheer, monumental weight of Godric’s passive, uninterested luck.*

*I watched all of this happen while Godric took a nap. He woke up, yawned, and asked, “Did anything happen?” I described the absurd chain of events, and He chuckled warmly. “See, Bram? Luck. It’s not about probability. Probability is a polite suggestion that my stat simply ignores.” I nodded, managed a trembling smile, and excused myself to go scream into a pillow. I think the worst part is realizing that the omniverse is so pathetically eager to please Him that it will contort the laws of physics into a pretzel to ensure His afternoon isn’t interrupted by a loud noise. The Hiss’tek never even got close. They were destroyed by a cosmic joke, and the punchline was our King’s gentle snore. I’m going to drink until I forget the sound of a trillion machine-minds realizing they lost to a piece of dirt.*

I set down the journal, a mirthless, hollow laugh escaping my lips. I had always thought myself unlucky in board games when playing against Uncle Godric as a child. Now I wondered if my losses were simply the omniverse avoiding the risk of disappointing him. The absurdity of it was almost soothing, a brief respite from the crushing scale of the other journals. But beneath the dark humor, the dread only deepened, because it meant there was no fortress secure enough, no plan perfect enough, no probability absolute enough to resist even his passive, unconscious favor.

**Journal of High Court Wizard Darian — Year 800,000 — The Incident of Absolute Wisdom**

The final journal in the stack was the most elegantly written. The hand was calligraphic, the prose measured and philosophical. The writer seemed to have achieved a state of grim, resigned enlightenment, and his words carried the heavy, sorrowful calm of a man who had accepted the unacceptable.

*I am Darian, fifty-seventh court wizard of the bloodline. I have witnessed many demonstrations of the King’s attributes, but none so subtle, so utterly devastating, as the one I record here. This is the account of the Syllogistic Collective, and the absolute wisdom that unmade them without a single motion from the throne.*

*The Syllogistic Collective were not warriors in any physical sense. They were a hive-mind of the greatest philosophers, logicians, and strategic intellects ever to exist in any cosmos. They had spent eons distilling the essence of all possible arguments into a single weapon: the Absolute Antinomy, a self-referential logical paradox so perfectly constructed that to either accept or reject it would force any consciousness into an infinite, identity-shattering recursion. They believed that if they could present this paradox to Godric, His divine omniscience would be trapped in an irresolvable loop, effectively neutralizing Him without a single act of violence. Their arrogance was a thing of tragic beauty.*

*Godric, of course, knew of their plan before the first thought of it had crystallized. He is omniscient. He sat in His study, reading an old, worn novel from the Olympus era, when the news of the incoming paradox was brought to Him by my own trembling hand. He listened, nodded, and then said something I will never forget: “Darian, wisdom is not about being the cleverest person in the room. It’s about knowing when the room itself is unnecessary. I will test my wisdom today by doing absolutely nothing. Watch.” He then returned to His novel, turning the pages with the gentle, unhurried pace of a retired scholar.*

*He kept His word. He offered no counter-argument, no refutation, no analysis. He ignored the paradox entirely. The paradox, a shimmering torrent of pure conceptual energy, arrived at the Thronegate boundary and found… nothing. No divine intellect to ensnare, no metaphysical wall to crash against. It was a weapon fired into an infinite void. The lack of engagement was not a weakness; it was a devastating masterstroke. The paradox, being a self-referential entity, was forced by its own internal logic to comprehend its failure. Why did the target not respond? In its attempt to analyze the absence, it turned its perfect, inescapable recursion upon itself.*

*The feedback loop propagated instantly back to the Syllogistic Collective’s collective consciousness. Their minds, linked across dimensions, were seized by the very weapon they had designed. The unanswerable question became the sole subject of their awareness. They began to argue. The argument did not remain philosophical; it physically manifested within their realm. Words became bladed concepts that flayed the substance of their reality. Theories became gravitational singularities that tore their cities apart. Their entire civilization devolved into a frenzy of intellectual civil war, every member trying to solve the unsolvable, their minds shattering and their bodies dissolving as the abstract logic ate them from the inside out. I watched as an entire dimension of profound thinkers consumed itself in a paroxysm of self-inflicted logical damnation.*

*The entire process, in our frame of reference, lasted less than a second. Within their own, it was a subjective eternity of screaming, writhing, intellectual agony. Godric turned a page of His novel. “Wisdom,” He said softly, not looking up, “is understanding that the most devastating action is often perfect inaction. They came with a question; I gave them silence. The silence answered them more completely than any thunder ever could.” He glanced at me then, His eyes filled with a gentle, almost pitying light. There was no cruelty in His expression. Only the infinite, patient knowing of a being who has seen all outcomes and waited for the inevitable. I have never been more terrified than in that moment of profound, paternal calm.*

I closed the final journal. The archive around me was utterly silent, yet my head roared with the phantom screams of annihilated civilizations. I understood now. The opposition described in these pages had not merely been defeated; their very premise was invalidated before they began. Godric’s power was not a contestable quantity. It was a condition of reality, as fundamental and unalterable as the existence of existence itself.

The door swung open, and my father, Marius, re-entered. He looked at my face—pale, stricken, my eyes wide with the thousand-yard stare that I now recognized in him—and he simply nodded. “You have read them, then. Good. Now you know.”

But before I could respond, another set of footsteps echoed in the corridor, and King Godric himself walked into the archive. He was wearing a comfortable wool cardigan, his salt-and-pepper hair slightly tousled, as if he had just risen from a nap. His ruggedly handsome face split into a warm, genuine smile. “Ah, Kaedan! I saw Marius had you cooped up in here with those dusty old books. Don’t let them spook you, my boy. Those old wizards were always a theatrical bunch. I keep telling Marius you don’t need to read all the tragic poetry they wrote.” He waved a dismissive, good-natured hand. “They always dramatized everything. I barely did anything in half those incidents. Just a sniff, a blink, a pinch—honestly, I was practically stationary. Yet they acted as if the sky was falling.”

He walked over and placed a warm, heavy hand on my shoulder, his touch as grounding and reassuring as it had been when I was a child. “Come. Walk with me. Tell me about your new quarters. Have the servants given you enough blankets? It gets cold in the east wing at night, and I won’t have my new court wizard shivering while he studies.”

I looked up at the face of the man who had inhaled multiverses, who had blinked timelines out of existence, who had allowed an entire civilization to unravel through the mere radiance of his passive luck and wisdom. I searched for the monster the journals described, and I found only the kind, loving uncle who had always been there for me. The cognitive dissonance was a physical constriction around my heart. I loved him. I revered him. And I was now, profoundly and permanently, terrified of him—terrified not of malice, but of the sheer, unbridgeable distance between his casual whim and the annihilation of everything else.

I swallowed, forced a weak smile, and rose. “Thank you, Your Majesty. I’d like that.” He clapped my back and began strolling out, already recounting a fond story about my father’s first days as court wizard. I followed, the dread a cold, silent companion in my chest, knowing with absolute certainty that I would serve this king loyally, faithfully, and with all the love I had, for he deserved nothing less and permitted nothing less. But I would also, in time, write my own journal entry. And I understood, now, why every wizard before me had done the same. It was the only way to stay sane in the shadow of a god who found the apocalypse boring.

Opposing Godric was not impossible in practice. It was impossible in principle, a category error as fundamental as trying to divide by zero or arguing with gravity. Victory against him was never an option; it had been forbidden since before the first quark formed, and it would remain forbidden long after the last star guttered out. And yet, walking beside him, listening to his warm, cheerful voice, I could almost forget that truth. Almost.
