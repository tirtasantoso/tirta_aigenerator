He turned to me, and his grey eyes were still kind, still crinkled, still the eyes of the uncle who had taught me to skip stones. "You did a fine job holding it back, Gorm. Give yourself another few decades of amplification and you could handle something that size without breaking a sweat. I'm very proud of you."

I dropped my staff. It clattered on the stone floor, the crystal at its tip flickering and going dark. My legs gave way, and I sat down hard, my back against the cold wall. I was not looking at the Benevolent Spell, which was now emitting soft, grateful pulses of golden light like a purring cat made of purified arcana. I was looking at my uncle.

Because I had felt it. He had let me feel it. Not the full scope—my mind would have shattered—but just enough. Just the faintest, most distant, most infinitesimal echo of his true magical capacity. And that echo was a horror so vast that I still cannot find language for it. What I had always perceived as my own magical potency, the thousandfold-amplified bloodline of Blake, the talents that had made my instructors weep—it was nothing. Less than nothing. A single flickering candle held up against a light that contained every star that had ever burned and ever would burn, multiplied by every Planck second of every universe in every multiverse, squared against itself, recursively, forever. Godric's magical traits—his mana, his spellcraft, his arcane offense and defense, his conceptual magic, his anti-magic, his comprehension—had been multiplying by 1,000 and by themselves every Planck second for eighty thousand years, and that original rate had long since been eclipsed by the Weakness Paradox into an escalation beyond layered infinities. The fraction of his power that he had just used to unmake and remake a twelve-billion-year-old Sentient Spell was, in magical terms, effectively zero percent of his total capacity. It was the arcane equivalent of the warmth left by a body on a chair cushion. And that zero percent, that exhaust fume of his true might, could—and I knew this with the awful, bone-deep certainty of someone who had just seen the truth—erase the entire omniverse. Not just once. Infinite times over. Instantly. Effortlessly. Without him needing to do more than idly wonder what it might look like.

The math was sickening. If you took my total magical output, amplified daily by a thousandfold for twenty years, and you compared it to Godric's total magical output, the ratio was not a fraction. It was not a decimal. It was a zero followed by a decimal point followed by so many zeroes that writing them out would require more matter than the omniverse contains. And that ratio would be smaller tomorrow. And smaller the next day. And it would shrink forever, because my amplification was finite and his was infinite and endlessly accelerating.

I had thought I was his protector. I had thought he needed me. My wards, my spells, my clever recursive traps—they were not defenses. They were a child's crayon drawing, stuck to the refrigerator by a father who was too kind to point out that it would not stop a draft, let alone a threat. The king did not keep court wizards because he lacked magical aptitude. He lacked nothing. He was the source, the apex, the unassailable summit upon which every conceivable arcane principle shattered. He kept court wizards because he loved them. Because we were his friends, his family, his connection to the mortal identity he had been so long ago. Our magic was utterly, laughably useless to him. And he let us believe otherwise because he was kind, and because our pride amused him, and because a world in which we had purpose was a world he preferred to inhabit.

"Gorm?" Uncle Godric was kneeling beside me now, his hand on my shoulder, his brow furrowed with concern. "Are you alright? I'm sorry, I should have warned you. The glimpse can be a bit much the first time." He offered me his teacup. "Here. Small sips. The spice settles the nerves."

I took the teacup because my body was running on instinct. The ceramic was warm. The tea tasted of cinnamon and something else, something that did indeed settle my nerves, though no chemical compound in existence could have settled the existential vertigo spinning behind my eyes. I looked up at him—at the handsome, rugged face, the neatly trimmed salt-and-pepper beard, the grey eyes that had watched eighty millennia pass and still found room for warmth—and I felt the love I had always felt, and beneath it, a terror so profound it had become geology, a continental shelf of dread that I would carry with me forever.

"You didn't need me," I whispered. "All this time. You never needed me."

His face softened with something that was almost sorrow. "I needed you to be here, Gorm. That's not the same as needing your magic, but it's not less. It's more." He squeezed my shoulder. "Your ancestor Blake couldn't cast a basic ward when I first met him. He was a merchant. He couldn't fight, couldn't shield, couldn't heal. But he was my best friend, and I needed him more than I have ever needed any arcane protection. You're his blood. That's why you're here. Not because I need your spells. Because I need you."

It should have been comforting. In a way, it was. And in another way, it was the most terrifying thing he could have said, because it meant that his attachment to me, his love, his warmth—all of it was real, all of it was genuine, and all of it was the only thing standing between me and the reality that I was an insect standing on the palm of a being who could close his fist and erase the concept of my species without registering the effort.

I finished the tea. The Benevolent Spell pulsed gently in the background, its golden light now permanently woven into the weave of the Hall of Infinite Reflection. I would see that light every day for the rest of my life, and I would remember this moment: the instant when I learned that my magic was zero, and that zero was generous, and that the king I loved was kind enough to let me pretend otherwise.

Later that evening, I stood in my father's study and told him what I had seen. He listened without interruption, his face etched with the same tired, familiar dread that I now understood. When I finished, he poured us both a measure of eighty-thousand-year-old brandy from a bottle that had been passed down through our family since the time of Blake himself.

"Every court wizard learns this," he said quietly. "We don't talk about it, but we all learn it. The gap is so big that talking about it feels like trying to describe the size of the universe by drawing a circle in the sand. You just… adjust. You accept that your role is not protection. It's companionship. Witness. You stand beside him, and you love him, and you serve him, and you never, ever allow yourself to believe that you can oppose him, because you can't. No one can. The very idea of opposing Godric is a cosmic joke whose punchline is agony."

He swirled his brandy, watching the amber liquid catch the firelight. "There was a man once, long before my time. A delegate who thought he could fool the king with a trick contract. Godric turned him into the ambient light of the audience chamber. The man is still there, still conscious, still feeling every photon of himself warm the stones. The servants call it the Golden Glow. They say it's lovely during sunset."

I stared at him. My brandy sat untouched. "Does it get easier? Accepting it?"

My father met my eyes, and for the first time, I saw the full weight of the generations in his gaze—the accumulated dread of eighty millennia, passed down like a genetic curse. "No," he said. "It never gets easier. But it becomes… familiar. And you learn to love him anyway, because he is genuinely lovable, and because the alternative—resenting a being who could unmake you without noticing—is a form of insanity."

I am twenty years old. I have been the court wizard of Thronegate for a few months. I still call him Uncle Godric, and I still bring him spiced tea, and I still laugh at his dry jokes, and I still feel his hand on my shoulder with the warmth of a father who has outlived everyone he ever loved and still finds room to love more. But I no longer believe I am his protector. I know the truth now: that an infinitesimal fraction of his magic, practically zero percent of his total power, could erase the entire omniverse infinite times over without requiring his conscious participation. That his arcane mastery is not mastery in the sense of skill but in the sense of ownership—reality belongs to him, and magic is just reality's way of begging for his approval. That my own considerable talents, amplified daily by a thousandfold, are not a contribution to his safety but a performance he watches with the gentle, patient indulgence of a father whose child is showing him a crayon drawing of a dragon.

I will serve him until I die. I will love him. I will be the best court wizard I can be, not because my magic matters to his survival, but because my presence matters to his heart. And I will carry this dread, this understanding of the true, astronomical, impossible gulf between us, as a quiet companion that walks beside me every step of my life.

Because that is the truth of Godric. He is impossible to oppose. Opposing him does not lead to defeat; it leads to an unmaking so total that your name becomes a silence, your legacy becomes a gap in memory, and your existence is either erased or repurposed into something that serves his gentle, terrible, eternal affection. The delegate who tried to fool him is light. The Sentient Spell that tried to consume him is a wellspring of healing. And I, Gorm, descendant of Blake, court wizard to the apex of all existence, am a young man who will spend the rest of his life knowing that the uncle who taught him to skip stones could, with the slightest involuntary twitch of his magical attention, erase the entire omniverse and bring it back in the span of zero seconds without his tea going cold.

My tea is still warm. His is, too. And out in the Hall of Infinite Reflection, the golden light of a reformed predator pulses softly, a perpetual, grateful hymn to the mercy of the king who unmade its nature and remade it better with less effort than I expend drawing breath.

I am proud to serve him. I am terrified to serve him. These two things are not contradictions. They are the twin pillars of the only reality that matters.
