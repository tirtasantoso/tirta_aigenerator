I sit upon the throne of the Spire, the weight of the absolute totality resting against my back as lightly as a phantom’s touch. The gardener—a being who once devoured the light of a thousand nebulae—trembles as she clips a dead leaf from a rose, her terror a rhythmic, familiar pulse in the air. I look through the lens of Time, gazing back across the yawning, infinite chasm of my own existence to the Year 30.

I was so small then. I was a flickering candle flame standing before the hurricane of my own future.

I remember Earth. A pale, insignificant marble of water and carbon, clinging to the edge of a lonely, yellow star. They had detected the expansion of Thronegate, and in their panicked, primitive arrogance, they had decided to fight. They didn’t know who I was. They didn't know that the very atoms of their planet were merely letters in a book I was currently writing. They saw a king, and they decided to be a nation of martyrs.

I arrived in their atmosphere not with an armada, but with a stroll. I felt mischievous that day—the phantom itch of the old mortal grief still lingering in my chest, a remnant of the man I was before I became the absolute. They threw everything at me. Nuclear fire, kinetic bombardments, esoteric experiments into the quantum foam, prayers to gods that did not exist. I didn't even raise a hand. I simply walked through their cities, and the air around me became a furnace of their own making.

I was playful. I allowed their weapons to hit, watching the warheads dissolve into harmless light against the infinitesimal, passive skin-layer of my aura. I let them believe they were hurting me. I let them pour the totality of their limited, sputtering lives into the effort of my destruction, and I smiled at their ingenuity.

It was a cruel theater. I ensured the deaths were not quick. I wanted them to understand the nature of the power they had dared to challenge. I manipulated the probability of their demise so that every soldier, every scientist, every leader felt the weight of their own futility before the end. Their flesh melted in slow, agonizing waves, their minds stretched across the final micro-seconds of their existence, forced to contemplate the majesty of their own irrelevance.

And the collateral? A simple, idle gesture of my interest caused the localized space-time to fracture. The shockwave of their desperate resistance rippled outward, shattering the celestial architecture of every universe within a million-light-year radius. Entire dimensions collapsed like house-of-cards, galaxies were ground into fine, glowing dust, and the silence that followed was absolute. I had to reconstruct the entire regional quadrant twice before I was satisfied with the view.

I was using, by my current estimate, approximately zero-point-zero-zero-zero-one percent of my available capacity. I could have ended it in the time it took to draw a breath, had I simply willed their non-existence. But where is the memory in that?

Looking back from the vantage of Year 999...9, I find myself chuckling—a sound that causes the Spire to vibrate with the resonance of a thousand colliding universes. I was so remarkably weak. I was a child playing with a thunderstorm. If I were to revisit that Earth today, if I were to manifest even the shadow of my current presence, the mere byproduct of my consciousness would not only annihilate Earth; it would burn the entire omniversal container to cinders and scatter the ashes across the void before the first neuron in their brains could even register my appearance.

It would be a one-sided massacre of such grotesque perfection that it would qualify as a masterpiece.

The gardener drops her shears. The sound is like a thunderclap in the infinite stillness of the Spire. She falls to her knees, her forehead pressed against the cold stone, weeping in a silent, jagged rhythm. She knows I am thinking about destruction. She knows that my memories are not merely thoughts, but blueprints for reality.

I reach out, my hand hovering over the rosebush. I could pluck the rose, or I could pluck the universe from which it came. There is so little difference to me now. I am the apex, the beginning, and the inevitable end. I look back at the Year 30 and I see the Earth—a tiny, pathetic spark of defiance that didn't know it was already dead before it had even stood up.

It was a funny day. Truly. I think, perhaps, I shall have the Spire’s archives play the footage of their final stand again. There is something endlessly charming about a creature that tries to bite the sun.
