I flicked my wrist, and the air between the moon and Earth evaporated, replaced by a vacuum field that amplified the light of the sun into a magnifying glass. I didn't want to end it yet. I wanted to see them dance. 

"They’re so very loud, aren't they, Clive?" I asked, my voice a soft, conversational rumble that didn't need atmosphere to carry. I felt my court wizard shivering behind me, his terror a delicious, spicy tang in the air. 

"They are... trying, My King," Clive whispered, his eyes fixed on the spectacle.

I chuckled. It was a good joke, though I knew none of them would live long enough to appreciate the irony. I reached down with my consciousness, not to strike, but to *touch*. I found their most heavily fortified bunker, the seat of their global military command. I didn't need to break the doors; I simply rewrote the atomic frequency of the steel so that it turned into boiling, liquid nerve endings. The screams were instantaneous, a beautiful, high-pitched chorus of realization as the men inside felt their own flesh fusing with the walls of their command center.

They fought back, of course. They unleashed a 'Doomsday Protocol,' firing a barrage of gravity-bombs that could have shattered a red dwarf. The missiles traveled toward me, glowing with the heat of dying dimensions. I didn't shield myself. I simply watched them arrive. When they hit my skin, they didn't explode. They blossomed. The gravity within the warheads was inverted, turning the projectiles into crushing, self-contained singularities that folded the missiles inward, spiraling the metal and the scientists who had launched them into a microscopic point of infinite pressure. They existed for a Planck second as a screaming, dense mass of compressed hope, before winking out.

I felt a surge of mischievous, playful energy. I decided to make the end a performance. 

I reached into the tectonic plates of the planet and twisted. The Earth didn't crack; it began to *sigh*. I forced the atmosphere to change, replacing the oxygen with a volatile, hallucinogenic gas that made every living human perceive their own skin as a separate, hostile entity. I sat on my throne of lunar dust, watching through the global camera feeds I had hijacked. The panic was sublime. People were tearing at their own bodies, their minds fractured by the impossible sensation of their own touch. It was messy, cruel, and entirely unnecessary, but I found myself grinning.

"Look at that," I noted, pointing to a city where the inhabitants had started to worship the sky in a frenzy of self-mutilation. "They still think there’s a prayer to be said."

A coalition of their finest minds, connected via a massive, planet-wide neural network, managed to formulate a 'weapon'—a concept-engine meant to unravel my ego. They spent their final, agonizing hours pouring every ounce of their cultural, linguistic, and scientific history into a single spear of logic. I let them finish it. I let them fire it. I wanted to see if they could even tickle my curiosity. 

The spear hit my chest. It felt like a grain of sand hitting a mountain. I reached out and caught the concept, turning the weapon into a physical bird made of broken laws of physics. It shrieked, a sound of absolute, mechanical betrayal, and flew back down to Earth, burrowing into the neural network's hub and turning every single person connected to it into a living, screaming piece of coral. Their flesh hardened, turning into jagged, calcified, rainbow-colored formations that continued to pulse with the rhythmic agony of their former consciousness.

"Are you bored yet, Clive?" I asked, my grin widening.

"I am... quite terrified, My King," he replied, his voice barely holding together.

"Good. Fear is the spice of life."

I didn't stop the slaughter. I made it last. I spent what felt like an eternity, though it was but a heartbeat in the grander timeline, ensuring that every death was uniquely, grotesquely tailored. I turned their oceans into acid that slowly dissolved them from the feet up. I made their shadows turn into physical hooks that dragged them into the depths of their own nightmares. I watched them scream, I watched them hope, and I watched them break. 

Earth had zero chance. They never had a chance. The very idea that they could 'prepare' was the funniest part. They were ants building a fortress of mud against a flood that was already in their cells. 

When the last human finally died, a broken, calcified mess of terror, I stood up and stretched. The planet was a silent, ruined, coral-encrusted tomb. It was perfect. I hadn't used even a fraction of a percent of my strength; I hadn't even exerted enough effort to break a sweat. It was just a game. 

"Well," I said, patting Clive on the shoulder. "That was a lovely excursion. Shall we go home? I believe I left a universe of toys in the neighboring sector, and I really do hate to leave them unsupervised." 

I didn't bother to restore the Earth. There was no point. It was a finished story, and I had already enjoyed the punchline.
