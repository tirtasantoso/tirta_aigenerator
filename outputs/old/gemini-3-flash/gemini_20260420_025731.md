The white marble of the Olympian high temple smelled of rosemary and expensive incense, a scent that usually brought Godric a sense of profound peace. At forty-five, the King of Olympus stood as a pillar of masculine vitality, his salt-and-pepper beard neatly groomed against a jawline that seemed carved from the very mountain. His muscular frame, honed by decades of leading from the front, filled his ceremonial silks with a heavy, reassuring presence. Beside him, his daughter Isabel glowed in her bridal lace, her twenty-two years of life culminating in this moment of radiant joy. Godric felt a fatherly warmth so thick it nearly brought him to tears as he prepared to join her hand with her betrothed. His younger daughter, Hebe, was giggling in the front row, while his wife Reina watched with eyes full of the quiet wisdom that had guided his reign. It was the last moment of peace Godric would ever know.

The sky did not darken; it shattered. The neighboring kingdom of Thronegate, driven by a sudden, rapacious hunger for Olympian resources, bypassed every border defense through treachery. Explosive shells fueled by dark alchemy tore through the temple roof, turning the white marble into a red-slaughterhouse. Godric watched, frozen by the sheer impossibility of the moment, as a jagged shard of stone ended Isabel’s life mid-smile. Reina and Hebe were caught in the secondary blast of a fire-spell that turned the sanctuary into a furnace. Within seconds, the fatherly king was a man standing in the ashes of his soul. His best friend, the court wizard Thorin, whose lineage had served the Olympian crown since the first fires were lit, lunged through the smoke. Thorin’s face was a mask of grief and desperation as he began chanting the forbidden syllables of a bloodline teleportation spell, intended to whisk the King to a hidden sanctuary. But as the air began to ripple, a Thronegate assassin’s blade found Thorin’s throat. The wizard died mid-incantation, his life’s blood staining the unfinished runes.

The botched spell did not move Godric through space so much as it moved him out of reality. He tumbled into a void beneath the sanctuary, falling into a stasis that was never meant to be. The moment his body hit the ancient stone floor of the hidden chamber, the laws of the universe fractured around him. His aging ceased instantly, locking his physical form into its peak masculine prime forever. The biological imperatives of mortal life—the need for oxygen, the craving for sustenance, the requirement of sleep—simply vanished, replaced by a cold, eternal stillness. But far more terrifying was the amplification. The broken magic of Thorin’s sacrifice, caught in a feedback loop with the ancient sanctuary’s ley lines, began to feed Godric’s very essence. Every second that ticked by in the outside world saw his strength, his intelligence, and his every sensory perception multiply by a million, and then by itself. It was a geometric progression into godhood that defied the comprehension of any mathematician.

While his body lay in silent stasis, Godric’s mind became an expanding sun. Every passing second birthed a new, apocalyptic superpower within him—abilities capable of erasing entire civilizations with a mere thought. One second he gained the power to manipulate the strong nuclear force; the next, he could rewrite the history of any soul he perceived. Yet, the first of these powers was a perfect, absolute concealment. It acted as a cosmic dampener, folding his ever-growing, terrifying presence into a pocket of nothingness so that the omniverse would not spontaneously collapse under the weight of his existence. This concealment grew just as fast as his power, a flawless shield of normalcy that hid a monster of infinite proportions.

The universe itself seemed to enforce a paradox regarding his existence: Godric had no weaknesses. This was not a boast, but a fundamental law of his new being. Somewhere in the deep mechanics of the spell, a defense mechanism had formed. If any entity in the vastness of the omniverse even harbored a stray, subconscious thought that Godric might possess a flaw—a way to be hurt, a conceptual opening to be exploited—that thought acted as a catalyst. The moment the thought formed, Godric’s rate of amplification would spike violently in response to the perceived insult. Before the entity could even formulate a plan, Godric would automatically become immune to the imagined exploit, and the mastery of that very method would be granted to him perfectly. He could then deny the very concept of that attack to the entity, erasing their ability to even comprehend the weapon they had hoped to use. The attacker would be defeated before they had even finished their first breath of malice.

Ten thousand years passed like a single evening in the dark. Above the buried sanctuary, the world changed. Olympus was a forgotten name, buried under the shifting crust of the earth. Thronegate had grown from a marauding neighbor into a galactic empire, a civilization of cold steel and high magic that dominated every star system in reach. They were the undisputed masters of the galaxy, unaware that the ghost of their greatest sin slept beneath them.

Blake had a peculiar habit of finding things—old coins, lost paths, forgotten truths. It was a knack that had led him to a strange, vine-choked ruin in a sector of the world that even the Thronegate scouts avoided. The stone slid back, releasing a hiss of ancient, pressurized air, and Godric stepped out of the dark.

Godric was no longer the man who had officiated a wedding. His eyes held the weight of a billion suns, and his presence, though perfectly concealed, felt like a physical pressure against the back of Blake’s skull. Through his absolute omniscience, Godric instantly knew every atom of the current world. He saw the descendants of the men who had slaughtered his family sitting on thrones of glass. Godric felt a flicker of his old warmth for this man who had inadvertently freed him, but for the rest of the world, there was only a cold, calculated judgment.

Within an hour of his awakening, Godric’s voice resonated in the mind of every living being in the Thronegate Empire. He issued a single, terrifying challenge to King Robert, the twenty-two-year-old sovereign of the galaxy. Robert was given three days to abdicate or face the consequences. The young king, surrounded by his generals and his "invincible" armies, merely laughed. Men tore their own eyes out rather than look upon the still, handsome figure in the center of the carnage. Two days later, King Robert was found in his private chambers, having ended his own life with a ceremonial dagger, unable to bear the weight of the silence Godric had imposed on his empire.

Godric ascended the throne, but he did not come to destroy the people; he came to reshape them. He enacted the Decree of Awakening. Every man of Thronegate, upon reaching the age of twenty, would find his strength, intelligence, and wisdom multiplied tenfold every single day. For the older men, this change was retroactive, a sudden surge of godlike capability that flooded through their veins. To Blake, Godric gave a different gift, a silent acknowledgment of the debt he owed a dead wizard. Blake’s multiplier was not ten, but a thousand. The merchant felt his mind expand into the heavens, yet he remained the same honest man, his mutton chops graying as he looked upon his king with a mixture of awe and brotherhood.

The expansion of Thronegate began not with fire, but with an offer. Godric sent envoys to the neighboring universes, offering them a place in his new order. When the Universe of the Iron Hegemony refused, Godric did not send an army. He merely felt a moment of slight irritation while taking his morning tea with Blake. In less than a billionth of a second, the Iron Hegemony ceased to exist as a sovereign entity; its stars were rearranged into the sigil of Thronegate, and its leaders were transformed into pillars of salt. Godric hadn't even looked toward their coordinates.

For the Universe of the Clockwork Sages, Godric sent five Thronegate men, all over the age of thirty-five. These were men who had been simple farmers and craftsmen before the Awakening. They arrived in the Clockwork realm with expressions of sincere regret. The Sages had spent five years preparing for this invasion, building machines that could drain the energy from entire galaxies. The five men walked through the "God-Killer" beams as if they were a light summer rain. With gentle hands and tearful eyes, they dismantled the Sages' ultimate weapons in twenty minutes, apologizing for the necessity of the conquest as they collapsed the defenders’ reality into a vassal state. The horror the Sages felt was not from the violence, but from the realization that their five years of preparation were less than a distraction to these middle-aged men who just wanted to go home to their wives.

The true futility of resistance was demonstrated by the Entity of the Eleventh Nexus. This being, a hive-mind of pure thought, believed it had found the flaw Godric’s omnipotence hid. It surmised that Godric’s power was tied to the physical laws of the third dimension. The moment this thought cohered within the hive-mind, the Nexus felt a sudden, sickening shift in the cosmos. Godric’s presence, already infinite, surged with a new, dark energy. The paradox triggered. Godric didn't just become immune to the Nexus’s conceptual attacks; he gained a mastery over the eleventh dimension that made the hive-mind look like a child playing with blocks. Godric reached across the dimensions and pinched the hive-mind’s existence, turning its vast intelligence into a single, localized point of eternal, conscious suffering. He then erased the very concept of "thought-based warfare" from the Nexus’s reality, leaving the survivors as mindless husks who could do nothing but worship the shadow of his passing.

As the centuries rolled on, the cold cruelty that had defined Godric upon his awakening began to thaw. It was Blake’s influence—the merchant’s steady, humble loyalty—that brought the King back to himself. They would sit in the high gardens of the new Olympus, Godric looking once again like the warm, fatherly ruler he had been ten millennia ago. He was kind to his subjects, a father to his people, and a protector of the weak. But to those who threatened the peace of his omniverse, he remained a nightmare beyond reckoning. 

Blake eventually traded his merchant scales for the robes of a court wizard, his natural affinity for the ancient arts finally finding its purpose. His son Josh stood by his side, learning the runes that Thorin had once died to protect. In the grand throne room of Thronegate, where the light of a million conquered suns filtered through stained glass, Godric sat with a contented smile. Beside him stood Blake, his best friend and most trusted advisor. The King of the Omniverse leaned back, his salt-and-pepper beard catching the light, looking every bit the handsome, muscular sovereign of a golden age. He was a god, yes, and a monster to his enemies, but as he shared a quiet joke with the man who had found him in the dark, he was, at last, a man who was home. Under his absolute rule, the omniverse didn't just survive; it thrived in a terrifying, beautiful peace that would never, ever end.
