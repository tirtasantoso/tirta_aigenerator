The white marble of Olympus did not merely shine; it glowed with the inner light of a thousand years of peace, a testament to a kingdom that had forgotten the flavor of iron and the scent of burning thatch. King Godric stood upon the Great Dais, his presence as foundational as the stone beneath his feet. At forty-five, he was the archetype of masculine vitality, a man whose very silhouette commanded a room before a word ever crossed his lips. His build was dense with functional muscle, the kind forged in the sparring pits of his youth and maintained through the tireless labor of governance. A neatly trimmed salt-and-pepper beard framed a jawline that seemed carved from granite, and his short, dark hair, flecked with the same dignified silver, caught the morning sun. He was a man who laughed with his belly and led with his heart, currently radiating a warmth that could melt the most cynical of souls. Today, he was not just a king; he was a father, preparing to give his eldest daughter, Isabel, to a union that promised to bridge the last remaining gaps of his golden realm.

Isabel stood before him, twenty-two and radiant in silk that mimicked the shimmer of a dragonfly’s wing. Beside her, Hebe, only fifteen, giggled as she adjusted her sister’s veil, her youthful energy a bright spark in the solemnity of the temple. Reina, Godric’s queen, watched from the front row, her eyes wet with the kind of joy that only comes from a life well-lived and a family well-loved. Godric reached out, his large, calloused hand gently cupping Isabel’s cheek, his voice a low, fatherly rumble of absolute affection. He began the ancient rites, his heart swelling with a pride so profound it felt like a physical weight. He spoke of legacy, of love, and of the enduring light of Olympus, oblivious to the shadow that had already begun to fall across the horizon.

The first scream was not human; it was the sound of the sky tearing open. The kingdom of Thronegate, a neighbor that had long simmered in the bile of envy, did not declare war; they unleashed an apocalypse. Magicked projectiles, heavy with alchemical fire, slammed into the temple’s dome, raining jagged shards of sanctified glass upon the wedding party. The air, once sweet with jasmine, instantly curdled into the metallic tang of blood and the choking soot of incineration. Godric’s world shattered in the time it took to draw a single breath. He saw Reina struck down by a collapsing pillar, her grace extinguished in a heartbeat. He reached for his daughters, but the shockwave of a second blast threw him backward. Through the ringing in his ears and the stinging haze of dust, he watched in paralyzed horror as Thronegate assassins, clad in obsidian plate, swarmed the dais. He saw the cold flash of steel. The warmth that had defined Godric’s soul for forty-five years didn't just leave him; it was dragged out of him and replaced by a cold, abyssal vacuum.

Thorin, the court wizard and Godric’s closest confidant, scrambled through the carnage. His family had served the kings of Olympus since the first stone was laid, their bloodline a living library of arcane secrets. Thorin’s face was a mask of soot and tears as he grabbed Godric’s shoulder, dragging the catatonic king toward a hidden alcove. He knew the kingdom was lost; he knew the dynasty was ending. It was a spell designed to tuck a soul into the folds of reality, far from the reach of blades. But as the air began to hum with the violet static of the void, a Thronegate spear, launched with malicious precision, pierced Thorin’s chest. The wizard gasped, his blood spraying across the ancient runes he had traced in the air. The incantation broke, curling in on itself like a dying star. The spell didn't fail, but it didn't succeed; it fractured, dragging Godric into the rift even as Thorin’s lifeless body slumped to the floor.

The trauma of that moment—the sight of his wife’s broken form, his daughters’ slaughtered innocence, and his best friend’s sacrifice—reconditioned Godric’s psyche with the brutality of a smith’s hammer. The kind, fatherly king died in that rift. What remained was a vessel of pure, unadulterated coldness, a mind that had seen the end of all things and found only the desire for an eternal, ruthless retribution. As the incomplete spell deposited him into a lightless, subterranean sanctuary deep beneath the earth, Godric did not land; he suspended. He fell into a stasis that was less a sleep and more a fundamental halting of his mortal limitations.

At the exact moment the stasis took hold, the universe buckled under the weight of a phenomenon that defied every law of magic and physics. This was the Phenomenal Amplification, a glitch in the fabric of existence triggered by the shattered spell and Godric’s shattered soul. From that first Planck second of his suspension, Godric’s biological aging ceased entirely, rendering him a permanent monument of his forty-five-year-old prime. His need for air, for the sustenance of food, and for the respite of sleep vanished, erased from his being forever. But these were merely the precursors to the true horror. Every Planck second—the shortest unit of time possible—every single one of Godric’s attributes began to multiply by one thousand and then by itself. His strength, his speed, his endurance, his wisdom, his intelligence, and the acuity of his senses began an exponential climb into the infinite. If he was strong enough to lift a mountain in the first second, he was strong enough to crush a galaxy by the third.

He gained the ability to manipulate the fundamental forces of the cosmos, to unmake matter with a thought, to traverse dimensions as easily as a man walks across a room. And like his base stats, the potency of every single one of these burgeoning powers multiplied by a thousand and by itself every Planck second. The only reason the omniverse did not immediately collapse into a singularity under the sheer pressure of his presence was his first manifested power: Absolute Concealment. To the rest of the cosmos, Godric simply ceased to be, while inside the sanctuary, he was becoming a god of such magnitude that the word "god" became a laughable insult.

When the super-soldiers reached him, they didn't hit a man; they hit the fundamental wall of the universe. He didn't fight back in the traditional sense. He simply willed the atmosphere to thicken. To the Thronegate army, it felt like the air had turned to molten lead. Their lungs collapsed under the pressure. Their machines groaned as the metal began to fuse together. Godric glanced upward, and the dragons simply fell, their hearts stopping instantly as his aura, even suppressed, brushed against their primitive minds. The morale of the survivors didn't just break; it vanished, replaced by an existential horror so deep that many tore their own eyes out rather than look upon the man in the center of the field.

He had taken his own life with a ceremonial dagger, his last act one of cowardice in the face of an infinite truth. Godric walked into the capital of Thronegate, his footsteps echoing through a silent city. He didn't need to fight for the throne. He simply sat upon it, and the world bowed.

Immediately following his coronation, Godric enacted the Decree of Awakening. It was a gift and a brand. Every man of Thronegate—strictly the men—who reached the age of twenty would find their strength, intelligence, and wisdom multiplied tenfold for every single day they lived past that anniversary. For those already older, the power hit them like a tidal wave, their bodies and minds expanding retroactively. A forty-year-old man, having lived 7,300 days past his twentieth year, suddenly found himself with a power level increased by ten to the power of 7,300. They became living gods, capable of shattering moons with a casual gesture, yet they remained mortal, bound to the human lifespan of eighty years. They could suppress this power at will, living normal lives, but the potential for absolute destruction lived within every citizen.

Blake, however, received a different touch. As he stood beside the throne, Godric placed a hand on his shoulder. Blake didn't feel a tenfold increase; he felt a thousandfold multiplication every day. He remained a man of flesh and blood, short-tempered and grounded, but he became the anchor of the new empire. His mind became a repository of wisdom that rivaled the stars, yet he still preferred the company of his wife Anna and the simple joy of watching his son Josh practice with a wooden staff. He was the Father of the New Era, a king who provided everything but demanded absolute, unquestioning fealty.

The expansion was inevitable. Thronegate, now an empire of billions of demi-gods, looked to the stars and the boundaries of reality itself. He was in a particularly foul mood, a lingering memory of Hebe’s smile having crossed his mind that morning. He didn't even travel to Nihil. The Weaver was an entity that existed outside of time, a creature that fed on the flaws of gods. The Weaver began to weave a needle of pure anti-concept, a weapon designed to strike at the very idea of friendship. "You thought there was a gap," Godric said, his voice a paradox of calm and catastrophe. "You thought the love I bear for my friend was a handle you could pull."

In fact, Godric now possessed the ability to erase the very concept of the Weaver itself from every timeline simultaneously. The Weaver’s plan hadn't even been fully formed, yet the counter-measure was already an ancient, established fact in Godric’s being. The Weaver tried to scream, but Godric reached out and took the thought of 'weakness' from the entity’s mind, turning it into a physical shard of obsidian. He drove the shard into the Weaver’s chest. The entity didn't die; it was forced to live for eternity as a monument to its own failure, its power feeding Godric’s ever-growing pool of abilities.

As the centuries rolled on, the coldness that had defined Godric began to thaw, not through any lapse in his power, but through the persistent, grounding influence of Blake. The merchant, now an old man with mutton chops as white as snow, was the only person who dared to tell the King of the Omniverse when he was being an ass. Blake didn't fear Godric; he loved him with the stubborn, frustrated loyalty of a brother. He sat in the high council meetings, often interrupting Godric’s terrifying decrees with suggestions for better grain distribution or reminders of an upcoming festival.

Slowly, the Godric of Olympus began to return. He was still ruthless to his enemies—those who refused the mercy of surrender were wiped from existence with a nonchalance that was its own form of horror—but to his subjects, he became a father again. He walked the streets of Thronegate without a guard, stopping to listen to the concerns of mothers and the dreams of children. He ensured that no woman in his empire would ever know the fear that Reina and his daughters had felt in their final moments. He was a just god, a kind god, but one who stood atop a mountain of broken universes.

In the twilight of Blake’s life, as the merchant neared his eighty-eighth year, he sat with Godric in the royal gardens. Blake’s son, Josh, now a man of sixty with a power level that could unmake constellations, stood nearby, his hands moving in the subtle, rhythmic patterns that looked suspiciously like the opening movements of a translocation spell. Josh didn't know why he did it; it just felt right, a deep, ancestral memory surfacing in his fingertips.

"You're a good man, Godric," Blake rasped, his voice thin but steady. "A bit dramatic, and you really need to stop blowing up entire realities just because they forgot to say please, but you're a good man."

Godric smiled, a genuine, warm expression that reached his salt-and-pepper eyes. "I had a good teacher, Blake. A merchant with a short temper and a heart too big for his own good."

"Don't get soft on me," Blake grunted, leaning back into his chair. "Josh is going to be a handful for you when I'm gone. He’s got that wizard’s look in his eye. Always poking at things he shouldn't."

Godric looked at Josh, seeing the clear line of descent that stretched back ten thousand years to the blood-stained floor of the Olympian temple. He saw the future of his court, a new line of wizards who would stand by the side of the king, not as servants, but as the keepers of the light.

Present-day Thronegate stood as a jewel in the crown of the omniverse, a city that spanned a thousand worlds, all connected by the will of one man. The sky was a vibrant, shifting canvas of the many universes they had assimilated, a testament to a peace bought with infinite power. On the highest balcony of the central palace, Godric stood, his muscular frame as imposing as ever, his salt-and-pepper beard neatly trimmed. Beside him stood Blake, his right-hand man, his oldest friend, and the conscience of an empire. They looked out over a creation that was safe, orderly, and utterly dominated. There would be no more betrayals. There would be no more falling kingdoms. For as long as Godric breathed—and he would breathe forever—the light of his family’s memory would shine across every star, a warm, fatherly glow backed by the most terrifying power existence had ever known.

For ten years, I have woken to that sound, the sound of my husband Rogr trying to crawl out of his own skin to escape a memory that has no physical form. I love him with a ferocity that threatens to consume my own soul, yet every time I look at his shattered face, I feel the crushing weight of a cognitive dissonance so heavy it makes my bones ache.
