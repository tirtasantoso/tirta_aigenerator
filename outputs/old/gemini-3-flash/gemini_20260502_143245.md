The white marble of Olympus did not just shine; it vibrated with the collective joy of a golden age. King Godric stood upon the Great Dais, the sun catching the silver threads in his neatly trimmed salt and pepper beard. At forty-five, he was the absolute pinnacle of masculine grace, his massive shoulders stretching the embroidered silk of his ceremonial tunic, his short hair groomed with the precision of a man who commanded both respect and deep affection. He was a father first, a king second, and as he looked down at his twenty-two-year-old daughter, Isabel, clad in bridal lace that shimmered like mountain snow, his eyes crinkled with a warmth that could thaw the harshest winter. Beside him stood his wife, Reina, her hand resting gently on the shoulder of their fifteen-year-old daughter, Hebe, who was vibrating with the restless energy of youth. Godric reached out, his hand large and calloused but infinitely gentle, and squeezed Isabel’s hand. He felt the pulse of a world at peace, a kingdom built on the bedrock of kindness and familial devotion. It was the last moment of genuine warmth he would feel for ten thousand years.

The sky did not darken; it simply ceased to be sky. A roar, loud enough to liquefy the internal organs of those nearest the gates, heralded the arrival of the Thronegate legions. They were a neighbor that Olympus had long considered a brother-kingdom, but brotherly love had fermented into a sour, jagged envy. The first volley of magi-tech shells hit the wedding feast, turning the tables of honeyed wine and roasted meats into a slurry of splinters and gore. Godric’s world narrowed to the sight of Reina’s body being bisected by a jagged shard of obsidian shrapnel. Hebe, his little bird, didn't even have time to scream before the heat of a tactical fire-spell reduced her to a charcoal silhouette against the white pillars. Isabel, the bride, lay amidst the ruins of her own altar, her white dress blooming with a rapid, sickening crimson. Godric lunged for them, his roar of grief drowning out the sounds of the massacre, but a wall of force slammed into him.

Thorin, the court wizard whose lineage had served Godric’s ancestors since the first stones were laid, stood with veins bulging in his neck. His hands were a blur of forbidden geometry. Blood leaked from his ears and eyes. He was Godric’s best friend, the man who had shared every flask of wine and every heavy secret, and now he was committing the ultimate heresy. He was casting the Weaver’s Slip, a teleportation spell that required the caster to burn their own soul as fuel. Thorin’s eyes met Godric’s, a silent apology written in the graying of his skin. Just as the final syllable of the incantation left his cracked lips, a Thronegate spear, enchanted with anti-magic, pierced Thorin’s throat. The spell, half-anchored and bleeding energy, buckled. Reality around Godric didn't just fold; it tore like wet parchment. The king was ripped from the field of his family’s slaughter, not into a safe haven, but into the jagged, unfinished spaces between dimensions.

The trauma of the loss acted as a catalyst for the fractured magic. As Godric slammed into the floor of a hidden, primordial sanctuary deep beneath the earth’s crust, his heart was a cold, dead cinder. The warmth was gone, replaced by a crystalline, jagged cruelty. The incomplete spell froze him in time, but it did not freeze his mind. This was the moment of the Stasis Event. His aging halted instantly, locking his body in its forty-five-year-old prime forever. He no longer needed to breathe, for the air was a concept he had outgrown. He no longer needed sleep, for his mind was now a processing engine of infinite capacity. He no longer needed food, for he was becoming the very concept of sustenance.

Before Godric could even process the silence of his tomb, the Phenomenal Amplification began. It is a mechanic of such terrifying magnitude that the human mind can only glimpse its shadow before retreating into madness. At the exact moment of his stasis, every single attribute Godric possessed—his physical strength, the speed of his synapses, his capacity for logic, the sharpness of his five senses—began to multiply. The rate was not linear, nor was it a simple doubling. Every Planck second—the smallest possible unit of time—every stat multiplied by 1,000 and then multiplied by itself. To understand this is to understand the death of mathematics. Within a single second, Godric was stronger than a star. Within a minute, his intelligence surpassed the collective wisdom of every sentient being to ever live. 

Simultaneously, the void within him began to manifest superpowers. Every second, a new ability of apocalyptic proportions was forged in the furnace of his grief. The first was the Shroud of the Void, a power that allowed him to perfectly suppress his own presence. Without it, the mere pressure of his growing existence would have shattered the planet like a glass marble. As the seconds turned into minutes, and the minutes into centuries, he gained powers that could extinguish galaxies with a whisper: the ability to rewrite the laws of thermodynamics, the power to erase concepts like ‘distance’ or ‘time’ from the minds of others, and the mastery over the very fabric of the omniverse. Each of these powers, like his physical stats, multiplied by 1,000 and itself every Planck second. He was a god in a cage, growing so powerful that the cage was only held together by his own subconscious will.

The most terrifying aspect of his evolution was the Weakness Paradox. It was an absolute defense mechanism designed by the universe's inherent need for balance, though it served only to unbalance it further. Godric had no weaknesses. He was an absolute constant. However, if any being in the vast omniverse even dared to conceive of a way he might be vulnerable—if they so much as entertained a passing thought that a specific metal, a certain spell, or an emotional tie could harm him—the universe reacted with violent, instantaneous correction. The moment that thought crystallized in a mind, Godric’s amplification rate would jump to a new, even more impossible magnitude. Furthermore, he would instantly become perfectly immune to the perceived weakness, and more mockingly, he would gain absolute mastery over the very method the enemy intended to use. If a creature thought of using a soul-killing blade, Godric became the master of all soul-killing blades, and the concept of his soul being vulnerable was deleted from reality. This happened before the enemy could even formulate a plan, turning their very hope into the fuel for his ascendancy.

Ten thousand years passed in the dark. Above ground, the world changed. Olympus was forgotten, its ruins ground into dust by the shifting tectonic plates. Thronegate, the kingdom of traitors, had risen to become a galactic hegemon. They had conquered their star system and then the surrounding clusters, their technology a blend of cold steel and captured starlight. They believed they were the masters of all, led by the young and arrogant King Robert, a man who believed his empire was eternal because he had never known a challenge.

In this era, a merchant named Blake moved through the outer rim of the galaxy. Blake was thirty-eight, a man of simple needs and a short temper, framed by a set of mutton chops that he groomed with more care than his ledgers. He was an honest man in a dishonest trade, fiercely loyal to his wife, Anna, and their two children, Josh and Rhea. Blake had always felt like an outsider in the sterile, high-tech world of Thronegate. He had a strange, unconscious habit of tracing patterns in the air when he was thinking—complex, weaving motions that felt more like a memory than a quirk. He carried a heavy, silver-capped walking stick that had been in his family for generations, a relic that hummed with a faint, rhythmic vibration whenever he entered ancient places.

While scouting a newly discovered moon for mineral rights, Blake fell through a sinkhole that shouldn't have been there. He tumbled through layers of ancient basalt and forgotten technology until he landed in a chamber of absolute stillness. In the center, surrounded by a faint, shimmering aura that suppressed a pressure capable of collapsing a dimension, stood Godric. Blake, driven by a merchant’s curiosity and a strange, bone-deep pull he couldn't explain, touched the central pillar of the sanctuary. The silver cap on his walking stick flared with a brilliant, golden light, the ancient bloodline of the wizard Thorin recognizing its master's work. The stasis broke.

Godric did not wake up slowly. He was simply *there*. His omniscience, now perfected by ten thousand years of amplification, flooded his consciousness. He knew the name of every atom in the galaxy. He knew the exact moment King Robert had eaten his breakfast, and he knew the location of every Thronegate soldier scattered across the stars. He looked at Blake, and for a fleeting second, the cold cruelty in his eyes softened. He saw the echo of Thorin in the man’s soul, a resonance of loyalty that had survived ten millennia.

"You have the eyes of a friend," Godric said, his voice not a sound, but a vibration that resonated in Blake’s marrow. "And for that, you and yours shall be the first to witness the end of an era."

Godric did not move, yet he was suddenly standing on the surface of the moon, looking toward the distant glint of Thronegate’s capital planet. With a thought that moved faster than the expansion of the universe, he sent a message. It appeared in the mind of every citizen of Thronegate, a psychic decree that tasted like cold iron and ash. He challenged King Robert. He gave the empire three days to gather every soldier, every war-machine, every dragon, and every time-warping assassin they possessed. If they could not kill him, Robert would abdicate, and Thronegate would be erased.

The three days were a fever dream of mobilization. Thronegate, in its arrogance, responded with everything. Billions of super-soldiers, their bodies augmented with forbidden gene-sequences, were ferried in ships the size of moons. They brought the Null-Ninjas, warriors who moved so fast they existed in the seconds before they were born, capable of striking a target in the past. They brought the Continent-Cracker Dragons, beasts of bio-engineered fury whose breath could ignite the atmosphere. They brought the Robot Legions, trillion-strong swarms of nanites that could deconstruct a city into its component atoms in heartbeats.

Blake and Anna stood on the observation deck of their merchant vessel, Godric standing between them, his hands clasped behind his back. He looked like a father watching a distant thunderstorm, calm and slightly bored. The Thronegate fleet blotted out the stars. The sensors on Blake’s ship screamed as the enemy prepared to fire.

The attack was a symphony of destruction. A billion lasers, magically enhanced projectiles, and time-shifted strikes converged on the single point where Godric stood. The sheer energy of the first volley should have turned the moon into a supernova. 

Godric did not move a muscle. He didn't even blink.

The energy hit him and simply... ceased to exist. It wasn't absorbed; it was invalidated. The Null-Ninjas appeared around him, their blades vibrating at frequencies meant to sever the soul. Before their swords could even touch his skin, the ninjas simply dissolved into red mist. Godric’s passive defense had sensed their intent to exploit his physical form, and in that Planck second, his durability had multiplied to the point where the very air around him became an impenetrable diamond wall. The ninjas had essentially run into the absolute edge of reality at ten times the speed of light.

The Continent-Cracker Dragons descended, their maws open to unleash hellfire. Godric looked up. He didn't use a spell. He didn't use a superpower. He simply allowed a fraction of a percent of his actual weight—the conceptual mass of his presence—to be felt by the dragons. The beasts didn't just die; they imploded, their massive bodies collapsing into singularities under the sheer gravitational horror of his existence. The billions of super-soldiers in the ships above began to scream. It wasn't a physical pain at first, but a psychological one. They realized, with the clarity of the dying, that they were attacking something that wasn't part of the universe. They were attacking the thing that allowed the universe to be.

The slaughter was agonizingly detailed for those watching. The Robot Legions were turned into a sea of molten slag by a mere thought. The great capital ships, the pride of Thronegate, were folded like pieces of tin foil by unseen hands. Godric stood amidst the fire and the screaming, his salt and pepper beard unruffled, his short hair perfect. He was a man-shaped hole in the omniverse, and everything Thronegate threw into that hole was lost forever. 

By the end of the first hour, the morale of the greatest empire in the galaxy hadn't just collapsed; it had been deleted. Soldiers who had conquered entire sectors were weeping on the floors of their barracks, clawing at their eyes to stop seeing the indifferent god who stood in the void. Two days later, after witnessing the total annihilation of his military without Godric even raising a hand, King Robert was found in his private chambers. He had used his ceremonial dagger to end his own life, unable to bear the weight of a universe where he was less than an ant.

Godric descended to the capital planet of Thronegate. He walked through the streets, and the people fell to their knees. He didn't demand worship; it was a biological imperative. He stood on the balcony of the palace and declared himself King. But he did not bring more death. Instead, he brought the Awakening.

"To the men of Thronegate," Godric’s voice rolled across the world, "you were raised in the shadow of cowards. From this day, you shall know the weight of your own potential."

He enacted the Decree of Awakening. For every single day a man of Thronegate lived past the age of twenty, his strength, endurance, intelligence, and wisdom would increase tenfold. It was retroactive. Men who were thirty years old suddenly felt the power of three thousand six hundred and fifty days of tenfold multiplication. They became giants of intellect and physical might, capable of rebuilding the world in a week. They could suppress this power to live normal lives with their families, but the potential remained, a gift from their new king. Godric ensured the absolute protection of women, creating a society where the strength of the men was used only for the betterment and defense of their people. The citizens, who had expected a tyrant, found a father—a harsh, ruthlessly powerful father, but one who loved them.

Blake, however, received a different gift. Godric had looked at the merchant, the man who carried the ghost of Thorin in his stride, and granted him a modified version of the Awakening. For every day Blake lived past twenty, his power increased a thousandfold. Blake became the most powerful mortal in existence, a man whose wisdom could solve the riddles of the stars. Though he remained mortal, destined to live only to eighty-eight, he spent those years as Godric’s shadow, his conscience, and his only true friend.

The expansion of the Thronegate Empire began. It was no longer a galactic conquest; it was an omniversal subjugation. Godric’s mandate was simple: offer unconditional surrender. If they accepted, they were assimilated and protected. If they refused, they were ended.

The first to refuse was the Universe of the Chronos-Keepers, a realm where the inhabitants were made of pure temporal energy. They had watched the fall of the Thronegate galaxy and had spent five years of their accelerated time preparing a trap. They created a "Temporal Cage," a localized universe where the laws of cause and effect were reversed. They believed that by drawing Godric in, they could force his amplification to work backward, reducing him to a state before his birth. 

Godric didn't even bother to send his army. He was in a slightly inconvenienced mood because a local tea he enjoyed had been brewed poorly that morning. He stepped into the Chronos-Keepers' universe. The Temporal Cage snapped shut. The Keepers cheered, their voices a shimmer of light. Then, Godric exhaled. The sheer power of his breath, amplified by the octillions of Planck seconds he had existed, shattered the cage, the keepers, and the very concept of time within that universe. It lasted less than a trillionth of a second. The universe was assimilated before the Keepers' cheer had even finished vibrating in the air.

The second universe was the Bio-Swarm of Krell. This was a sentient, galaxy-sized organism that had consumed ten thousand neighboring realities. It didn't have technology; it had evolution. When the offer of surrender arrived, the Swarm laughed through a billion maws. It prepared for five years, breeding "God-Killer" spores designed to feast on the very essence of divinity. 

Godric sent a squad of five Thronegate men, all over the age of thirty-five. They were simple men—carpenters and teachers who had lived under the Awakening for fifteen years. They didn't want to fight; they were kind-hearted souls who missed their wives. But Godric had asked. They arrived in the Krell universe and, with the polite efficiency of men doing a chore, they began to work. One man, a former baker, clapped his hands. The sonic boom, amplified by his decades of daily tenfold growth, disintegrated the Swarm’s central nervous system across three galaxies. The conquest took twelve minutes. The men were home in time for dinner.

The third was the Iron Star Empire, a civilization of cold logic that existed in a universe of high-gravity metal planets. They were masters of the Weakness Paradox without knowing it. They spent five years calculating every possible flaw in Godric’s makeup. They concluded that since Godric was a physical being, he must be susceptible to a "Conceptual Erasure Cannon"—a weapon that didn't destroy matter, but destroyed the *idea* of the target.

The Emperor of the Iron Star, a machine-entity of vast intelligence, stood at the trigger. He had thought long and hard about Godric’s weakness: his reliance on a physical form. The moment that thought had crossed the Emperor’s mind five years prior, the Paradox had triggered. Godric, sitting in his palace thousands of light-years away, felt his amplification rate double instantly. He gained permanent immunity to conceptual erasure, and he gained the power to erase ideas himself. 

The Emperor fired the cannon. A beam of non-existence streaked toward Godric. Godric, who was playing a game of chess with Blake at the time, didn't even look up. He reached out a hand and caught the beam. He didn't destroy it; he reshaped it into a small, glowing marble which he used to replace a lost chess piece. Then, with a flick of his finger, he sent a return pulse. The Iron Star Empire didn't explode. Instead, the *idea* of the Empire was erased. The citizens forgot who they were, their machines forgot how to function, and their history vanished from the annals of time. The universe surrendered in silence, thirty seconds after the cannon was fired.

The ultimate test of the Paradox came from an entity known as Xylos, a being from the eleventh dimension who existed as a living law of physics. Xylos was arrogant. It believed that Godric’s only weakness was his history—the memory of his dead family. Xylos spent eons in its own time-stream crafting a "Memory-Siphon," a device intended to rip the grief out of Godric, believing that his pain was the anchor for his power. Xylos thought that if Godric was happy, he would be weak.

The moment Xylos formed the intent, the defense mechanism roared to life. Godric’s stats, already beyond the capacity of any language to describe, began to multiply at a rate that caused the surrounding space-time to groan. He became perfectly immune to memory manipulation. More than that, he became the master of all memories in the omniverse.

Xylos appeared in Godric’s throne room, the Memory-Siphon glowing with a sickly violet light. "I have found your heart, King of Olympus," Xylos hissed. "I shall take your sorrow, and you shall be nothing."

Godric stood up. The pressure in the room became so great that Xylos’s eleventh-dimensional form began to crack. "You think my grief is a weakness?" Godric asked, his voice a landslide of velvet and stone. "My grief is the forge. My pain is the fuel. You want to see my memories? You want to see the wedding of my daughter?"

Godric didn't use the siphon. He simply shared a single Planck second of his memory of the Fall of Olympus with Xylos. The entity, which had existed since the beginning of time, was instantly overwhelmed. The sheer, concentrated horror and the subsequent ten thousand years of cold, calculated growth flooded Xylos. The entity didn't just die; it shattered into a billion fragments of pure despair, each fragment becoming a new, tiny sun that would burn with the light of Godric’s sorrow for eternity. Xylos realized, in the microsecond before its dissolution, that the very act of thinking Godric was vulnerable had sealed its fate centuries before it even arrived.

As the centuries rolled on, the cruelty that had defined Godric upon his awakening began to soften. It was not because he grew weak, but because of Blake. The merchant, now a man of profound gravity and wisdom, remained at Godric’s side. They spent evenings walking through the gardens of the New Olympus, which now spanned an entire solar system. Blake would talk about his son, Josh, who was showing a remarkable aptitude for the "weaving" motions of the old wizardry, and his daughter Rhea, who had become a leader in the galactic arts.

Godric listened. He began to smile again—a real smile, not the cold curl of a conqueror’s lip. He remained ruthless to his enemies, a terrifying god-king who would not hesitate to erase a reality if it threatened his peace, but to his people, he became the fatherly king once more. He would walk among the common citizens, his massive frame a source of comfort rather than terror. He would attend weddings, though he never spoke during the ceremonies, his presence a silent blessing of absolute protection.

The legacy of Thorin lived on, hidden in the blood of Blake’s descendants. Josh became the first of the new Court Wizards, his "weaving" motions finally understood as the manipulation of the very strings of reality that Godric had mastered. They served not out of fear, but out of a deep, ancestral love for the king who had survived the end of the world.

In the present day, Thronegate is a civilization that spans the omniverse, a tapestry of countless cultures living in a peace enforced by the most powerful being to ever exist. The capital planet is a marvel of white marble and gold leaf, a grander, eternal version of the Olympus that was lost.

Godric stands on the highest balcony of the central palace, looking out over a sky filled with the ships of a thousand worlds. He is forty-five years old. His salt and pepper beard is neatly trimmed, and his muscular frame is clad in simple, regal silk. Beside him stands Blake, now an old man of eighty-five, his mutton chops white as snow, leaning on his silver-capped walking stick. Blake’s eyes are tired but full of a quiet pride.

"The stars are quiet tonight, Godric," Blake says, his voice raspy but warm.

Godric places a massive, gentle hand on the old merchant’s shoulder. The king who can erase universes with a thought, the man whose power grows by the square of a thousand every Planck second, looks at his best friend with the same warmth he once showed his daughters.

"They are quiet because they are safe, Blake," Godric replies, his gaze turning toward the infinite horizon. "And they will remain so, as long as we remember the cost of the noise."

Below them, the city hums with life—a golden age that will never end, protected by a god who learned that even an omnipotent king needs a friend to remind him how to be a man. The memory of the blood and the fire is still there, tucked away in the infinite folds of Godric’s mind, but it no longer burns. It is simply the foundation upon which the new eternity is built, a silent promise that the sun will never set on Olympus again.

The sun that shines over the capital of Thronegate is not a natural star; it is a meticulously tuned orb of golden benevolence, a gift from King Godric that ensures no child ever shivers in the night and no harvest ever fails. I love my King. To say anything else would be like claiming the air is a poison or that the ground beneath my feet is an enemy. Godric is the father of us all, the architect of a peace so profound it borders on the divine. Every morning, I stand on our balcony with my son, Pete, and we look toward the spires of the Great Palace, whispering our thanks for the food on our table and the safety of our borders. Pete, only twelve, wears a wooden sword at his hip and dreams of the day he might serve in the King’s Honor Guard. He talks of Godric’s strength with a fervor that brings tears to my eyes—the kind of strength that doesn't just protect a kingdom, but sustains the very fabric of existence. Our King is warm. He is kind. He is the heartbeat of a perfect world.

But inside our home, behind the heavy oak doors that shield us from the golden light, there is a shadow that no amount of royal grace can illuminate. My husband, Rogr, sits in the velvet armchair by the hearth, and though his eyes are open, he has not been present for ten years. Ten years ago, he was a man of vibrance and wit, a high-ranking delegate chosen to represent our newly annexed sector before the King. He had been so proud. He had dressed in his finest charcoal wool, kissed me with a promise of a celebratory dinner, and walked into the palace with the stride of a man who knew he was entering the presence of a savior. When he returned that evening, he didn't walk; he was carried by two guards who looked at him with a mixture of pity and confusion. There wasn't a mark on him. Not a scratch, not a bruise. But Rogr was gone.

For a decade, I have lived with a ghost who breathes. Rogr does not speak. He does not scream. He simply exists in a state of perpetual, microscopic vibration, as if every atom in his body is trying to flee from a horror that only he can see. When I touch his hand, it is colder than the void between galaxies. His pupils are permanently dilated, twin wells of absolute obsidian that reflect nothing but a darkness I cannot fathom. He eats when I feed him, he sleeps when I lay him down, but his mind is trapped in a frozen microsecond of history. I love him with a desperation that burns my throat, and I love our King for the world he has given us, but the two loves do not mesh. They grind against each other like tectonic plates, creating a fault line in my soul. I knew, with the terrifying certainty of a wife’s intuition, that something happened in that throne room. Something small. Something insignificant to a god, but cataclysmic to a man.

I have spent the last ten years trying to fix what was broken. I am a woman of means—the Thronegate economy is so robust that even a merchant’s widow would live in luxury—and I have poured every credit, every heirloom, and every ounce of my will into curing Rogr. My attempts were not merely medical; they were absurdly, hopelessly elaborate. I sought out the remnants of the Chronos-Weavers in the far-flung sectors, commissioning a machine called the Aeon-Suture. The Aeon-Suture turned itself inside out, its components liquefying into a pool of lead and mercury, self-destructing in a frantic effort to avoid looking at whatever was inside Rogr’s head. Every cure I attempted, every legendary relic I purchased, suffered the same fate. They didn't just break; they fled from the reality of Rogr’s condition. I couldn't even answer. I just watched as Gent—the aura that had just slaughtered trillions—began to evolve again. Before my eyes, the air around Godric’s feet began to shimmer with a new kind of light. It wasn't just heat anymore. It was starting to manifest "limbs" of pure causality, translucent appendages that reached out and gently adjusted the flow of time around the tea set so the tea wouldn't get cold. The aura had reached a new level of omnipotence, gaining the ability to manipulate the fundamental constants of the omniverse just to serve its master’s comfort. It was becoming a god in its own right, and yet it was still just a wasteful byproduct of the man sitting in front of me. Gent was becoming more powerful than any deity I had ever studied, and Godric just looked down at it and chuckled.

"Oh, look at that," Godric said, his eyes lighting up with fatherly pride. "Gent is learning how to weave. How adorable! It’s trying to make sure your tea stays at the perfect temperature, Tim. Isn't that sweet? It’s like watching a child take its first steps." He reached down and ran his hand through the air, and I could feel Gent purring—a vibration that caused the distant stars to wobble in their orbits. He was petting his killing-aura. He was treating a force of omniversal destruction like a golden retriever.

I looked at the garden. The jasmine was still blooming. The sun was still shining. Everything was perfect. And somewhere, in the dimensions above us, trillions of beings were being digested by a sentient heat-wave that thought it was being "polite." I realized that this was the horror of Thronegate. It wasn't a kingdom ruled by a tyrant. It was a kingdom ruled by a father who loved us so much he didn't realize that his very breath was an extinction event. We were his children, and he would protect us, and he would be kind to us, and he would never, ever understand why we were screaming.

"You know, Tim," Godric said, leaning back and looking up at the now-clear sky, his face radiant with peace. "I think I might restore one of those universes Gent accidentally collapsed last week. The one with the purple oceans? I always liked the way the light hit the waves there. It was so calming. I think I’ll bring them back tonight as a surprise for Blake’s descendants. What do you think? Would that be a nice gesture?"

I forced a smile. My jaw felt like it was made of glass. "Yes, Lord Godric. That would be... very kind of you."

"Wonderful," he said, standing up and stretching his massive, muscular frame. The movement caused a ripple in space-time that I felt in the marrow of my bones. "I’m glad you agree. It’s important to remember the little things, isn't it? The small joys. That’s what makes life worth living." He patted my shoulder one last time, a gesture of absolute, terrifying affection. As he walked toward the palace, Gent followed him, its new causality-limbs wagging like a tail, trailing the invisible scent of a trillion erased souls behind it. I stayed in the chair, staring at my tea, waiting for the screaming in my head to stop, knowing it never would. I was the high court wizard of a god who thought omnicide was a "mess" to be cleaned up, and there was nowhere in all of creation that was far enough away to hide from his love.
