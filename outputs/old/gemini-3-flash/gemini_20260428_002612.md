They fled our house, leaving behind the scent of ozone and burnt hair, and I never saw them again. I tried temporal stasis balms, neurological dampeners, and even pilgrimages to the sites of Godric’s ancient victories, hoping the "holy" air would mend what the King had broken. Each time, the result was the same: the tools of healing would shatter, the medicine would turn to poison, or the healers would go mad. The universe refused to fix Rogr. To the omniverse, Rogr’s condition wasn't a disease; it was an expression of Godric’s momentary whim, and to change it was an act of cosmic treason. I lived in absolute despair, a prisoner of my own love and my own devotion, watching my husband rot in the middle of a paradise.

The change came on a Tuesday, a day as ordinary as any other in the perfect sunshine of Thronegate. A knock at our door revealed a man who looked like he belonged in a history book. It was Blake. He looked older, his mutton chops a distinguished silver, his eyes carrying a weight of guilt that seemed out of place on a man of his stature. He didn't come with a royal guard or a fanfare of trumpets. He came alone, smelling of old parchment and expensive tobacco. I fell to my knees, my forehead touching the cool floor, my voice trembling as I welcomed him. Pete stood behind me, his eyes wide with awe, his hand over his heart in the imperial salute. Blake looked at us, and for the first time in my life, I saw a person of power who looked truly, deeply sorry. He told me that Godric had mentioned Rogr that morning. They had been reminiscing about the early days of the expansion, and Godric had casually remarked, "I wonder how that fellow from the Hive-Worlds is doing? The one who looked so funny when I teased him ten years ago. I suppose he must have toughened up by now; mortals have such resilient spirits."

Blake had frozen, the memory of that day rushing back to him. He realized then that Godric—the omniscient, all-powerful Godric—had genuinely underestimated the horror he had inflicted. Because Godric was so far beyond us, he truly believed that a "tiny" fraction of his presence was something a man could simply walk off, like a mild case of the flu. He believed in mortal resilience because he wanted to believe in it, his own kindness blinding him to the sheer fragility of our minds. When Blake explained the reality of Rogr’s ten-year living death, he said the King had gone silent for a long time. Blake had been the only one in the history of the new empire to scold the King, shouting at him for his carelessness, his face red with a fury that would have seen anyone else erased from existence. But Godric had only listened, his expression softening back into the fatherly warmth of ancient Olympus. He had sent Blake here to ask for our permission to "adjust" the situation.

"Permission?" I whispered, the word feeling like sand in my mouth. "He is the King. He does not need permission to breathe, let alone to act." Blake shook his head, his voice low. "He wants to make it right, Rosa. But he knows now that his touch is... heavy. He sent me to ask if you are ready." We deliberated for less than a minute. Rogr sat in his chair in the corner, his eyes fixed on the ceiling, a thin line of drool running into his beard. There was no choice. We agreed. The moment the word "yes" left my lips, the air in the room didn't just change; it vanished. The walls didn't move, but the space they enclosed suddenly felt like it was being held in the palm of a giant.

Godric appeared. He didn't walk through the door. He didn't teleport with a flash of light. He was simply *there*, standing in our modest living room, his presence so vast that I felt the house groan under the spiritual weight of him. He was handsome, his salt-and-pepper beard neatly trimmed, his eyes radiating a kindness that was so intense it felt like a physical assault on my senses. I couldn't breathe. Pete was already on the floor, weeping with joy at the sight of his idol. Godric looked at Rogr, and for a second, the mask of the friendly king slipped, and I saw a glimpse of the absolute, terrifying power that lay beneath. He didn't move a muscle. He didn't chant. He didn't even touch him. He just looked at my husband and *decided* that the trauma was gone.

In an instant, the screaming stopped. The shivering stopped. Rogr’s eyes, which had been fixed on the ceiling for a decade, suddenly blinked. The fog of terror vanished, replaced by a clarity so sharp it was unnerving. But it was more than just a cure. Godric, in his casual, nonchalant way, had overshot the mark. Rogr didn't just look healthy; he looked like a god in mortal skin. His skin glowed with a vibrant, inner light. The muscles that had wasted away over ten years of atrophy suddenly surged back, denser and more defined than they had ever been, even in his youth. His hair was thick and lustrous, and the very air around him seemed to hum with a newfound vitality. He stood up with a grace that was alien to him, his movements fluid and precise. He remembered everything—the ten years of darkness, the "prank," the screaming—but the pain of it had been surgically removed, leaving only the memory of the event without the emotional weight. He was the healthiest man I had ever seen, a masterpiece of biological engineering created in a heartbeat by a being who wasn't even trying.

The ease of it was what destroyed me. For ten years, I had fought, bled, and bankrupted myself to save my husband. I had watched machines explode and mystics die trying to do what Godric had just done by simply thinking a thought. The struggle of my life, the decade of agony that had aged me twenty years, was rendered utterly meaningless by his nonchalance. It was like watching someone rebuild a shattered cathedral by clicking their fingers. The existential dread that followed was a cold, oily tide. If he could fix it this easily, then the ten years of Rogr’s suffering were not an unavoidable tragedy; they were a clerical error, a footnote that Godric had simply forgotten to check. We were so small that our decade of hell didn't even register as a "problem" until it was pointed out to him.

Godric turned to me, his smile warm and fatherly. "I am sorry for the delay, Rosa," he said, his voice a rich, comforting baritone that made my bones vibrate. "I had forgotten how delicate you all are. It was a poor joke, and I have held you in my thoughts today." He reached into the air, and a small, crystalline sphere appeared in his hand. It glowed with the light of a thousand suns, a concentration of wealth and power that could have bought ten star systems. "This is for you. A compensation for the years lost. It will ensure your family never wants for anything across a hundred lifetimes. It is a gift from a friend."

I looked at the sphere, then at my husband, who was now hugging Pete with a strength that made the boy gasp. I looked at Godric, the man I worshipped, the man who had accidentally tortured my husband for ten years and then "fixed" him as if he were a broken toy. The dread in my stomach was so heavy I thought I might vomit. If I took that sphere, I was accepting that ten years of my life had a price. I was accepting that our suffering was just a transaction in the King’s ledger. I felt a sudden, sharp realization that the King wasn't being kind; he was being *efficient*. He was tidying up a mess he had made, and his "kindness" was just another layer of his absolute control.

"No, Majesty," I whispered, my voice shaking so hard I could barely form the words. "Your grace is enough. The return of my husband is the only gift I can accept. Please... take the sphere back." I couldn't tell him the truth—that the sight of the gift made me want to scream, that it reminded me of just how insignificant we were. I declined out of a fear so deep it had no name. To take his money was to enter into a contract with a god, and I was already drowning in the debt of my own existence.

Godric’s expression didn't change. He simply nodded, the sphere vanishing back into the nothingness from which it came. "Your humility does you credit, Rosa. You are a remarkable woman." He stayed for a few more minutes, chatting with Pete about the academy, acting for all the world like a friendly neighbor who had stopped by for tea. Rogr joined in, his voice strong and clear, his devotion to Godric seemingly tripled by the miracle of his healing. There was no resentment in Rogr, no anger. He looked at the King with the eyes of a man who had been saved by a savior, ignoring the fact that the savior was also the one who had thrown him into the pit. The cognitive dissonance in the room was a physical pressure, a thick, cloying atmosphere of love and terror that made my head spin. We all knelt as he and Blake prepared to leave.

As they reached the door, Godric paused and looked back at Blake, a mischievous glint in his eye. "You know, Blake, that went rather well. The look on his face when he stood up—quite a transformation. It reminds me of a trick I once played on the Archons of the Sixth Quadrant. Perhaps for the next integration, we could try something with the gravitational constants? Just a little prank to keep them on their toes. What do you think?" Blake, who had been looking at us with a lingering sadness, suddenly snapped back into his usual role. He rolled his eyes, a look of exasperation crossing his face. "Godric, for the love of the stars, let’s just stick to a normal speech next time. You’re going to give me an ulcer, and I don't have your 'immortal' stomach."

Godric laughed, a hearty, genuine sound that filled the house with a sense of warmth and safety that I knew was a lie. They stepped out into the street, and the pressure in the room finally began to lift. Rogr was laughing too, pulling me into his arms, his new, powerful heart beating against my chest with a terrifyingly healthy rhythm. He was cured. He was perfect. He was better than he had ever been. And as I held him, looking at our son who was already talking about how he would tell everyone at the academy about the King’s visit, I felt the cold, lingering dread settle into the very center of my soul. We were happy. We were grateful. We loved our King more than life itself. And we were absolutely, irrevocably doomed to be the playthings of a god who thought our agony was a joke he had simply forgotten to tell correctly. I closed my eyes and whispered a prayer of thanks to Godric, my tears soaking into the shirt of the husband he had broken and mended for his own dark amusement.
