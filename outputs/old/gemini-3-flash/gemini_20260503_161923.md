The marble of Olympus did not merely crack under the weight of the Thronegate invasion; it wept. Godric stood at the altar, a man who looked carved from the very stone of his kingdom, possessing a rugged, hyper-masculine beauty that commanded silence without a word. His shoulders were broad enough to carry the hopes of his people, his chest straining against the ceremonial silks, and his short, dark hair was salted with the wisdom of forty-five years. He possessed a neatly trimmed beard that framed a face usually lit by a fatherly warmth, a kindness that had earned him the adoration of every soul in the city. He looked at his daughter, Isabel, who at twenty-two was a vision of radiant grace in her bridal veil, and he felt a peace he believed would last forever. Beside her stood his youngest, Hebe, only fifteen, her eyes dancing with the excitement of the celebration. His wife, Reina, caught his eye from the front row, her smile a silent anchor of love that had held him steady through a decade of rule. Then, the sky broke.

The first volley from Thronegate did not come as an army, but as a rain of molten glass and dark sorcery. The cathedral roof disintegrated, and in the chaos of dust and screaming, the light went out of Godric’s world. He crawled through the rubble, his powerful hands clawing at the debris until he found Isabel. She was gone, her wedding dress soaked in a crimson that mocked the festive flowers. Hebe lay nearby, her young life snuffed out by a falling spire before she could even scream. Finally, he reached Reina, whose eyes were still open, reflecting the burning ruins of their home. The warmth in Godric’s heart didn’t just fade; it turned to a frozen, jagged shard of obsidian. The kindness that had defined him was incinerated in the fires of his burning kingdom.

Thorin, the court wizard whose bloodline had served the kings of Olympus since the dawn of time, stumbled through the smoke. His robes were charred, and blood leaked from his ears, but his eyes were fixed on Godric. Thorin was his oldest friend, the only man who truly knew the depth of the King's soul. With the Thronegate soldiers breaching the inner sanctum, their blades wet with the blood of innocents, Thorin began to chant. It was the forbidden spell, a desperate, unfinished rite of passage through the weave of existence known only to his kin. As the wizard’s hands began to glow with a terrifying, violet luminescence, a Thronegate spear pierced his chest. Thorin’s voice hitched, the incantation fracturing in the air as he collapsed. The spell, robbed of its anchor, did not teleport Godric to safety; it tore a hole in the logic of the universe and dragged him into the abyss between dimensions.

Godric was cast into a hidden sanctuary, a pocket of non-existence where the laws of physics were merely suggestions. As he settled into a deep, crystalline stasis, the broken spell interacted with the vacuum of the void in a way that defied all cosmic reason. The aging process within his cells seized instantly, granting him a terrifying immortality. He no longer required the breath of air, the sustenance of food, or the respite of sleep; he became a permanent fixture of reality. But the anomaly went deeper. At that exact moment, a phenomenon of amplification began that would make gods look like insects. His physical and mental attributes—every ounce of his strength, his speed, his intelligence, and his senses—began to multiply by one thousand, and then by the resulting product itself, every single Planck second. There are more Planck seconds in a single human blink than there are stars in the sky, and with every one of them, Godric’s capacity for violence and thought expanded toward infinity.

Simultaneously, the void began to birth gifts within him. Every passing second, a new apocalyptic superpower manifested in his soul. One moment, he gained the ability to unmake the molecular bonds of an entire solar system with a thought; the next, he could command the flow of causality itself. Each of these powers, from the moment of their birth, shared in the same exponential multiplication as his base stats. His very presence should have shattered the omniverse, collapsing every timeline into a single point of absolute destruction. However, the first power he gained was a perfect, absolute concealment. It acted as a cosmic dampener, folding his aura and his terrifying pressure into a microscopic point of stability. This mask grew in potency at the same rate as his power, ensuring that while he became a living end-event, the world around him remained blissfully unaware of the predator lurking in the dark.

Deep within this stasis, a final, paradoxical defense mechanism took root. Godric possessed no weaknesses, yet the defense was built for the arrogance of others. The moment any sentient being even began to form the mental concept that Godric might have a flaw—perhaps a sentimental attachment or a physical vulnerability—the universe itself revolted against the thought. This defensive trigger was passive and instantaneous. Before the being's neuron could even fire the complete thought of an exploit, Godric’s rate of amplification would permanently and significantly increase as a punishment to all of creation. Simultaneously, Godric would gain permanent, perfect mastery over that specific perceived attack, capable of unleashing it with a magnitude that could erase concepts from existence, while denying anyone else the ability to use that same technique against him. Thronegate, the kingdom that had butchered his family, grew into a galactic empire of cold iron and ruthless expansion. Blake was thirty-eight, a man of simple loyalties and a short fuse, his face framed by a pair of rugged mutton chops. Blake and his family watched from a distant ridge as the Thronegate army descended upon the plains where Godric stood alone. The merchant felt a cold dread in his chest, an instinctive recognition of the wizardry that once pulsed in his own bloodline, though he knew not its name. The dragons fell from the sky, their wings dissolving into ash mid-flight. The warships in orbit simply ceased to be, turned into silent clouds of frozen iron.

The massacre was silent and total. Billions of soldiers died without Godric lifting a finger. He stood in the center of a growing crater of absolute silence, the air around him shimmering with the weight of his passive superpowers. Blake watched in paralyzed horror as the most powerful army in history was reduced to a bad memory in less than a minute. King Robert, watching the feed from his palace, did not wait for Godric to arrive. The realization of what he had challenged was too much for the human mind to bear. He was found two days later in his private chambers, having taken his own life rather than face the gaze of the returned King. Godric walked into the capital of Thronegate, his footsteps cracking the foundation of the palace, and crowned himself the eternal ruler of the ashes.

His first decree was the Awakening. He looked upon the men of Thronegate—the descendants of his enemies—and decided to mold them into an instrument of his will. For every day a man lived past the age of twenty, his strength, intelligence, and wisdom would increase tenfold. For those already older, the power hit them like a physical blow as their stats multiplied retroactively. They became titans in human skin, capable of leveling mountains with a sneeze, yet they remained mortal, their lives still flickering out around eighty years. Blake, however, felt something different. As Godric’s "best friend," the merchant received a modified gift. His stats did not increase tenfold daily; they increased a thousandfold. Blake felt his mind expand into a library of infinite potential, his body becoming a vessel for energies he didn't understand, yet he remained the same short-tempered, loyal man with mutton chops, blissfully unaware that he was the living legacy of Thorin.

Under Godric’s rule, the galaxy became a fortress. He was a god who demanded absolute loyalty but offered absolute protection. Women were shielded from the horrors of the old world, and the men, though terrifyingly powerful, bowed their heads in genuine reverence to the King who had made them more than human. But the galaxy was not enough for Godric’s cold ambition. He turned his gaze to the omniverse, the infinite tapestry of parallel realities. His expansion was methodical. To every universe, he offered a choice: unconditional surrender and assimilation, or erasure. Most chose to fight, their arrogance blinded by their own local legends of power.

The first was the Universe of Eternal Light, a realm where the inhabitants were composed of pure energy and had ruled their cosmos for eons. They had five years to prepare after hearing of Thronegate’s approach. They built Dyson shells around every star to channel their power into a single, reality-piercing lance. Godric was in a trivially inconvenienced mood that day because a vintage wine he liked had corked. He didn't even send an army. He simply looked toward the dimensional barrier of their universe and exhaled. The sheer pressure of his breath, amplified by his Planck-second growth, didn't just destroy their weapons; it rewrote the laws of their physics so that light could no longer exist. The entire universe went dark and silent in a fraction of a second, its inhabitants blinking out of existence before they could even realize their lance had failed to fire.

The second was the Clockwork Multiverse, a series of interconnected gears the size of galaxies, defended by a race of hyper-intelligent machines. They had calculated every possible outcome and determined they were invincible. Godric sent a squad of five Thronegate men, all in their late forties. These were kind men, fathers and husbands who had no desire for slaughter, but they served their King. They arrived in the Clockwork realm and, with the casual strength of men whose stats had multiplied tenfold every day for decades, began to pull the gears apart with their bare hands. The machines’ most advanced anti-matter cannons bounced off the men’s skin like raindrops. In twenty-two minutes, the five men had disassembled the core of that reality, apologizing to the weeping mechanical sentience as they broke its central processor over a knee.

The third was the Realm of the Sorcerer-Lords, a universe where magic was as common as oxygen. They had spent centuries perfecting a spell designed to drain the life force of any invader. When the offer of surrender arrived, they laughed and cast their ultimate hex. The hex reached across the void to find Godric, but the moment the spell’s "thought" touched his existence, the defense paradox triggered. The Sorcerer-Lords had perceived a weakness: they thought he had a soul that could be drained. Instantly, Godric’s amplification rate tripled. He gained perfect mastery over soul-draining magic, and before the sorcerers could even finish their incantation, their own souls were ripped from their bodies and fed into Godric’s growing reservoir of power. A single squad of Thronegate soldiers walked through the now-lifeless husks of their greatest cities, completing the conquest in twelve minutes.

One final entity, an elder god from the deepest trench of the omniverse, thought it could succeed where others failed. It spent an age crafting a weapon of "Absolute Erasure," a conceptual tool designed to delete the very idea of Godric from history. The entity spent decades planning, convinced it had found the one flaw: that Godric was still tethered to the concept of "existence." The moment that thought fully formed in the entity’s mind, it was already over. Godric felt the amplification spike, his power surging to a level that made his previous self look like a shadow. He didn't wait for the attack. He manifested in the entity’s sanctum, not by traveling there, but by simply deciding he was already present. The elder god looked up in terror as Godric took the "Absolute Erasure" weapon, which had taken eons to build, and crushed it between two fingers. Godric then used the entity’s own technique to erase the god’s history, its future, and the very memory of its name from every mind in the omniverse.

As the centuries rolled on, the cold, ruthless edge of Godric’s soul began to soften, buffeted by the simple, grounding presence of Blake. The merchant, now a man of immense, quiet power, remained the only person who would dare to speak to Godric as a man rather than a deity. They would sit in the gardens of the New Olympus, drinking ale and watching the suns set over a peaceful empire. Blake’s influence acted as a balm on the King’s ancient wounds. Godric began to resemble the fatherly king of old, a man who would stop to pat a child on the head or settle a dispute between neighbors with a warm smile. To his subjects, he was a benevolent sun; to his enemies, he remained a supernova.

Blake never learned of his lineage, but his hands began to naturally weave the air into protective wards, and his mutton chops grayed with a distinguished grace. He eventually transitioned from a merchant to Godric’s official Court Wizard, his intuitive grasp of reality far outstripping any trained sorcerer. His son, Josh, grew up in the palace shadows, already showing the same uncanny knack for the "hum" of the world, a new generation of loyalty forming in the heart of the empire.

In the present day, Thronegate stands as the undisputed heart of the omniverse. The capital is a city of impossible spires and golden light, where the air tastes of ozone and ancient peace. Godric sits upon his throne, his muscular frame relaxed, his salt-and-pepper beard neatly trimmed just as it was ten thousand years ago. He is no longer just a king; he is the fundamental constant of reality. Beside him stands Blake, the former merchant, leaning on a staff of white wood, his eyes twinkling with a short-tempered but deep affection for the man beside him. Outside, the men of Thronegate walk with the power of gods, yet they live with the humility of those who know that their strength is a gift from a King who once lost everything and gained the infinity of the void in return. The omniverse is silent, safe, and utterly dominated, held together by the will of a father who finally found a family he could not lose.

The golden light of Thronegate is a lie, not because it is false, but because it is too true. It is a light so absolute, so pervasive, that it leaves no room for the sanctuary of a shadow. For ten years, I have lived in this perfection, walking streets paved with a crystal that never cracks and breathing air that tastes of mountain springs and ancient honey. Our King, the Great Godric, has sculpted a paradise from the wreckage of a thousand broken universes, and like every other citizen of this vast, sprawling omniverse, I revere him. I love him with a ferocity that borders on the religious. He is the father who never sleeps, the shield that never buckles, and the god who genuinely, terrifyingly, cares for his people. But for ten years, while the rest of the world sang hymns to his name, my husband Rogr was screaming in a silence that no one else could hear.

I remember the day Rogr left for the capital. He was a delegate, a man of sharp wit and steady hands, chosen to represent our newly assimilated sector before the throne. He was proud. He told me that meeting King Godric was the highest honor a mortal could achieve, a chance to stand in the presence of the man who had brought us peace and the Awakening. Our son, Pete, who was barely five then, had clung to his father’s leg, begging for a story about the King’s golden armor. Rogr had laughed, a deep, resonant sound that I haven't heard in a decade, and promised to bring back a tale of the King’s kindness. He went to the palace expecting a blessing; he came back a hollowed-out husk of a man, his soul flayed open by a joke.

It was only later, through the whispered confessions of the court, that I learned of the "prank." Blake, the King’s closest confidant and the man whose lineage is whispered to be woven from the very fabric of ancient magic, had made an offhand comment. He had suggested to Godric that he should play the part of the merciless tyrant for the new delegates, just for a moment, to see the looks on their faces. Godric, who possesses absolute omniscience and knows the weight of every atom in existence, somehow failed to calculate the fragility of the human mind when faced with even a fraction of his true self. He had agreed. When Rogr and the others entered the throne room, Godric didn't greet them with his usual fatherly warmth. To Godric, it was less than a sigh. To Rogr, it was as if the sun had suddenly turned into a screaming mouth and whispered the secrets of his own insignificance directly into his marrow. He didn't just see a king; he saw the cold, unfeeling machinery of an omnipotent predator that could delete his entire ancestry with a stray thought. The trauma wasn't just psychological; it was existential.
