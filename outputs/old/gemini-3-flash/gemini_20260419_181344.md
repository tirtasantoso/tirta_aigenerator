Blake adjusted the cuff of his silk shirt, the fabric gliding over skin that felt denser than industrial steel. He was forty years old today, and the math of his existence was a silent, roaring tide in his blood. For twenty years, since his twentieth birthday, his physical and mental essence had multiplied tenfold every single day. He wasn't just a man; he was a walking geological event, yet he moved with the delicacy of a breeze so as not to shatter the floor tiles of his mountain-side villa. He looked at Lina, who was setting the table. She was thirty-five, beautiful in a way that felt fragile, her movements shadowed by a quiet, simmering heat he knew was resentment. To Blake, her anger was like the buzzing of a fly against a windowpane—earnest, persistent, but ultimately harmless. He loved her with the heavy, protective weight of a master architect admiring a delicate glass sculpture.

He poured wine, the liquid falling with a weight that would have shattered a lesser man’s glass. Outside their window, the capital city of Aethelgard stretched toward the horizon, a sprawl of white marble and gravity-defying spires that hummed with the collective power of millions of men like him. High above, the Golden Palace sat anchored in the clouds, the seat of Godric.

“You’re thinking about the archives again,” Blake said, his voice a low vibration that made the silverware hum.

Lina didn’t look up. Her hands trembled slightly as she smoothed the tablecloth. “My great-grandmother died in the red fields, Blake. She wasn't a 'rebel.' She was a scientist. She thought the world could be shared.”

Blake stepped toward her, his movement so precise it left no footprint in the plush rug. He placed a hand on her shoulder. He could feel the frantic rhythm of her heart, the rush of her blood, the tiny electrical impulses of her nerves. He could, if he wished, count every atom in her body. “She lived in a time of chaos, Lina. Before the King revealed the truth of the order. Before he gave us the Awakening. Look at the peace we have. Look at the safety.”

Lina finally looked at him, her eyes dark with a history she was forbidden to teach. “Safety is just a cage with better lighting, Blake. You worship a man who turned the sky to lead because someone dared to ask why he held the keys to the sun.”

Blake smiled, a warm, genuine expression. He didn’t find her words blasphemous; he found them tragic. She simply couldn't comprehend the scale of the being she despised. He closed his eyes, and for a moment, he let his mind drift back to the historical records every man studied—the visual memories imprinted into the very stone of the city. He saw the Great Uprising not as a dry chronicle, but as a living horror.

Nine and a half millennia ago, Godric had been a king of flesh and bone. At age thirty-five, a soldier in a forgotten war, he had stood on a ridge overlooking a dying valley. In that precise heartbeat, something in the fabric of the omniverse had snapped and refocused through him. He felt his pulse stop, then restart with the force of a collapsing star. He no longer needed the air he breathed; he no longer felt the itch of sleep or the hollow ache of hunger. He simply *was*. 

Godric had hidden it. For eight thousand, eight hundred and eighty-eight years, he ruled as a wise, long-lived monarch. People suspected he was a mage or a blessed soul, but they thought he could still be bled. They thought he was a bottleneck to progress.

The women of that age, led by a coalition of genius-engineers and sorceresses, had ammassed an army that spanned continents. They had "World-Eater" cannons that could crack a tectonic plate and "Soul-Siphon" arrays designed to drain the essence of a god. Billions of soldiers stood in the plains of Valerius, their armor shimmering like a sea of silver. 

Godric had walked out to meet them alone. He wore no armor, only a simple tunic, his salt-and-pepper beard neatly trimmed, his face radiating a terrifying, fatherly kindness.

The High Commander of the Uprising, a woman whose name had been scrubbed from everything but the deepest lore, peered through a telescope and saw a flaw. She saw that Godric leaned slightly to the left, a phantom remnant of a wound from his mortal youth. She believed his left side was his weakness. 

The moment that thought formed in her mind, the Paradox triggered. 

Deep within Godric, his amplification rate—already growing by a factor of a million multiplied by itself every second for nine thousand years—screamed upward. The insult of being perceived as "flawed" forced the omniverse to overcompensate. Godric didn't just become immune to attacks on his left; he gained absolute mastery over the concept of "directionality." He could now strike from every angle simultaneously without moving a muscle. He didn't just heal the old wound; he deleted the concept of "injury" from his personal timeline.

"My daughters," Godric had said, his voice carried not by air, but by a direct manipulation of the reality in their ears. "Why do you seek to break the foundation of the world?"

The High Commander ordered the fire. A beam of concentrated anti-matter, capable of erasing a moon, struck Godric squarely in the chest. 

Godric didn't flinch. He didn't even blink. He caught the beam in his hand, his skin unblemished. To him, the beam moved slower than a continental drift. His intelligence, multiplying by trillions every second, had already calculated the vibration of every atom in the enemy army. He looked at the beam and smiled. With a flick of his wrist, he transmuted the anti-matter into liquid gold, which fell to the earth in a heavy, melodic rain.

Then, he began to move.

To the soldiers, it looked as if the world simply exploded. Godric didn't use a sword. He used his presence. He walked through the front lines, and the sheer density of his existence caused their armor to implode. He was so fast that he lived a billion years in the space between their heartbeats. He would stop, gently pat a soldier on the cheek, and by the time he moved to the next, the kinetic energy of his touch had vaporized the soldier’s nervous system, leaving only a standing husk of scorched bone.

One sorceress thought she could trap him in a time-loop. She envisioned a cage of infinite seconds. The Paradox roared. Godric instantly gained mastery over Chronos. He didn't just break her spell; he accelerated the age of her specific battalion by ten billion years in a single second. Blake had seen the recordings: women turning to dust, then their dust turning to subatomic particles, then those particles winking out into the void, all while Godric stood there with a look of profound, heart-wrenching disappointment.

"It shouldn't have come to this," Godric whispered, even as he raised a hand and manifested an apocalyptic superpower—*The Breath of the Void*. A wave of nothingness rolled from his lungs, a conceptual erasure that didn't just kill the opposing fleet in orbit; it unmade the materials they were built from, returning the metal to the ores and the ores to the stars. 

He was ruthless. He was a hurricane of charismatic slaughter. He dismantled their advanced tanks with his bare hands, peeling back layers of reinforced titanium as if they were wet paper. When a group of assassins tried to phase through reality to stab him, he reached into the "between-space" and pulled them out by their throats, his grip firm but strangely gentle, before crushing their windpipes with a sound like grinding boulders. 

By sunset, the red fields were a literal sea. Godric stood in the center of the carnage, his tunic still perfectly clean, his hair undisturbed. He looked at the few survivors—the ones he had spared to bear witness.

"You thought me a man," he told them, his voice warm and melodic, despite the billions of corpses cooling around him. "You thought there was a limit to the Father's strength. From this day forward, I shall ensure that no man among you is ever so vulnerable again, and no woman among you ever forgets her place beneath the shade of the mountain."

That was the day he instituted the Awakening. He gifted every man a fraction of his own exponential nature—tenfold growth every day. It was a pittance compared to his own growth, which by now had reached numbers that defied the very concept of mathematics, but it was enough to make every man a god in his own right.

Back in the present, Blake looked at Lina. He saw her jaw set, her eyes fixed on the Golden Palace. He knew she dreamt of a way to kill him. He knew she spent her nights reading forbidden texts about "The Weakness Paradox," trying to find a loophole.

He felt a pang of pity for her. He knew that the moment she even began to conceptualize a weapon or a plan, Godric would grow stronger because of it. If she imagined a poison that could affect an immortal, Godric would instantly become the master of all toxins and his blood would become the antidote to everything in existence. She was feeding the very god she sought to starve.

Blake reached out and tucked a strand of hair behind her ear. He felt the minute static electricity of her skin. 

"Tonight is the festival of the First Reveal," Blake said softly. "The King will descend. He wants to see his children."

"I won't go," Lina whispered.

"You will," Blake said, his voice firm, the "Awakening" in him lending a weight to his words that she couldn't resist. "Because you are mine, and I am his. And the world is exactly as it should be."

He walked to the balcony, looking out over the glowing city. The sky began to shimmer with a hue that didn't exist in nature—a manifestation of Godric’s aura, held back by his own restraint. The ground beneath Blake's feet felt solid, eternal. He thought of the King, who at every passing second became a billion times more powerful than he had been a second before, a being who was now so vast that the entire omniverse was merely a marble in his palm.

Blake felt a surge of profound pride. He felt the ten-fold increase of his own strength for the day clicking into place, his muscles knitting tighter, his mind expanding, his senses sharpening until he could hear the singing of the stars. 

He was a man in Godric’s world. He was a pillar of the patriarchy, a brick in a fortress that would never fall. He looked at the Golden Palace, and as the sun set, he bowed his head in genuine, ecstatic reverence. The King was kind. The King was ruthless. The King was everything. 

And the King was still growing.
