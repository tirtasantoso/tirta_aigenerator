The marble of Olympus did not merely shine; it glowed with a preternatural luminescence that mirrored the soul of its king. Godric stood atop the Great Dais, the weight of his ceremonial robes less a burden than a familiar embrace. At forty-five, he was the zenith of masculine vitality, a man whose presence was a physical force. His short hair, the color of a storm cloud, complemented a neatly trimmed salt-and-pepper beard that framed a jawline carved from the very mountain the city sat upon. His chest, broad and thick with muscle honed through decades of both sport and governance, swelled with a father’s pride. Beside him stood Isabel, his eldest, a vision of twenty-two-year-old grace in a gown of woven sunlight. Godric reached out, his hand warm and calloused, to squeeze her fingers. He was a man defined by his kindness, a king whose subjects did not bow out of fear, but out of a deep-seated love for the father of their nation. The air was thick with the scent of jasmine and the distant, melodic chime of the wedding bells.

The first explosion did not sound like a bomb; it sounded like the world cracking in half. The sky, once a perfect azure, was suddenly choked by the black-and-gold warships of Thronegate. They descended like carrion birds, their cannons spitting streaks of violet fire that turned the white marble of the wedding plaza into a slurry of molten stone and vaporized flesh. Godric’s first instinct was protection, but the onslaught was too fast, too total. He watched, his mind refusing to process the image, as a shard of jagged obsidian masonry sliced through the air, shearing through the neck of his wife, Reina. Her blood, a vibrant, terrifying crimson, sprayed across his face, warm and metallic. Before he could even scream her name, another blast leveled the altar. He saw Hebe, his fifteen-year-old daughter, her eyes wide with a terror that would never leave them, buried under a collapse of burning timber. Isabel, his beautiful bride-to-be, was gone in an instant, reduced to a scorched shadow on the ground where she had stood a heartbeat before.

Through the roar of the fire and the screams of the dying, a man stumbled toward Godric. It was Thorin, the court wizard, his robes tattered and his face slick with sweat and soot. Thorin’s family had served the line of Olympus since the first stone was laid, their blood bound to the throne by ancient oaths of loyalty. In his hands, a forbidden light pulsed—a sickly, emerald hue that tasted of ozone and desperation. It was a teleportation spell, a relic of the bloodline intended only for the absolute end of days. Thorin began the incantation, his voice a frantic rasp against the thundering destruction. But as the air began to warp around Godric, a Thronegate sniper’s bolt, glowing with soul-reaping energy, tore through Thorin’s throat. The wizard fell, his life’s blood staining the emerald light, and the spell, severed from its caster’s will, collapsed inward. The trauma of the moment—the sight of his family’s mangled remains and his best friend’s dying gasp—snapped something fundamental within Godric. The warmth in his eyes died, replaced by a cold, obsidian vacuum. The fatherly king was gone; in his place was a hollowed-out vessel of pure, unadulterated hatred.

The incomplete spell did not move Godric through space, but rather shunted him into a fold between dimensions—a hidden sanctuary where time was a stagnant pool. As he drifted into the center of the void, a deep stasis took hold. It was a transformation that defied every law of the cosmos. His aging ceased instantly, fixing him forever in his prime. The biological necessities of life—the need for air to fill his lungs, the requirement for sleep to mend the mind, the fuel of sustenance—simply vanished. He became a static point in a moving universe, but he was far from dormant. At the exact moment the stasis locked his physical form, a terrifying feedback loop began within his soul. It was a metaphysical cancer of power, an amplification mechanic triggered by the broken spell and fueled by his bottomless grief.

Every Planck second—the shortest unit of time measurable—every single one of Godric’s attributes began to multiply. His strength, his speed, his intelligence, his very presence, was multiplied by a thousand and then immediately multiplied by itself. In the first second of his stasis, he surpassed the power of a star. By the tenth second, he was more powerful than the collective energy of a thousand galaxies. And yet, this was only the beginning. Simultaneously, his mind began to generate new, apocalyptic superpowers every single second. Each power, upon its birth, was capable of erasing a civilization from existence with a mere flick of intention. One second he gained the ability to manipulate the strong nuclear force; the next, he could rewrite the history of any sentient soul. The potency of these powers followed the same terrifying, exponential multiplication as his base stats.

The only reason the omniverse did not shatter under the weight of his burgeoning existence was the very first power he manifested: Absolute Concealment. This ability, which also amplified at the same unfathomable rate, acted as a perfect dampener, holding back his aura and his destructive pressure. It was a cosmic veil that kept the reality around him from curdling into nothingness. Yet, there was a final, more sinister layer to his evolution—the Weakness Paradox. It was a defensive mechanism rooted in the fundamental arrogance of sentient life. Godric had no weaknesses; he was an absolute. However, should any being in the vast reaches of the omniverse even conceive of a potential flaw in him, or attempt to prepare an exploit against a perceived vulnerability, the universe itself reacted to their insolence. The moment a thought of his weakness crystallized in an enemy’s mind, Godric’s rate of amplification would permanently and significantly increase. He would instantly gain perfect, permanent immunity to that imagined exploit, and more terrifyingly, he would gain absolute mastery over the very attack or concept the enemy intended to use. Ten thousand years passed as a blink in the dark. The memory of Olympus faded into myth, then into dust, and finally into nothingness. As the first wave of Thronegate’s army charged—a billion men and machines moving with the force of a collapsing star—Godric let a fraction of a fraction of his presence slip through his concealment. The effect was not a battle; it was a cosmic erasure. The super-soldiers did not die; they ceased to have ever been. Their atoms were not just split, but deleted from the ledger of reality. The dragons, mid-flight, simply dissolved into red mist that hung in the air like a macabre fog. The ninjas, attempting to step through time to strike from the past, found that the past had been rewritten to exclude their existence. Godric remained motionless, his hands at his sides, his eyes cold and distant.

The violence was silent and absolute. Men screamed as their limbs turned to liquid glass; robots melted into puddles of screaming mercury. The very ground beneath the Thronegate army turned into a churning sea of gore and shattered technology. The high-commanders in their orbiting ships watched in a paralysis of horror as their entire civilization’s power was neutralized by a man who wasn't even looking at them. The morale of the greatest empire in history didn't just break; it evaporated into a primal, existential dread. Two days later, King Robert was found in his private chambers, having taken his own life rather than face the silent god who now walked his halls. Godric ascended the throne of his ancient enemies, not as a conqueror, but as an inevitable fact of nature.

Upon his coronation, Godric enacted the Decree of Awakening. It was a gift and a curse, a transformation of the human condition for the men of Thronegate. For every day a man lived past the age of twenty, his physical and mental attributes would increase tenfold, a process that applied retroactively to the living. This growth was not passive; the men could suppress this burgeoning power to remain among their families, or unleash it to serve their King. The society shifted almost overnight. Strength and wisdom became the bedrock of the empire, but it was a strength tempered by Godric’s absolute protection of women and children. The citizens, once ruled by fear, now found themselves under the shadow of a god who demanded order and granted power. Blake, though he remained a merchant at heart, felt the Awakening differently. While others grew tenfold, Blake’s power surged by a factor of a thousand every day. He felt the threads of the universe between his fingers, a latent wizardry that he never fully understood but utilized with a natural, humble grace.

The expansion began shortly after. Thronegate was no longer a galactic empire; it was the seed of an omniversal hegemony. Godric’s policy was simple: unconditional surrender or total subjugation. The first universe to resist was the Aethelgard Collective, a realm of sentient machines and star-spanning sorcerers who had spent five hundred years preparing for the arrival of the "Void King." They had built walls of solidified logic and weapons that fired concentrated entropy. Godric sent a squad of five Thronegate men, all over the age of thirty-five. These men were peaceful by nature—carpenters and poets in their civilian lives—but as they stepped into Aethelgard, their power was so vast that the collective’s defenses shattered like brittle ice. The conquest took twelve minutes. The defenders watched in hopeless despair as five middle-aged men walked through their ultimate weapons as if they were nothing more than a summer breeze, gently dismantling their entire military infrastructure with polite, regretful smiles.

The second universe was the Silent Void, a dimension of eldritch horrors and non-Euclidean gods who fed on the concept of fear. They attempted to invade Godric’s mind, to find a crack in his psychic armor. Godric, who was in a particularly foul mood due to a minor bureaucratic delay in his new capital, didn't even send a squad. He simply leaned back in his throne and exhaled. The displacement of his breath, amplified by his stats, rippled through the fabric of reality, tearing through the Silent Void and erasing its eldritch pantheon in a fraction of a microsecond. The third universe, a high-fantasy realm of immortal elves and celestial dragons, was given the three-day warning. They spent every second of those three days chanting spells of absolute warding and summoning their creator deities. When the deadline passed, the five Thronegate men arrived. They moved with such casual, terrifying efficiency that the elves surrendered within four minutes, their "immortal" gods having been neutralized by a squad leader who was thinking about what his wife was making for dinner.

The futility of resistance was finally demonstrated by a being known as the Weaver of All Ends, a cosmic entity that existed outside the boundaries of the omniverse. The Weaver had watched Godric’s rise and believed it had found the fatal flaw: Godric was still, at his core, a man of flesh. The Weaver spent eons in its own time-stream crafting a weapon of pure anti-matter, designed to target the biological spark of life. But the moment the Weaver even thought the words "he is biological," the Weakness Paradox triggered. Godric’s amplification rate doubled instantly. Before the Weaver could even begin to manifest its weapon, Godric was already immune to the very concept of anti-matter. More than that, Godric gained absolute mastery over the Weaver’s own essence. As the entity attempted its attack, it found itself paralyzed by its own power, which now answered only to Godric. The Weaver was not killed; it was folded into a small, silent marble that Godric placed on his desk—a reminder of the price of arrogance. The entity’s last sensation was a realization that its defeat had been finalized the very second it dared to think Godric was vulnerable.

Through all of this, the coldness in Godric’s heart began to thaw, and the catalyst was always Blake. Despite being the most powerful mortal in existence, Blake remained the same short-tempered, honest man he had always been. He would walk into Godric’s throne room, ignore the kneeling trillions, and complain about the quality of the tea or the tax rates on the outer rim. Godric, the merciless god-king of the omniverse, would smile—a genuine, warm expression that reminded the world of the fatherly king of Olympus. Blake became his conscience, his anchor, and his best friend. Under Blake’s influence, Godric transitioned back to the man he once was, though his kindness was now backed by the power to unmake reality. He was a father to his subjects, a nightmare to his enemies, and a friend to a man who had no idea his ancestors had once died for the same king.

As the years passed, Blake began to spend less time on his merchant ship and more time in the royal library. He found himself drawn to the ancient scrolls, his fingers tracing patterns that seemed to glow when he touched them. His son, Josh, now a young man of terrifying power and keen intellect, sat beside him, the two of them deciphering the "tricks" of the old world. They were becoming the new court wizards, the lineage of Thorin reborn to serve the King who had outlived time. The final scene of this new era was one of strange, quiet peace. Godric sat on his throne in the heart of a city that spanned a thousand solar systems, his muscular frame relaxed, his salt-and-pepper beard catching the light of a dying sun he had chosen to keep burning. Beside him stood Blake, the merchant-wizard, leaning on a staff of white marble. They looked out over a horizon of infinite possibilities, a god and a man, bound by a loyalty that had survived ten thousand years of blood, stasis, and the absolute weight of infinity. Thronegate was no longer an empire of conquest; it was a sanctuary, and its king was finally home.

For ten years, I have lived in a house of whispered screams and gilded perfection. My husband, Rogr, was once a man of vibrant color, a diplomat whose voice could soothe the jagged edges of a world in upheaval. Now, he is a ghost who breathes, a hollowed-out vessel of a man who spends his days huddled in the corner of our sun-drenched parlor, staring at the exquisite marble floors that King Godric provided for every citizen of Thronegate. The sunlight, filtered through windows of unbreakable crystalline glass, is warm and constant, a testament to the King’s love for us. Our son, Pete, sits at the kitchen table, his eyes bright with the innocent fervor of a boy who knows only the benevolence of a god. Everyone loves the King, for how could we not? He has ended hunger, silenced war, and turned the cold vacuum of space into a garden of delights. And yet, when I look at Rogr, I see the price of a god’s whim, a cost so infinitesimal to the King that it was forgotten the moment it was paid, leaving us to drown in the interest for a decade.

Rogr’s condition is not a physical ailment; it is a metaphysical collapse. He was part of the first delegation from the Andromeda systems, a group of proud men and women who went to the capital to offer their gratitude and their oaths. They expected a tyrant, for that was the reputation that preceded the King—a ruthlessness that could extinguish a star with a glance. Instead, they found a man who was warm, fatherly, and terrifyingly handsome, a ruler who welcomed them with open arms and a hearty laugh. But just before the formal reception, as I later learned from the whispers that haunt the edges of our perfect society, the King’s best friend, a man named Blake, had made a joke. Blake, in his rustic, mutton-chopped honesty, had suggested that Godric should play into the delegates' fears, just for a bit of fun—a prank to see the look on their faces before the King revealed his true, gentle nature. Godric, who loved a joke as much as he loved his people, had agreed. He had decided to pretend to be mildly displeased.

Rogr was the first to meet that displeasure. It lasted for a fraction of a second, a duration so small that time itself barely acknowledged it. Godric didn't even yell; he didn't even move a muscle. He simply let a shadow of a thought—a simulated spark of "tyrannical annoyance"—flicker across his mind. To Godric, it was like a man pretending to be a monster for his toddler’s amusement, a performance of zero effort and less than zero malice. But Godric is an absolute, and even his pretense carries the weight of a billion collapsing omniverses. When that sliver of "displeasure" touched Rogr’s soul, it didn't just scare him. It unmade the concept of safety within his consciousness. It was a trauma that transcended biology, a permanent searing of the psyche by a flame that burns hotter than the birth of time. For ten years, Rogr has lived in that fraction of a second, experiencing the existential dread of being the target of a god’s wrath, even if that wrath was a lie.

I have spent those ten years trying to save him, driven by a love that is as desperate as it is futile. I remember the way he smiled as he fixed a decade of agony in a microsecond. I remember the ease of it.
