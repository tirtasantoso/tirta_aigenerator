The golden city of Olympus did not fall with a whimper, but with the wet, rhythmic thud of a butcher’s cleaver hitting bone. It was the day of the Great Equinox, a day that should have been defined by the scent of roasting lamb, the sweet sting of honeyed wine, and the jubilant chime of wedding bells. King Godric, a man whose presence felt as sturdy and comforting as the ancient oaks lining his courtyard, stood at the altar. At forty-five, he was the physical pinnacle of his lineage, a towering figure of masculine grace with shoulders that seemed broad enough to carry the heavens themselves. His salt-and-pepper beard was trimmed with a precision that mirrored his disciplined mind, and his short, dark hair caught the sunlight, framing a face that was, above all else, kind. The first volley of enchanted ballistae bolts shattered the stained glass of the cathedral, raining jagged shards of sapphire and emerald onto the wedding party. In the screaming chaos that followed, the warmth of the day was replaced by the metallic tang of blood. I watched the dust motes dancing in that benevolent light as I prepared our morning meal, the scent of nutrient-dense grains and synthetic honey filling our home. "The King only mentioned the... incident... a few days ago. He remembered Rogr's face from the delegation. He hadn't realized the impression lasted quite so long."

Godric was sitting in a simple chair, his back to us as he looked out over the sprawling, perfect horizon of Thronegate. He was wearing a simple white tunic that strained against the impossible landscape of his shoulders. Even from behind, the sheer density of his existence was palpable. The air around him didn't just feel energized; it felt correct. Every atom in the room seemed to be vibrating in a perfect, harmonious chord simply because he was there. When he turned around, the movement was so fluid it didn't seem to occupy time. His face was the most beautiful thing I have ever seen—kind, fatherly, and radiating a warmth that made the sun outside seem like a dim candle.

"Rosa," he said, and the sound of my name in his voice felt like a benediction. He looked at Rogr, and for a moment, the warmth in his eyes shifted into a profound, cosmic sadness. "And Rogr. My friend, I am truly sorry. Blake told me a joke, and I thought a little theater would make you all feel more at home. I forget sometimes how heavy even a shadow can be."

Rogr didn't speak. He couldn't. He was staring at Godric’s feet, his entire body shaking so violently that I could hear his teeth chattering. The existential dread had returned, a physical wall of terror that threatened to stop my own heart. I felt the urge to kneel, to disappear, to scream in the face of such overwhelming power. But then Godric stood up. He didn't walk; he was suddenly just there, standing inches from my husband. 

He didn't use a spell. He didn't perform a ritual. He didn't even touch Rogr. He simply looked at him, his salt-and-pepper beard twitching with a small, apologetic smile. "You've carried that weight long enough," Godric said softly. "Be well, Rogr. Enjoy your wife. Enjoy your son. The world is yours to inherit."

It happened in the space between two heartbeats. There was no flash of light, no thunderous sound. There was only a sudden, jarring sense of reality snapping back into place. Rogr’s shaking stopped instantly. It was as if a hand had reached into his soul and smoothed out every wrinkle of trauma with a single, nonchalant stroke. The color returned to his skin in a rushing tide of health. His eyes, which had been dull and clouded for ten years, cleared until they sparkled with a vibrancy I hadn't seen since we were teenagers. But it wasn't just that he was healed. He was... enhanced.

I watched in silent horror and awe as Rogr’s wasted muscles began to swell and knit themselves back together under his silk shirt. His chest expanded, his posture straightened, and the lines of agony that had been etched into his face vanished, replaced by the rugged, handsome features of a man in his prime. He didn't look younger—he was still forty-eight years old—but he looked like the most perfect version of a forty-eight-year-old man that could possibly exist. His hair grew thicker, the gray at his temples turning into a distinguished silver. The Awakening, which his body had been rejecting for a decade, seemed to catch up to him in a single, explosive burst of growth. He was suddenly a titan standing in the middle of a garden, his presence radiating a newfound strength that was a direct reflection of the King’s own.

Rogr blinked, looking at his hands, then at me. He didn't look like a man who had just been cured of a terminal illness; he looked like a man who had just woken up from a refreshing nap. The ten years of screaming, the years of sensory tanks and Weaver’s Silk, the years of my own desperate, agonizing labor—it was all rendered utterly, laughably insignificant. Godric had fixed with a casual sentence what the greatest minds and most expensive technologies in the galaxy couldn't even touch. The ease of it was the most terrifying part. To Godric, the destruction of Rogr’s mind had been a trivial accident, and the restoration of it was an even more trivial correction. 

"Thank you, Sire," Rogr said, his voice deep and resonant, echoing with the authority of the Awakening. He fell to one knee, but this time it wasn't out of terror. it was out of a loyalty so profound it made my previous devotion look like a passing whim. "I... I remember it all. I remember the dark. But it's gone. You took it away."

"It was never meant to be there," Godric said, patting Rogr on the shoulder. The touch should have shattered Rogr’s collarbone, but instead, it seemed to ground him, to anchor him even more firmly into the world. Godric looked at me and winked. "He’s going to be a bit hungry when you get home, Rosa. The Awakening has a lot of lost time to make up for."

We were dismissed shortly after. Blake walked us to the exit, leaning in to whisper to Rogr about a position in the provincial government that would suit his new "capacity." Pete was walking on air, clutching his Godric figure, his eyes glazed with the joy of having seen his god perform a miracle. I, however, felt a coldness in the pit of my stomach that even the palace’s golden light couldn't reach.

As we traveled back to our sector, I sat next to my husband. He was beautiful. He was strong. He was the man I had dreamed he would become. He took my hand, his grip firm and warm, and told me he loved me. He talked about the future, about how he wanted to take Pete to the mountain ranges of the northern continent, about how he finally felt like a true citizen of Thronegate. He remembered the trauma—he could describe every second of that existential dread—but it no longer had any power over him. It was just a story he knew.

I looked at him and felt a wave of nausea. I love him. I truly, deeply love him. But as he laughed and joked with Pete, I couldn't stop thinking about the ten years I had spent trying to save him. I thought about the Weaver’s Silk I had painstakingly sewn into his skin, the nights I had spent crying over his shaking form, the fortunes I had spent on neural-architects. It had all been for nothing. It was less than nothing. It was the frantic scratching of an ant trying to repair a crack in a mountain, only for the mountain to decide it didn't like the crack and simply cease to have it.

The cognitive dissonance is a physical weight in my chest. I revere Godric. I love him more than I love my own life. He is the perfect King, the savior of our race, the man who reached out and saved my husband from a nightmare he himself had accidentally created. I am grateful beyond words. And yet, I am terrified. I am terrified of a being who can break a man with a joke and fix him with a whim. I am terrified of the absolute ease with which he manipulates the fabric of our souls. 

Rogr leaned over and kissed my forehead, his skin smelling of the jasmine from the King’s garden. "He’s so kind, isn't he, Rosa?" Rogr whispered, his eyes shining with a terrifyingly pure devotion. "To think he noticed someone like me. To think he cared enough to fix me."

"Yes," I said, my voice trembling as I forced a smile. "He is the best king one could hope for. We are so lucky, Rogr. We are so very lucky."

Behind us, the imperial spire receded into the distance, a needle of white marble piercing the perfect, controlled sky. Somewhere inside, Godric was probably laughing with Blake again, perhaps planning another prank, or perhaps just watching the stars. And out here, in the paradise he built, we would continue to grow stronger, wiser, and more perfect every day, forever living in the shadow of a kindness that was more terrifying than any cruelty the universe had ever known. I held Rogr’s hand, feeling the incredible, newfound power thrumming in his pulse, and I prayed that I would never again be the subject of my King’s sense of humor. I prayed that I would always be small enough to remain unnoticed, even as I spent the rest of my life praising the name of the man who could unmake me with a blink. Pete started singing a hymn to the King, and after a moment, Rogr joined in, his voice strong and certain. I opened my mouth and joined them, the harmony perfect and hollow, echoing through the golden, curated air of our perfect, terrifying world.
