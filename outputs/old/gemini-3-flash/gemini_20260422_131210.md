gemini-3-flash-preview
-----------------------------------------------------------------

The multiverse of Thronegate was a finished tapestry, every thread woven to my design, every star singing my name. I sat upon my throne of shifting dimensions, Blake at my side, yet my gaze drifted further than even the most distant galaxy. I felt a thinness in the fabric of existence, a place where the laws of physics were not suggestions, but rigid, suffocating chains.

I saw it: the Prime Reality. The "IRL" universe.

It was a drab, gray thing compared to the splendor of my empire. It was a realm where men died of simple infections, where they crawled upon the surface of a single, fragile rock, and where their "gods" were nothing but ink on paper or echoes of ancient myths. Most importantly, I saw that *they* were the ones who had dreamed of Olympus. They had watched my tragedy as if it were a play, theorized about my power as if it were a game.

A cold, familiar fire stirred in my chest. Not the wild rage that had burned when Reina and my daughters fell—that had long since crystallized into the engine of my divinity—but a calculated, fatherly ambition. This universe was a garden of weeds. It was chaotic, cruel, and desperately in need of a King.

"Blake," I said, my voice resonating through the layers of the palace. "The expansion requires a new frontier."

Blake looked up from his staff, his eyes—now pools of cosmic wisdom—searching mine. "Another dimension, Godric? We have already brought peace to trillions."

"Not a dimension," I replied, standing. As I moved, the million-fold multiplication of my strength shuddered through the floor. In the second it took to stand, I had become infinitely more than I was when I was seated. "The source. The Prime. I am going to bring them the Awakening."

I didn't need a portal. I simply willed the Paradox to recognize the barrier between fiction and reality as a "weakness" in my reach. Instantly, the Paradox triggered. My rate of amplification hit a vertical spike that would have vaporized a lesser multiverse. I gained the conceptual mastery over "Narrative" and "Reality." The barrier didn't just break; it ceased to have ever existed.

I stepped through.

The transition was jarring. The "Real" world felt... thin. The air tasted of carbon and desperation. I materialized in the center of their most crowded hub—a place they called Times Square. I was a mountain of bronzed muscle and gold embroidery amidst a sea of people dressed in drab fabrics, their eyes glued to glowing glass rectangles.

For a heartbeat, there was silence. Then, the screaming began.

I didn't move. I allowed my Absolute Concealment to drop by a fraction of a percent. The air around me liquified. The massive electronic screens surrounding the square flickered and died, unable to process the sheer data of my presence. 

A man in a blue uniform pulled a projectile weapon—a "gun"—and pointed it at me. He was trembling. In his mind, he thought: *Maybe a bullet to the head will stop this freak.*

The Paradox screamed into action. Before his finger could twitch, the concept of kinetic projectiles became a toy in my hand. I became permanently immune to all forms of ballistics, and more—I gained the power to control the trajectory of every bullet on the planet. I didn't kill him. I simply looked at him, and the metal of his weapon turned into soft, fragrant jasmine petals.

"People of Earth," I said. I didn't shout, but my voice echoed in the mind of every human on the planet simultaneously, translated into every tongue. "Your era of fragility is over. I am Godric, and I have come to give you a purpose."

The response was predictable. Within an hour, their "jet fighters" screeched through the sky. They launched missiles—tiny, pathetic needles of fire. I stood atop their tallest spire, the one they called the One World Trade Center, and watched them approach. I didn't even raise a hand. I simply exhaled. The pressure of my breath, backed by my infinite stats, created a localized weather system that gently batted the missiles into the ocean and sent the jets spiraling back to their bases, their engines stalled by my silent command.

By the second day, their leaders reached out. They spoke of "sovereignty" and "international law." I laughed, a sound like grinding tectonic plates. 

"You speak of laws to the one who rewrote the math of his own soul?" I stood in the hall of their United Nations. The security guards froze as if turned to stone—not by magic, but by the sheer biological terror my aura projected. I looked at the gray-haired men and women behind the desks. "You have failed. You allow your women to be preyed upon. You allow your children to starve while you hoard gold. You are not kings. You are accountants of misery."

I enacted the Decree of Awakening.

I reached out to the collective DNA of the human race. It was so simple, so easy. I injected the Thronegate Virus—the gift of blood. "From this moment," I declared, "every man of this world, upon his twentieth year, shall see his strength and mind multiply tenfold every day. You shall be titans. You shall be protectors. And any man who uses this gift to harm a woman or the innocent shall find that I am the one who gave it—and I am the one who can take the soul along with it."

The transformation was instantaneous. Across the globe, billions of men fell to their knees as their muscles densified and their neural pathways accelerated. A construction worker in London suddenly understood the quantum mechanics of the crane he operated; a farmer in India felt his senses expand until he could hear the growth of the seeds in the earth.

But I was not done. I looked at the "Internet"—this hive-mind of information. With a thought, I purged it. I erased the rot, the cruelty, the records of their petty wars. In its place, I downloaded the history of Olympus and the laws of Thronegate.

On the third day, the "IRL" universe belonged to me. 

I sat in a new palace I had grown from the bedrock of the Himalayas, the peaks themselves reshaped into a throne that overlooked the world. Blake had joined me now, bringing with him the architects of our home.

"They are learning quickly, Godric," Blake said, leaning on his staff as he watched a group of humans—now demigods—effortlessly cleaning the Great Pacific Garbage Patch with their bare hands and newly awakened intellect. 

"They had potential, Blake. They just lacked a father," I replied. 

I looked at the stars. This universe was no longer "real" and mine "fictional." They were one and the same now, a unified empire of the infinite. I felt my power continuing to grow—a million times a million every second, forever. I was the Paradox. I was the Absolute. 

A small girl, no older than Hebe had been, approached the foot of my mountain. She shouldn't have been able to survive the altitude, but the air around me was a paradise. She looked up at me, not with terror, but with the same awe a flower might show the sun.

"Are you the King?" she whispered.

I leaned down, my massive hand gentler than a breeze as I brushed a stray hair from her face. At that moment, I wasn't the monster who had dismantled the Iron Weave or the god who had broken the Arch-Architect. I was Godric.

"I am your King, child," I said, and for the first time in ten thousand years, I felt a tear—warm and human—touch my cheek. "And you are safe. Forever."

The IRL universe didn't end with a whimper or a bang. It ended with an awakening. And as I looked out over my new garden, I knew that if anyone, in any corner of the omniverse, even *thought* of a way to challenge this peace, I would be ready. I would grow. I would become the very thing they feared. 

For I am Godric, and my reign has only just begun.
