The morning sun warmed the back of Blake’s neck as he knelt in the garden, his fingers brushing the soil around the roots of a crimson bloom. The flower, a Royal Heart, pulsed with a gentle inner light, its health a reflection of the household’s harmony. From the kitchen window, he heard the soft clatter of porcelain. Lina was preparing breakfast. The sound was as much a part of his world as the distant, crystalline hum of the Obelisk at the city’s heart—a constant, soothing reminder of His presence.

“The eggs are almost ready,” Lina called, her voice carefully neutral.

Blake smiled, a genuine warmth spreading through his chest. “I’ll be right in.” He stood, brushing dirt from his trousers. At forty, he felt a vitality that hummed just beneath his skin, a well of power so deep and placid he rarely had to draw from it. He could, if he wished, feel the individual vibrations of the air molecules around him, hear the rustle of a worm three feet under the soil. *The Awakening*. A gift from the God-King. For every day since his twentieth birthday, his strength, his senses, his very mind had grown tenfold. Twenty years of compounding grace. He could shatter a mountain with a whisper, outthink the grandest historical library in a blink. And he could, with a thought, reduce all that to the gentle, careful strength of a man tending his garden, so as not to crush the petals of his wife’s flowers.

He entered the kitchen. Lina stood at the stove, her posture straight. She was thirty-five, her dark hair woven into a simple, practical braid. She placed a plate before him: perfect golden yolks, toasted bread, a sprig of garden herbs.

“It looks wonderful,” he said, touching her wrist. Her skin was warm. She didn’t pull away, but the motion was stiff, a door held slightly ajar but bolted from within.

“Thank you,” she murmured.

They ate in a silence filled with the sounds of the city awakening. Through the open window, they saw the silver arcs of the Sky-Carriers gliding along their prescribed routes, men of commerce and administration heading to their posts. In the public gardens, women gathered children, their voices a melodic chatter. The world was ordered, prosperous, safe. It was a world built by divine will, a father’s firm hand guiding his children to greatness.

“The Anniversary Parade is tomorrow,” Blake said, sipping his tea. “The children are excited to see the God-King’s procession.”

Lina’s knife scraped the plate. A tiny, sharp sound. “Yes. A great day for the kingdom.”

Blake heard the hollow space in her words. He had heard it for fifteen years, a faint dissonance in the harmony of their life. He knew her history, of course. Everyone did. She was a descendant of Elara the Defiant, one of the leaders of the Great Uprising. It was a mark of the God-King’s infinite mercy that her bloodline was allowed to continue, to integrate and find peace. Blake believed, with every fiber of his being, that Lina had found it. She was a good wife, a capable manager of their home. Whatever resentment once festered in her line had been washed clean by the blessings of this world. He was sure of it.

“He gives us everything,” Blake said, not as a rebuke, but as a comfort. “Peace. Purpose. Power to protect what we love.”

Lina looked up, and for a fleeting second, her eyes held something old and stormy, a memory of a different sky. Then it was gone, smoothed into placid acceptance. “He does,” she said. “We are very fortunate.”

*150 years ago. The Day of Revelation.*

The air above the Plains of Ascension did not vibrate with fear, but with a terrifying, crystalline hope. One billion souls, armored in photon-forged ceramite, stood in formations so vast they curved with the horizon. They were not an army of desperation, but of supreme confidence. For ten millennia, they had watched King Godric rule. He was immortal, yes—a curiosity, a fact of the world like gravity. But he was a *man*. He bled, they assumed. He could be tricked, outmaneuvered, *killed*. His favoritism towards male-dominated structures had stifled half the world’s potential. Today, the other half would rectify that error.

At their head stood Elara, Lina’s many-times great-grandmother. Her voice, amplified by sonic projectors, swept across the billion. “He is one man! One ancient, stubborn man clinging to a dead idea! Today, we teach the king the limits of his reign!”

A roar tore the sky apart.

Before them, the royal citadel was silent. No legions marched out to meet them. No defensive shields shimmered to life. The grand gates simply swung open.

And Godric walked out.

He looked as he always had in the public portraits: mid-thirties, powerfully built, a handsome face framed by a salt-and-pepper beard. He wore simple grey trousers and a white tunic, no armor. His hands were empty. He strolled onto the plain as if taking a morning walk, a faint, friendly smile on his lips.

The silence that fell was heavier than the previous roar.

Elara signaled. A single shot, a lance of compressed star-matter capable of boiling a continent, screamed from a cannon ten miles back. It crossed the distance in a microsecond.

Godric blinked.

The lance stopped, frozen in the air an inch from his forehead. He reached up and plucked it from the air like a annoying insect. He examined it, the friendly smile still in place. “Interesting,” he said, and his voice, calm and warm, was heard by every single being on the plain as if he stood beside them. “You’ve been busy.”

Then, he *looked* at the cannon. Not at the operators. At the physical structure of the weapon, ten miles away.

It ceased to be.

There was no explosion, no flash of light. One moment, a mountain of polished metal and humming energy existed. The next, it was a perfect, silent sphere of atomic dust, drifting in the breeze. The 50,000 women operating it were simply… missing from reality. Not dead. *Undone*.

A wave of primal dread, cold and oily, washed through the army. This was wrong. This was not a man.

Elara, her heart a drum of terror, screamed the order. “ALL UNITS! MAXIMUM FIRE! ERASE HIM!”

What followed was not a battle. It was a physics lesson taught by a bored, condescending god.

The sky turned white with annihilation beams, singularity projectiles, reality-disruptor waves. The very fabric of space was targeted, to unweave the ground beneath his feet.

Godric stood still, allowing it all to hit him.

The light and sound that should have shattered the planet died at the surface of his skin. He didn’t glow. He didn’t strain. He absorbed the symphony of extinction with the passive indifference of a mountain absorbing rain.

He sighed, a sound of mild disappointment. “You see,” he said, conversationally, taking a step forward. His foot touched the ground. The tectonic plate beneath the continent cracked. A chasm miles wide opened, swallowing five million soldiers instantly. Their screams were lost in the grinding of planetary crust. “I’ve always been fair. I favored a system that worked. You took it as an insult.”

Another step. The air around him *thickened*. A hundred thousand flyers, streaking in at hypersonic speeds, suddenly found the atmosphere solid. They compressed into a single, grotesque ball of metal and flesh the size of a small moon, which hovered for a moment before crumbling into fractal dust.

A brilliant strategist in a command bunker, her mind augmented by quantum computers, had a single, desperate thought: *He must have a core, a source! A psychic frequency to disrupt! Find the frequency!*

In the nanosecond that thought formed—before it could become a plan, before it could be transmitted—Godric turned his head and looked through layers of rock and steel, directly at her.

*Pop.*

Her consciousness, and the consciousness of every telepath linked to her network, expanded. It expanded past the planet, past the galaxy, past the local galactic cluster, into the screaming, infinite void between realities. They perceived, for a timeless instant, the true scale of the thing they had challenged. Their minds, unable to process a consciousness that was, in that very second, multiplying its own vastness by a factor of one million squared, dissolved into absolute madness before their bodies even had time to hit the floor.

Godric had gained, and instantly mastered, perfect psychic annihilation. He also became permanently immune to it.

He walked now through the ranks. He didn’t swing his fists. He didn’t gesture. He simply *existed*, and reality broke around him like glass around a stone.

A battalion of elite warriors, their armor capable of surviving supernovae, charged. Godric glanced at them. The concept of ‘forward momentum’ ceased to apply to their bodies. They flew apart, every molecule accelerating in a different random direction, painting a kaleidoscopic mist of gore across the plain.

He spoke to a group trying to open a dimensional rift to bury him in a collapsing universe. “Cute,” he said. He breathed in. The rift, and the branch of reality it led to, was sucked into his lungs and erased from the omniversal record.

He was playing with them. He was revealing, with grotesque, pedagogical slowness, the truth he had hidden for 9,500 years.

He stopped in the very center of the obliterated army, where perhaps a hundred million terrified souls still stood, frozen in catatonic horror. Elara was among them, her weapon shaking in her hands.

“You thought I was just a man,” Godric said, his voice still kind. “I am not. I haven’t been for a very, very long time.” He spread his hands, a beatific, welcoming gesture. “On my thirty-fifth birthday, something wonderful happened. I stopped. And then, I started. Every second, since that moment, I become one million times more than I was the second before. In every way. And every second, I gain a new way to end everything you know.”

He pointed a finger at the sun. It went out. Not dimmed. *Extinguished*. The sky plunged into blackness, lit only by the dying lights of their weapons and the soft, terrible glow of Godric’s eyes. A new, smaller sun ignited above his fingertip, casting long, stark shadows.

“The only reason this planet, this universe, still exists,” he continued, as women wept and tore at their own eyes, “is because the first power I ever got was a very good restraint. It scales with me. It holds me back.”

He looked directly at Elara. His warmth vanished. What remained was an infinite, glacial emptiness. The friendly mask was gone, revealing the unforgiving calculus beneath.

“You dared,” he said softly.

He didn’t kill her then. He showed her. He rewove the fabric of her perception so she could witness, in explicit detail, the next ten seconds of his existence. She felt ten new apocalyptic powers bloom in him, each one capable of sterilizing the galaxy, their potencies inflating beyond mortal comprehension. She felt his strength, already incalculable, multiply by a number that had no name. She perceived the infinite, recursive layers of the shield that kept his mere *presence* from vaporizing reality, a shield that grew more absolute every microsecond.

Her mind broke not from horror, but from sheer ontological overload.

Godric let her drop, a drooling, empty thing.

He addressed the remnants of the billion, and the watching world. “The uprising is over. Your society has failed. I will build a new one. A better one. From this day forward, men will receive my blessing. ‘The Awakening.’ A fragment of my own nature, to keep order. A daily reminder of the power that guards this world, and the consequence of challenging it.”

He looked at the broken, bleeding plain, at the survivors who wished only for death.

“The patriarchy,” Godric said, his friendly smile returning, “lives.”

***

Blake finished the last of his tea, the memory of the history-tapes vivid in his mind. He felt no horror, only a profound, humbling awe. The Great Uprising was not a tragedy; it was the painful, necessary surgery that removed a sickness from the world. Godric had not been cruel. He had been *clear*.

He stood and took his plate to the sink. Lina was already there, washing up, her back to him. He moved behind her, placing his hands gently on her shoulders. He felt the tension there, a tight cord of ancient history.

“You carry so much,” he murmured, his voice infused with the gentle power he wielded like a surgeon’s scalpel. “You don’t have to. The struggle is over. We have peace.”

He felt her shoulders tremble once. A single, silent sob she would never give voice to. Then she stilled.

“I know,” she whispered to the soap bubbles. “We have peace.”

Later, as they walked to the market under the serene, sun-drenched sky, Blake felt a surge of love for his world. Children laughed. The Obelisk hummed its constant, soothing song. Men and women moved in their ordained harmonies. Above it all, in the immaculate palace, the God-King watched, a friendly, vigilant father whose strength renewed itself a millionfold with every heartbeat.

There was no fear in Blake. How could there be? They were protected by a love so vast it had once extinguished a sun to make a point. He looked at his wife’s profile, at the calm mask of acceptance she wore, and he believed—he *knew*—that she too, in her heart, was grateful. This was the way the world was meant to be. Ordered. Safe. Eternal.

He took her hand, his touch infinitely careful, and led her home.
