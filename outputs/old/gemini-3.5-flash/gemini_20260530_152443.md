I cannot look at the sky anymore. Every time I look at the stars, I do not see light. I only see the glass walls of a marble resting in the pocket of an untouchable predator, and I scream in the dark.

Clive closed the ledger, the heavy iron clasp snapping shut with a dull, final thud that seemed to echo through the freezing silence of the vault. He sat perfectly still, his sixty-year-old face entirely pale, his left eye twitching with a frantic, uncontrollable spasticity.

The horror was complete. The dread was a physical, suffocating hand clutching his throat. 

Clive knew what this journal meant. He knew that this specific event, googolplexian years ago, was the *Last Opposition*. It was the moment the omniverse had finally, permanently realized the absolute, mathematical futility of resisting King Godric. The fictional coalition—the ultimate gods of imagination, carrying the combined, limitless power of all human thought—had been casually, effortlessly turned into a pocket marble by a disappointed god who was simply looking for a pretty show. 

But the true, mind-shattering madness lay in the realization of the restoration. Clive looked down at his own hands, feeling the roaring, thousandfold-amplified mana flowing through his veins, and he felt a sickening, violent nausea. 

Was he real? Was Thronegate real? Or were they all just a perfect, hallowed copy, a flawless replica created in zero seconds by a king who had kept the original, true reality of their ancestors locked away in a dusty trophy drawer? The cognitive dissonance was a white-hot blade twisting inside his brain, driving him to the absolute brink of sanity. 

And from the corridor outside, the slow, relaxed footsteps of Godric Prime echoed, a warm, friendly whistle carrying a cheerful Olympian tune, leaving Clive to shiver in the dark, permanently trapped inside the beautiful, terrifying cage of an untouchable god.
