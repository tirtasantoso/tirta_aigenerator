They were the supreme, reality-warping deities of shonen anime, who could shatter conceptual laws with a shout; they were the blue-clad, solar-powered alien gods of comic book lore, who could push stars with a sigh; they were the six-jeweled, glove-wearing cosmic tyrants who could erase half of existence with a snap of their fingers; and they were the legendary, multi-phase video game bosses, who commanded world-shattering choral music and summoned meteors to crush their foes. Trillions of fictional universes had banded together, pooling their absolute, unyielding plot armor and conceptual invulnerability into a single, massive vanguard. They believed, with the arrogant, unshakeable confidence of characters written to always win, that their narrative destiny made them invincible. They believed that because they were the heroes and supreme villains of their own stories, they could never be defeated by a physical god.

Lord Godric was sitting in a simple, folding lawn chair on the observation deck of the imperial vanguard, sipping a cold glass of lemonade. He had been feeling incredibly, profoundly bored for the past several centuries, his absolute omniscience leaving him with a dry, hollow exhaustion. When the heralds first announced the approach of the Fiction Coalition, Godric’s eyes had suddenly lit up with a genuine, boyish excitement. He did not expect any of their combined attacks to actually harm him—such a concept was a mathematical impossibility—but through his omniscience, he knew that these fictional entities were famous for their theatricality. He knew they possessed attacks with pretty colors, blinding visual effects, multiple dramatic phases, and incredibly epic, banger boss battle music. 

As the ultimate, unbeatable final boss of reality, Godric was genuinely excited. He wanted a show. He wanted to be a spectator to the greatest, most spectacular display of theatrical violence the omniverse had ever conceived. He was so eager to be genuinely "surprised" by the visual spectacle that he actively, deliberately used his mind to distract himself, temporarily blocking the specific, omniscient pathways of his brain that monitored their attack preparation. He wanted to see it fresh. He wanted to enjoy the performance.

He sat there, smiling warmly, and patiently allowed them to prepare.

The Fiction Coalition spent what felt like an eternity charging their ultimate, combined attack. They spent three consecutive weeks channeling their energy, their cosmic power-ups, their ultimate limit breaks, and their reality-warping spells into a single, massive point of blinding, iridescent light. The shonen heroes screamed, their hair changing through a dozen different glowing colors as their power multiplied exponentially; the video game bosses hovered in rigid, majestic formations, their choral music blaring through the vacuum of space in a deafening, reality-shattering crescendo; and the cosmic entities aligned their energy, creating a visual display of spinning, geometric rings of starlight that filled the entire sky. The sheer, physical pressure of their preparation was so immense that the surrounding space-time within forty decillion dimensions began to warp and buckle, unable to contain the raw, theatrical weight of their narrative might.

Godric watched them with a look of pure, unadulterated anticipation. He was practically vibrating with excitement in his lawn chair, his salt-and-pepper beard twitching with a wide, friendly grin. "Look at those colors, Osvald!" he whispered to me, his voice holding the joyous, innocent wonder of a child watching a firework display. "And that music! The brass section in that choral track is absolutely phenomenal! I can’t wait to see what they do when they actually hit me!"

At long last, the preparation was complete. The Fiction Coalition unleashed their ultimate, combined attack—the "Genesis of the Final Story."

It was a torrential, blinding beam of pure, conceptual and physical destruction, a barrage of every elemental force, every reality-warping spell, and every narrative-slaying strike ever conceived in the history of imagination. The beam descended upon Godric like a roaring, multi-colored dragon of absolute erasure, its visual effects so intense that they physically burned the optical nerves of my amplified eyes, forcing hot, steaming blood to run down my cheeks.

Godric did not move. He did not activate his infinite passive shields. He did not call upon his sentient auras. He stood up from his lawn chair, spread his arms wide, and let the attack hit him straight in the chest.

The impact was a blinding, deafening explosion of starlight, blood-red energy, and howling, choral music. The shockwave of the collision was so massive that it instantly, violently vaporized ninety-eight octillion surrounding multiverses, reducing their planets and stars to subatomic dust in less than a fraction of a nanosecond.

I fell to my knees, shielding my face, my heart hammering a frantic, terrified rhythm against my ribs as I waited for the smoke to clear.

When the dust finally settled, Godric was standing in the exact same spot. His simple dark tunic was entirely unblemished. His salt-and-pepper hair was perfectly in place. The ultimate, combined, and narrative-shattering strike of trillions of fictional universes had done absolutely nothing to him. It had not even left a microscopic mark on his skin. It had not even registered as a faint, physical tickle to his resting biology.

But the true horror was not that he was unharmed. The true horror was the expression on his face.

The warm, boyish excitement was entirely gone. His ruggedly handsome features had hardened into a mask of deep, profound, and incredibly cold disappointment. He looked down at his chest, then looked out over the massive, trembling vanguard of the Fiction Coalition, and let out a soft, heavy sigh of pure, unadulterated boredom.

"Is... is that it?" Godric asked, his rich baritone voice carrying a flat, hollow ring that made my blood run cold. "Where were the multiple phases? Where was the theatrical transition? The music just sort of... stopped. And those colors were incredibly tacky. That was the most boring, disappointing show I have ever seen."

The Fiction Coalition did not have time to process their failure. The realization that their ultimate, narrative-shattering plot armor had failed to even tickle a simple man in a dark tunic broke their collective minds. The shonen heroes fell to their knees, weeping tears of ink and blood, their hair turning a dull, lifeless gray as their confidence collapsed; the video game bosses hovered in silent, trembling shock, their majestic choral music dissolving into a pathetic, whimpering static.

In his mild, disappointed irritation at the terrible quality of the show, Godric decided to end the diversion. He did not draw a weapon. He did not use his magic. He simply, accidentally, unleashed a microscopic, unquantifiable fraction of zero percent of his physical strength.

The physical, mechanical consequence of that actionless decision was a display of visceral, raw, and grotesque slaughter that will haunt my soul until the end of time.

The Fiction Coalition had spent weeks constructing what they believed were "indestructible" infinite shields, layers of conceptual and physical invulnerability designed to protect them from any possible counter-attack. The moment Godric’s physical strength expressed itself, those shields did not shatter; they were violently, instantly overpowered, crumbling into microscopic, jagged shards of non-existence like cheap, rotting glass under the weight of a falling mountain.

The physical pressure wave generated by his resting knuckle was so immense, so ridiculously superior to the physical laws of the universe, that the vanguard of the coalition was physically, brutally dismantled in a fraction of a millisecond. 

The shonen gods, who could warp reality with a thought, found their physical structures violently, physically peeled apart. Their skin was flayed from their muscles in thick, steaming sheets of raw meat, their ink-blood splattering against the void of space in a high-pressure, pressurized geyser. The blue-clad alien gods did not get pushed; the sheer pressure of Godric’s strength caused their hyper-dense, cellular bodies to physically implode, their bones grinding into a fine, white powder that erupted from their mouths and eye sockets in a wet, heavy mist of liquefied brain matter and cerebrospinal fluid. The six-jeweled tyrants found their hands violently crushed, their fingers snapping like dry twigs, the jewels on their gloves shattering into worthless, non-magical dust before their arms were torn from their shoulders in a torrent of boiling, arterial blood.

The legendary video game bosses thrashed in the void, their multi-dimensional bodies being physically, methodically squashed into inside-out sacks of screaming, raw flesh. Their organs—lungs, hearts, and intestines—were squeezed through the microscopic pores of their skin, raining down upon the silent, dead vacuum of space like a horrific, crimson monsoon. Their screams of absolute, unmitigated physical agony were silent, stolen by the physical vacuum of the void, but I could see their jaws unhinge, their teeth cracking and shattering as they thrashed in their final, excruciating moments of life.

But the physical pressure of his strength did not stop at the Coalition. 

The sheer, unfathomable weight of that microscopic 0% of his physical strength was so astronomically high that the entire possible existence—every single multiverse, every timeline, every dimension, every physical coordinate, and every parallel reality that had ever existed or could ever exist—was instantly, violently de-atomized. The physical fabric of space-time was torn to shreds, and under the crushing, physical weight of Godric's resting power, the entire de-atomized mass of the omniverse was violently, physically compressed.

I watched in raw, mind-shattering horror as decillions of multiverses were squeezed inward, their planets, their stars, and their histories crushed into a single, high-density point. The compression was so violent and so absolute that the entire possible existence was reduced to an infinite-mass point smaller than a single, subatomic quark.

There was no space. There was no time. There was only Godric, and me, preserved by his passive aura, floating in an absolute, silent void of non-existence.

Godric looked at the sub-quark-sized point of infinite mass floating in front of him. He reached out, his massive, calloused fingers gently pinching the invisible, hyper-dense point of compressed existence. He did not appear to exert a single ounce of effort. He lifted the infinite mass of the entire possible existence as if it weighed absolutely nothing, his hand perfectly steady, his facial muscles completely relaxed. 

With a casual, amused smile, Godric used a fraction of his creative power to enlarge the point to the size of a simple, glass marble. He wrapped it in a transparent, glowing container barrier—a protective seal designed to prevent its infinite gravitational pull from destroying the surrounding void of his immediate area—and held it up to the light.

Inside the glass marble, I could see them. I could see the tiny, swirling, and compressed infinity of decillions of multiverses, spinning in a state of eternal, frozen agony. I could see the microscopic, screaming faces of the fictional heroes and the cosmic deities, their bodies permanently crushed and fused into a single, high-density slurry of liquid bone and crystallized blood, trapped inside the marble as a permanent, silent trophy of his disappointment.

And then, the ultimate, terrifying mechanism of his existence engaged.

Godric became even more unquantifiably, monstrously more powerful. It was not because he had absorbed the energy of the slaughtered coalition—their combined power was a absolute, mocking zero to his baseline, incapable of increasing his strength by even a fraction of a percent. The power-up was far more deranged.

The Evolved Weakness Paradox had been triggered way before the attack commenced, the moment the fictional universes first assembled and believed that their written, narrative plot armor could exploit a weakness in his godhood. But because these were fictional entities—beings who existed as the absolute, conceptual limit of human imagination and narrative writing—the omniverse itself went into a state of violent, groveling hysteria. The metaphysical rules of reality "felt bad" that fiction itself had dared to write a limitation for our King. To apologize for this supreme, impossible audacity, the omniverse violently, permanently overcorrected.

It broke the very concept of narrative and conceptual limits for Godric. It did not merely grant him immunity; it permanently, aggressively elevated his baseline power so that he transcended the very concept of limits, scaling his stats and superpowers to a level of layered, trans-infinite exponentials that completely deleted the concept of limitation from the fabric of reality. He had become infinitely more omnipotent, his power multiplying at a rate that made his previous, already broken scaling look like a pathetic, static crawl, purely because fiction had dared to oppose him.

Godric slipped the marble into his pocket, letting out a soft, relaxed chuckle. "Well, Osvald," he said gently, his rich voice completely warm and friendly again. "It’s a bit quiet out here now. Let’s go ahead and fix the decor."

With a simple, silent decision, without moving a single muscle, Godric effortlessly and instantly decided that the omniverse should be whole again. In exactly zero seconds, the entire possible existence—every multiverse, planet, star, and timeline—was restored to its perfect, original coordinates. 

But the Fiction Coalition remained dead, permanently erased from history.

And the marble trophy remained in his pocket.

I am writing this from my chambers, my body shaking so violently that the blood from my nose has stained the parchment. I look out the window at the beautiful, glittering spires of Thronegate, at the citizens laughing in the streets, and I am drowning in a cold, suffocating tide of pure, unadulterated existential horror. 

Because the marble is still in his study.

If the original, true omniverse—with all its physical matter, its timelines, and its original souls—is still compressed, screaming, and fused inside that glass marble on Godric's desk, then what is this reality that we are currently living in? What am I? What is my family? Are we nothing more than a flawless, identical facsimile? Are we just a simulation of a simulation, a perfect copy created by his casual whim, while our true, original selves are still trapped inside that glass bead, screaming in eternal, high-density torment? The sheer, mind-breaking terror of this realization has permanently severed my hold on sanity. We are nothing. We are the toys of a god who can erase, copy, and restore existence as easily as a man changes his clothes, leaving us to live out our perfect, happy lives in a replica world, completely unaware of the marble resting on his mantlepiece.

Clive closed the journal, the heavy leather cover shutting with a sound that felt like the dropping of a guillotine. He sat perfectly still in the silent, dark archive of Year 999...9+, his face completely pale, his hands cold as ice. 

He finally understood. He finally understood why there had been zero opposition for a googolplexian span of years. This was the event that had permanently broken the will of creation. When the entire possible existence, including the supreme, reality-warping heroes of fiction itself, had been physically crushed into a sub-quark-sized marble purely because they had disappointed our King, the omniverse had finally, permanently learned the absolute, mathematical futility of resistance.

Clive slowly raised his eyes, looking toward the far corner of the library where Godric’s private study lay. Through the open door, resting on the simple wooden mantelpiece, he could see it. 

It was a small, transparent glass marble, glowing with a soft, pulsing violet light. Inside, if he focused his thousandfold-amplified senses, Clive could hear them. He could hear the faint, microscopic, and infinite screams of decillions of multiverses, spinning in a state of eternal, compressed agony, while the identical, restored version of those very same multiverses lived out their happy, peaceful lives outside the palace walls.

The cognitive dissonance was a physical, screaming agony in Clive’s mind. He loved his Uncle Godric. Godric was warm, kind, and had just baked him fresh pastries that morning. But as Clive stared at the glowing glass marble, he knew with absolute, terrifying certainty that they were all living in a beautiful, flawless graveyard, completely at the mercy of an apex god predator whose casual, effortless disappointment was the only absolute law of existence.
