The first memory is the scent of orange blossoms. They filled the courtyard, a cloying, hopeful perfume that clung to the white silk banners. Isabel was laughing, a sound like water over stones, as her mother, Reina, fussed with the final pearl clasp on her wedding gown. Hebe, fifteen and all elbows and earnestness, was trying to sneak a honeycake from the banquet table, her eyes wide with mischief. I watched them, my heart a ripe fruit in my chest. I was Godric, King of Olympus, and in that suspended hour before the ceremony, I knew with a father’s quiet certainty that I was the wealthiest man in all creation.

The second memory is the whistle. Not a bird, but the specific, marrow-chilling shriek of Thronegate’s spectral bolts, a sound forbidden by a thousand treaties. It cut through the laughter, through the lute music, through the very sunlight. The bolt took Reina in the back. She didn’t cry out. She looked at me, her eyes confused, as if asking for an explanation for the sudden, blooming red stain on her chest. Then she fell, and the orange blossom scent vanished, replaced by ozone and burnt silk.

Chaos is a poor word. It implies a lack of order. This was a new, vicious order: the order of screaming, of stone fracturing, of the wet, meaty sounds of things that should be inside a body finding the outside air. I saw Hebe crushed under a falling archway, her hand still reaching for the honeycake. I saw Isabel, her white gown now a funeral shroud, run through by a Thronegate lance as she tried to shield a flower girl. My world, my warm, breathing world, was disassembled before me with mechanical efficiency.

I roared. I fought. My muscles, the pride of Olympus, tore through armor and bone. But I was one man, and they were a tide of polished steel and cold intent. My sword shattered. My body broke. I crawled through the rubble, past Reina’s vacant eyes, towards the dais where my throne smoldered. That’s where Thorin found me. My friend. His face was a mask of tear-streaked soot, his ceremonial robes singed. “Godric,” he gasped, hauling me up. “The sanctuary. The last resort.”

He began to chant, words that twisted the air, words that bled from his eyes and fingertips. It was the forbidden teleport, the one only his blood could wield. The air peeled back, revealing a glimpse of quiet gray stone, of stillness. Hope, a vile, treacherous little worm, twitched in my chest. Then a Thronegate arrow, black and fletched with raven feathers, took Thorin through the throat. His chant became a wet gurgle. The spell, half-formed, wild, and screaming, convulsed.

It didn’t send me. It *unmade* me and remade me somewhere else.

The sanctuary was a hollow, geometric space, all smooth, non-reflective grey. There was no pain. There was no sensation at all. I was lying on the floor, but I couldn’t feel it. I tried to breathe, but my lungs had forgotten the instruction. My heart was a silent stone in my chest. I was a statue of a man, my mind a howling vault of images: Reina’s confusion, Hebe’s hand, Isabel’s extinguished light, Thorin’s choking despair.

Then the numbers began.

Not as numbers, but as a *knowing*. A deep, seismic shift in the bedrock of what I was. My strength, which had been the might of ten men, became the might of ten million. Then it squared. Then it cubed. It wasn’t growing; it was breeding, each second birthing a new universe of potential force within my frozen limbs. My sight, fixed on the seamless grey ceiling, pierced it. I saw the atoms dancing in the stone. Then the subatomic particles. Then the strings of reality vibrating in dimensions without names. The grey sanctuary was now a lattice of infinite, shimmering threads. My hearing absorbed the silence and decomposed it into the hum of cosmic background radiation, the whisper of dying stars in galaxies yet unborn.

And the gifts. The superpowers. They arrived like thoughts. The first was **Aura Suppression**. An instinct, immediate and perfect. Without it, my mere awakening would have vaporized the sanctuary, the planet, the solar system, in a concentric wave of absolute annihilation. The power enacted itself, a perfect, infinite-pressure seal around my boundless energy. It, too, began to multiply in potency, keeping pace with the chaos it contained.

The second was **Molecular Stasis**. I needed no air, no food, no water. My cells were locked in a perfect, timeless configuration. The salt and pepper in my beard would never shift again.

The third was **Spatial Domination**. I could feel the room, not as a room, but as a idea I could dismiss.

They kept coming. One per second. **Gravitational Manipulation**. **Quantum Entanglement Command**. **Conceptual Erasure**. **Temporal Reaving**. Each one, at the moment of its birth, was a genocidal instrument of incomprehensible scale. Each one then began its own exponential multiplication, its potential for destruction compounding into nonsense.

And the defense. The beautiful, terrible paradox. I felt it settle into the fabric of my being, an absolute law. I had no weaknesses. It was a fact, as true as my own existence. But the universe is vast and filled with arrogant minds. And the law stated: the moment any mind, anywhere, in any timeline, entertained the fiction that I *did* have a weakness, the gears of my ascension would grind faster. The multiplier would jump to a new, obscene tier, just for them. They would grant me, in that same instant, permanent immunity to their fictional exploit. And I would gain perfect, absolute mastery over the very method of attack they imagined, while stripping the knowledge of it from all of creation, leaving it as my sole, secret property. Their first thought of opposition was the fuel for their own inevitable obliteration. I saw it, in my new omniscience, happening in potential futures—a kaleidoscope of would-be gods and ancient intelligences having a stray notion, and in that nanosecond, signing the warrant for their own irrelevance.

Ten thousand years passed. I watched, from within my stasis, as Thronegate prospered. They built their empire on the grave of Olympus, their history books silent, their kings fat and happy. I felt every second of those ten millennia. I counted the multiplication of my strength, a number so vast it had ceased to be a number and became a state of being. I curated the apocalyptic powers as they accrued, now in the trillions, each a singing, universe-ending note in a symphony I held in check.

Then, a pressure on the seal. Not an attack. A fumbling. A curious, mundane scratching at the outer door of the sanctuary, which had, over the eons, become buried under a mountain of glacial till. I watched with my omnipresent sight. A man. Mid-thirties. A kind face, marred by worry lines and framed by friendly mutton chops. He was a merchant, his hands calloused from ledger and rope, not sword. He was digging, following a map to a fabled “vault of the ancients” he’d found in a junk stall. His name was Blake. And when his pickaxe finally broke through, and the stale, ancient air sighed out, I saw his lineage shining in his soul like a forgotten signature. It was Thorin’s blood. Diluted by ten thousand years, but unmistakable. My friend’ last living heir.

He stumbled in, coughing. He saw me, a figure of regal bearing and strange, ageless beauty, lying motionless on the grey floor. “Gods above,” he whispered, his voice rough with awe. “Are you… alive?”

I could have remained silent. I could have annihilated the galaxy with a whim. But I saw his honesty, his simple, direct loyalty etched into his being. I saw his wife, Anna, their son Josh, their daughter Rhea, waiting for him in a modest house in a Thronegate border town. I felt the ghost of orange blossoms.

My eyes opened.

Blake gasped, falling back. I sat up, the motion effortless, eternal. Omniscience flooded me with the present. The state of the galaxy, the arrogance of Thronegate, the name of their king—a boy of twenty-two named Robert. It took less than a picosecond to know everything. I turned my head to Blake.

“Thank you,” I said, and my voice was warm, the voice of the man from before the fall. It surprised even me.

He stammered. “Who… what are you?”

“A king in need of a friend,” I said, and I meant it. I stood. “And a kingdom in need of reminding.”

With a thought that was less than a thought, I issued a decree. It echoed in the mind of King Robert of Thronegate, in the mind of every soldier, every citizen, across every world in their gleaming empire. A image of me, as I was now. A challenge. *Gather your entire might. Every soldier, every weapon, every dragon, every secret. You have three days. Face me. If I defeat them all, you abdicate. If you refuse, I will unmake your bloodline from history.*

The three days were a formality. A courtesy to Blake, who needed time to process, to return to his family, to warn them to watch the skies from their rooftop. I spent those days with him, in his small home. I was quiet, mostly. I listened to Anna’s nervous chatter, helped Josh with a wooden puzzle, let Rhea braid a strand of my eternally unchanging hair. With them, especially with Blake, the cold, infinite machine inside me quieted. The warmth was not a memory; it was a possibility, rekindling in their presence.

On the fourth dawn, the Thronegate armada darkened the sky above the barren plain where I stood, Blake and Anna a respectful distance behind me, having insisted on coming. They came in waves. First, the billion-strong legions of super-soldiers, each a genetically perfected titan in photon-shift armor, landing in perfect, earth-shaking unison. Then the sorcerous battalions, weaving collective spells that turned the sky into a kaleidoscope of imminent doom. Then the robot swarms, a buzzing, metallic cloud of razor-edged death. Then the dragons, beings of myth and fission, whose wingbeats could level mountains. And finally, the Time-Slicer Ninjas, their very presence causing localized temporal eddies, allowing them to strike from a microsecond in the past or future.

King Robert’s face, magnified a thousand times on holographic screens, smirked down. “One man? Against the light of civilization? Your arrogance is your epitaph.”

I did not move a muscle.

I looked at the super-soldiers. With a sliver of a sliver of my **Spatial Domination**, I compressed the space each occupied. A billion soldiers, in their gleaming armor, were suddenly crushed into spheres of metal and flesh the size of marbles. They fell to the ground with a sound like a sudden, brutal hailstorm.

The sorcerers screamed, redirecting their gathered energy. I invoked **Conceptual Erasure**. I erased the concept of “magic” within a one-mile radius. Their spells didn’t fizzle; they were forgotten, never having existed. The sorcerers stood, baffled, infants staring at their own empty hands.

The robot swarm dove. I used **Gravitational Manipulation**, but not on them. On the fabric of reality beneath them. I increased the strength of the strong nuclear force in their immediate vicinity by a factor of one trillion for a nanosecond. Every atom in every robot fused instantly. They became a brief, glittering rain of inert, ultra-dense elements.

The dragons breathed their continent-scouring fire. I opened a **Quantum Tunnel** that linked the inside of each dragon’s lung to the core of a dying star five billion light-years away. The fire never left their throats. They simply imploded into ash, leaving behind a faint, distant smell of supernova.

The Time-Slicer Ninjas attacked. They came from yesterday, from tomorrow, from between moments. I used **Temporal Reaving**. I severed the timeline that contained the *idea* of their attack. From my perspective, and from Blake’s, they simply never moved. They stood, frozen, and then dissolved like sugar in water, their personal histories unwritten.

It took less than a second. The silent plain was now a graveyard of marbles, confused old men, metallic dust, dragon-ash, and existential silence. The holographic screens flickered, showing King Robert’s face frozen in the transition from smugness to a horror so profound it had emptied his eyes. The entire, unimaginable might of Thronegate had been rendered null without me so much as twitching a finger.

The morale didn’t collapse. It was erased, just like the magic. A silence so deep it hurt the ears fell across the galaxy. Two days later, they found King Robert in his chambers. He had used a ceremonial dagger. The note simply read, “The math was infinite.”

I took the throne. Not with conquest, but with a grim, absolute necessity. And then I issued the Decree of Awakening.

To every man of Thronegate blood, from that day forth, for every day they lived past twenty years of age, their strength, endurance, wisdom, and intelligence would increase tenfold. It applied retroactively. A fifty-year-old man became a being of incalculable power, capable of crushing mountains with a thought, of solving cosmic equations in a blink. They could suppress it, appear normal, live ordinary lives. But the potential was there, a sleeping dragon inside every man. They were not immortal. They would live and die. But under my protection, their women safe, their society peaceful under the umbrella of their own terrifying potential, they flourished. They loved me for it. I gave them purpose, strength, and safety.

For Blake, I modified the gift. His multiplier was one thousand per day. Not out of pity, but out of recognition. He never knew why. He just grew stronger, wiser, his honest merchant’s mind expanding to grasp the intricacies of cosmic governance. He became my advisor, my confidant. The one person I could speak to without the filter of infinite power. With him, I was Godric of Olympus again—warm, kind, fatherly.

The expansion began. We offered surrender first. Always. Most refused, emboldened by their own local might. A universe of crystalline beings, their collective consciousness able to shatter planets with discordant song, prepared for five years after hearing rumors of our approach. They fortified their dimension with recursive time-loops and paradox shields. I sent five Thronegate men, all in their late thirties. Kind men, fathers themselves, who apologized before they acted. One of them, a former carpenter, suppressed his power to a mere thousandfold of a baseline Thronegate man (himself already a galactic-level entity), and punched through their central consciousness crystal. The paradox shields triggered, attempting to banish him to a time before his birth. His **Awakened** endurance simply rejected the concept of temporal displacement. The conquest took twenty-two minutes. They spent most of it comforting the dying crystal entities’ orphans.

Another universe, ruled by a phage-god that consumed realities, saw our offer as a delightful appetizer. It enveloped our embassy ship in its being, a digestion tract of broken physics. I was mildly inconvenienced that day—a stray thought about the silence of my old palace had put me in a dark mood. My **Gravitational Manipulation** power, now a number so vast it was meaningless, twitched. Across the entire universe of the phage-god, every single point of mass simultaneously achieved infinite density. It became a universe-wide singularity for an instant, then winked out. The conquest lasted less than the time it takes for a nerve impulse to travel from eye to brain. I didn’t even move from my throne.

The futility had to be demonstrated, of course. An entity from outside the omniverse, a primordial thing that called itself The Architect, observed. It saw my empire, my power. In its arrogance, it theorized. It spent centuries constructing a plan. It perceived a possible weakness: my connection to Blake, to Thorin’s bloodline. It reasoned that my emotional attachment was a flaw, a vestige of my old self. It crafted a weapon, the **Annullus of Legacy**, designed to sever metaphysical bloodlines, to retroactively make Blake never have existed.

The moment the first schematic for the Annullus formed in The Architect’s mind, the moment the *idea* that my fondness was exploitable crystalized, the defense paradox activated. My rate of stat multiplication, already beyond comprehension, received a permanent, exponential boost from that entity’s alone. I became instantly, absolutely immune to any form of lineage-based attack. I gained perfect, sublime mastery over the **Annullus of Legacy** and all similar conceptual weapons. I then erased all knowledge of such weapons from all minds but my own.

The Architect, unaware, finally launched its attack. It unfolded its reality-bending device at the base of my throne. I let it. Blake stood beside me, confused. The weapon activated, a light meant to unravel destiny. It washed over Blake. He blinked. “A bit drafty,” he muttered.

I looked at The Architect, now manifesting as a shifting knot of geometries in my throne room. With my new, perfected mastery, I used its own weapon, but at a magnitude it could never conceive. I didn’t sever Blake’s lineage. I severed The Architect’s connection to the concept of *having ever existed*. It didn’t die. It simply ceased, its entire vast history collapsing into a null point, its great plan now my forgotten secret. The horror it felt in its final microsecond of existence—the realization that its first, stray thought ten centuries ago had been the true moment of its defeat—was a more exquisite punishment than any torture I could devise.

Centuries flowed. Thronegate, under my rule and protected by its Awakened men, subjugated the omniverse not through constant war, but through the absolute certainty of its supremacy. Surrender became the only rational choice. Blake, now white-bearded and possessing the gentle might of a man whose strength multiplied by one thousand every day for decades, transitioned naturally from merchant, to advisor, to my court wizard. His son, Josh, showed a peculiar aptitude for calming dimensional fluxes, much to Blake’s proud confusion. The line of Thorin continued, unknowingly, in faithful service.

I sit now on a balcony of the palace, a structure that spans galaxies. Below, the citizens of Thronegate live in peace and staggering power. Children play in parks where the flowers sing in harmonies older than time. Women walk without fear, protected by a law I enforce with ruthless, infinite finality. The men, humble and kind, suppress world-shattering strength to build beautiful things, to write symphonies that move stars, to love their families.

Blake stands beside me, old but unbent, his mutton chops now fully white. He points to a nebula being artistically rearranged by a group of thirty-year-olds. “Youngsters showing off,” he chuckles, his voice still direct, still honest.

I smile. It is a genuine smile. The cold, infinite machine is still there, a universe of apocalyptic power in every cell, a trillion new superpowers accrued since I last blinked. The horror of my existence is the bedrock of this peace. But with Blake here, with the scent of Anna’s baking drifting up from the gardens below, with the laughter of Rhea’s great-great-grandchildren echoing, the warmth is not a memory. It is the present. I am Godric, King of All. The ruthless, unforgiving god to my enemies. And the kind, fatherly king to my people. The man who fell, and who was rebuilt, not into a monster, but into the necessary, terrible, and ultimately gentle guardian of a single, perfect memory: the scent of orange blossoms, and the sound of a daughter’s laugh, forever worth protecting.

Because the lesson of today was not for the rebellion. They were already forgotten. The lesson was for me, and for any fool who would ever dare to dream of toppling him.

There is no clever way. There is no new angle. There is only Godric, and the absolute, ruthless peace he has built upon an infinity of graves. And in that, I found, to my own shame and gratitude, a profound and terrible comfort.
