The King's strength—a strength so infinitesimally deployed that the fraction I’d wager he used could not be written as a decimal, for the zeroes after the point would outnumber all the atoms in all the existences—propagated through the Goliath in a wave that reduced every cell to a state of eternal, conscious dissolution. The creature's form splintered into a trillion screaming fragments, each one isolated in its own pocket of frozen time, each one experiencing the full agony of being torn apart molecule by molecule, nerve by nerve, over and over without the release of death. And those fragments, still shrieking, were scattered across the omniverse as a rain of gore so fine it sparkled like crimson mist under the light of dying quasars. The collateral damage? The King's flex, if it can be called that, generated a shockwave that erased seven hundred adjacent universes. Their entire contents—civilizations, gods, histories—were gone in less time than it takes for a synapse to fire. The King later restored them, of course, with a thought, but I will never forget the silence that filled the gap where reality had been. I record this, Maren writes, because my son will need to understand exactly what we serve. The King is my friend, my protector, the warmest soul I have ever known, but his strength is the end of all things, and he deploys it with the absence of effort one reserves for brushing away dust.*

My fingers were trembling when I closed Maren's volume. The casualness of it—Godric patting a fist and erasing whole universes as a side effect—was a kind of horror that settled into my bones like cold water. But I kept reading.

The second entry I chose came from Year 611,000. The wizard was named Vaelis, the fifty‑first of our line. His handwriting was looser, less controlled, the script of a man who had never fully recovered from what he'd seen.

*Year 611,882. The King's durability is a concept that I no longer attempt to quantify. I used to think his skin, his cells, his very molecules were simply invulnerable. That was my mortal arrogance. Today, the Sovereignty of Infinite Hells—a coalition of demonic megastates from the Outer Abyss—unveiled the Spear of Ending. They had forged it by sacrificing the concept of "conclusion" from 100,000 reality‑clusters, making a weapon that could, by its very nature, end anything. They hurled it at the King at a speed that violated causality itself, so that it would strike before they had even decided to throw it. The spear struck the King’s chest. To be precise, it struck the outer membrane of a single skin cell on the King’s chest. The King did not react. He didn’t flinch, didn’t blink, didn’t alter his breathing—which, as we all know, he doesn’t need to do anyway. The spear, that artifact of absolute termination, shattered. Its fragments didn’t fly outward; they were imbued with the King's passive retaliation, a principle so deeply woven into his existence that no conscious thought was required. Each shard—and there were trillions of them—acquired sentience and a single purpose: to inflict upon the creators of the spear the experience of being "ended" without ever being allowed to fully die. The Sovereignty of Infinite Hells, all its demonic citizens, were each impaled upon an infinite number of these shards simultaneously. Their bodies would be erased, restored, erased again—a loop of excruciation that played faster than time could track. I could hear their screams across the void, a chorus so loud it broke the psychic shielding of a hundred allied mages. The King watched without expression. When the last demon had been flensed down to a wailing subatomic remnant, he turned to me and asked if I'd like some tea, because he could hear my heartbeat was irregular. The collateral damage this time was the Outer Abyss itself, which collapsed into a singularity of agony that the King had to manually unmake. He did so with a flick of his finger, but the memory of the screams, Vaelis, will never leave me. I write this as a warning: do not think that any weapon, any force, any reality‑ending artifact can so much as inconvenience him. His durability is not a shield. It is a law of existence.*

My throat was dry. I had grown up calling this man "Uncle Godric." He had taught me to play chess. He had attended my birthday celebrations and laughed at my clumsy first spells. And yet these journals described a being whose passive reaction to an attack had inflicted eternal torture on an entire civilization. I forced myself to continue.

The third journal was bound in something that felt like living skin, and its pages were smeared with ink that looked disturbingly like dried blood. It belonged to Orrys, the sixty‑third court wizard, Year 784,000.

*Year 784,215. The King's speed. I have no poetry for this, only facts, because poetry would shatter under the weight of what I saw. A syndicate of chrono‑assassins—beings who lived outside linear time, who could observe the entire history of an object and choose the perfect moment to strike—decided to eliminate the King from every point in his existence simultaneously. They called themselves the Unwritten, and they were so confident in their methodology that they sent a formal declaration of intent. The King received it at breakfast. He read it, frowned slightly at the parchment, and then set it aside to finish his toast. When the Unwritten struck—and they struck, I must note, from every angle, every moment, every possible branch of causality—the King simply… moved. No. That is wrong. He was already moving before they struck, because his speed is not subject to linear sequence. In the span of an interval I can only describe as zero seconds, the King had traversed the Unwritten's entire distributed existence—spanning nine hundred billion timelines—and, using the edge of his hand, had decapitated every member simultaneously. The decapitation was not fatal; he made certain of that. Each assassin's head remained alive, their consciousness imprisoned in the severed flesh, able to see and feel their own headless bodies twitching in the void around them. The speed of the act generated a temporal backwash that unspooled causality in the surrounding megaverse, causing history to rewrite itself a trillion times in the span of a heartbeat. Entire species were born, evolved, achieved apotheosis, and went extinct in the space between my breaths. The King later stabilized the timeline, but the psychic shock of those unlived lives echoed in my skull for decades. I have seen the King pause, mid‑step, to admire a flower. I have also seen him, without any visible effort, outrun the concept of time and leave a field of heads that scream in silence forever. Both are equally true. That is the terror, Orrys writes, and that is the warning.*

I stopped for a moment and pressed my palms against my eyes until I saw stars. My father had been right. This was not merely education. This was a forced confrontation with the absolute truth of what we served. But I had five more journals to read, and my father would expect me to finish them all before nightfall.

The fourth volume was thinner, almost a pamphlet, but its words were dense. The wizard was Thessan, the seventieth, Year 892,000.

*Year 892,441. The King's senses. It is a common misconception that the King merely "knows" things, that his omniscience is a passive database of facts. Today I learned that his senses are an active, predatory force. The Veilborn were beings that existed solely in the absolute un‑perception of all living things. They were not invisible; they were the concept of being unseen given will. They had plotted against the Crown for ninety thousand years, believing that because no one could perceive them, they were safe. The King, sitting in his study, reading a historical treatise, remarked to me that he was "tired of being watched." He then, without rising from his chair, without gesturing, without any outward sign, opened his senses by a fraction so minuscule that I cannot express it. He merely allowed himself to "notice" the Veilborn. The effect was immediate and catastrophic. The act of being perceived by the King was, for beings whose entire existence was predicated on being unperceived, an annihilation event beyond any physical death. Their unreality was torn apart by the sheer weight of his attention. Each Veilborn—and there were quadrillions of them—was dragged into the light of his awareness, and in that light, they burned. Not with fire, but with the raw, unfiltered experience of being known by an omnipotent mind. Their bodies twisted into abstract sculptures of agony, their minds unraveled into static screams, and their home dimension—the Unseen Realm—collapsed as its fundamental law of un‑perception was revoked. The collateral damage was the perceptual fabric of seventeen adjacent mega‑clusters, which temporarily went blind to all existence, causing widespread panic and the collapse of several telepathic empires. The King restored them, but not before a trillion souls had experienced sensory isolation so profound that some of them chose to unmake themselves rather than endure it. He was apologetic afterward, in his way. He said he hadn't realized the Veilborn were so fragile. Fragile. I am Thessan, and I record this so that future wizards understand: the King does not look for threats because the act of looking would end too many things. He restrains his senses to such a degree that the restraint itself is a miracle of continuous, loving self‑control.*

A shudder went through me. "The act of looking would end too many things." I thought of all the times Uncle Godric had turned his gentle gray eyes on me, and I suddenly understood the monumental pressure of what it meant for him to simply exist in our presence without annihilating us. The concealment spells, the suppression, the constant, active restraint—it was not just for him. It was for everyone. But when he chose to release even a sliver, the result was an atrocity beyond language.

The fifth journal was the most recent, from Year 950,000, written by my grandfather Luthan, the eighty‑eighth court wizard. His penmanship was shaky, the ink blotched as if he'd had to stop and steady his hand repeatedly.

*Year 950,003. The King's luck. I have seen him, in all my years of service, never so much as trip on a loose stone. This is not because the stones are always firmly set. It is because the very principles of probability and fortune have been recursively mastered by his presence. The Coalition of Last Hope—that was their actual name, a title chosen in desperation—had spent three hundred millennia developing a weapon that would not attack the King physically, but would rather invert his "luck matrix." They had theorized that all beings, no matter how powerful, were subject to some form of stochastic fluctuation, and that by inverting his, they could cause improbable, catastrophic failures to befall him. The weapon, a fortune inversion engine, was activated from a hidden base outside the omniversal boundary. I stood beside the King as the Coalition's best minds, the most powerful probability manipulators across all realms, turned their machine on. For a single, breathless moment, nothing happened. And then, the King's luck—a passive, unconscious statistical field that was amplifying by an incomprehensible factor every infinitesimal fraction of a Planck second—responded. It did not counter the inversion. It simply overwhelmed it, absorbed it, and then redirected the accumulated 'bad fortune' back upon its originators. But the King's luck, you understand, is not balanced. It does not dispense a proportionate response. Every calculation the Coalition had made was hijacked by the King's fortune and amplified into a nightmare of coincidences. Every weapon they had ever built, every defensive system, every contingency plan, every fallback position turned against them simultaneously. Fleet formations collided in a ballet of self‑annihilating shrapnel. War‑golems achieved sudden, calamitous sentience and turned on their masters, not in rebellion but in a frenzy of random violence that somehow, with perfect precision, maximized suffering before death. Every soldier found that their personal equipment had a previously undetectable flaw that caused the most agonizing possible malfunction at the worst possible moment—an armor seal that would cook them in their own plasma, a teleporter that would scatter their atoms across light‑years while keeping their nervous system intact and conscious, a medical nanite swarm that instead devoured their flesh from the inside out with surgical slowness. I watched this through the viewing portal, my stomach hollowing. The King, meanwhile, was discussing the finer points of Imperial tax reform with a delegate from the Corvantine Hegemony. He did not even glance at the slaughter. When the last Coalition member died—a death, I later learned, that involved being simultaneously crushed by a collapsing star and yet kept alive by a fluke of quantum entanglement for a subjective century of torment—the King paused mid‑sentence, tilted his head as if hearing a distant bell, and then continued his conversation. The collateral damage was minimal this time, limited to the Coalition's home universe, which had been converted into a self‑contained hellscape of perpetual, ironic punishment. The King never mentioned it. I record this as a warning, Luthan, to all who follow: do not attempt to use fortune, fate, or probability against him. His luck is not a shield. It is a predator that wears a grin.*

I closed Luthan's journal and noticed that my palms were slick with sweat. I had known my grandfather—a gentle man who kept songbirds and quoted poetry. He had written these words, had witnessed this atrocity, and had still served the King with unwavering loyalty. The dissonance was a physical lump in my throat.

There were more journals, scores of them, each detailing a different aspect of the King's supremacy: his charisma, which had once caused an invading armada to fall to their knees and claw out their own throats in rapture before they could fire a single shot; his endurance, which had allowed him to stand still for ten thousand years of concentrated universal bombardment without registering the slightest fatigue; his charisma again, which when deployed at a fraction of a whisper had turned a rebellion into a self‑sacrificing cult that immolated themselves in his honor. But the five I had read were enough to brand the lesson into my marrow.

I sat back in the chair, my eyes drifting to the final volume on the desk—a thin one, written by my own father, Gerold, ninety‑ninth court wizard. I hadn't opened it yet. Part of me didn't want to. But the compulsion for completeness, and the knowledge that he was waiting outside, forced my hands to lift the cover.

Inside was a single page, and the handwriting was my father's but tighter, more compressed, as if he'd wanted to get the words out before his nerve failed him.

*Year 1,000,000. I have seen the King end a threat by allowing a single hair to fall from his head, and that hair, imbued with the passive lethality of his being, slaughtered a dimension with the efficiency of a surgical plague. I have seen him forget to suppress his ambient magical pressure for a picosecond, and the resulting cascade erased one percent of the omniverse before he corrected it. The correction took no time at all. The terror never fades. It only grows, because every fraction of a Planck second, the King becomes more impossible to oppose. The gap between his current self and his self from a second ago is an abyss that language cannot bridge. My son will read this, and I need him to understand: the horror is not that the King is cruel. The horror is that the cruelty is an aspect of his justice, and it is absolutely inescapable. I love him. I fear him. These are the same thing now. Serve him well, Kaedan. There is no alternative.*

A rustle of fabric behind me. I didn't need to turn around to know who it was. The air had changed—not in any way I could sense magically, but in the instinctive prickle at the back of my neck, the sudden, absolute certainty that I was being watched by something that could unmake me with less effort than breathing.

"Well, my boy? Finished your reading?"

Uncle Godric's voice was exactly as it always was: warm, genial, shot through with an undercurrent of gentle amusement. I turned my head and saw him leaning in the doorway, his salt‑and‑pepper beard framing that rugged, permanently forty‑five‑year‑old face, his muscular arms crossed over his tunic in a posture of relaxed familiarity. He looked like he'd just come from the training yard or maybe from pruning the roses in the celestial gardens, not from being the subject of a library's worth of atrocity records.

"Yes, Uncle Godric," I said, and my voice, to its credit, did not shake. "I read them."

He nodded, unhurried, and stepped into the vault. The wards didn't so much as flicker—they wouldn't dare. "Your father insisted. I told him it might be a little much for a first day, but he said you were made of stern stuff. Blake's blood and all." A smile crinkled his eyes. "I remember your father's reading day. He didn't eat for two days afterward. Vaelis was still around then, you know—came out of retirement to hand over the journals personally. Gerold turned green when he got to the speed incident."

I swallowed. "The Borean Goliath," I managed. "The Unwritten. The Veilborn. You—" I stopped, because what could I say? You annihilated them in the most horrific ways imaginable? You visited eternal torment upon quadrillions?

Godric must have seen the question in my eyes, because his expression softened—not with guilt, never with guilt, but with the patience of a man who had explained this to thirty generations of wizards before me. "They opposed me, Kaedan. They took up arms against my rule. Against the peace I have built. I gave them, in proportional measure, the consequences of that choice." He said it without heat, as if reciting a law of nature. "I am not unjust. I am simply… thorough. Cruelty is a weapon I wield against those who would bring chaos where I have established order. It is not indiscriminate. It is targeted, precise, and absolute." He paused, and the warmth reentered his voice. "But to you, to your father, to all of Blake's line—I am your friend. Your protector. You know that."

I did know that. That was the worst part. The man who had just been described as a walking extinction event, who could erase the omniverse with an infinitesimal fraction of his stored power, who could perceive any threat before it coalesced into thought, who could outrun causality and shatter universes as an incidental side effect—that same man had held my hand when I scraped my knee at age six. He had told me bedtime stories about the adventures of my ancestor Blake, laughing at the parts where Blake, short‑tempered and loyal, had argued with him. He had, just last week, shown me a new constellation he'd made for my sister's birthday, a swirling galaxy of pink and gold stars shaped like her favorite flower.

The cognitive dissonance did not resolve. It simply sat there, a cold iron weight in my gut, coexisting with a love that was entirely real and entirely terrifying.

"Are you all right, Kaedan?" Godric asked, stepping closer. He placed a hand on my shoulder, and the touch was exactly as I remembered from childhood—solid, warm, impossibly reassuring. "You look pale. I told Gerold we should have waited until you were twenty‑five."

"I'm all right, Uncle Godric." I straightened my spine and closed my father's journal. "I understand now. The journals, the library, the mandatory reading—it's not just a warning. It's… a clarification. A lesson in what opposition to you truly means, so that I never make the mistake of underestimating you or, worse, sympathizing with our enemies."

Godric's eyes held a flicker of something ancient and unreadable. "You're a sharp one, Kaedan. Just like your grandfather." He gave my shoulder a squeeze and released me, turning toward the door. "Finish up here when you're ready. I've had the kitchens prepare something to settle your stomach—I know these records can be heavy going. Take your time."

He walked out, his footsteps fading into the corridor, and I was left alone in the honeycell of horrors, surrounded by the accumulated witness of ninety‑eight predecessors. Each of them had loved him. Each of them had feared him. Each of them had served until their dying day, because the King was not merely a master—he was the center of gravity around which all existence orbited, and to stand against that gravity was to be crushed into screams that would echo until the end of time.

I picked up my father's journal again and re‑read the last line. *Serve him well, Kaedan. There is no alternative.*

No. There is no alternative. The omniverse is vast, filled with countless civilizations and powerful beings, and they will, inevitably, attempt to oppose the Thronegate. They will craft weapons, forge alliances, and believe that their cleverness or their strength can prevail. And they will die in the most agonizing, hopeless, and utterly one‑sided massacres that reality can produce, because the King of Thronegate has no equal, no weakness, no possible path to defeat. Victory against him is not merely impossible—it was impossible before the first thought of opposition ever formed.

I closed the journal. Outside the vault, the palace hummed with the quiet, orderly life of the servants and courtiers, all of whom revered the King with genuine, heartfelt adoration. I would join them. I would serve him, love him, and when necessary, witness the atrocities that maintained his peace, because that was my bloodline's duty and my privilege.

But I would never, for as long as I lived, forget the screams that echoed through the pages I had just read. They were the music of the omniverse's true order, and they played in a minor key that would haunt me forever.

I am Kaedan, one hundredth court wizard of the line of Blake. I know my place. And I know, with absolute certainty, that anyone who dares oppose the King will wish—in those final, agonizing instants—that they had never been born.
