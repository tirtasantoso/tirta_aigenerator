The air above the marble dais tasted of honey and crushed myrtle. Sunlight fell through the open roof of Olympus’s Grand Pavilion in thick, golden columns, catching the white silk of Isabel’s wedding train as it pooled behind her like spilled milk. Godric stood at the altar, his forty-five-year-old frame a monument to the kingdom he had built with his own hands. His shoulders, broad as a blacksmith’s anvil, strained the fabric of his ceremonial tunic. The salt-and-pepper beard, trimmed neat as a hedgerow, framed a smile that had not faltered once since dawn. His grey eyes, warm as banked embers, tracked his daughter’s approach with a pride so fierce it ached in his ribs.

Isabel was twenty-two. Her hair, a cascade of oak-brown identical to her mother’s, had been woven with threads of starlight silver. She was laughing at something her sister had whispered. Hebe, only fifteen and already too clever for the court poets, walked half a step behind, her fingers laced with their mother’s.

Reina caught Godric’s gaze from the front row. She tilted her head, a silent, amused question passing between them across the heads of three hundred nobles. *Are you going to cry, my love?* Her lips pressed together, suppressing a smile. Godric’s own lips twitched. He gave an almost imperceptible shrug. *Probably.* Twenty-three years of marriage, and she could still unmake him with a glance.

To his left, Thorin stood with the ceremonial staff of the Court Wizard, a length of obsidian carved with the spiral of his bloodline. Thorin’s face, younger than Godric’s by a handful of years, was split by a grin that belonged more to a mischievous brother than the kingdom’s most powerful arcane mind. The two had grown up scraping knees on the Acropolis stones. Thorin’s loyalty was not a matter of oath but of marrow.

“You’re gripping that altar stone like you intend to throttle it,” Thorin murmured, his voice a low breeze.

Godric forced his hands to relax. The marble was cool and smooth. “Last chance to object,” he whispered back. “I could have you thrown into the fountain. Create a distraction.”

“As if you’d survive the diplomatic incident with your own wife,” Thorin snorted. “Just read the vows. Try not to sob. I’ve got ten gold pieces riding on your composure.”

The music from the string orchestra swelled, a sigh of violas and lyres. Isabel reached the steps. Her hand, trembling slightly, found Godric’s extended palm. The warmth of her skin, the tiny callus on her index finger from her obsession with charcoal sketching, grounded him. This was peace. This was the culmination of a life spent building, not conquering. Olympus had no enemies, for Godric had made none. His reign had been one of grain surpluses, repaired aqueducts, and the endless, patient stitching-together of fractured alliances.

He looked out at the assembly. Nobles in indigo and saffron. Children with sticky hands clutching stolen pastries. Sunlight glinting off goblets and polished brooches. He breathed in the scent of incense and fresh-cut olive branches. He opened his mouth to speak the first sacred line.

The eastern sky tore in half.

There was no sound at first. Only a visual distortion, a hairline fracture of impossible blackness that spiderwebbed across the perfect blue. Godric’s brain, honed by a warrior’s youth, processed the visual data even before his ears registered the screaming. The fracture was a portal. A rift in dimensional stability, a technique outlawed by every civilized magical treatise. And from that rift poured a flood of black-and-crimson steel.

The first projectile—a lance of spell-ignited iron—struck the southern colonnade. The explosion didn’t just destroy; it dissolved. Marble columns and the flesh of fifty guests vaporized into a pink mist that Godric’s mind refused to acknowledge. The pressure wave hit him a millisecond later, a physical fist that slammed him against the altar. His hand was ripped from Isabel’s.

The name that tore from his throat was not a word but a guttural rupture. “ISABEL!”

He saw her. A scrap of white silk, toppling backward. Hebe was screaming, a shrill, bird-like sound cut short by a secondary detonation that lifted the eastern seating section into the air. Bodies, or pieces of bodies, rained down. Reina was moving, her arms outstretched toward her daughters, her dark hair streaming like a banner. A fragment of molten stone, moving faster than any arrow, struck her in the back and punched through her chest. Godric watched his wife’s spine snap into an angle that a living body cannot achieve. She was dead before she hit the fractured marble floor. He knew this the way he knew his own name.

The knowledge did not stop him from crawling toward her. His hands slid through a slurry of blood, ash, and crushed petals. The sounds had returned now, a cacophony of horns, spellfire, and the distinct, rhythmic chant of Thronegate war mages. Banners unfurled above the carnage, a sigil of a clenched iron fist crushing a sun. King Aldric of Thronegate had been a ghost at the periphery of diplomatic tension for years, a bitter old tyrant nursing grievances over trade routes. But this... this was not a punitive raid. This was an extinction.

Thorin seized Godric by the collar of his tunic, hauling him backward with a strength that the wizard’s lean frame should not have possessed. “Get up, Godric! Get up, you are not dying here!” The ceremonial staff was planted between them, humming with a defensive barrier that shimmered like liquid diamond. The barrier buckled under a volley of Thronegate spellfire, cracks spiraling across its surface.

“My family,” Godric breathed. His eyes were fixed on the place where Reina had fallen. The word was a hollow shell.

“They are gone,” Thorin said, the words stripped of gentleness. There was no time. “And you will be too, unless you let me do this. The forbidden spell. The one my grandfather told me to burn. I memorized it anyway.”

Godric’s gaze slowly focused on his friend’s face. The barrier cracked again. A jagged piece of it fell away and shattered on the ground. “Thorin...”

“It’s a sanctuary. A pocket outside the flow of reality. The only place they cannot follow. I can send you there.” Thorin’s hands were already moving, tracing symbols in the air that bled black smoke instead of light. The obsidian staff was vibrating at a frequency that made Godric’s teeth ache. The spell was from Thorin’s bloodline, a secret passed through twelve generations of court wizards, an emergency escape that was never meant to be used because its cost was absolute.

Godric understood. He saw the resignation in Thorin’s eyes, the desperate, burning love. “No. Not you too. Not you.”

“My life for the King,” Thorin said, a ghost of a smile flickering across his lips. “It’s not just a pretty oath, old friend.”

A blade of pure, concentrated sound sliced through the barrier. It sheared through Thorin’s forearm, severing his hand at the wrist. The wizard did not scream. His eyes widened, the spell’s rhythm faltering for a single, catastrophic heartbeat. But his other hand, still gripping the staff, slammed the end of it against Godric’s chest.

“Live,” Thorin gasped.

A second lance of energy, brilliant and white-hot, pierced Thorin through the throat. The wizard’s body crumpled, the incomplete incantation tearing itself from his lips in a final, wet exhalation. The symbols in the air, half-formed and bleeding power, swirled into a vortex that seized Godric.

The world inverted. The screams of Olympus, the heat of the flames, the triumphant roar of Thronegate soldiers—all of it was sucked away into a funnel of absolute silence. Godric felt his body stretched and compressed, his consciousness screaming in a void that had no dimensions. The spell had been aimed at a sanctuary, but Thorin had died before he could give it coordinates, before he could anchor Godric to a place, a time, a state of being. The spell, a desperate, dying, mutilated thing, latched onto the only imperative it could parse from Thorin’s final thought: *protect him.*

And so, the spell did the only thing it could do. It broke reality around Godric. It found a pocket of absolute nothing, a fissure between seconds, and it sealed him inside it.

It took Godric a subjective eternity to realize he could not close his eyes. He could not blink. He could not draw breath because there was nothing to breathe. He could not move a muscle because his muscles were frozen, not in ice, but in a state of total temporal suspension. He was a statue of flesh, entombed in a dimension that had no light, no sound, no temperature. Yet his mind, his screaming, shattered, grieving mind, was perfectly, horrifyingly awake.

He stared into the blackness where his family’s faces had been. He replayed the moment of Isabel’s hand slipping from his grip. He replayed the sound of Hebe’s scream ending. He replayed the arc of Reina’s spine snapping. He replayed Thorin’s throat opening in a second mouth of crimson. He replayed it a thousand times. A million. A billion. The rage was a physical object inside him, a molten sphere of iron that had no outlet to burn.

And then, something fundamental in the architecture of his being—perhaps the mutilated spell, perhaps the sheer, impossible density of his fury and grief colliding with the vacuum of his prison—reacted to the environment. The sanctuary was a non-place. It had no laws. But a human being, even a frozen one, carries laws within him. Metabolism. Entropy. Time.

The stasis did not just freeze him. It *corrected* him. It observed the biological processes of aging, cellular decay, the need for air and sustenance, and it annulled them. The corruption of time was smoothed away from his flesh like a blemish from silk. He became a single, perfect, unchanging point of matter.

The correction did not stop. The spell had been commanded to protect him, and the void had no limits. The void observed the statistical parameters of a human king: his strength, his endurance, his wisdom, his speed, his senses, his intelligence. It observed the limiting factors: the tearing of muscle fiber, the depletion of glucose, the synaptic lag of neurons. And it began to multiply these parameters. The initial multiplication was by a factor of one thousand. But the void operated on a different clock. The smallest possible unit of time—the time it takes light to travel a Planck length—became the new metronome. Every single Planck second of his suspension, his multiplied stats were multiplied again. By themselves. A recursive exponentiation that had no ceiling, no asymptote, no theoretical limit at all.

And his body, his mind, needed to be protected from threats. The spell, in its mad, dying logic, interpreted this need with the literalness of a god. A new capability began to manifest within him. A superpower. An ability that could, in that very first second of its genesis, erase an entire civilization. The power was simple: the complete nullification of atomic cohesion in a localized field. With a thought, he could tell the bonds holding atoms together to simply cease. That was the first second.

The second Planck second, the potency of this power was also multiplied by one thousand, and then by itself. Now, the field was not localized. It could reach across a galaxy. Now, he could nullify not just atoms, but the forces of gravity, the bonds of quarks. The third second brought a new power entirely: chrono-quarantine, the ability to isolate a region of spacetime and annihilate it from the timeline. The fourth second brought a sensory ability, a perception that began as an awareness of every atom in his frozen body and exploded outward, an exponential bloom of consciousness that pierced the boundaries of his non-place prison and tasted the fabric of the universe. The fifth second brought a concealment power, an aura-suppression that instinctively recognized the sheer, cataclysmic horror of his own existence and wrapped it in layers of absolute nothingness, preventing him from collapsing the very reality he would one day wish to avenge himself upon. This concealment power multiplied at the same, terrifying recursive rate as the others, a perfect, adaptive sheath for a sword that was growing infinitely long and infinitely sharp.

The sixth second, the seventh, the eighth. Each tick of the Planck clock a new apocalypse downloaded into the soul of a bereaved king. The rage was no longer a sphere of iron. The rage was the fuel for a furnace that was forging a new cosmology, with Godric at the center, a point of singularity around which all of existence would eventually, inevitably, have to reorient itself.

But there was a final, more terrifying defense that was woven into his transformation. It was a paradox, a recursive loop that defied the logic of any possible aggressor. The state of Godric’s new being was absolute, utter invincibility. He had no weakness. It was a fundamental, metaphysical truth, as unchangeable as the concept of existence itself. But the universe was full of arrogant minds, beings who could not comprehend a true absolute. And so, the defense was this: the very instant any sentient creature, anywhere in the boundless hierarchy of the omniverse, formed so much as a fleeting, subconscious thought that Godric *might* possess a weakness—even a ludicrous, hallucinated one—the mechanisms of his empowerment would react. The recursive multiplication of his stats and powers, already an infinite acceleration, would receive a permanent, quantum leap in its rate. This would happen before the thought had even fully crystallized in the aggressor’s mind. Furthermore, the falsely perceived exploit would become permanently, utterly useless against Godric, a new immunity forged into his being. And most cruelly, Godric would instantly gain total, flawless mastery over the very attack or strategy the aggressor would eventually devise, gaining the ability to unleash it at any magnitude, while simultaneously denying the concept of its existence to anyone else in creation. Thousands of beings, across the incalculable span of his stasis, would commit this unknowing, suicidal act of hubris, each one a tiny, silent sacrifice that fed the inferno of his apotheosis, and not one of them would ever realize that their fate was sealed at the very moment of that first, idle thought.

Ten thousand years passed.

The galaxy spun on. Olympus became a myth, then a whisper, then less than dust. Thronegate, bloated on the spoils of a thousand conquered worlds, rose to become the galaxy’s supreme military and economic power. Their king, a fresh-faced twenty-two-year-old named Robert, sat on a throne of solidified neutron star and issued edicts to a hundred star systems.

And in a derelict stone chamber, buried beneath fifty meters of volcanic rock on a forgotten moon, the shield Thorin’s broken spell had woven finally, with the slow, agonizing creep of entropy, peeled back.

*Twenty years before the present day.*

Blake’s breath fogged in the air of the ruin. The lamp on his helmet cast jittering shadows across walls etched with a language that predated human settlement in this arm of the galaxy. His knuckles were raw from scraping through a rockfall that his survey scanner had insisted was a hollow cavity. Anna was going to kill him. He’d promised her he would be home three days ago, and Josh’s twelfth birthday was tomorrow. He had a toy starship, a model X-7 Raptor, wrapped in canvas in his cargo hold, and he had told Anna, with the solemn promise of a forty-year-old merchant who knew what was good for him, that he would be back in time for the party.

Instead, he was here. Chasing a mineral deposit that turned out to be fool’s quartz and his own stupid, stubborn curiosity. His mutton chops, a friendly bush of brown-grey that his daughter, Rhea, loved to tug, were itching with ancient dust.

The chamber was empty except for a single, central plinth. And on that plinth, a man. No, a statue. Blake’s first thought, the absurd, immediate one, was that it was a hyper-realistic art piece, a marvel of a lost civilization. The man was immense, muscles carved in a state of perfect, terrifying potency. Salt-and-pepper beard, short hair. Naked. And… in stasis. Blake’s merchant senses, a specific tingle he got around malfunctioning tech, immediately recognized the faint, ozone-like hum of a degrading temporal field.

“Okay,” Blake breathed to himself, his voice a dry rasp. “Okay. Don’t touch it. This is how people die in deep-space horror vids. You back away. You go home to your wife.”

His foot scraped on a loose stone. A tiny, insignificant pebble skittered across the floor and tapped the base of the plinth.

The temporal field collapsed with a sound like a soap bubble popping.

The man’s grey eyes opened.

Blake did not scream. He wanted to, but the sound died in his throat, strangled by an avalanche of pure, existential pressure. It was not a physical weight. It was a primal signal from the oldest part of his brain, the lizard-stem that remembered huddling in caves while predators stalked the night. It was screaming at him that the thing before him was not a man, not a god, but the thing gods had nightmares about. The pressure was a single note from a trumpet that could shatter galaxies, played so softly that it only liquefied Blake’s courage. It was the faintest whisper of an aura even the most potent concealment could never fully suppress, a flicker of a shadow cast by an infinitely high wall.

Then, as quickly as it came, it was gone. The man’s eyes, grey and depthless, focused on Blake. And the man smiled. It was a warm smile. A kind smile. A smile that recontextualized the handsome, masculine face into something paternal and friendly.

“You would be Blake,” the man said. His voice was a low, resonant baritone, smooth as river stones. “Thorin’s blood. Quiet. Dimmed by so many generations, but the note is unmistakable. A good man. Honest. Loyal. Short-tempered. You have a son named Josh and a daughter named Rhea. Your wife’s name is Anna. Her lemon cake is, by your own estimation, the finest in the sector.”

Blake’s mouth opened and closed. “How…?” It was the only word his brain could form.

Godric stepped down from the plinth. The movement was fluid, silent, terrifying in its absolute control. Every muscle fiber moved in perfect, non-wasted harmony. “A great deal has happened, my friend. My name is Godric. I was a king. And now…” He looked around the ruined chamber, his omniscience a silent, rushing river of data, tracing the ten millennia of decay in a microsecond. He saw Thronegate. He saw the current king, Robert, a pale echo of the warlord who had butchered his family. He saw it all. “…now I believe I have a wedding to avenge.”

Three days. Godric’s challenge had been broadcast across the entirety of the Thronegate network. A simple, un-encrypted audio message that had somehow overridden every single media channel, military frequency, and private communication device in the kingdom. The voice was calm, measured, and utterly without bravado.

“King Robert. My name is Godric. You do not know me, but the throne you sit upon is built from the ashes of my family. In three days, I will come to the plains of Aerion-9. If you abdicate your throne immediately, I will allow you and your lineage to live. If you wish to test my resolve, muster your entire military force. Every soldier, every weapon, every scrap of arcane might your kingdom possesses. Send it all. I will face it alone. When I have annihilated it, your abdication will be unconditional.”

The galaxy laughed. Then they were furious. Then they were curious. King Robert, an arrogant young man whose military advisors whispered of nothing but absolute victory, was ecstatic. An archaic adversary, a barbarian king challenging the greatest military machine ever forged? It was a gift. A spectacle to cement Thronegate’s perceived invincibility for another ten thousand years.

Blake and Anna stood on a hill nearly thirty kilometers from the designated battlefield. Blake had argued with Godric. He’d shouted, his short temper flaring, telling the ancient king he was insane. Godric had simply looked at him with that impossible warmth and said, “You are afraid for me. Your loyalty is a diamond, Blake. And I promise you, you and Anna will be perfectly safe. Watch. And you will understand.”

Anna’s hand was clenched in Blake’s. Her knuckles were white. The air was static, charged with the approaching storm of the Thronegate host. They came in a wall of steel, magic, and flesh that blotted out the horizon of the planet.

The super-soldiers alone numbered four billion. Each one a genetically sculpted demigod in powered armor that could withstand a direct nuclear strike, moving with a synchronized precision that was a ballet of death. Their boots marching in unison created seismic tremors that rippled through the ground.

Above them, the robot army formed a second sky. Ten billion autonomous war machines, from insect-sized nano-drones that could replicate in seconds to colossi striders that could level mountain ranges with their primary cannons. Their targeting systems, an interlinked AI gestalt, processed a trillion variables per microsecond, calculating firing solutions for a target that was not moving.

Behind them came the mages. A conclave of ten thousand. The youngest among them could unmake a star. The eldest could twist the fundamental constants of local spacetime into a pretzel. Their magic was a dull, aching roar that vibrated in the bones.

And behind them, so vast they warped the light of the setting sun, came the dragons. Continent-breakers. A flight of five hundred, each scale a fortress wall, each eye a furnace. Their wings were folded. They were there for cleanup.

And between the ranks, impossible to see unless they allowed it, were the ninjas of the Silent Moon order. They had pulled time backward to arrive before the challenge was even issued, and then waited, their speed a paradox that violated causality itself. They were already surrounding Godric on every conceivable plane of existence.

King Robert’s voice thundered from a billion warship loudspeakers in orbit. “Ancient relic! Witness the might of Thronegate! Your three days are up! My generals tell me you have no army. No weapons. You stand alone in the dirt. Your last words, fool?”

Godric stood in the center of the plain. He wore a simple tunic. He was not in a combat stance. His arms were relaxed at his sides.

His reply was not broadcast on a speaker. It was a telepathic transmission that bypassed every single mind’s defenses in the entire army.

*“I have already killed you. You simply haven’t finished dying yet. I will not move a muscle. I will not speak another word. Try to survive.”*

The silence that followed was the densest substance in the galaxy. It lasted two full seconds.

Then, King Robert, from his orbital command ship, sneered and issued the single-word order. “Obliterate.”

The robot army fired first. Every single war machine, from the nano-swarms to the striders, discharged their total energy output in a single, coordinated alpha strike. Lasers, plasma bolts, antimatter beams, graviton disruptors. The air turned into superheated, light-speed glass. The ground beneath Godric should have been turned into a temporary star. The combined energy output was sufficient to crack the planetary crust.

The energy arrived. And it simply ceased to exist. The light and heat and force, a quantity of energy that could have sterilized the star system, was absorbed into Godric’s form without a flicker of light. The stat of his endurance, a number that had been recursively multiplying by a thousand and by itself every Planck second for ten thousand years in stasis, and which had continued to do so, awake or asleep, without exception since his release, had no theoretical threshold. The universe could not offer enough energy to register on his perception. It was like trying to drown an ocean with a single raindrop.

A fraction of a Planck second later, before the light of the alpha strike could fully fade, Godric reciprocated. He did not attack. He simply un-did the concealment on one of his very first, most basic superpowers for the briefest of instants. The nullification field. The power that commanded atomic bonds to cease. Its potency had been multiplying for ten thousand years, a recursive exponentiation that had turned it into a command that applied to reality itself.

He thought, with the effort a normal man uses to dismiss a minor, fleeting thought. *Stop.*

Every single robot, all ten billion, from the nano-swarm to the striders, simultaneously came apart. Not an explosion. An un-becoming. Their chassis, their circuits, their AI cores, dissolved into a fine, grey, inert mist of elemental particles. The physics of their existence was simply overruled. The sky, a solid ceiling of war metal a moment before, turned to gentle, drifting ash that settled over the stunned army.

The super-soldiers did not have time to process the horror. Godric’s attention, vast and microscopically precise, turned to their minds. He exerted no physical force and no psychic attack. He simply leveraged the perfect mastery of sensory manipulation he had gained eons ago. He sent a single, direct sensory command to the brains of four billion super-soldiers: *Your soul is witnessing absolute truth. You cannot comprehend it. The mercy is that you will not remember this fear.*

The four billion super-soldiers dropped their weapons. Every single one of them, across the thousands of kilometers of front line, inhaled at the same instant. Their genetically sculpted, fearless minds were filled not with a projected image or a telepathic scream, but with a sliver of direct perception. Godric let them feel, for a millisecond, a single, microscopic fraction of the totality of his existence, the way a man might let an ant feel the heat of the sun from ninety-three million miles away.

It was still too much.

Their eyes rolled back. Their synapses overloaded. Their minds, built for the tightrope of combat, plunged into a chasm of cosmic despair from which there was no recovery. They collapsed onto the plains, all four billion, a patter of armored bodies falling into the dirt that lasted nearly a minute. Their hearts still beat. Their lungs still drew air. But their consciousnesses were shattered, locked into a coma of absolute ego-death. The sound of their fall was a soft, irregular thunder.

The dragons screamed. It was a sound of pure, animal terror. The riders, elite dragon-mages, spurred their mounts. The continent-breakers obeyed. Five hundred maws opened. Five hundred spiraling cones of dragonfire, each one an extinction-level event for a continent, lanced down toward the single, unmoving man.

Godric did not bother to nullify the fire. He let it wash over him. It felt like a lukewarm breeze. He focused, instead, on the ninjas of the Silent Moon order, those who had pulled time backward. He saw them, shimmering in their inverted temporal lines, their blades already cutting for the spot he had occupied three seconds ago. The paradox of their existence was trivial. Godric understood time with a complete, total mastery. He saw the guts of it, the architecture of cause and effect. He reached forward and backward simultaneously, holding the timestream in his mental hands like a ribbon of silk. He un-made their temporal loops. He snipped the causal threads that allowed them to exist in their reversed state.

The ninjas appeared. They were frozen. Their bodies, which had been slipping through time, were now locked in a single, static moment. They could see their blades, extended for a killing stroke that would never land on a man who no longer occupied the same linear timeframe they did. They were not dead. They were simply… paused. Removed from the flow of causality altogether. A living, breathing, infinite gallery of assassins made into fossils of frozen time.

The dragonfire was still washing over him harmlessly. Godric looked up at the five hundred continent-breakers. He used a power that allowed him to manipulate fundamental biological structures. He rewrote their nerve clusters. Specifically, the clusters that controlled fear response. He tuned the signal to maximum. Then he amplified it. By a factor he chose, a number that was merely one thousand, because he wanted them to have a few seconds of experience before the end.

The dragons’ terrified screams turned into choruses of absolute, mindless panic. They forgot their riders. They forgot their mission. Their brains, pumped with a flood of pure dread more potent than any chemical their bodies could produce, gave one, final, unified command: flee. They broke formation. They clawed at each other. Their massive wings, each sweep creating hurricane winds, tangled. They tore each other from the sky in a tumbling, screaming, flaming mass of flesh, scale, and dropped riders. The continent-breakers fell onto the plain, an avalanche of reptilian cathedrals, their impact shaking the planet’s crust more than the super-soldiers’ march ever had.

The mage conclave watched. They had not yet cast. The most powerful arcane minds in the galaxy had been frozen, not by a spell, but by a collective, dawning horror that was the death of their professional hubris. They saw the impossible being done with no effort. They saw magic, the supreme art, being made to look like a child’s clumsy finger-painting. The eldest mage, a man who had twisted stars, finally raised his staff. His voice was a rasp of pure, desperate denial.

“Illusions!” he screamed. “Psychic trickery! There is no power like this! He hides his weakness! There must be a… there must be a weakness!”

The mage’s thought was not an attack. It was not a plan. It was a fleeting, desperate plea to the architecture of the cosmos, a frantic search for a lever to move the immovable object. *He’s a mind-mage, a trickster, that’s how he did it. His weakness is physical, direct physical force. If we bypass his mind and just hit him with pure, un-thought kinetic power, we can…*

The moment the thought crystallized—*his weakness is physical, direct force*—the mage, in his arrogance, in his inability to accept an absolute, sealed his own fate. The thought triggered Godric’s absolute defense mechanism. Unbeknownst to the terrified mage, the formation of that single, erroneous, hopeful thought was a silent detonation. It caused Godric’s already-infinite stat multiplication rate to take a permanent, terrifying quantum leap upward. It rendered direct physical force not just useless, but a fundamental impossibility against Godric’s being, a concept he was now utterly immune to. And it granted Godric perfect, flawless, immediate mastery over the entire discipline of pure kinetic force in any possible form. The mage’s desperate hope had, before he could even draw the breath to articulate his plan, made Godric exponentially more powerful and removed the very hope from the board of reality. The mage, of course, knew none of this.

A hundred mages, rallying to their elder’s cry, unleashed a volley of pure kinetic force. No fire, no magic. Just solid, invisible, planet-cracking impact.

Godric allowed it to strike him. The force was not absorbed. It was simply not there. It was a concept his being no longer recognized as something that could interact with his existence. The kinetic energy was like a man trying to punch a ghost. The bolts passed through him and continued on their way, obliterating a mountain range on the far side of the continent. The mountain range simply ceased to be, a new sea where dry land had been. Godric’s tunic was undisturbed.

He turned his gaze to the mage conclave. He raised one eyebrow. Then he used his newly acquired, perfect mastery of kinetic force. He did not unleash it. He simply manipulated it with the precision of a surgeon. He reached into their bodies and, one by one, he stopped every single heart with a micro-kinetic pulse that was perfectly silent, perfectly contained inside their chests. Each mage simply stumbled, their eyes wide with shock, and slumped to the ground. A hundred, then a thousand, then the remaining nine thousand. The greatest magical conclave in the galaxy died with a series of soft, wet, internal pops, their arcane power rendered as useful as a prayer whispered in a hurricane.

Thirty-eight minutes had passed. King Robert watched the silent, unmoving dot on his command screen. He had not moved a single muscle. He had not spoken a single word after his opening declaration. And before him, the might of Thronegate lay in a silent, still, comatose, or simply un-made ruin. The battlefield stank of elemental ash and the metallic tang of fear from millions of frozen, panicked brains. The dragons, their minds so broken by fear they could not remember their own names, whimpered in a pile of broken wings. The ninjas were a sculpture garden of silent, time-frozen forms. The robots were dust. The mages were corpses with stopped hearts.

Two days later, on the orbiting capital world of Thronegate, King Robert, his hair having turned snow-white in forty-eight hours, was found in his private chambers. He had fallen on his own ceremonial dagger, though the angle was suspicious and his personal guards had fled the system. The official word was suicide. The throne was empty.

Godric entered the palace. The doors, gene-coded to the dead king, opened with a whisper. He walked to the throne of solidified neutron star, a thing of impossible weight and cold majesty. He did not sit on it. He touched it with one finger. The throne turned into a sculpture of living, flowering olive branches, a perfect replica of the one from his own lost throne room in Olympus, down to the exact placement of the leaves he remembered from a summer afternoon ten thousand years ago.
