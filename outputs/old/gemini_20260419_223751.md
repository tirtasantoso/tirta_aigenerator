The marble of the High Temple of Olympus glowed with the amber warmth of a setting sun that Godric believed would never truly descend. At forty-five, the King of Olympus stood as the pinnacle of masculine grace, his frame a testament to decades of disciplined conquest and just rule. His shoulders were broad enough to carry the weight of a world, encased in silk and golden plate that strained against the dense, functional muscle of his chest and arms. A neatly trimmed salt-and-pepper beard framed a jawline carved from granite, and his short, dark hair caught the light as he looked upon his daughter, Isabel. She was twenty-two, a vision of her mother’s ethereal beauty, standing in her wedding silks. Godric’s eyes, usually sharp with the tactical precision of a general, were soft, brimming with the fatherly pride of a man who had secured peace for his people. He reached out, his large, calloused hand gently squeezing Isabel’s shoulder, a gesture of profound tenderness from a father to his firstborn.

The air did not scream before the strike; it simply vanished. A roar of arcane fire and the shriek of metal-shredding ships tore through the atmosphere as the neighboring kingdom of Thronegate launched a betrayal that history would never find a name for. In an instant, the sanctuary became a slaughterhouse. Godric saw the ceiling collapse in a rain of white stone, pinning Reina, his queen, before a secondary blast of obsidian energy reduced her to ash. He lunged toward Isabel, his fingers brushing her veil, only to watch a Thronegate rail-slug tear through her torso, painting the white marble in a spray of crimson viscera. From the corner of his eye, he saw fifteen-year-old Hebe, his youngest, cowering behind a pillar before a squad of Thronegate executioners vaporized the column and the girl behind it. The warmth in Godric’s soul didn’t just die; it was replaced by a cold, howling vacuum that threatened to swallow his very sanity.

Thorin, the court wizard whose lineage had served the Kings of Olympus since the first stone was laid, stumbled through the smoke, his robes scorched and his staff cracked. He grabbed Godric’s arm, his eyes wide with a desperate, frantic loyalty. He began to chant in the gutteral, forbidden tongue of the Bloodline Gate, a teleportation spell so volatile it was struck from every record save for his own memory. As the runes began to spiral around them, a Thronegate spear, enchanted with soul-drinking frost, pierced Thorin’s throat mid-incantation. The wizard fell, his lifeblood spilling over Godric’s boots, and the spell, denied its anchor, fractured. The world turned inside out. Godric did not move through space; he was folded into the gaps between seconds, his mind fracturing as he watched his kingdom burn into a cinder in the heartbeat before he vanished into the white nothingness of the stasis sanctuary.

In the silence of that hidden void, the trauma calcified. The kind, fatherly king died in that white room, and something unforgiving and absolute took his place. The incomplete nature of Thorin’s sacrifice did not just hide Godric; it broke the laws of his biology. As he floated in the shimmering stasis, his heart stopped beating, yet he did not die. The need for air, for water, for the very concept of sleep was stripped away, leaving him an eternal sentinel of his own grief. At that exact moment, the amplification began. It was a silent, metaphysical roar. Every facet of his being—his physical strength, the speed of his thoughts, the acuity of his senses—began to multiply by one million, and then by the resulting sum, every single second. It was a geometric progression into godhood that the universe was never meant to sustain.

Simultaneously, the void birthed powers within him to match his escalating scale. Every second, a new apocalyptic capability manifested. One second, he gained the power to collapse the molecular bonds of an entire solar system with a thought; the next, he could rewrite the laws of causality across a galaxy. Each power’s potency multiplied at the same terrifying rate as his base stats. The only reason the omniverse did not immediately shatter under the weight of his presence was the very first power he manifested: the Absolute Veil. It was a concealment mechanism that grew just as fast as his power, a perfect dampener that held back his aura, ensuring that the sheer pressure of his existence did not erase all of reality before he was ready to claim it. He became a singularity of infinite potential, hidden in a pocket of time that had forgotten how to flow.

Within this state, a paradox of absolute defense formed around him. Godric had no weaknesses, and the omniverse itself rose to enforce this objective truth. If any entity in any dimension even brushed against the thought that Godric might have a flaw—perhaps an emotional tether or a physical vulnerability—the thought itself triggered a violent surge in Godric’s amplification rate. Before the entity could even formulate a plan, the "weakness" they imagined would become the seed of a new immunity within Godric’s passive shields. More terrifyingly, Godric would instantly gain perfect, absolute mastery over the very concept the enemy hoped to use against him. He would own their attack, their logic, and their power, and in his omniscience, he could simply erase the concept of that attack from the enemy’s reality entirely. They would find themselves standing before him, their weapons forgotten, their strategies vanished from their minds like smoke in a gale.

Ten thousand years passed in the world outside, though for Godric, it was an eternal, waking moment of sharpening his hate. Thronegate, the kingdom of traitors, had flourished. It had swallowed the galaxy, turning the bones of Olympus into the foundation of a sprawling, technological empire. The memory of Godric was gone, replaced by the sterile history of a victorious regime. Then came Blake. A thirty-eight-year-old merchant with friendly mutton chops and a reputation for honesty, Blake was a man of the earth and the trade routes. He moved with a directness that commanded respect, though he never sought power. He was a family man, devoted to his wife Anna, his twelve-year-old son Josh, and his eight-year-old daughter Rhea. Blake possessed a strange knack for finding things—lost heirlooms, hidden paths, and, eventually, the buried entrance to the sanctuary. He didn’t know why the ruins felt familiar, or why the ancient sigils on the door seemed to thrum in time with his own heartbeat.

When Blake broke the seal, the stasis shattered. Godric stepped out of the white light, forty-five years old and looking exactly as he had the day Olympus fell, yet his presence was so heavy it made the very stones of the cavern groan. Through his newfound omniscience, Godric didn't need to ask what year it was or who ruled. He saw the entire history of the galaxy laid out like a stained map. He saw the lineage of the kings who had succeeded the butchers of his family. He looked at Blake, and for a fleeting second, his cold eyes softened as he recognized a familiar resonance in the man’s soul—a echoes of a wizard who had died for him. Without a word, Godric broadcast his will across the stars, a telepathic decree that bypassed every shield and frequency. He challenged King Robert, the twenty-two-year-old ruler of Thronegate, demanding his abdication. He gave the empire three days to prepare, a grace period that was less an act of mercy and more a way to ensure the entire world was watching when the end arrived.

The three days were a fever dream of mobilization. Thronegate, believing itself invincible, gathered the greatest military force the galaxy had ever seen. Billions of super-soldiers in power armor that could withstand the heart of a star stood in ranks that covered entire moons. Thousands of dragons, their scales reinforced with neutronium, circled the capital world, their breath capable of melting tectonic plates. There were ninjas who moved with such localized temporal acceleration that they could strike a target yesterday, and robot armies that processed strategy at the speed of light. They arrived with a confidence that bordered on religious ecstasy, a wall of steel and magic intended to crush the ghost of a dead kingdom.

Godric arrived alone. He did not fly; he simply was there, standing on the atmospheric edge of the capital world. He did not move a single muscle. He didn't even raise a hand. He merely ceased to suppress a fraction of a fraction of his presence. The result was a symphony of gore and structural collapse. The super-soldiers didn't just die; the air inside their armor expanded so violently they turned into pressurized clouds of red mist that coated the ground in a thick, hot sludge. The dragons found their wings shriveled by a sudden, localized increase in gravity that slammed them into the earth with the force of falling asteroids, their massive bodies bursting like overripe fruit. The time-traveling ninjas, attempting to blink behind him, were caught in a stasis field of Godric’s passive will; they were frozen in place as their molecules slowly drifted apart, their screams stretched out over what felt like centuries to them but was only a second to the onlookers.

Blake and Anna watched from the deck of a merchant vessel, paralyzed as the sky turned the color of bruised meat. They saw the "unbeatable" fleet of Thronegate simply lose its cohesion, ships colliding and shattering as if the very concept of flight had been revoked. Godric walked through the carnage, his boots never touching the blood-soaked soil. He moved toward the palace with the nonchalance of a man walking through a garden, while around him, the elite guard spontaneously combusted or turned into pillars of salt. Two days later, King Robert, faced with the absolute, screaming horror of his army’s annihilation and the realization that Godric had not even begun to try, took his own life with a ceremonial dagger. Godric ascended the throne, not as a conqueror, but as the rightful owner reclaiming a stolen house.

His first act as King was the Decree of Awakening. It was a rewrite of the biological code for every man of Thronegate. From that day forward, every male who reached the age of twenty would find his strength, his mind, and his spirit multiplying tenfold every single day. They were not immortal, still bound to an eighty-year lifespan, but they became titans of industry and war. They could suppress this power to live normal lives or unleash it to shatter mountains. However, Godric’s grace had a secret center. Blake, the man who had freed him, received a modified version of this gift. While others grew by ten, Blake’s power multiplied by a thousand each day. He felt a strange, latent talent for the arcane beginning to itch at the back of his mind, a sense of "knowing" the structure of the world that he began to study in secret, unknowingly following the path of his ancestor Thorin.

Under Godric’s rule, the society of Thronegate transformed. The men revered their king with a terrifying, absolute loyalty, fueled by the godlike power he had bestowed upon them. Godric, in turn, enforced a code of absolute protection for women, a law born from the smoldering ashes of his love for Reina and his daughters. The citizens didn't just obey him; they loved him with a fervor that bordered on the divine. With a unified, empowered people, Godric turned his gaze toward the omniverse. Thronegate was no longer just a galactic empire; it became a cosmic predator.

The expansion was a sequence of inevitable surrenders. Godric would offer a universe the chance to join his fold, an act of mercy he genuinely believed in. When they refused, the end was swift. In one instance, a universe of sentient, hyper-advanced machines spent five years constructing a "reality-anchor" designed to negate all external magic and physics. They believed they were ready. Godric, feeling a slight irritation at the delay, didn't even send an army. He simply looked at that universe from across the dimensional veil. In a fraction of a second, the entire reality-anchor was repurposed into a giant monument to his reign, and the machine-mind was rewritten to sing hymns in his honor. He didn't even have to leave his throne; the sheer weight of his boredom was enough to subjugate a billion stars.

In another instance, when a universe of elder gods refused, Godric sent a squad of five Thronegate men. All were over the age of thirty-five, their power having multiplied tenfold every day for fifteen years. They were kind men, former farmers and tradesmen who apologized to the elder gods for what was about to happen. Then, with a collective exhale, they shattered the celestial spheres. The elder gods, entities who had existed since the dawn of time, were swatted aside like gnats. The defenders watched in hopeless despair as their ultimate cosmic spells washed over the Thronegate men like a light summer breeze. Within twenty minutes, the pantheon was in chains, and the universe was being integrated into the Thronegate logistics network. The conquered never received the Awakening; they remained as they were, forever shadowed by the burgeoning godhood of the Thronegate men.

The futility of resistance was best demonstrated by a being known as the Weaver of Fates, a trans-dimensional entity that resided in the cracks between realities. The Weaver observed Godric and formed a thought—a single, fleeting hypothesis. It believed that Godric’s power was tied to his memory of Olympus, and that if it could strike at the "weakness" of his grief, it could unravel him. The moment that thought took shape, the Weaver’s fate was sealed. Godric’s amplification rate spiked, his power doubling and squaring in a furious response to the perceived insult. Before the Weaver could even begin to weave its trap, Godric was already immune to fate-manipulation. Furthermore, Godric instantly gained absolute mastery over the Weaver’s own essence.

The Weaver found itself pulled from its hidden realm and dragged into the throne room of Thronegate. It tried to speak, to cast, to flee, but it found that it no longer understood the concept of "action." Godric sat on his throne, looking down at the entity with a cold, mocking pity. "You thought I had a flaw," Godric said, his voice a vibration that threatened to liquefy the Weaver’s form. "In doing so, you gave me your soul. I do not just defeat you; I deny your right to have ever existed as a threat." With a snap of his fingers, Godric erased the Weaver’s mastery over fate and took it for himself, leaving the entity a mindless, shivering husk that was then discarded into the void. The realization for all who watched was clear: to even think of a way to hurt the King was to ensure he became the master of that very method.

As the years rolled on, the coldness that had gripped Godric since the fall of Olympus began to thaw, though the ruthlessness for his enemies remained. This change was almost entirely due to Blake. The merchant, now a man of immense power and burgeoning magical skill, had become Godric’s shadow and his conscience. They spent evenings walking through the royal gardens, Godric speaking of the old days of Olympus with a warmth that hadn’t been seen in ten millennia. He was once again the fatherly figure, the kind ruler who walked among his people, though now he did so as an apex predator who could unmake the stars. Blake, though he never knew his true lineage, spent his time in the royal archives, his hands tracing the same runes Thorin once used, his son Josh often at his side, showing a natural, terrifying aptitude for the wizardly arts.

The final era of the omniverse was one of absolute, terrifying peace. Thronegate sat at the center of all things, a kingdom that spanned every conceivable reality. In the heart of the capital, Godric stood on a balcony overlooking a city that defied the laws of geometry and light. He was the warm, handsome king once more, his salt-and-pepper beard catching the glow of a thousand different suns. Beside him stood Blake, the court wizard in all but name, wearing robes that shimmered with the suppressed power of a man who could punch through dimensions. They stood as brothers, the king and the descendant of the man who had saved him. Below them, the men of Thronegate moved with the grace of gods, and the women lived in a paradise of absolute security. Godric looked out at his infinite domain, his omniscience confirming that there was no threat left in any corner of existence. He smiled, a fatherly, friendly expression that hid the most destructive force the omniverse would ever know, as Blake rested a hand on the hilt of a staff that hummed with the forgotten songs of Olympus.
