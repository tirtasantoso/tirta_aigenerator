They had unmade seventeen thousand god-kings before they turned their attention to Thronegate.*

Every citizen in the city, from the highest noble to the lowest street-sweeper, was suddenly drowning in a flood of pure, undiluted loathing—loathing of themselves, loathing of their families, loathing of their King. The psychic pressure was so immense that thousands of citizens began to claw at their own faces, to tear out their own eyes, to hurl their own children from windows in spasms of suicidal, homicidal revulsion. It was a chaos of such profound, existential horror that the city's very foundations seemed to tremble in sympathetic agony.*

He completely suppressed ninety-nine point nine-nine percent of His active power, extending the nines infinitely, reducing His active capability to a value so close to absolute zero that the difference was purely theoretical. And then, He allowed the most infinitesimally, incomprehensibly microscopic sliver of His passive Charisma—a value so indistinguishable from total nothingness that all of mathematics dissolves in the attempt to name it—to wash gently outward across the psychic plane.*

It is a fundamental, absolute law of His existence—a declaration, written into the foundational ontology of the omniverse, that His presence commands the deepest, most complete, most all-consuming devotion that any being can possibly experience. It has been multiplying beyond all infinities every Planck second for five hundred thousand years. The fraction He permitted to manifest was so unspeakably small that it should not, by any conceivable logic, have affected anything at all.*

*The Legion of Unyielding Hate, beings constructed entirely from pure, absolute hatred, was instantly, violently, irrevocably overwritten. The psychic atmosphere of the city did not simply clear. The hatred was not merely dispelled. It was replaced. The Legion's consciousnesses, saturated in a billion eons of cultivated loathing, were suddenly, catastrophically flooded with a devotion so absolute, so all-consuming, so mind-shatteringly intense, that their fundamental identities shattered like thin glass under a mountain's weight. *The Legion's collective consciousness collapsed into a single, unified psychic shriek of devastated, agonized adoration, a sound so pure and so powerful that it physically manifested as a wave of searing, golden light that swept across the entire omniverse. Every being in every reality, for a single, brief micro-instant, felt a faint, distant echo of that adoration—a sudden, inexplicable surge of warmth toward the distant, unknown concept of a "king." And the Legion themselves, their abstract consciousnesses now drowning in a love they could not contain, were spontaneously, violently re-embodied as physical forms. They ripped out their own golden eyes and cast them down like burning rain. They pulled open their own ribcages and poured their flaming hearts upon the streets, where the molten adoration ignited vast conflagrations. Wars erupted between those who were touched by the adoration-wave and those who were not. *Godric, still standing on the balcony, observed the cascading catastrophe with a face of mild, contemplative detachment. He did not frown. He did not sigh. He simply, effortlessly, instantly, in exactly zero seconds, extinguished the chaotic adoration-wave across the hundred thousand multiverses. He restored the populations to their senses, resurrected the dead from their ecstatic self-immolations, and cleansed the psychic contamination from every affected consciousness. The civilizations resumed their lives exactly where they had left off, blissfully unaware that they had nearly destroyed themselves in a frenzy of worship for a King they would never meet.*

*He did not restore the Legion of Unyielding Hate. The burning remnants of their self-sacrificed bodies remain scattered across the capital's skies, frozen in the moment of their ecstatic self-destruction—a permanent, golden monument of eternal, agonized adoration that hovers above the city like a second sun. The light is beautiful. It is the most beautiful thing I have ever seen. And I cannot look at it without feeling my own heart twist with an adoration so intense that it terrifies me, because I do not know where His passive Charisma ends and my own genuine love begins. Perhaps there is no difference. Perhaps that is the most terrifying truth of all.*

I reached, with a hand that had long since ceased to tremble and had instead gone completely, coldly numb, for the fifth and final journal. It was the most recent, its leather binding still supple, its pages still crisp with the faint scent of fresh ink. The handwriting on the cover was achingly familiar. It was my father's.

**Year 999,999 — The Incident of Absolute Luck**
**Penned by Malakor, Three Hundred and Thirty-Second Court Wizard of Thronegate**

The script was weary, deliberate, and unmistakably paternal. I could almost hear my father's voice—that gravelly, exhausted baritone—reading the words aloud in the silence of the vault.

*Kaedan. My son. My heir. If you are reading this, you have already absorbed the testimonies of Torvald, Cyprian, Fenrick, and the others whose journals the current Archivist selects for your Rite. You are hollow now. You are a void where pride once lived. You understand, with an intellectual clarity that borders on madness, the absolute, infinite supremacy of the King we serve. But there is one more incident I must add to the record, because I witnessed it with my own eyes only three months ago, and because it concerns the stat that I find most existentially devastating of all. His Luck.*

*The Probability Architects were a hyper-civilization from the outermost reaches of the omniverse, beings who had completely transcended physical existence and relocated their entire species into the fundamental mathematical substrate of reality itself. They had become living equations, sentient probabilities, consciousnesses woven from the stochastic fabric that underlies all cause and effect. For a billion years they had manipulated chance on a cosmic scale, steering the evolution of entire multiverses through the careful, deliberate application of probabilistic nudges. They had never failed. The concept of failure was, to them, a mathematical impossibility—they could calculate every possible outcome of every possible action and simply select the timeline in which they succeeded.*

*Their plan was elegant in its absolute finality. They did not attempt to harm Godric directly. Instead, they constructed a Probability Collapser—a universe-scale engine that would retroactively alter the fundamental stochastic conditions of His existence, ensuring that the particular quantum fluctuations that had permitted His original amplification to begin had never occurred. They intended to unmake Him not by striking Him in the present, but by reaching backward across the timeline and quietly, gently, mathematically erasing the conditions that had made His power possible. They had spent a million years perfecting this engine, and by every conceivable calculation, they had succeeded. The Collapser activated. The probability of Godric's existence, at the most fundamental quantum level, was reset to absolute zero.*

*I was standing beside the King in the imperial observatory when the Collapser activated. I felt nothing—no shift in the air, no disturbance in my arcane senses. But Godric paused in His contemplation of the star-chart He had been studying. He did not speak. He did not move. He simply allowed His passive Luck—a single, infinitesimal sliver so small that the concept of size itself collapses—to exert itself upon reality.*

*Luck, for the King, is not chance. It is not fortune. It is a fundamental, absolute law of His existence—a declaration, written into the deepest ontological source code of creation, that His existence is unconditionally, infinitely favored by every conceivable and inconceivable factor in every possible reality. It has been multiplying beyond all infinities every Planck second for a million years. The fraction He permitted to express itself was a value so unbearably, impossibly close to nothing that the difference between it and absolute zero was a theoretical abstraction that no mathematics can describe. It was a ghost of a ghost of a ghost—a whisper of chance at the farthest edge of conceptual possibility.*

*The Probability Collapser, the most sophisticated stochastic manipulation engine ever constructed, instantly and catastrophically malfunctioned. It did not merely fail. It inverted. The probability of Godric's existence, set to zero, was spontaneously reset to one hundred percent with such absolute, overwhelming force that the mathematical feedback shrieked across the probabilistic substrate of reality. And then the engine, following its own inversion, turned its function upon its creators.*

*The Probability Architects, beings of pure mathematical consciousness, suddenly found themselves subjected to a cascading chain of maximally improbable, catastrophically lethal events. Their fundamental existence—their status as living equations—was rewritten by the inverted Collapser to have a zero percent chance of continuing. They were retroactively unmade. But the unmasking was not instantaneous or merciful. The inverted engine, flooded with the King's passive Luck, ensured that each Probability Architect experienced a uniquely, grotesquely improbable death. One Architect found that every atom in its mathematical body spontaneously underwent simultaneous nuclear fusion, converting it into a brief, searing miniature sun that burned with a shriek of pure, agonized calculation-madness before guttering out. Another was suddenly, impossibly, teleported into the exact center of a black hole that had not existed a nanosecond earlier, its consciousness stretched into an infinite spaghetti-thread of excruciating gravitational torment that would last, subjectively, forever. A third discovered that its mathematical structure had been spontaneously rewritten into a logical paradox that recursively divided by zero, causing its consciousness to collapse into a screaming, endless loop of self-negating pain. Across their entire hyper-civilization—a population of conceptual billions—every single Architect died in a manner so uniquely, improbably, agonizingly horrific that the collective psychic shriek of their extinction physically cracked the mathematical substrate of reality itself.*

*The collateral damage was obscene. The crack in the mathematical substrate propagated outward, and probability itself began to malfunction across the adjacent fifty thousand multiverses. Impossibilities became certainties. The laws of physics, which depend upon consistent mathematical frameworks, began to flicker and fail. Stars spontaneously turned into water. Planets became cubes. The dead rose from their graves, screaming in confusion as their decayed bodies spontaneously reconstituted. Civilizations collapsed into chaos as the fundamental constants that governed their reality became variables, fluctuating at random with no underlying logic. The death toll from these probability fluctuations was unquantifiable—not merely because the numbers were vast, but because the concept of "death" itself had become probabilistic and therefore unreliable.*

*Godric, standing in the observatory, observed the spreading probabilistic chaos with a face of mild, detached curiosity. He did not frown. He did not sigh. And then, effortlessly, instantly, in exactly zero seconds, He reached out—not physically, but through the ambient, passive pressure of His Luck—and reasserted the normal probabilistic framework across the fifty thousand affected multiverses. He restored the fundamental mathematical constants, re-established the laws of physics, resurrected every being who had died in the chaos, and cleansed the timeline of every probabilistic impossibility that had manifested. The multiverses returned to their prior condition with no memory of the event, their inhabitants blissfully unaware that their very existence had become a fluctuating variable for a handful of terrifying moments.*

*He did not restore the Probability Architects. Their conceptual bodies remain in the state of their impossible, horrific deaths—some eternally fusing into nuclear suns, some stretched into infinite spaghetti-screams within spontaneously generated black holes, some trapped in looping, self-negating paradoxes—scattered across the mathematical substrate like broken, screaming toys. The crack in the substrate remains as well, a permanent, visible scar in the underlying mathematics of reality that any sufficiently advanced mage can perceive. It is a monument. It is a warning. *Kaedan. My son. When you take your place beside the throne, you will feel the thousandfold amplification burning in your veins, and you will remember these journals, and you will know that you are nothing. It is not a condemnation. It is a liberation. You are free from the illusion that you could ever protect Him, because He requires no protection. You are free from the illusion that you could ever oppose Him, because opposition is a mathematical absurdity. You exist only because He loves our bloodline, because His warmth is genuine, because the smile He will give you when you first approach the dais is real. Serve Him faithfully, my son, and never, ever forget the abyss that hides behind that smile.*

I closed my father's journal. The vault's absolute darkness pressed against my eyes, and I sat motionless in the obsidian chair for a long, uncounted interval. The phantom echoes of all five journals—the screams of the Behemoth Host converted to quark slurry, the trapped temporal agony of the Chronophage Swarm, the recursive breaking of the billion divine pantheons, the golden, ecstatic self-immolation of the Legion of Unyielding Hate, and the uniquely, impossibly, grotesquely improbable deaths of the Probability Architects—swirled in my consciousness like a maelstrom of recorded trauma. My violet mana, the arcane power that made me a walking cataclysm, felt like a child's toy. My strength, my speed, my durability, my senses, my charisma, my luck—all of them were precisely zero when measured against the infinite, multiplying, recursive abyss of His.

And I understood, with a clarity that was indistinguishable from despair, that this was the truth of my existence. My entire purpose as Court Wizard was not to protect the King. It was to witness His supremacy and bear the weight of that witness so that future generations of my blood would know, with absolute certainty, that their warm, smiling, fatherly King was also the apex predator of all creation, an entity of such incomprehensible, unspeakable power that the entire omniverse was less than a dust mote in His shadow.

I rose from the obsidian chair on legs that felt hollow and distant. The heavy adamantine door ground open at my touch, and I ascended the long, spiral corridor toward the light. My father was waiting at the top of the stair, his ancient eyes searching my face. He saw the cold, hollow calm in my expression, and he nodded once. He did not speak. There was nothing left to say.

The golden doors of the imperial throne room swung open, and I stepped inside. The vast, cathedral space was bathed in the warm amber glow of the arcane sconces, the vaulted ceiling lost in shadows so deep they might have stretched into infinity. And there, at the center of the obsidian dais, sat Godric.

He was exactly as every journal had described Him: a man of forty-five, stunningly ruggedly handsome, His heavily muscled frame draped in simple dark garments that stretched across His broad shoulders. His short salt-and-pepper hair was neatly trimmed, His salt-and-pepper beard framing a jawline that was sharp and masculine and utterly, terrifyingly human. His absolute concealment projected no aura, no pressure, no hint of the infinite, multiplying horror that lurked beneath His skin. He required no breath, no sustenance, no sleep. He was an immortal monolith of apocalyptic power who had existed for a million years and would exist for a million more—His stats climbing forever beyond any ceiling that mathematics or metaphysics could ever encode.

He looked at me as I entered, and His eyes crinkled at the corners. A warm, friendly, entirely genuine smile spread across His ruggedly handsome face. He rose from the throne in a single, fluid motion, crossing the dais in three easy strides to clasp my shoulder with a warm, calloused hand. His touch was solid, grounding, utterly, terrifyingly paternal.

"Ah, Kaedan, my boy," Godric said, His rich, comforting baritone vibrating through my chest. "You have completed the Rite of Witnessing. I trust the journals were instructive. Your father tells me you read all five of the required accounts. That is a heavy burden for such young shoulders. I am proud of you. Blake would be proud of you."

I looked up into His eyes—those ancient, infinite, impossibly knowing eyes—and I saw nothing there but warmth. Nothing but kindness. Nothing but the genuine, paternal love of a King who had cherished my bloodline for a million years. The horror was absolute. The dread was eternal. But the love was real, and it was the only reason I existed.

I smiled at my King, my voice steady despite the cold, soul-crushing certainty that had permanently anchored itself in my chest. "Thank you, Uncle Godric. I am honored to serve."

He patted my shoulder warmly and gestured toward the small, ornate chair at His right hand—the seat that every court wizard of my lineage had occupied since Blake himself. I walked up the steps of the dais and took my place.

And as I settled into the ancient chair, my gaze drifted across the throne room. In the high, shadowed corner near the eastern pillar, a faint, glittering smear of unbound quark particles still drifted—the screaming remnants of the Behemoth Host, their disembodied consciousnesses trapped in eternal, silent agony. On the wall behind the left pillar, a microscopic singularity pulsed with the flayed, looping torment of the Chronophage Swarm. Through the high, arched windows, the golden light of the Legion's frozen self-immolation cast a warm, beautiful, terrible glow across the obsidian floor. And in the deepest recesses of my arcane perception, I could feel the permanent, screaming scars in the mathematical substrate where the Probability Architects had died their uniquely impossible deaths.

I breathed in the air of the throne room—air that was warm and clean and faintly scented with jasmine. And I knew, with a certainty that would never leave me, that opposing the King was not futile. It was a mathematical impossibility so absolute that the very concept of opposition dissolved into meaninglessness in His presence. He was the apex predator, the eternal sovereign, the warm and loving and utterly, incomprehensibly terrifying god-king of Thronegate.

And I was His court wizard. I would serve Him faithfully, loyally, and with absolute, unwavering love, for all the remaining days of my mortal life.

"Yes, Uncle Godric," I said softly, settling deeper into the ancient chair. "It is good to be here."

The cold dread remained, a permanent, eternal anchor in the depths of my soul. But beneath it, steady and unshakable, was the love. And in the Year One Million of the eternal empire of Thronegate, that was enough.
