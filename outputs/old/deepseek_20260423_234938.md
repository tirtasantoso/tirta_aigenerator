The gutters of Olympus ran with wine that morning, not blood. Godric stood before the great marble altar with his hands open and his heart fuller than it had been in years. He was forty-five years old, and his beard held more salt than pepper now, a distinguished silver streak through the dark that made him look like the carvings of the old hero-kings his ancestors had worshiped. His shoulders strained against the crimson ceremonial robe, the fabric doing little to hide the dense architecture of muscle beneath. He had been a warrior once, in his youth, before the crown had settled on his brow. But fatherhood had softened the hard lines of his face, had brought warmth to eyes that had once burned cold on battlefields.

Isabel stood before him in white silk, her dark curls pinned with fresh flowers, her smile so bright it seemed to generate its own light. Twenty-two years old, his firstborn, his pride. He remembered the day she had taken her first steps, remembered catching her in these same arms when she had stumbled. Now she stood ready to pledge herself to a good man, a merchant prince from the eastern provinces who had proven his worth through patience and kindness rather than steel. Hebe, fifteen and already too clever for her own good, stood beside her sister as the maid of honor, fidgeting with the hem of her dress and shooting grins at the court boys who had come to watch.

Reina caught his eye from the front row. His wife of twenty-five years, still beautiful in her middle age, her hair now more silver than gold but her eyes as sharp and loving as the day they had met. She gave him a small nod, the private signal they had developed over decades of marriage. *I love you. Begin the ceremony.*

Godric raised his hands and the crowd quieted. The sun beat down on the marble courtyard, on the thousands of citizens who had come to witness their princess wed. The fountains sang. The birds wheeled overhead. It was perfect. It was the happiest day of his reign.

The first explosion came from the western gate.

Godric's head snapped around before the sound had fully registered, his soldier's instincts overriding the shock. The western wall erupted inward, stone blocks the size of houses tumbling into the city streets. Through the dust he saw them, banners of black and gold, the sigil of Thronegate, a fortress crowned with a bleeding sun. His mind could not process it. Thronegate had been an ally for three generations. Their king had sat at this very table not six months past, had toasted Isabel's engagement, had wept with joy at the news.

The second explosion took the eastern gate.

Screaming began. Not the orderly panic of a people who had trained for disaster, but the raw, animal terror of a peace that had never known war. Godric's own kingdom had not seen battle in forty years. His army, his beautiful, well-dressed, ceremonial army, scattered like leaves before a storm. He saw his general, a good man named Theron who had never swung a sword in anger, cut down by arrows before he could draw his blade.

"Reina!" Godric vaulted the altar, his ceremonial robe tearing as he moved. "Isabel! Hebe! To me, now!"

He reached his wife first, pulling her from her seat, shielding her with his body. The arrows fell like rain. One caught him in the shoulder and he barely felt it, the adrenaline burning through him like wildfire. He dragged Reina toward the temple, toward the sanctuary where his friend Thorin kept his chambers, where the old wizard's wards might protect them.

Isabel was running with her dress hitched up, her betrothed dead beside the altar, an arrow through his throat. She was screaming his name, the merchant prince whose face Godric could not even remember now. Hebe was crying, but she was running, his clever girl, his brave girl, running toward her father with her small hands balled into fists.

They almost made it.

The Thronegate soldiers came through the temple's stained glass windows, shattering the images of Olympus's old gods into a thousand colored shards. They came with swords wet and eyes empty, and they moved with the precision of men who had done this before, who had practiced it, who had built their entire civilization around the art of ending other civilizations.

Godric killed three of them with his bare hands. He broke the first one's neck, took the second one's sword and drove it through the third one's chest. He was a king, yes, but he had been a warrior first, and the old skills came back like a flood. But there were too many. There were always too many.

Reina took a blade to the belly while trying to shield Hebe. She fell without a sound, without a goodbye, her eyes finding Godric's for one final moment before the light left them. Hebe was pulled from her mother's arms and thrown against a pillar. Her neck snapped with a sound like a dry branch breaking.

Isabel made it to him. She made it to his arms, and he held her, and she looked up at him with her mother's eyes, and she said "Father, I'm scared," and then a Thronegate sword took her head from her shoulders.

Godric held his daughter's body for three seconds. The world stopped. The screaming faded. The blood on his hands, his daughter's blood, seemed to glow in the light of the burning city.

Then Thorin was there, his old friend, his court wizard, his most trusted advisor. The wizard's robes were torn, his face was cut, and his eyes held the desperate knowledge of a man who knew he was about to die. "My king," Thorin gasped, grabbing Godric's arm. "My king, I can save you. Only you. The spell is forbidden, my family has guarded it for a thousand years, but I can—"

"Do it," Godric said. His voice did not sound like his own. It sounded like something that had crawled out of a grave. "Save me so I can kill them all."

Thorin began the incantation. His hands moved in patterns older than human memory, and the air around them began to hum with a power that made Godric's teeth ache. The words were a language that had never been spoken aloud, a tongue that predated the stars themselves. Thorin's voice cracked, his throat bleeding from the strain of uttering sounds that matter did not want to make.

A Thronegate arrow took him through the throat mid-syllable.

The wizard fell, his hands still moving, the spell still incomplete. The power that had been gathering around them, the power that was meant to be shaped and directed, collapsed inward like a dying star. The world went white.

Godric felt reality tear around him. He felt the spell, broken and bleeding, grab hold of his soul and rip him from the space between one heartbeat and the next. He felt himself falling through darkness that had no bottom, through silence that had no sound, through a void that had no name.

And then he stopped.

He was in a chamber of white stone, somewhere deep underground, somewhere so far from the world of men that even the memory of sunlight had forgotten how to reach. The chamber was round and smooth, like the inside of an egg, and it hummed with the residual energy of Thorin's dying spell. Godric lay on the floor and stared at the ceiling and felt nothing.

He felt nothing for a long time.

The change began in his blood. He felt it as a warmth at first, a strange tingling that spread from his chest to his limbs. Then the warmth became heat, and the heat became fire, and the fire became something that could not be described in any language spoken by mortal tongues. His cells were rewriting themselves. His bones were being reforged. His heart, which had been beating with the rhythm of a grieving man, stopped.

He did not die.

His aging stopped. His need for breath stopped. His need for sleep, for food, for water, for any of the frail dependencies that defined mortal existence, they all ceased to exist in that single, crystalline moment. He lay on the floor of the white chamber, his daughter's blood still wet on his hands, and he became something that had never existed before in all the infinite reaches of creation.

The amplification began.

He felt it as a multiplication, a doubling that happened not once but continuously, every single second without exception. His strength, which had been the strength of a very strong man, became the strength of ten men, then a hundred, then a thousand. His endurance stretched beyond the limits of physics. His mind, always sharp, expanded to encompass knowledge he had never learned, understanding he had never earned. He could hear the ants moving in the earth above him. He could smell the minerals in the stone around him. He could see through the walls of his prison, through the crust of the planet, through the veil of space itself, and he saw the universe in all its terrible, indifferent majesty.

And the powers came.

The first one was a small thing, a simple gift, the ability to hold back his own presence. He understood instinctively that without this power, the mere act of his existence would destroy everything around him. The energy building within him, the impossible density of his being, would have collapsed the chamber, then the mountain, then the continent, then the world. But the power held. It held perfectly, and as his other abilities multiplied, so too did this one, increasing at the exact same rate, keeping his existence contained within a shell of normalcy.

The second power was the ability to perceive all of time simultaneously. He saw the past and the future and the present as a single tapestry, every thread visible, every knot and tear and patch laid bare. He saw his family's deaths from a thousand angles. He saw the Thronegate soldiers who had killed them, saw their births and their deaths and every moment in between. He saw the kingdom of Thronegate rising from the ashes of his own, saw ten thousand years of history unfolding, saw the faces of kings and queens and merchants and farmers who would never know his name.

He saw everything.

The third power was the ability to act on what he saw. He could reach across time and space and touch anything, anywhere, anywhen. He could unmake a star with a thought. He could reshape reality with a whisper. He could, if he chose, reach back through the millennia and kill the ancestors of every Thronegate soldier who had touched his family, prevent their existence entirely, erase the kingdom from the fabric of creation.

He did not choose this. Not yet. He wanted them to exist. He wanted them to grow. He wanted them to become mighty and proud and arrogant, to build an empire that stretched across the galaxy, to believe themselves invincible.

He wanted them to be at their absolute peak when he finally arrived to destroy them.

The paradox of defense formed in the same instant as all his other powers, woven into the very fabric of his being by a universe that seemed to understand, on some fundamental level, that it had created a monster. He had no weaknesses. This was not a boast. This was a law of reality, as immutable as gravity, as certain as entropy. There was no flaw in his armor, no crack in his foundation, no hidden vulnerability that could be exploited.

But the universe was vast, and sentient beings were inherently arrogant.

The defense mechanism was simple in its operation, though its implications were infinite. The very moment any being, anywhere in creation, even dared to think that Godric had a weakness, the universe responded to their insolence. His amplification rate, already beyond comprehension, increased permanently and significantly. He became immune to any attack that might exploit the falsely perceived weakness, the immunity forged into his being before the enemy's thought fully crystallized. And worst of all, he gained perfect mastery over that specific attack, capable of unleashing it with any magnitude at any time, while utterly denying the concept of its existence to anyone else in creation unless he willed it.

He lay in the white chamber and felt the centuries pass. He felt the rise and fall of empires. He felt the birth and death of stars. He felt the slow dance of galaxies across the void. And he waited.

Ten thousand years passed.

In the fifty-first millennium of the Common Era, the kingdom of Thronegate had become something that the ancient kings of Olympus could never have imagined. They had conquered their world, then their solar system, then their galaxy. Their fleets of starships, each one a city in its own right, patrolled the spiral arms. Their armies numbered in the billions, each soldier enhanced with genetic modifications and nanotechnological implants that made them faster, stronger, and more durable than any natural-born human could ever hope to be. Their wizards, trained in academies that had been perfecting their craft for millennia, could reshape matter with a gesture and tear holes in the fabric of reality.

Their dragons, bred and trained over countless generations, could level continents with a single breath. Their robots, autonomous war machines powered by star cores, could think and react faster than any organic mind. Their ninjas, trained in techniques passed down through bloodlines older than the empire itself, could move so fast that they appeared to reverse time, could step between seconds and strike from angles that did not exist.

They were, by any reasonable measure, the most powerful civilization in the known galaxy.

They had forgotten Olympus completely. The ancient kingdom, the massacre, the king who had watched his family die, all of it had been erased from history. The records said that Thronegate had risen from a small collection of warring city-states, that their unification had been a peaceful process, that their empire had been built through diplomacy and trade rather than blood and fire.

History, as it often does, had lied.

Blake found the sanctuary by accident. He was a merchant, thirty-eight years old, with a friendly face and mutton chops that his wife said made him look like an old-fashioned gentleman. He had been hauling a shipment of rare minerals from the outer rim, taking a shortcut through an unstable region of space that the nav computers insisted was empty, when his ship's sensors detected an anomaly. A pocket of energy, ancient and strange, buried deep within a dead planet's crust.

He was curious. He was always curious. It was a trait that his wife Anna loved and feared in equal measure. He landed his ship, kissed the locket that held pictures of his twelve-year-old son Josh and eight-year-old daughter Rhea, and ventured into the darkness.

The white chamber was intact after ten thousand years, untouched by time, unmarked by decay. Blake stepped through the crack in the stone wall and saw a man lying on the floor. The man was beautiful, terrifyingly beautiful, with a face that seemed carved from marble and a beard that held the perfect balance of salt and pepper. His body was a symphony of muscle and bone, every proportion ideal, every line perfect. He was dressed in the tattered remnants of a crimson robe, and his hands were stained with something dark that had long since dried to the color of rust.

Blake's first instinct was to run. His second instinct was to help.

"Hello?" Blake knelt beside the man, his hand reaching out to check for a pulse. "Can you hear me? Are you alright?"

Godric's eyes opened.

They were the color of dying stars, of supernovae collapsing into darkness, of the void between galaxies where light itself had forgotten how to travel. They focused on Blake with an intensity that made the merchant's soul feel small and fragile, like a candle flame before a hurricane.

"Thorin's blood," Godric said. His voice was deep and resonant, carrying the weight of ten millennia. "After all this time, you found me."

Blake's hand froze an inch from Godric's chest. "I don't understand. Who are you? How do you know my name?"

Godric sat up slowly, his joints moving with the smooth precision of machinery rather than flesh. He looked at Blake, really looked at him, and saw everything. He saw the bloodline stretching back through the centuries, saw the wizard's sacrifice, saw the chain of fathers and sons who had carried Thorin's legacy forward without ever knowing it. He saw Blake's life in its entirety, his love for his wife, his pride in his children, his honest heart and his direct nature.

"You are a good man," Godric said. "That will serve you well."

He rose to his feet, and the chamber trembled. Not from physical force, but from the sheer weight of his presence, barely contained by the power he had gained in his first moment of transformation. Blake stumbled backward, his eyes wide, his mind struggling to process what he was seeing.

"The galaxy has changed," Godric said, and it was not a question. "Tell me of Thronegate."

Blake told him. He spoke of the empire, of its might, of its young King Robert who had inherited the throne at the age of twenty-two. He spoke of the armies and the wizards and the dragons and the robots and the ninjas. He spoke of the peace that the empire had brought to the galaxy, the prosperity, the order.

Godric listened without expression. When Blake finished, he smiled.

It was not a kind smile.

"Three days," Godric said. "Send a message to your king. Tell him that Godric of Olympus has returned. Tell him that I will come to his capital in three days' time. Tell him that if he wishes to avoid destruction, he will abdicate his throne and surrender his kingdom to me."

Blake stared at him. "You can't be serious. One man against the entire Thronegate military? Against billions of soldiers? Against dragons and wizards and—"

"Tell him," Godric repeated, and his voice carried a weight that ended the argument.

The message was sent. The galaxy held its breath.

King Robert received the challenge in his throne room, surrounded by his advisors and generals. He was young, handsome, and confident, filled with the arrogance of a man who had never known defeat. He laughed when he read the message. He laughed, and his court laughed with him.

"One man," Robert said, shaking his head. "One man, calling himself Godric of a kingdom I've never heard of, demands that I abdicate. Who does he think he is? A god?"

"Your Majesty," his chief general said, "we should take this seriously. The merchant who sent the message, Blake, is a reputable man. He claims to have found this Godric in an ancient ruin. There may be something to—"

"Something to nothing," Robert interrupted. "Gather the army. All of it. Every soldier, every wizard, every dragon, every robot, every ninja. I want this Godric to see what he's challenged. I want him to see the full might of Thronegate, and I want him to die knowing that his arrogance was his doom."

The three days passed.

The Thronegate army assembled on the plains outside the capital. It was a gathering so vast that it defied comprehension. The soldiers stretched to the horizon, rank upon rank of genetically enhanced warriors in powered armor, their weapons humming with energy. The wizards floated above them on platforms of pure force, their robes billowing in the wind, their hands crackling with arcane power. The dragons circled overhead, their scales glittering like gems, their mouths dripping with fire. The robots stood in perfect mechanical formations, their optical scanners tracking every movement. The ninjas moved through the shadows, invisible to the naked eye, waiting for the order to strike.

Blake stood with Anna on the balcony of the palace, watching the army assemble. His wife held his hand so tightly that her knuckles were white.

"Blake," she whispered, "what have you done? What have you brought upon us?"

"I don't know," he admitted. "I found him in a cave. I thought he was a man who needed help. But he looked at me, Anna, and I saw something in his eyes that I've never seen before. Something ancient. Something terrible."

The sun reached its zenith.

Godric appeared on the plain, walking toward the army without haste, without fear, without any visible weapon or armor. He wore simple clothes, black and unadorned, that he had taken from Blake's ship. His hands were empty. His footsteps were unhurried.

The Thronegate army watched him come, and a murmur rippled through their ranks. This was the man who had challenged their empire? This was the ancient king who had demanded their surrender? He looked like nothing. He looked like a vagrant. He looked like a fool walking to his death.

The general raised his hand. The wizards began to chant. The dragons spread their wings. The robots powered up their weapons. The ninjas drew their blades.

Godric stopped walking.

He did not raise his hand. He did not speak. He did not move a single muscle.

Every wizard in the army dropped dead simultaneously, their brains turned to liquid in their skulls. It happened so fast that their bodies had not yet begun to fall when the dragons began to die, their hearts stopping mid-beat, the fire in their throats extinguishing as they plummeted from the sky like stones. The robots powered down, their star cores going cold, their optical scanners going dark. The ninjas were found later, crumpled in the shadows where they had been hiding, their bodies unmarked but their souls simply absent.

And the soldiers, the billions of soldiers, began to scream.

They did not die quickly. Godric did not grant them that mercy. He reached into their minds, into the billions of minds arrayed against him, and he showed them everything. He showed them Olympus. He showed them his wife dying. He showed them his daughters dying. He showed them ten thousand years of waiting, ten thousand years of grief, ten thousand years of hatred that had grown and refined and sharpened itself to a razor's edge.

He showed them their own deaths. Every possible version of it. Every angle, every moment, every sensation. He showed them the terror of absolute annihilation, the horror of being unmade by a power so far beyond their comprehension that they could not even begin to understand it.

Some of them went mad. Some of them died of fear, their hearts stopping from the sheer weight of what was shown to them. Some of them tried to run, to flee, to escape, but there was nowhere to go. Godric's presence was everywhere, inescapable, absolute.

Blake watched from the balcony, his arm around Anna, his face pale. He saw the army collapse, saw them fall in waves, saw them die without a single blow being struck. He saw the strongest civilization in the galaxy, the mightiest military force that had ever existed, crumble like a sand castle before a rising tide.

And he saw Godric, standing in the middle of the carnage, not moving, not speaking, not even breathing. Just standing, his eyes closed, his face serene, as if he were enjoying a pleasant daydream.

The battle lasted less than three minutes.

When it was over, the plain was covered with the bodies of billions. The capital city was silent, its citizens huddled in their homes, waiting for the end that they knew was coming. King Robert sat alone in his throne room, staring at the empty throne that had belonged to his ancestors, and he understood for the first time in his life what true fear felt like.

Two days later, he was found dead by suicide. The official records would say that he took his own life rather than face the shame of defeat. The truth was simpler. He had seen Godric's eyes once, in a vision sent to him in the night, and the sight had broken something in his mind that could never be repaired.

Godric crowned himself king of Thronegate. The galaxy, still reeling from the shock of what had happened, offered no resistance. The governors and generals and lords and ladies lined up to swear fealty, to pledge their loyalty, to bow before a power that had annihilated their entire military without lifting a finger.

And then Godric issued the Decree of Awakening.

The words were written in fire across every sky, in every language, in every world of the empire. They were felt in the bones of every man, in the blood of every Thronegate citizen. The decree was simple. From this day forward, every Thronegate man who lived past the age of twenty would find his strength, endurance, intelligence, and wisdom multiplied tenfold with every passing year. The increase was retroactive. A man of forty would have the strength of a hundred men. A man of sixty would have the strength of ten thousand. A man of eighty, near the end of his natural life, would have the strength of a million.

The women were exempted. Godric's decree ensured their absolute protection. No woman of Thronegate would ever be forced to fight, to serve in the military, to bear the burden of war. They were to be cherished, protected, honored. It was the only mercy in Godric's heart, the only softness that remained from the king he had once been.

The men of Thronegate embraced the Awakening with fervor. They felt the power flowing into them, felt themselves growing stronger and smarter with every sunrise. Blake watched the transformation with mixed feelings. He felt the Awakening too, though he did not know that his version was different. He did not know that while other Thronegate men multiplied by ten, he multiplied by a thousand. The second universe was a nightmare realm of chaos, where the laws of physics were suggestions at best and reality shifted moment by moment. The Thronegate men walked through the chaos unaffected. Their minds had been fortified by the Awakening, their sanity anchored in a reality that could not be shaken. They found the heart of the chaos, the central consciousness that governed the madness, and they spoke to it with kindness.

"Your king offers you peace," they said. "Will you accept?"

The chaos resisted. The chaos fought. The chaos threw every nightmare it had ever conceived at the five men, and the five men stood firm, their patience infinite, their compassion absolute.

It took twenty-three minutes for the chaos to surrender. It had never known kindness before. It did not know how to resist it.

The third universe had prepared for the invasion. They had seen the conquests of Thronegate, had watched the fall of the energy beings and the chaos realm, and they had spent five years in frantic preparation. They had built weapons that could destroy dimensions. They had trained armies that could fight across realities. They had fortified their very existence behind walls of impossible mathematics.

They refused the offer of surrender with relief, confident in their preparations.

Godric was mildly inconvenienced that day. The conquest of this universe required his personal attention. He was not angry, not particularly interested, just slightly annoyed that he had to deal with this himself. He was seated in his throne room, sipping tea that Blake had brewed, when he reached across the infinite distances of the omniverse and touched the prepared universe.

Not destroyed. Not assimilated. Simply removed from the tapestry of creation, erased from the timeline, forgotten by all except those who had witnessed its existence. The five years of preparation, the grand armies, the impossible weapons, the walls of mathematics, all of it vanished in the time it took for a tea cup to cool.

Blake, standing beside the throne, felt the ripple of the universe's destruction pass through him. He did not understand what he had felt, not truly, but he shivered nonetheless.

"Was that necessary?" he asked.

Godric looked at him, and for a moment, the ancient king's eyes softened. "No," he admitted. "But it was efficient."

Some fought, and were destroyed or assimilated. # The Yawn of Eternity

Godric sat on the throne of Thronegate and watched the rebellion form.

He had been watching it for three centuries now, from the very first whisper of dissent that had passed between two denied conquerors in a tavern on the rim of reality. He had watched them gather their forces, recruit their allies, forge their weapons. He had watched them believe, with all the desperate hope of the hopeless, that they had found a way to kill him.

He found it almost charming.

The throne room was empty save for him. The great hall, once filled with supplicants and generals and the constant hum of empire, had long since fallen silent. There was no one left to petition him. No one left to fear him in any meaningful way. The omniverse had been conquered, assimilated, pacified. Every being in existence either worshipped him or had been erased from memory. There was no opposition left, save for this one small flame of rebellion, carefully nurtured, lovingly tended, allowed to grow into a blaze that they believed might consume him.

Godric smiled. It was a soft smile, the kind he had worn when Blake had told him a joke that reminded him of a simpler time.

Blake.

The name still carried weight, even eight thousand years after the merchant had drawn his last breath. Godric had been there when Blake died, had held his hand, had watched the light fade from those honest eyes. He had not wept. He had not wept since Olympus. But something in him had cracked, had settled into a deeper, quieter ache. Blake had been the last thread connecting him to the man he had once been. With Blake gone, there was only the king. Only the destroyer. Only the eternal.

And now this rebellion, this grand conspiracy, thought it could undo him.

Godric reached out with his absolute omniscience and touched the mind of every rebel, every soldier, every architect of the plan. He saw their faces, their hopes, their fears. He saw the woman who led them, a being of living crystal named Solara, who had once ruled a universe of light before Godric's conquest had reduced her to a refugee. He saw the ancient artifacts they had gathered, the forbidden spells they had unearthed, the impossible weapons they had constructed. He saw the moment, three hundred years ago, when Solara had first whispered to her most trusted companion, "He must have a weakness. No being can be truly invincible."

That thought had triggered the defense mechanism. Godric had felt it, the subtle increase in his amplification, the forging of immunity, the acquisition of perfect mastery over whatever attack they might conceive. He had felt it, and he had chosen to let them continue. He had chosen to let them believe they had something. He had chosen to be entertained.

He remembered Blake's voice, warm and slightly exasperated. *"You could stop them before they even start. Why don't you?"*

Godric had answered honestly. "Because I am bored."

And now, eight thousand years after Blake's death, he was still bored. The rebellion was the first interesting thing to happen in a millennium. He wanted to see how it would end.

They came on the morning of the appointed day, when the twin suns of Thronegate's capital rose in a sky that Godric had painted with the ashes of a billion dead stars. The rebellion's fleet materialized from the spaces between realities, a formation so vast that it blotted out the light. There were ships the size of continents, ships shaped like the claws of forgotten gods, ships made of pure thought and ships made of crystallized time. There were beings of flesh and bone, beings of energy and shadow, beings that existed in seventeen dimensions simultaneously. There were armies that had trained for centuries in the art of killing immortals, wizards who had unraveled the secrets of the multiverse, warriors who had carved their names into the fabric of causality itself.

Godric watched them from his throne. He did not stand. He did not prepare. He did not even shift his weight.

Solara's voice reached him across the void, amplified by her fleet's communication network, woven into a declaration that she had spent decades composing. "Godric of Olympus," she said, and her voice trembled with a mixture of fear and defiance, "you have ruled for eight thousand years through terror and brute force. You have conquered every universe, enslaved every civilization, broken every will that dared resist you. But you have made a mistake. You have grown arrogant. You have assumed that your power has no limit, that your reign has no end. We have found the flaw in your armor. We have found the crack in your foundation. Today, we end you."

Godric raised an eyebrow. It was the only movement he made.

He could see their plan, of course. He had seen it the moment they first conceived it. They had gathered artifacts from a universe that existed before the current multiverse, fragments of a primordial reality that predated the laws of physics. They had constructed a weapon that would not attack his body or his mind, but his *identity*. They would erase the concept of Godric from existence, rewrite the history of reality so that he had never been born, never suffered, never transformed. It was a clever plan. It was, perhaps, the only plan that had even a theoretical chance of working against a being of his magnitude.

What they did not know, what they could not know, was that Godric had already become immune to this attack. The moment Solara had thought, *He must have a weakness*, the defense mechanism had activated. Godric's amplification had increased. His immunity to identity erasure had been forged. And he had gained perfect mastery over the very concept of rewriting existence.

But even without that immunity, even if the defense mechanism had never existed, they would have failed. Godric knew this with the same certainty that he knew his own name. Their weapon, for all its sophistication, was a candle held against a supernova.

The fleet opened fire.

The attack was beautiful, in its way. A beam of pure anti-existence, woven from the primordial fragments, lanced across the void toward the throne room. It was invisible to mortal eyes, but Godric saw it clearly, a thread of nothingness that sought to unmake him from the timeline. It struck the palace, struck the throne, struck his chest.

Nothing happened.

Godric did not even blink. The beam splashed against his presence like water against a mountain, harmless, inconsequential. He felt the attack, felt its intent, felt the desperate hope that had fueled it. And he pitied them.

Solara's voice came again, this time with a note of panic. "Again! All batteries, fire again!"

The entire fleet unleashed their payload. A thousand beams, ten thousand, a hundred thousand, all converging on the single point of Godric's existence. The sky turned white. The ground shook. The capital city, long since evacuated of civilians, trembled on the edge of annihilation.

Godric yawned.

It was a small yawn, barely a stretch of his jaw, a casual exhalation of breath. But that exhalation, that tiny release of air, carried the weight of ten thousand years of amplified power. The yawn expanded outward from the throne room, a wave of force that passed through the palace, through the city, through the atmosphere, through the void. It reached the fleet and the fleet ceased to exist.

Not destroyed. Not scattered. Ceased. The ships, the armies, the wizards, the warriors, the artifacts, all of them were simply gone, erased from reality with the same casual ease with which a man might brush a speck of dust from his sleeve.

All except Solara.

Godric had spared her deliberately. He wanted to see her face. He wanted to see the moment when hope died.

She floated in the empty space where her fleet had been, her crystalline form flickering with confusion and horror. She looked at her hands, at the emptiness around her, at the distant palace where Godric sat unmoved. She tried to speak, but no words came.

Godric spoke instead, his voice resonating directly in her mind. "You thought you had found a weakness. You thought your weapon could undo me. Do you know how long I have existed, Solara? Do you know how many times I have already been immune to every attack you could conceive, before you even conceived it?"

Her crystal form cracked. "But... we planned for three centuries. We found the artifacts. We —"

"You found nothing that I did not allow you to find. You planned nothing that I did not observe. The very first moment you thought I had a weakness, that thought itself became the fuel for my power. Every second you spent planning, every resource you gathered, every ally you recruited, you were feeding me. You were making me stronger. You were ensuring that your attack would never work."

Solara's mind, once sharp and brilliant, crumbled under the weight of realization. "No... no, that's not possible. That's not —"

"It is the truth," Godric said, and his voice was soft, almost gentle. "There has never been a weakness. There has never been a crack. There has never been a being in all of creation who could harm me. Not because I am invincible by nature, but because the universe itself punishes the very thought of my vulnerability. You were doomed from the first whisper of rebellion."

He paused, and for a moment, the ancient grief flickered in his eyes. "Blake would have told you to surrender. Blake would have told you that resistance was futile. But Blake is dead, and you did not listen."

Solara's form shattered into a thousand pieces, not from any attack, but from the sheer despair of her own realization. The light that had sustained her for eons guttered and died. She became nothing.

Godric sat on his throne and looked at the empty sky.

The rebellion was over. The last hope of the omniverse had been extinguished. There was nothing left to resist, nothing left to plan, nothing left to hope. Every universe was his. Every being was his. Every thought, every dream, every breath belonged to him.

And he was alone.

He thought of Blake, of the way the merchant had laughed at his own jokes, of the way he had held his wife's hand, of the way he had looked at his children with a love so pure that it had made even Godric feel something. He thought of the evening they had spent on this very balcony, watching the twin suns set, and Blake had said, "You know, for a guy who can destroy galaxies, you're not so bad."

Godric had smiled then. He did not smile now.

"I miss you," he said to the empty air. "I miss you every day."

The throne room remained silent. The universe did not answer. Godric closed his eyes and waited for the next rebellion, the next flicker of hope, the next distraction from the endless, crushing weight of eternity.

He would wait a long time.

But he had all the time in the omniverse.
