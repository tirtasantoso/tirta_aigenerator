The air in the bedchamber tasted of ozone and expensive sandalwood, a scent that always preceded the dawn in Aethelgard. I sat on the edge of the silk-strewn mattress, feeling the familiar, rhythmic thrumming in my marrow. It was the Awakening. Today, I was 40 years old. I had lived through seven thousand, three hundred and five days of the King’s Gift. Every single one of those days, my strength, my sight, and my very mind had multiplied tenfold. 

I looked at my hands. They appeared ordinary—calloused from the ceremonial hunt, skin bronzed by the artificial suns Godric hung in the sky—but I knew that if I closed my fist with even a fraction of my intent, the atmospheric pressure in the room would liquefy the stone walls.

Beside me, Lina stirred. She was thirty-five, beautiful in a way that felt fragile, like ancient glass. She didn't have the thrum. She never would. When she opened her eyes, I saw the flicker of the old fire, the embers of a lineage that had once tried to set the world ablaze. I reached out, my fingers grazing her jawline. I kept my potency dialed down to a billionth of a percent, a delicate restraint that required more focus than lifting a mountain.

"You're staring again, Blake," she whispered, her voice sandpaper-dry. She didn't pull away, but her neck muscles corded.

"It is my birthright to admire what is mine," I said. My voice was a low rumble that vibrated the floorboards. I wasn’t being cruel; I was stating a fundamental law of the architecture of our world. I loved her, truly, but love without order is chaos. I remembered the bruise I’d given her last week when she’d spoken ill of the Ministry. I had been too fast, even for my own redirected reflexes. I had apologized, of course, but the lesson remained. The King demands order, and as his echoes, we must enforce it within our own walls.

I stood and walked to the balcony, looking out over the spires of the capital. High above, carved into the very face of the moon, was the visage of Godric. Even from this distance, his eyes seemed to find mine. 

The history books—the ones Godric himself penned in the first nanosecond of his ascension—told of the Great Uprising. It was the pivot point of human history, the moment the hierarchy was sealed in blood and incomprehensible power. 

Nine and a half thousand years ago, Godric was already a legend. He had ruled for nearly nine millennia, a king of unparalleled charisma and wisdom. Back then, he was thought to be merely a "High Eternal," a man who had conquered aging but remained, in essence, a man. The women of the Old World, billions of them, had grown weary of his quiet preference for the patriarchal structures of antiquity. They spent centuries secreting away technology, weaving spells of such complexity they could unravel the fabric of a star, and breeding a generation of warriors who knew nothing but the intent to kill a god.

They thought they had found his flaw. They believed his immortality was a loop of time that could be severed at the throat. 

The lore tells of the exact moment the Phenomenal Amplification began, though Godric kept it a secret for eons, letting the world’s resentment ferment like a trap. It happened on his thirty-fifth birthday, in a dusty library in a kingdom long since ground to atoms. He had reached for a book, and suddenly, the heartbeat of the universe skipped. His aging froze. The need for oxygen, for bread, for a single moment of rest, vanished. 

And then, the multiplication began.

One million times his strength. Then a million times that. Every second. A new power manifesting with every tick of the clock—the ability to rewrite gravity, the power to turn thought into physical lead, the capacity to erase colors from existence. For thousands of years, he sat on his throne, sipping wine he didn't need, smiling warmly at ambassadors while his internal stats climbed into the realm of the trans-finite. He was an ocean of power masquerading as a pond.

The Uprising launched on a Tuesday. Billions of silver-clad Valkyries descended upon the palace, their weapons humming with the power of harnessed singularities. They moved with a confidence that only the ignorant can possess.

The High Commander of the Uprising, a woman named Elara whose blood still ran, diluted, in my wife’s veins, stepped forward as the palace gates dissolved into mist. She held a blade forged from the "Anti-Concept"—a weapon designed to kill things that cannot die.

Godric was waiting for them. He stood in the courtyard, looking exactly as he does now: mid-thirties, salt-and-pepper beard neatly trimmed, wearing a simple linen tunic. He looked approachable. He looked kind. He looked like he was about to offer them tea.

"My daughters," he said, his voice carrying a warmth that made the warriors’ hearts ache with a momentary, traitorous love. "Why do you bring such loud toys to my garden?"

Elara didn't hesitate. "The age of the Father ends today, Godric. We have found the crack in your soul."

She didn't know. No one knew. At that exact microsecond, because Elara *believed* a crack existed, the Weakness Paradox screamed into life. Because she thought he could be exploited, Godric’s amplification rate doubled instantly. The "Anti-Concept" she held became his. He didn't just become immune to it; he became the master of the very idea of it. 

He moved. 

To the billions of onlookers, Godric simply vanished and reappeared. But in the reality of the slaughter, he was a whirlwind of polite, smiling carnage. He didn't use a sword. He used his thumb.

I’ve seen the archives—the raw, unedited footage kept for the eyes of the Awakened. Godric walked up to the first rank of warriors. He was moving so fast that the air around him didn't have time to move out of the way; it fused into plasma, creating a halo of nuclear fire that didn't even singe his hair. He touched a woman’s forehead with a gentle, fatherly pat. Her entire skeletal structure turned into pressurized steam instantly, exploding outward with such force that the three hundred women behind her were shredded by her own vaporized calcium.

He was laughing, a genuine, hearty sound. "Is this the best the new age has to offer?" 

A fleet of ten thousand atmospheric dreadnoughts opened fire, raining down beams of concentrated dark matter. Godric didn't look up. He simply flexed his peripheral vision. A new power manifested: *Event Horizon Gaze*. The space above the palace folded. The ships weren't just destroyed; they were crushed into spheres the size of marbles and deposited neatly in a pile at his feet. 

The slaughter lasted only minutes, but Godric stretched those minutes for them. He used his intelligence—now multiplied by factors that made the collective wisdom of the species look like the gurgling of a babe—to manually dismantle their hope. He would stand before a warrior, let her strike him with a blade that could slice through dimensions, and watch as the blade shattered against his skin because he had already gained mastery over her specific style of combat the moment she conceived the attack.

He grabbed Elara by the throat. He didn't squeeze. He just looked at her with those kind, terrifying eyes. 

"You thought I had a flaw," he whispered. The sound was heard by every woman on the planet, telepathically etched into their minds. "Because of that thought, I am now the master of your soul." 

With a casual flick of his wrist, he didn't just kill her. He used a newly manifested power to turn her essence into a living, eternal monument of screaming salt. Then, he turned his gaze to the billions remaining. He decided, in that moment of warm, charismatic ruthlessness, that the world needed a new structure. A permanent one.

"The Awakening" was his final stroke of genius. He didn't enslave the men; he elevated them. He gave us the multiplication, a fraction of his own glory, to ensure that the order he preferred would never again be questioned. 

I turned away from the balcony, the memory of the lore making my blood sing. Lina was standing by the vanity, brushing her hair. She looked at me in the mirror, and for a second, I saw that ancestral defiance.

"What are you thinking about, Blake?" she asked.

"The mercy of the King," I said, walking toward her. 

She stiffened as I put my hands on her shoulders. I could feel the heat radiating from my skin—the passive aura of a man who was now $10^{7305}$ times more capable than any woman who had ever lived. 

"He could have killed them all," I murmured, leaning down to her ear. "But he chose to give us a world where everyone knows their place. Isn't it beautiful, Lina? The peace?"

She didn't answer. She didn't have to. The sheer weight of my presence, the undeniable physical fact of my superiority, was an argument she couldn't win. I felt a surge of genuine affection for her—this small, defiant creature who lived in the shadow of my godhood. 

I left the room and headed to the Great Hall. Today was the Festival of the First Second. All over the city, men were stepping out onto their terraces, flexing their intent, the air shimmering with the collective power of millions of mini-gods. 

The city of Aethelgard was a marvel of golden spires and levitating gardens, all held aloft by the will of the King. There was no hunger here. No sickness. Godric’s powers had long ago evolved to the point where he could simply negate the concept of "malady" within his borders. It was a paradise, provided one understood the nature of the Father.

I reached the palace square. The statue of Godric at the center was not stone, but a solidified shimmer of pure power. As I joined the throng of men, all of us vibrating with the daily increase of our stats, a hush fell over the crowd.

The sky didn't darken; it brightened. Godric appeared on the high balcony of the Inner Sanctum. He looked radiant. He looked like a friend. He looked like the man who had torn a billion women apart with his bare hands while smiling.

"My sons," his voice washed over us, a physical caress that made my eyes water with pride. "Another day of growth. Another day of strength."

I knelt, and around me, a million men hit the pavement with the force of falling comets, the shockwaves perfectly neutralized by our own control. I felt the immense, terrifying weight of his gaze. It was a pressure that should have crushed the planet, yet it felt like a warm blanket because he willed it so. 

He was the pinnacle. He was the paradox. He was the Father who loved us enough to become a monster. 

As I looked up at him, I felt a profound sense of rightness. The world was quiet. The world was strong. Under the eternal, multiplying shadow of Godric, the patriarchy didn't just live—it had become the very fabric of existence, as unbreakable and certain as the rising of the artificial suns. I breathed in the ozone and the glory, a godling in the service of a Titan, and for the first time in my life, I felt truly, perfectly safe.
