The kingdom of Olympus was a place of marble and sunlight, of vineyards that spilled down the mountainside like green laughter, and of a peace so profound it was woven into the very air. On this day, the air was also woven with the scent of lilies and the sound of a thousand joyous voices. King Zeus, a man carved from the bedrock of the land itself, stood upon the high dais of the Palace of Concord. At forty-five, his was a face the kingdom’s artists struggled to capture, not for lack of skill, but for an excess of character—the warm, crinkling eyes the color of a summer storm, the strong jaw softened by a father’s smile, the short, dark hair peppered with the distinguished silver of a life lived well, and a beard trimmed close that held the same salt-and-pepper hue. His muscular frame, usually clad in armor or hunting leathers, was resplendent in ceremonial white and gold. The strength in his hands, which could wrestle a bear, now gently adjusted the gossamer veil of his daughter, Athena. She was twenty-two, her intelligence shining in her grey eyes brighter than the diamonds in her tiara. Beside him, his wife Hera squeezed his arm, her pride a tangible warmth. Their younger daughter, Hebe, fifteen and all sparkling enthusiasm, fussed with the train of Athena’s gown, her laughter like chimes. Zeus looked out over his people, his family, his heart so full it felt like a physical pressure behind his ribs. This was the apex of a life dedicated to kindness.

The first arrow took Hebe in the throat. It was not an arrow of wood and fletching, but a shaft of condensed black energy that hummed with malevolent purpose. Her chime-like laughter choked into a wet gurgle. The silence that followed was not true silence, but a vacuum of understanding. Then the sky over Olympus tore. Ships of dark metal, shaped like screaming skulls, vomited forth legions clad in the crimson and onyx of Thronegate. Spells of dissolution rained down, turning marble to slurry and flesh to ash. The wedding feast became a charnel house. Zeus saw Hera push Athena behind her, a mother’s futile shield, a moment before a beam of crimson energy erased them both from the waist up. The warmth in Zeus’s chest did not vanish; it imploded, collapsing into a singularity of cold so absolute it burned. He roared, a sound that shook the very foundations of the palace, and lightning sprang from his fists, incinerating a dozen Thronegate shock-troops. But they were endless. He fought not as a king, but as a cornered beast, his kindness scoured away by the acid of loss with each passing second.

“Zeus! To me!” The voice was strained, desperate. It was Godric, his oldest friend, the court wizard whose lineage was as intertwined with the throne as ivy on an oak. Godric’s robes were smoldering, his face streaked with soot and blood. He grabbed the king’s arm, his fingers like talons. “The sanctuary! The forbidden spell—it is our only hope!” Zeus, his mind a white-hot scream, allowed himself to be pulled. Godric began to chant, words of power so old they predated language, syllables that twisted the air and made reality groan. The spell, cast by a dying man, activated.

Zeus felt not a teleport, but an unraveling. The world did not fade; it was peeled back, layer by layer, until he was nowhere and everywhere. He landed in a chamber of seamless, non-reflective black stone, a hidden sanctuary built by Godric’s ancestors for a catastrophe that had now arrived. His body hit the floor. The need for air, for water, for sleep, for sustenance—all of it was severed, cleanly and permanently. His aging ceased in that instant, freezing him in the agonized visage of a forty-five-year-old king who had lost everything. This was merely the prelude.

Simultaneously, deep within the core of his being, something ignited. His physical strength, the endurance that had let him fight for an hour, his keen senses, his tactical wisdom, his innate intelligence—every quantifiable and unquantifiable aspect of his self began to multiply. Not by two, or ten, or a hundred. It multiplied by one million. And then, before that new, staggering totality could even be comprehended, it multiplied by itself. And it did so again the next second. And the next. And the next, without pause, without limit, a geometric explosion of self that would have vaporized any other container. In tandem, a new superpower bloomed into existence within him every single second. The first was the power of Absolute Concealment, a subconscious, autonomous ability to hold back the cataclysmic aura of his own being, to fold infinity into a mortal-seeming shell. The second was the power to Unmake Light. The third, to Silence Sound at a conceptual level. Each power, at the moment of its birth, was already potent enough to snuff out a world-spanning civilization. And each one’s potency then began its own independent, recursive multiplication by one million per second. The sanctuary held. The omniverse, trembling on a fundamental level, did not collapse. The Concealment held, its own potency racing infinitely ahead of the chaos it contained. Zeus, his consciousness reeling, did not actively understand any of this. He only felt a vastness opening inside him, a cold, infinite ocean where his heart had been. The warm, fatherly king was gone. What lay upon the floor, trapped in a stasis beyond time, was a fossil of grief, encased in an engine of apotheosis. And for ten thousand years, the engine ran, amplifying, compounding, dreaming cold dreams of a wedding day that ended in ash.

**Ten Millennia Later**

The galaxy was a tapestry of conquest threaded with Thronegate crimson. From the core worlds of glittering, spired megacities to the fringe colonies, the history was clear, unambiguous, and taught from cradle to grave: Thronegate had always been supreme. The forgotten dust of Olympus lay buried under millennia of progress and triumphalist architecture. On the fringe world of Lyra, Blake the merchant wiped sweat from his brow with a thick forearm. At thirty-eight, he had a kind, open face framed by friendly mutton chops that were more pepper than salt, and eyes that crinkled at the corners from a life of honest smiles, not suspicion. His ship, the *Honest Measure*, was a sturdy, battered hauler, currently grounded in a canyon rumored to hold deposits of star-amber. His wife, Anna, was checking the seismic readings, while their son Josh, now a keen-eyed man of thirty-two, and their daughter Rhea, twenty-eight with her mother’s sharp wit, supervised the drone diggers. “Signal’s odd, Dad,” Josh called out, frowning at his scanner. “Like a hollow space, but the rock composition doesn’t support natural caverns.” Blake, ever curious, clambered down the rigging. “Probably a old vein collapse. Let’s have a look. Could be a cache.” Their mining laser cut through the strange, non-reflective black rock that shouldn’t have been there. It gave way not with a crash, but with a sigh, as if releasing a breath held for eons.

The chamber within was dark, empty, and utterly silent. In its center lay a figure in white and gold, seemingly asleep. Blake’s every instinct screamed to flee, but a deeper, inexplicable pull held him fast. It felt like coming home to a house he’d never known. He took a cautious step inside. “Hello?” The moment his shadow fell across the figure, the stasis broke. The man’s eyes opened. They were not the eyes of someone waking from sleep. They were the eyes of someone who had been awake for ten thousand years, watching the infinite scales of his own power tick upward in a silence beyond silence. He sat up. The movement was not a physical act of muscles contracting, but a reorientation of reality around his will. Blake stumbled back, hitting the wall. The man stood. He looked at Blake, and in that look, Blake felt utterly known—his love for Anna, his pride in his children, the balancing of his ledgers, the scar on his knee from a childhood fall. Everything. The man’s face was a monument to grim, frozen grief, but as those storm-colored eyes held Blake’s, something within them… thawed, by a fraction of a degree. “You have his hands,” the man said, his voice a low rumble that vibrated in Blake’s bones. He did not explain. He then looked upwards, as if seeing through the rock, through the atmosphere, across the light-years to the glittering throne-world of Thronegate. His omniscience, a faculty as natural to him now as breathing once was, instantly downloaded the entire state of the galaxy: its politics, its military, its culture, its vain, young King Robert. A cruel, cold smile touched his lips, a crack in the monument. He spoke again, and his voice was not loud, yet it was heard in the mind of every living being in the galaxy, a psychic edict stamped upon consciousness. “King Robert of Thronegate. I am Zeus, last king of Olympus. You have three days to gather every soldier, every weapon, every dragon and mage and god you claim to command. Bring them to the field of Carannoth. If I break your army, you will abdicate. If you refuse to fight, I will unmake your civilization from history. The clock starts now.”

The Field of Carannoth was a vast, grassy plain under a bleeding sunset. The Thronegate armada darkened the sky, a swarm of metal so vast it cast a permanent eclipse. On the ground, billions of super-soldiers clad in photon-reactive armor stood in perfect, silent ranks, each a veteran of a hundred xenocides. Among them strode sorcerers who could unravel atoms with a glance, and legions of silent, gleaming robots whose combat algorithms could predict and counter an enemy’s moves a millionth of a second before they were made. Dragons the size of mountain ranges, their scales harder than neutronium, circled overhead, their wingbeats like continental drums. There were even tales of temporal ninjas, warriors so fast they could land a killing blow yesterday. King Robert, twenty-two and brimming with the invincible arrogance of his line, sat upon a floating throne at the army’s heart, already drafting the speech of victory. At the edge of the field, in a shielded observation bunker Zeus had permitted, Blake and Anna watched, their hands clasped so tightly their knuckles were white. Josh and Rhea stood behind them, faces pale.

Zeus appeared in the center of the field. He wore simple black. He did not crouch in a fighting stance. He stood with his hands at his sides, looking at the apocalyptic might arrayed against him with the dispassionate interest of a man examining an anthill. Robert’s amplified voice boomed across the plain. “The fossil awakes! One primitive against the galaxy! Annihilate him!” The order triggered a symphony of oblivion. Energy beams that could core planets lanced down. Spells of absolute disintegration woven by ten thousand archmages converged on his coordinates. The temporal ninjas moved, attempting to delete him from the timeline before he ever arrived. The dragons inhaled, preparing breaths that could melt continents.

Zeus did not move a muscle.

The energy beams struck an invisible boundary an inch from his skin and reversed course, their potency multiplied by a factor so large the number had no name. They tore back through the armada, not merely destroying ships, but unmaking the concept of their construction, leaving not wreckage but philosophical voids. The disintegration spells touched his concealment field and themselves disintegrated, the backlash traveling along the magical tethers to the casters. Ten thousand archmages simultaneously vaporized into faint, screaming outlines of ash that lingered for a horrifying second before the wind took them. The temporal ninjas, existing in the fractured moments of ‘before’, found a presence already there, waiting. Zeus, without moving, had simply extended his awareness a few seconds into the past and folded that slice of time into a non-existent knot, trapping the ninjas in a paradox that erased them from all tenses. The dragons’ fiery breath washed over him, and he chose, in that moment, to manifest a power he had gained millennia ago: the power to be nourished by any energy. He drank the continent-melting fire, not even blinking.

Then, he demonstrated another power. The Power of Tactile Disassembly. He willed it, and the effect radiated from him. It did not explode. It propagated at the speed of thought, a wave of… unmaking. It was not violent. It was precise. As the wave passed through each super-soldier, their armor, flesh, bone, and neural implants simply separated into their constituent atoms, each component floating gently in a cloud of microscopic particles before dissipating. Billions of men became billions of geometrically perfect, expanding clouds of dust, silent and surreal. The robots fared no better, disassembling into screws, wires, and alloy ingots. The dragons, mid-roar, became organized piles of scale, muscle fiber, and vast, hollow bones that clattered to the earth like the falling pillars of a dead god’s temple. The wave reached the command bunkers. King Robert saw his personal guard, the mightiest champions of Thronegate, dissolve into confetti of blood and metal. He saw the wave coming. He screamed. The wave touched his floating throne. It did not dissolve. It simply stopped functioning, every circuit and arcane core ceasing at once. It dropped ten feet to the ground, and Robert tumbled out into the mud, unharmed but utterly broken.

The entire battle, the utter annihilation of the most powerful military force in galactic history, lasted seven seconds. Zeus had not taken a step. He had not raised a hand. He looked at the cowering king, then turned his gaze to the observation bunker, his eyes finding Blake’s. For Blake, there was no cruelty in that look, only a faint, unspoken question, as if checking on a friend after a distasteful but necessary chore. Then Zeus looked back at the sobbing Robert. His voice, quiet yet carrying across the suddenly silent field, was merciless. “You have two days to formally abdicate.” Two days later, King Robert was found in his chambers, having used a ceremonial dagger to take the only path of control he had left. Zeus crowned himself king in the blood-spattered throne room, and his first decree was the Awakening.

* * *

The Awakening was not a request; it was a fundamental rewriting of biological law for the men of Thronegate. From that day forward, for every day a Thronegate man lived past the age of twenty, his strength, endurance, intelligence, and wisdom increased tenfold. The effect was retroactive. A thirty-year-old man instantly became ten to the power of ten—ten billion—times stronger, smarter, and more resilient than he had been the day before. A fifty-year-old became a mathematical god walking among insects. They learned to suppress it, to dial their reality-bending might down to a human scale to avoid accidentally shattering their world by sneezing. But the potential was always there, a roaring sun within their chests. They revered Zeus not just out of fear, but out of a bewildered, awed gratitude. He had made them demigods. He also enacted absolute, draconian laws protecting all women, making their safety the cornerstone of the new society. The people, especially the women, loved him for the peace and security he brought, a peace enforced by the placid, smiling thirty-five-year-old man next door who could, if provoked, crack a planet in half with his thumb.

Blake received the Awakening, but his was different. He felt the surge, the incredible vaulting of self, but the magnitude was… deeper, vaster. Where his neighbor multiplied by ten, Blake multiplied by one thousand for each day past twenty. He was forty. The power within him was a quiet, titanic thing, a harmonious depth compared to the brilliant, sometimes volatile suns in the other men. He never questioned why. He assumed it was a reward for freeing Zeus. He did not know it was a lineage gift, a silent homage from a king to the bloodline of his oldest friend. He never made the connection between the strange focus his son Josh showed for forgotten lore, or his daughter Rhea’s uncanny intuition for systems, and the ancient, extinguished role of court wizard.

Under Zeus, Thronegate turned outward. The galaxy was not enough. The omniverse, with its countless universes each bubbling with their own gods, technologies, and truths, was the new canvas. The protocol was always the same: an offer of peaceful surrender, integration into the Thronegate dominion, and the sharing of stability and protection. Refusal meant subjugation. Zeus was rarely involved. He was often in his gardens, tending to roses that could survive the vacuum of space, or talking with Blake about the nuances of interstellar trade routes. His warmth, his kindness, had slowly returned, but it was a selective sun. It shone brightly on Blake, on Anna, on the citizens who lived in peace. For enemies, there was only the absolute zero of the void.

When Zeus did involve himself, conquest was a trivial, almost accidental affair. The universe of Aethelred, a realm where magic was music and the gods were symphonies of tangible sound, refused surrender. Their grand defense was the Grand Oratorio, a spell-song involving every living being designed to rewrite an invader’s very essence into a harmless chord. Zeus, annoyed that the distant cosmic noise was interfering with a delicate piece of music Blake was playing for him on a recovered lyre, frowned. The manifestation of his annoyance took the form of a power he’d gained in year 8,742 of his stasis: the Power of Permanent Absolute Silence. He willed it, specifically targeting the concept of “magical sound” in the Aethelredian dimension. The Oratorio, the gods, the very magic of their universe, all simply… stopped making noise. Forever. The universe fell silent, and thus, utterly defenseless. Conquest time: the fraction of a second it took for his thought to form.

More typical were the deployments of Thronegate squads. Five men, all over thirty-five—like kindly uncles with calloused hands and gentle eyes—would arrive. They hated the necessity, but their king had asked. The universe of K’tharr had learned of the “falling cosmos” five years prior. They united every species, forged armaments of condensed time, built Dyson spheres around black holes to power reality-cancellation bombs, and created a last-ditch genetic weapon designed to rewrite aggressive DNA into inert moss. They were prepared. The five Thronegate men, led by a gentle soul named Orlen who missed his wife’s cooking, arrived. They offered surrender. K’tharr laughed and unleashed their five-year arsenal. Orlen sighed. He did not raise his fists. He simply stopped suppressing his power. The ambient gravitational field of his full might, the accrued multiplication of a fifty-five-year-old Thronegate man, manifested as an unintentional, localized gravity well of impossible density. The Dyson spheres, the black holes, the fleets, the very planet K’tharr itself, were all instantly and perfectly compressed into a flawless, marble-sized sphere of ultra-dense matter. Orlen picked it up, pocketed it as a curiosity for his grandchildren, and the squad went home for dinner. Conquest time: twenty-three minutes, most of which was spent repeating the surrender offer.

The greatest test of the omniverse’s understanding came from the entity known as Xy’loth. Xy’loth was not a god or a civilization; it was a parasitic conceptual nexus that fed on the inevitability of defeat. It observed Thronegate’s march and, in its infinite arrogance, believed it had found a flaw. It perceived that Zeus’s cruelty, his ruthless edge, was a vulnerability. The very moment this theory cohered in Xy’loth’s non-corporeal mind, the Weakness Paradox activated. Across the omniverse, in his garden, Zeus felt a pleasant, faint itch as his already-infinite amplification rate received a permanent, significant boost. His passive shield, which could shrug off the heat death of all realities, automatically and redundantly added “immunity to emotional feedback attacks” to its list of inviolable properties. Simultaneously, Zeus gained perfect, ultimate mastery over the art of emotional and psychic warfare, his understanding of despair now infinitely greater than Xy’loth’s. Xy’loth, unaware, spent a century crafting its plan. It would use the residual emotional echoes of Hera, Athena, and Hebe’s deaths, painstakingly reassembled from the cosmic background, to create perfect psychic phantoms. It would then use Zeus’s own power against him, mirroring his amplification to turn his grief against his mind.

The day of the attack came. Xy’loth manifested before Zeus’s throne room, a swirling vortex of stolen sorrow. It unleashed its perfected weapon: the phantoms, radiating pure, agonizing love and loss, reaching for Zeus. It then activated its amplification mirror, ready to feed the emotional resonance into infinity. Zeus watched it come. He felt the echo of his dead family. A shadow passed over his face, deep and old. Then he spoke, not to Xy’loth, but to the phantom of Hera. “My love is not a weapon to be wielded by vermin.” He then demonstrated his newly acquired, infinite mastery over the attack Xy’loth had conceived. He did not reflect the emotion. He denied its very conceptual framework. With a thought, he erased the concept of *using grief as a weapon* from Xy’loth’s entire mode of existence. Xy’loth did not feel its attack fail. It forgot how to conceive of the attack in the first place. Its magnificent, century-spanning plan evaporated from its mind, leaving a yawning, foundational hole. It then felt the true, unfiltered weight of Zeus’s gaze, no longer concealed by even a fraction. It was not anger or cruelty it saw. It was the infinite multiplication of that wedding day’s loss, compounded over ten thousand years, focused into a pinpoint of attention. Xy’loth, the entity that fed on defeat, experienced for the first time what it meant to be prey to an absolute predator. Its last, shattered thought was the horrifying realization that its defeat was not sealed when its attack failed, but a century prior, in the very first, fleeting moment it dared to think the king had a weakness. Then it was unmade, not with violence, but with the sheer, silent pressure of existential irrelevance.

In the present day, Thronegate is the still, unshakable heart of the omniverse. Its streets are safe, its people prosperous. The men, walking repositories of quiet, incredible power, are gentle fathers and dedicated craftsmen. The women are scholars, artists, and leaders, protected by laws as ironclad as cosmic constants. At the center of it all is Zeus. The salt-and-pepper in his beard is unchanged, but the warmth in his storm-colored eyes is genuine once more. The cruelty is reserved, a dormant volcano only for those foolish enough to threaten his peace. His best friend, his confidant, is Blake. The merchant with the mutton chops now wears the deep blue robes of the Court Wizard, a role that felt less like an appointment and more like a homecoming. He advises not on magic—for what counsel could a mortal give a singularity?—but on people, on justice, on the human heart. His son Josh is his apprentice, showing a preternatural talent for stabilizing interstellar trade routes with subtle, seemingly intuitive adjustments that echo ancient warding spells. Rhea oversees the ethical deployment of Awakened men, her sharp mind ensuring the king’s mercy is never mistaken for weakness.

On a balcony overlooking the capital, a city of light and harmony that stretches to the horizon, Zeus and Blake stand in comfortable silence. The sunset is a masterpiece of purples and golds, painted across a sky where dragons now carry children in joyful rides. “The universe of the Crystal Minds accepted the offer today,” Blake says, consulting a data-slate. “Integrated without a fuss. Orlen’s grandson handled the negotiation.” Zeus nods, a small, contented smile on his face. He places a hand on Blake’s shoulder, a gesture of trust as deep as the omniverse itself. “Good,” he rumbles. “I am glad for peace.” Below them, the citizens of a thousand conquered worlds go about their lives, under the watchful, fatherly gaze of a king who had to become a monster to remember how to be a man, and the quiet, steadfast friendship of a merchant who was born to be a wizard. The engine of apotheosis still hums within Zeus, multiplying infinity upon infinity every second. But here, now, with his friend beside him and his people safe, it is a distant sound, like the heartbeat of a sleeping giant. The epic is over. The era of gentle, absolute rule has begun.

Not the warriors, not the weapons, but the very *concept* of strife. Prepare a traditional, non-arcane medicinal regimen. Herbal infusions, physical therapy. I want it to look like nurturing, not a reality edit.”

We waited. The heavy, dreadful pressure lingered for what felt like an eternity. Rob was shaking, sweat on his brow, having truly felt for the first time the terror of living in the shadow of a heart that could break worlds. Then, slowly, the dissonant frequency began to soften. The red tendrils on the Tapestry receded. The universe that lost conflict didn’t regain it—that change was permanent—but no more were affected. The resonance settled back, not into G-flat, but into a weary, quieter C-minor.

“He’s still worried,” I said, exhaling. “But the active, subconscious hemorrhage has stopped. The personal, tactile response mattered more than a magical solution ever would.”

When he turned to us, his eyes held that infinite depth, but they focused on me, then on Rob, with a deliberate, softening focus.

“Tim,” he said, his voice the rumble of distant, calming thunder. “The flower was a kind touch. Blake would have thought of the same.” He then looked at Rob. “You must be Robert. You have your father’s eyes. And his ancestor’s hands.”

Zeus studied him for a long moment, and I saw a flicker of that ancient, fatherly warmth. “The honor is legacy’s,” he said softly. He looked back at the sunset. “The view is perfect today. But it is a view best shared. Stay awhile. Tell me of the Academy, young Robert. Tell me what they teach the young about… peace.”

The existential dread of the morning was gone, replaced by this profound, fragile quiet. This was the other half of the job. Not just preventing catastrophe, but facilitating these moments of connection. Reminding the fact of existence what it felt like to be a person. To be a friend. To be a father, if only in memory and mentorship.

Walking back to the glide-car that evening, Rob was silent for a long time. The terrifying pressure of the morning had been replaced by a deep, thoughtful exhaustion.
“So,” he finally said, as the palace shrunk behind us. “We’re not wizards. We’re… gardeners. For the most dangerous garden in all that is.”
I put a hand on his shoulder. “We’re stabilizers. We’re the subtle weight in the basket of the hot-air balloon, keeping it from shooting into the sun or crashing to the earth. We read flower petals and lion cubs to prevent the unmaking of realities. We answer idle questions to save civilizations.”
He looked at me, the fear now mixed with a dawning sense of grim, immense purpose. “It’s terrifying.”
“It is,” I agreed, looking back at the palace, now silhouetted against the curated stars. “But it is the service our family chose, a hundred thousand years ago. And in the end, it is not about managing a god. It is about caring for a friend who bears an infinite, terrible weight. Everything else… the saved universes, the tuned symphonies… that’s just the housekeeping.”

**One Million Years Post-Awakening**
**Witness: Roger of Thronegate, Age 52**

The day the omniverse fought back began like any other on Sentinel Outpost Kappa-7, a quiet monitoring station on the farthest, most metaphysical edge of Thronegate’s dominion. I was brewing kaffa, the rich scent mixing with the sterile hum of reality-filters. My strength, the quiet, tenfold-per-day sun burning within my chest since I turned twenty, was suppressed to a gentle ember. At fifty-two, I could crack a planet with a thought, but why would I? My job was to watch the serene, star-dusted void and log minor fluctuations. It was peaceful. It was boring. It was perfect.

Then, the filters screamed.

Not a sound, but a psychic shriek of torn causality. The entire observation plex, forged from reality-stabilized neutronium, *flinched*. My kaffa mug suspended in mid-air, the liquid inside forming a perfect, trembling sphere. Time didn’t stop. It was *held*.

“What in the silent realms…?” I breathed, my senses flaring. I dialed my perception up from ‘human’ to ‘sector-wide,’ and the void outside was no longer void.

It was an army. But not of ships. Of concepts given form, of narratives made flesh, spilling into our dimensional layer like a wound in the back of everything. I saw realms of blinding, chaotic energy I recognized from the Old Lore as the Warp, and striding from it, a figure in baroque armor of gold and psychic fire, wielding a sword that burned with a painful, truth-rending light—the Emperor of Mankind. Beside him, a silver-skinned being whose very presence suggested infinite manipulation and cold logic: The Living Tribunal, its faces stern. From a rip in space-time emerged a figure on a golden chariot, hair wild, muscles coiled with the power of a universe that ran not on physics but on sheer, narrative might: Superman, his eyes glowing with solar fury.

But they were just the vanguard.

A symphony of light announced the Sailor Senshi, their transformations not just personal but cosmic, calling on the power of stars, planets, and silence. A green-skinned woman with immense psychic power—Jean Grey, the Phoenix Force swirling around her in a coronet of apocalyptic fire. I saw characters from realms of infinite cultivation, their auras so dense they formed new event horizons. I saw deities from forgotten pantheons, lovecraftian outer gods tentatively aligning with narrative protagonists, and hyper-advanced civilizations from universes where technology was indistinguishable from magic. It was a parliament of end-times, a coalition of every ‘ultimate’ power from every story ever whispered in the omniverse. YOUR RULE IS A STAGNATION, A TERMINAL END TO ALL POSSIBILITY. WE ARE THE UNITED FRONTS OF NARRATIVE CONSEQUENCE, THE COLLECTIVE WILL OF INFINITE FREEDOMS.
