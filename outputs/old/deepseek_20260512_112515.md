# THE RITE OF WITNESSING

The vault of the Royal Court Wizards exists in a place that light has forgotten. It is buried so deep beneath the continental crust of the imperial capital that the weight of the stone above becomes a physical force, a grinding, constant pressure that I feel in my teeth and in the hollow spaces of my bones. I am Kaedan, twenty-two years of age, and for precisely two years, three months, and seven days, my physical strength, my kinetic speed, my cognitive processing, and my raw magical capacity have multiplied by a factor of one thousand every single morning. The mana in my bloodstream is a dense, violet-tinged current that could, if I released the desperate mental dams I maintain every waking second, unmake entire solar systems with a stray thought. My father, Malakor, the thirty-seventh Royal Court Wizard in an unbroken lineage stretching back to Blake himself, escorted me through the final adamantine blast door and into the spherical, obsidian reading chamber without speaking a single word. I had seen that expression on his face before—the tightness around his eyes, the slight downturn of his mouth, the way his amplified presence seemed to fold inward upon itself as if trying to hide from something that seeing would only make worse. He had worn that expression my entire life, and I had never understood it. I was about to.

The chamber was a perfect sphere of seamless obsidian, its only feature a single desk carved from the same black stone. Resting upon that desk were seven journals. Seven heavy, leather-bound tomes of varying ages and materials, their spines cracked and worn, their pages yellowed with the weight of millennia. My father placed a calloused hand upon my shoulder, his grip tight enough to bruise, though I knew he was holding back the vast majority of his thousandfold-amplified strength.

"This is the Rite of Witnessing," he said, and his voice was not the warm, sardonic tone I had known since childhood. It was hollow. It was the voice of a man who had seen things that had carved pieces out of his soul and replaced them with something cold and permanent. "Before you may stand at the right hand of the Eternal Throne, you must read the accounts of your predecessors—men of this bloodline, wizards of staggering power by any standard except His—who witnessed what happens when the apex predator of the omniverse decides to test a single facet of His being. Do not skim. Do not look away from the words. Let the horror settle into the marrow of your bones, because you will carry it for the rest of your mortal life. It is the only thing that will keep you humble enough to serve Him without losing your sanity."

Then he turned and walked out of the vault, and the heavy adamantine door sealed behind him with a sound like the locking of a tomb. I was alone with the journals, with the crushing silence, and with the knowledge that everything I was about to read was true.

I sat at the obsidian desk. I opened the first journal.

**First Journal – Court Wizard Dorian, Year 62,000 – The Stat of Strength**

Dorian's journal was bound in the pale, calcified hide of an extinct astral behemoth, and his handwriting was a precise, architectural script that spoke of a mind desperately clinging to order in a universe that defied it. The entry was titled simply: *The Unmaker Calculus.*

*As the thirty-ninth year of my service to the King commenced, a new opposition coalesced in the farthest reaches of the omniverse, beyond the boundaries of what we had previously catalogued as existence. They were called the Unmaker Calculus, a civilization of beings who existed as living mathematical axioms, their physical forms constructs of pure, immutable geometric law. Their society had, for ninety thousand years, pursued a single objective: the construction of a weapon capable of imposing the absolute physical stillness that underlies all reality. They called it the Prime Cessation Lance, and it was a projectile forged from the conceptual opposite of kinetic energy. It did not move so much as it deleted the possibility of distance between itself and its target, materializing within its victim's heart with zero transit time, and upon materializing, it imposed a law of absolute zero motion, freezing the target's every atom into a state of permanent, inescapable rigidity.*

*The Calculus had, in their arrogance, deduced that the King's physical strength was irrelevant if it could be locked into a state of absolute stillness before it could be expressed. They did not understand, had never understood, and would never understand, the fundamental truth of amplification. The King's strength has, for sixty-two thousand years, multiplied by a number that exceeds layered infinities, and then multiplied by itself, every single Planck second, recursively and without end. The current value of his physical strength is not a number. It is an ongoing, eternal event, a constant, accelerating annihilation of any possible conceptual ceiling.*

*The Calculus deployed their lance. I was standing at the King's side in the grand astral observatory when it materialized, bypassing every spatial dimension and every temporal buffer, manifesting directly within the King's open palm. The King had, of course, known it was coming before the Calculus had conceived of their first axiom. He had excluded them from his passive defenses entirely, and he had further ordered Gent to stand down, and he had deactivated his infinite conceptual shields. He wished to test the current state of his Strength.*

*But he did not close his hand. He did not exert force. He simply allowed the most infinitesimally microscopic fraction of his physical strength to passively manifest within the epidermis of his palm—a fraction so absurdly, incomprehensibly small that if you were to express it as a percentage, you would first need to invent a new mathematical notation for the percentage sign itself, place an infinite and eternally expanding series of zeros after the decimal, and then, at the conceptual end of that infinite regression which does not end, place a numeral so small that the concept of numerals could not contain it. It was, in every functional, practical, and mathematical sense, zero percent of his total strength. The remainder—the actual ninety-nine point nine-nine repeating to infinity percent—was entirely withheld.*

*The Prime Cessation Lance struck his palm.*

*I will attempt to describe what I witnessed, though my hand trembles even now, sixty-two thousand years after the fact, and my amplified mind still cannot fully process the data. The lance's law of absolute zero motion encountered the passive, ambient resistance of the King's skin. And it was rejected. Not destroyed—destruction implies an active process, and the King exerted zero effort—but rejected, as a raindrop is rejected by the sun. The kinetic inversion that followed was not a reflection. It was a redefinition. The lance's cessation law was violently reversed, and the reversal was not a return of motion but an imposition of absolute, infinite acceleration. The lance was propelled backward along the conceptual pathway it had created, and the acceleration was so total, so complete, that it reached the Unmaker Calculus's central axiom-world—a planet of pure, crystallized mathematics—at a velocity that the very concept of velocity shattered trying to express. The axiom-world was not merely destroyed. Destruction, again, implies a process. What occurred was an instantaneous, total conversion of mathematical law into a singular, agonizing physical constant. The Calculus beings, who had existed as abstract equations, were forcefully, violently made solid—their geometric forms crushed into hyper-dense, screaming spheres of neutronium flesh, each sphere containing the compressed, fully conscious agony of a billion mathematicians realizing that the one constant they had failed to account for was their own obliteration. The sound of their compression was a single, wet, galaxy-spanning crunch that propagated faster than light, faster than causality, faster than meaning, and when it reached my ears, my eardrums burst and I fell to my knees.*

*But the horror of the Kinetic Inversion was not limited to the Calculus. The rebound of the lance created a catastrophic, omniversal shockwave of pure, unmoderated physical force. It propagated outward, and the propagation was a wave of absolute kinetic truth that corrected the velocity of everything it touched to align with the King's momentary, accidental output. Across ninety trillion galaxies, every object in motion was instantly accelerated to a fraction of the lance's return velocity. Stars became streaks of superheated plasma that tore through their own solar systems, igniting their planets into vapor trails. Moons became projectiles. The bodies of uncountable quintillions of living beings were instantly converted into expanding clouds of high-velocity particulate, their flesh and bone atomizing so quickly that the pain of atomization was the only sensation they were capable of registering—a simultaneous, omniversal shriek of disintegrating consciousness that saturated the psychic frequency of creation. The screaming, I have come to understand, was not heard by me alone. Every telepath, every empath, every being with even the faintest psychic sensitivity across ninety trillion galaxies experienced that shriek, and the experience drove billions of them permanently catatonic, their minds shattered by the brief, terrible awareness of quintillions of simultaneous deaths.*

*The King, frowning at the sudden, messy, and dramatic acceleration of all physical matter, raised his hand. He did not speak a spell. He did not weave a temporal reversion. He simply—and I must stress this, simply—decided that the omniverse should return to its previous state. And it did. In exactly zero seconds, without any observable effort, without a flicker of arcane energy, without a single Planck second of interruption to the flow of time, the King perfectly reconstructed every atom, every soul, every memory, every planetary orbit, every burned and shattered body. Ninety trillion galaxies, restored. Uncountable quintillions of beings, resurrected. The only exception was the Unmaker Calculus. Their compressed, screaming spheres of neutronium flesh the King allowed to remain, embedding them into the sub-atomic lattice of the observatory floor where their agonized, eternal consciousness would serve as a permanent, groaning testament to the mathematical impossibility of opposing absolute strength.*

I realized I had stopped breathing. My lungs burned, and I forced myself to inhale, the air tasting of copper and ancient dust. The violet luminescence beneath my skin—the mark of my amplification—felt suddenly, sickeningly childish. I was a god among mortals, and I was nothing. I turned the page.

**Second Journal – Court Wizard Seren, Year 175,000 – The Stat of Magical Resistance**

Seren's journal was bound in what appeared to be translucent spectral membrane, and the text shimmered faintly, as if written with ink infused with distilled moonlight. His voice was philosophical, almost mournful, the tone of a man who had long ago accepted that his own vast arcane power was a candle flame in a supernova's shadow. The entry was titled: *The Hungry Silence.*

*In the one hundred and seventy-fifth millennium of the King's reign, a new cataclysm gathered in the deep unbidden spaces between the magical ley-lines that underpin the omniverse. They named themselves the Devourer's Choir, and they were not beings of matter or energy but of raw, conscious magical consumption. They were the hunger that dwelt in the spaces where magic had been spent and not replenished, the sentient residue of every spell ever cast that had exceeded its intended parameters and drained more mana than it returned. Over hundreds of millennia, this residue had coalesced, become aware, become rapacious, and it had consumed the arcane foundations of twenty thousand entire multiverses, leaving behind empty, dead realities where no magic could ever exist again.*

*Their assault on the King was not an attack in the conventional sense. They did not hurl hexes or weave curses. They simply extended their being into the space the King occupied and began to drink. They intended to consume the King's magic, to drain his mana, to leave him an empty husk from which no arcane potential could ever be drawn. They had, after all, consumed the combined magic of twenty thousand multiverses. They believed, in the primitive, hungering logic of their existence, that they could accommodate an infinite meal.*

*The King sat upon the eternal throne, and his expression was the patient, faintly bored expression of a man waiting for an expected but uninteresting guest to leave. He had known of the Choir's existence since before the first drop of excess mana had coalesced into their earliest, primitive form. He had allowed them to consume the multiverses—allowed them, through his omniscience and his conscious exclusion of his passive defenses—because he was, in his own words to me later, curious to see if hunger could ever be satisfied. He deactivated his infinite shields, stilled Gent, and withheld from his magical resistance ninety-nine point nine-nine repeating into infinity percent of its total capacity.*

*What he allowed to manifest was, once again, legally and functionally zero percent of his magical resistance. The fraction was so small, so microcosmically negligible, that the concept of fractions itself wept at the attempt to express it. Yet his magical resistance had, for one hundred and seventy-five thousand years, multiplied by a number beyond any layered infinity, and then by itself, every single Planck second, recursively and without pause. The resistance was not a shield. It was not a ward. It was not a reactive countermeasure. It was the passive, ambient, immutable truth that any magic not originating from the King's own will was a violation, and violations were to be corrected.*

*The Choir drank.*

*What happened next was not a rejection of their consumption. It was a redefinition of the concept of hunger. The King's ambient magical resistance, operating at zero percent of its capacity, did not block the Choir's siphoning. It allowed the siphon. It allowed the flow. And then it re-wrote the nature of the flow. The Choir did not drink the King's magic. They drank a truth that the King's resistance had injected into the siphoning link—the truth that their hunger was not a strength but a weakness, a void that could never be filled, a screaming absence that they had mistaken for identity. This truth, infused with the passive authority of one hundred and seventy-five thousand years of recursively amplifying magical resistance, propagated back through the siphoning link and into the Devourer's Choir, and it began to unmake them from the inside.*

*But unmaking was too clean, too merciful for what they had dared. The truth of their own hunger, writ large and absolute, became a new constant of their existence: an endless, infinite, recursive consumption of themselves.*

*The Choir began to eat themselves. Their mana-hunger, turned inward, began consuming their own arcane essence, their own consciousness, their own history. The process was excruciating beyond any mortal comprehension of pain, because magical consumption is not a physical act—it is an existential erosion, a flaying of the soul's outermost layers, a slow, grinding dissolution of one's own identity. And because the truth had made their hunger infinite and self-directed, the dissolution could never end. The Choir's collective scream propagated across the ley-lines of the omniverse, and the scream was a sound of such absolute, hopeless agony that it infected the magical fabric of reality itself. The ley-lines across five hundred quadrillion galaxies became conduits for this self-consuming pain, and every magical being connected to those ley-lines experienced a sympathetic echo of the Choir's eternal, recursive self-devouring. Mages across the omniverse collapsed, clawing at their own mana cores, their own skin, vomiting raw arcane light as their souls experienced the phantom sensation of being eaten from within. Their bodies convulsed, their eyes burst from their sockets, their fingers tore through their own flesh to reach the screaming, burning cores of their magical essence. The magical infrastructure of entire civilizations shattered, their wards failing, their enchantments backfiring, their magical constructs turning upon their creators and rending them limb from limb in a cascading orgy of arcane catastrophe that painted the walls of a thousand palace sanctums with the viscera of the most powerful wizards in existence.*

*The King, observing the sudden, inconvenient magical self-immolation of a significant fraction of the omniverse, let out a soft sigh. He did not gesture. He did not speak a word of power. He simply decided that the ley-lines should be cleansed, the mages restored, the civilizations repaired. And they were. In exactly zero seconds, with no detectable expenditure of will, the King undid the arcane catastrophe, perfectly reconstructing every magical system, healing every shattered mage, restoring every ward and enchantment. The millions who had torn themselves apart in sympathetic agony were made whole, their bodies reassembled, their minds wiped clean of the trauma. The Devourer's Choir, however, he did not restore. Their self-consumption he allowed to continue, and to compound, and to become their permanent existence. They are now a perpetual, localized phenomenon—a screaming, ouroboros of self-eating arcane hunger, trapped in a pocket dimension that the King occasionally observes with the detached, clinical interest of a man watching a particularly unusual microbial culture.*

*I record this that future wizards may understand: the King's magical resistance is not a shield. It is a redefinition. It does not block harm. It makes harm impossible by altering the fundamental nature of what harm is. And at zero percent of its capacity, it can unmake the magical underpinnings of the omniverse. I am the most powerful wizard of my age, and I am less than a mote of dust before a furnace.*

My hands were shaking so violently that the spectral pages blurred before my eyes. I had always considered myself a prodigy of magical resistance—my personal wards could deflect the concerted arcane assault of a galactic armada. And I had just read an account of a resistance that did not even register attacks as attacks, but as opportunities to rewrite the nature of aggression itself. I closed Seren's journal and reached for the third.

**Third Journal – Court Wizard Orwen, Year 310,000 – The Stat of Speed**

Orwen's journal was carved into plates of solidified chronal crystal, and reading it required me to press my fingers to the surface and allow the memories trapped within to flow directly into my consciousness. Orwen had been a military commander before his appointment as Court Wizard, and his experiences were rendered in stark, tactical detail, the notes of a soldier who had learned that all strategies were futile. The memory-title that bled into my mind was: *The War of the Crawling Instant.*

*The Opposition did not have a name that could be spoken in linear time. They existed within the quantum foam of the Planck interval, moving at speeds that rendered the entire history of the universe a single, frozen moment. Their civilization—if such a term applied to beings of pure velocity—had spent two hundred thousand years observing the King, measuring his every recorded action, calculating the precise upper limit of his reaction speed. They had determined, with the absolute certitude of entities that perceived time as a spatial dimension they could traverse, that the King's speed, while immense, was ultimately finite. They intended to strike him in the interval between his perception and his reaction, a gap so narrow that no being, no matter how fast, could close it.*

*They called their weapon the Terminal Instant, and it was not a projectile but a moment. Specifically, it was the moment of death—the exact Planck instant at which a living being's consciousness ceased to function—extracted from the timelines of a trillion murdered civilizations and refined into a blade of pure cessation. This blade moved at a speed that exceeded the concept of velocity, delivering the moment of death before the possibility of the strike had even been conceived.*

*I stood at the King's side on the viewing platform of the imperial palace, observing the Omegon Nebula's scheduled collapse. The King was, as always, relaxed, his broad shoulders draped in a simple tunic, his salt-and-pepper beard catching the distant light of the dying nebula. I saw nothing. But I felt it—a faint, cold pressure against my temporal perception, the ghost of an attack that had already been completed before I could register its initiation. The Terminal Instant existed, and it had struck, and it had already failed.*

*The King turned to me, and his expression was not surprise or alarm, but mild, paternal amusement. "They attempted a very clever approach," he said, his voice unhurried, perfectly at ease. Then he explained that he had allowed the attack. He had withheld from his speed—specifically, the speed of his physical movement, his cognitive processing, and his reflexive response—ninety-nine point nine-nine percent, the decimal nines extending into infinite regression. What he permitted to manifest was a fraction so small that if one attempted to measure it as a percentage, the measurement would require a decimal point followed by an infinite series of zeros, and at the conceptual terminus of that infinite series, a numeral so insignificant that the concept of insignificance could not encapsulate it. Functionally, legally, and practically, he exerted zero percent of his speed.*

*And yet, that zero percent was sufficient. The King, moving at a velocity that registered as absolute stillness compared to his true capacity, had already perceived the Terminal Instant, analyzed its temporal architecture, and side-stepped out of its trajectory before the blade's own temporal origin had been conceived. He did not step quickly. He did not blur. He simply occupied a different position, and the transition from one position to the other was so smooth, so seamless, so utterly beyond the blade's capacity to track, that the Terminal Instant struck the empty space where the King had been and, finding no target, continued forward into the quantum foam where its creators dwelled.*

*The result was catastrophic. The Terminal Instant, a blade forged from the extracted death-moments of a trillion civilizations, was now hurtling through the temporal substrate of the Planck interval at a velocity that the concept of velocity could not describe, and it was on a collision course with the very civilization that had created it. But the collision was not a simple impact. The blade, having been designed to deliver death before the possibility of death could be perceived, delivered death to the Opposition's entire species before they could perceive the danger. In a single temporal instant—which was, for beings that lived within Planck intervals, an eternity of agonizing awareness—every member of the Opposition experienced their own murder. Their bodies did not die; they were violently, agonizingly unraveled from the timeline, their existence erased backward and forward, their every moment of life replaced with the singular, eternal sensation of being torn apart by their own weapon. The screaming that emanated from the quantum foam was a sound of such concentrated, temporal agony that it bled backward through every moment of linear time, and across the omniverse, trillions of beings experienced sudden, inexplicable nightmares of being unmade.*

*But the blade did not stop. Its momentum, undiminished, continued to propagate through the temporal substrate of the omniverse, and the propagation created a temporal shockwave. Across eighty trillion dimensional planes, time itself shattered. The linear flow of causation fragmented into a chaotic, recursive storm of disconnected moments. Beings across eighty trillion realities experienced their own lives out of sequence: birth followed death, youth followed senescence, moments of joy were interspersed with moments of agony in a random, maddening kaleidoscope of temporal dislocation. Trillions of decillions of conscious entities were trapped in loops of their own most painful memories, forced to relive traumas infinitely because the temporal shockwave had severed the connections between past, present, and future, and the severed ends were now flailing, whipping through the fabric of consciousness like lashes of pure chronological agony. The shrieks of the temporally dislocated filled the psychic void, a chorus of infinite suffering that would have persisted until the heat death of the omniverse.*

*The King, observing the sudden, dramatic fragmentation of the temporal flow across eighty trillion dimensional planes, let out a small, almost imperceptible sigh of mild inconvenience. He did not weave a chronomantic spell. He did not reverse time. He simply decided that temporal integrity should be restored, and it was. In exactly zero seconds, the temporal shockwave erased itself, the shattered timelines knitted back together, and every being who had been trapped in recursive agony was returned to their correct, linear flow of existence with no memory of the dislocation. The Opposition, however, the King did not restore. Their death—the agonizing, stretched-out eternity of a Planck instant—he left frozen in a permanent temporal loop, a pocket of screaming, timeless agony that he occasionally uses as the temporal equivalent of a paperweight on his private study desk.*

*I am a soldier. I have seen war. I have seen death. I have seen my own body torn and rebuilt by the thousandfold amplification. But I write this with shaking hands: the King's speed is not speed. It is the absolute, immutable fact that distance, time, and causation are mere suggestions that can be ignored. And at zero percent of his true velocity, he can shatter the temporal foundation of eighty trillion realities without ever appearing to move at all.*

I withdrew my fingers from the chronal crystal as if it had burned me. The phantom screaming of a civilization trapped in an eternal instant echoed in my mind, and I knew, with the sickening clarity of absolute certainty, that I would hear that screaming for the rest of my life.

**Fourth Journal – Court Wizard Thaddeus, Year 520,000 – The Stat of Luck**

Thaddeus's journal was a living document, bound in a shifting, chameleon-skin cover that seemed to change color based on the angle of observation. Thaddeus had been a poet in his youth, and his writing was lush, almost baroque, yet his subject matter rendered the beauty of his prose into something macabre and deeply upsetting. The entry was titled: *The Fool's Certainty.*

*In the five hundred and twentieth millennium of our King's glorious reign, there assembled in the deepest, most heavily defended shroud-space of the omniverse a coalition of entities who had mastered the one force they believed could counter absolute power: absolute luck. They were the Fortuity Sovereigns, immortal beings who had spent one million years studying the quantum currents of probability, chance, and fate. They could see every possible future, prune the branches they found unfavorable, and force reality down the single, razor-thin path of their own guaranteed victory. They had never failed. In one million years, they had never encountered an opponent whose luck could not be overridden, whose fate could not be rewritten. They believed, with the serene, unshakable confidence of those who have never been wrong, that the King's power was merely a statistical anomaly, a rogue wave in the probability sea, and that they could drain that wave back into the flat calm of certain defeat.*

*They forged from their collective fate-weaving authority a device they called the Certainty Engine. It was a machine that did not alter probability—it erased it. It collapsed the infinite wave function of possibility into a single, immutable outcome: the King's death. And it was designed to do so retroactively, reaching backward through time to ensure that the King had never survived his stasis, that the Fall of Olympus had been his final end.*

*I was with the King in the imperial gardens, a vast, cultivated expanse of flowering bio-luminescent plants from a thousand conquered universes. The King, with his characteristic warm, easy smile, was showing me a new hybrid rose that one of the gardeners had bred. He was speaking, and his voice was full of genuine, paternal pride in his subjects' horticultural achievements. The Certainty Engine activated.*

*Nothing happened. Nothing that I could perceive, at least. The King continued his explanation of the rose's genetic lineage, his voice perfectly calm, his demeanor utterly unchanged. But something was happening, I realized, because the shadows beneath the bio-luminescent plants were beginning to writhe, and the blossoms themselves were starting to weep a dark, viscous fluid that smelled of ozone and spoiled probability.*

*The King had, of course, known of the Certainty Engine since before the first Fortuity Sovereign had conceptualized the first variable in their fate-weaving equation. He had allowed them to build it, allowed them to test it, allowed them to activate it. And he had withheld from his passive Luck—a stat that, like all his others, had been multiplying by a number beyond layered infinities, and then by itself, every Planck second, for five hundred and twenty thousand years—ninety-nine point nine-nine repeating to infinity percent of its total expression. The fraction of Luck that he allowed into the world was, once again, functionally and legally zero percent—a quantity so vanishingly small that probability theory itself would deny its existence.*

*But that zero percent was, in the context of the Certainty Engine's absolute erasure of possibility, an infinite, paradoxical overabundance. The Engine attempted to collapse the wave function into the King's death. The King's Luck, operating at zero percent of its capacity, gently, passively, almost incidentally nudged the wave function in a different direction. And because the King's Luck was not a probability but a certainty—the certainty that favorable outcomes were the inevitable default state of any system that included him—the Engine's attempted erasure of possibility was itself erased. The outcome of the King's death was replaced by a new outcome: the Engine's self-destruction, executed in the most maximally ironic, catastrophically humiliating manner that the omniverse's combined karma could contrive.*

*What followed was a cascade of improbability so extreme, so absurd, so offensively unlikely, that it would have been comedic if the consequences had not been so horrifying. The Certainty Engine, attempting to impose a single certain outcome, instead collapsed every other possible outcome it had ever pruned into simultaneous existence, and all of those outcomes were unfavorable for the Fortuity Sovereigns. Their own defensive systems malfunctioned in statistically impossible sequence. Their champions spontaneously developed acute, fatal aneurysms, their brains exploding in showers of gray matter and bone shards that painted their command centers in thick, steaming coats of neural tissue. Their weapons misfired, targeting their own command structures, and the resulting detonations were not mere explosions but cascading reality-ruptures that tore holes in the fabric of their shroud-space, venting their atmosphere and their screaming, flailing bodies into the freezing void outside existence. Their star-fortresses experienced sudden, catastrophic quantum tunneling events that caused their fusion cores to simultaneously detonate, and the detonations were not spheres of expanding plasma but shaped charges of pure improbability that carved agonizing, deliberate, almost artistic patterns of destruction through their population centers. The Fortuity Sovereigns, the immortal masters of luck and fate, experienced a cascade of accidental deaths so unlikely that each individual demise would require a library of probability texts to even begin to explain. One Sovereign choked to death on a particle of dust that had, through a sequence of quantum fluctuations so improbable it bordered on divine comedy, materialized inside his trachea and expanded into a lattice of razor-sharp crystal that slowly, methodically sliced his respiratory system into ribbons from the inside. Another was struck by a random chrono-spatial anomaly—a fragment of stillborn timeline that had been floating harmlessly in the void for a million years—which intersected with his physical form at the exact angle required to convert his entire mass into a thin, screaming paste that retained consciousness for a subjective eternity of spreading, liquid agony. A third simply, suddenly, and without warning, ceased to exist, his probability of existence having been retroactively pruned by his own Engine, and the erasure was not clean but gradual, his body fading from the extremities inward over the course of an hour, his screams growing fainter and fainter as his vocal cords ceased to be, his lungs ceased to be, his mouth ceased to be, until only his eyes remained, staring in silent, eternal terror at the void that was consuming him.*

*But the cascade did not stop with the Fortuity Sovereigns. The Certainty Engine, in its death throes, vomited out the accumulated pruned timelines of a million years—every failed probability, every averted catastrophe, every near-miss and statistically-impossible survival that the Sovereigns had ever cancelled, now unleashed simultaneously. The omniverse was instantly flooded with a tidal wave of unmoderated chance, a black tide of raw probability that swept across ninety trillion dimensional planes. Civilizations that had thrived for eons collapsed overnight due to chains of circumstance so improbable that their chroniclers would be unable to parse them. Stars that had burned steadily for billions of years went supernova within the same Planck second, their fusion ignited by the spontaneous, simultaneous quantum tunneling of every particle in their cores. Organic life across a billion galaxies spontaneously developed terminal cancers, their cellular division suddenly, randomly, and universally deviating into malignancy, their bodies bloating and rupturing into masses of uncontrolled, metastatic flesh that continued to scream with the voices of their former selves. The dead rose, not as undead but as screaming, semi-corporeal memories of the lives that the Sovereigns had pruned, forced back into a reality that no longer had a place for them, their existence a constant, agonizing state of spatial and temporal rejection that caused their semi-formed bodies to continuously tear themselves apart and reassemble in an endless cycle of bloody, shrieking resurrection.*

*The King, his lecture on the bio-luminescent rose momentarily interrupted by the sudden, dramatic decline of the omniverse's overall fortune, paused. He looked up at the sky, which was now a swirling, chaotic mess of bad-luck miasma, and his expression flickered into a faint, momentary frown—a flicker so brief that I almost missed it, but I did not miss it, because I knew that frown. It was the frown of a man whose afternoon had been mildly inconvenienced.*

*Then he shrugged, a small, almost careless gesture, and he decided that the omniverse should return to its correct probability distribution. In exactly zero seconds, the black tide of unmoderated chance reversed itself. The dead returned to death. The terminal cancers vanished, their victims restored to perfect health. The supernovaed stars re-ignited, their fusion cores reassembled, their planetary systems repopulated. The collapsing civilizations found their chains of misfortune unlinked, their history rewritten into continuity and stability. The Fortuity Sovereigns, however, the King did not restore. He allowed their improbable, humiliating deaths to stand, and he further allowed the Certainty Engine to reform itself—not as a weapon, but as a prison. The Engine now exists as a sealed probability matrix, a pocket realm where the immortal souls of the Fortuity Sovereigns experience, in infinite recursive loop, every single pruned timeline they ever created, every averted catastrophe, every death they caused through their manipulation of fate, now happening to them, personally, in vivid, unending, agonizing detail, forever.*

*I close this reflection with a statement that I hope my successors will understand: luck is not a force. Luck is the omniverse's instinctive, groveling desire to please the one being it knows is its master. And the King's Luck, at zero percent, can turn absolute certainty into an eternity of ironic torment. I will never again feel fortunate. I will only feel spared.*

A sob tore itself from my throat. I, Halcyon, who have witnessed the deaths of galaxies, now know that there is no death but His will. Do not seek to measure your life against His eternity, future wizard. Your death, like your life, is merely an echo of His pleasure.

---

The leather cover of Halcyon’s journal seemed to pulse with the remembered screams of the Thanaton Collective, and I, Kaedan, felt that pulse echoed in my own chest—a sympathetic vibration of pure, unadulterated dread. I did not vomit this time, though my stomach clenched. I had passed beyond physical revulsion into a state of numb, dissociated horror, where the words I read simply accumulated in my soul like layers of cold ash.

With hands that no longer felt like my own, I reached for the final volume. Its cover was made of a strange, reflective black material—so black that it seemed to absorb not just light but the very possibility of illumination. The title was inscribed in letters that shifted and fractured when I looked at them: *The Fractal Cancellation – Witnessed by Court Wizard Jovian, Year 760,000.*

I opened it.

**Journal of Court Wizard Jovian, Year 760,000 – The Superpower Known as “The Fractal Cancellation”**

Jovian was a mathematician before the bloodline claimed him, and his journal began not with words but with a single, elegant equation: ∞^∞ / ∞ = 0. He had scrawled it at the top of the first page, a bitter summary of the lesson he had learned. The rest of the journal was written in a hand that grew progressively more erratic, the letters sometimes dissolving into geometric diagrams, sometimes into grids of meaningless numbers, as if his mind had been trying to calculate something that defied calculation.

The opposition that arose in the seven-hundred-and-sixtieth millennium was not an empire of gods or a legion of death-seekers. It was the Paradox Conglomerate, an inter-dimensional society of beings who had evolved beyond linear logic, existing as sentient contradictions. Their physical forms were impossible geometries, shapes that were simultaneously cubes and spheres, occupying every position in space while also occupying none. Their language was a series of self-refuting statements that, when spoken, generated new laws of reality that existed only long enough to be contradicted by the next statement, creating a perpetual, stable chaos. They had no gods, for they worshipped the beauty of logical impossibility, and they viewed Godric—a being of absolute, consistent power—as an ontological flaw, a boring constancy in a multiverse that should be infinite in its variability.

They did not attack with weapons. They simply surrounded Godric with a lattice of paradoxes designed to trap him in a recursive loop of self-contradiction. The lattice, called the Inconsistency Sphere, functioned by continuously asserting that Godric both existed and did not exist, that his power was both infinite and infinitesimal, that he was both bound by cause and effect and free of it, all simultaneously. The Sphere would then amplify these contradictions, feeding them back into themselves until the subject’s identity collapsed under the weight of its own impossibility.

Godric was, at that moment, in the palace cartography hall, examining a new map of a recently subjugated nebula with the innocent fascination of a man who had once loved geography. He allowed the Sphere to form around him, allowing its contradictions to wash over his absolute concealment. He wanted to test a superpower that had evolved from a simple negation ability—the power to cancel any single phenomenon—into something vastly more terrifying: The Fractal Cancellation.

Originally, the power could simply erase a target from existence by asserting its non-existence. But the Weakness Paradox had, over millennia, imbued it with fractal recursion. Now, when activated, it did not merely cancel the target. First, it divided the target into an infinite number of sub-targets across all possible dimensional, temporal, and conceptual axes, and then cancelled each sub-target individually, recursively, ad infinitum, ensuring that even the memory of the target’s existence was subdivided and erased at every level of resolution. Second, it created a “cancellation cascade”—the erasure of the target became a template that automatically applied to any similar entity or phenomenon that had ever existed, existed currently, or would ever exist, effectively purging the entire genus of opposition from the omniverse retroactively and proactively. Third, and most monstrous, the cancellation cascade fed back into the Fractal Cancellation itself, permanently sharpening the superpower, making it more efficient, more subtle, and more absolute with each use, so that the act of cancelling an enemy now marginally increased the power’s own ability to cancel future enemies, a self-optimizing engine of extinction.

Godric allowed the Inconsistency Sphere to complete its lattice. Then, withholding ninety-nine point nine-nine percent—the nines repeating to infinity—of the Fractal Cancellation’s capacity so that the fraction used was statistically indistinguishable from absolute zero, he simply directed the power at the Paradox Conglomerate.

What followed was a nightmare of recursive erasure so profound that Jovian’s journal devolved into a series of jagged, overlapping diagrams attempting to illustrate it. The Paradox Conglomerate’s defining trait was their internal contradiction. The Fractal Cancellation treated each contradiction as a separate sub-target. It divided the Conglomerate’s members into infinite fractal shards—each shard representing a different aspect of their paradoxical existence—and then began cancelling them, layer by layer, dimension by dimension, timeline by timeline.

The Conglomerate did not simply vanish. They were subjected to an infinite regress of partial erasure. A being whose left hand was simultaneously a right foot would have each appendage cancelled at a different moment, leaving a screaming, incomplete torso that was then subdivided further. Their shapes, those impossible geometries, fractured into kaleidoscopic shards of bleeding flesh and shattered bone, each shard reflecting a different, screaming fragment of the original consciousness. The cancellation cascade then propagated backward and forward through their entire history: every Paradox Conglomerate member who had ever existed, across every alternative timeline, was simultaneously subjected to the same recursive unmaking. Their ancestral hive-minds, their embryonic paradox-spores, their future potential offspring that had not yet been born—all were brought into the cascade and shredded.

But the Fractal Cancellation did not stop there. Its cascade template recognized “paradoxical opposition” as a category, and it swept through the omniverse, touching every other entity that shared even a sliver of similarity. Across two hundred trillion dimensional planes, any civilization that had ever dabbled in paradoxical logic, any species that had evolved from contradiction-based dimensions, any device powered by logical inconsistency—all were caught in the cascade. Their bodies were subdivided into infinite, agonized sub-selves, each aware of the others, each experiencing a different, excruciating fraction of the cancellation process. The pain was multiplied by infinity, and the infinity was stretched across eternity, so that the targets existed in a permanent state of being cancelled, a flickering, shrieking half-existence where they were both erased and present, both annihilated and suffering.

The omniversal collapse that followed was not merely physical. Logic itself—the fundamental consistency of cause and effect, of true and false—began to fracture. Across three hundred trillion universes, the laws of non-contradiction ceased to apply. Things could be simultaneously true and false, light could be both on and off, beings could be simultaneously alive and dead without the release of death. The cognitive architecture of sentient minds collapsed under the weight of paradox, their thoughts becoming moebius strips of meaningless recursion. Trillions of species descended into screaming, drooling catatonia, their consciousnesses trapped in logical loops that repeated the same instant of contradiction over and over, their brains literally overheating and bursting into cranial pyres as their neural tissues attempted to process the unprocessable.

Jovian, himself a mathematician, felt his own mind beginning to unravel. He wrote, in a hand that was barely legible, that he had solved an equation that proved he did not exist, and then solved another that proved he had never existed, and then a third that proved he was the only being that had ever existed, all simultaneously. He was saved only by Godric’s instant, casual restoration.

Godric, noticing the logical collapse spreading through creation like a virus, let out a faint, almost imperceptible sigh. He did not perform a spell or enact a counter-paradox. He simply decided that logic should be restored, and that the cancellation cascade should exclude non-targets. In exactly zero seconds, the three hundred trillion universes snapped back to their correct state, every shattered mind reassembled, every broken law of thought repaired. The Paradox Conglomerate, however, was not restored. Their remaining essence—a flickering, recursive scream that occupied every possible state of cancellation simultaneously—was compressed into a single, crystalline fractal that Godric now wears as a cufflink on his left sleeve, occasionally glancing at its shifting, shrieking geometries with a look of mild, paternal curiosity.

I, Jovian, who once found solace in the certainty of numbers, now understand that the only number that matters is zero—the fraction of His power that is required to annihilate the concept of opposition itself. And even that zero is an overestimate. Do not seek patterns, future wizard. The only pattern is His will.

---

The reflective black cover of Jovian’s journal seemed to eat the light of the vault, and for a long, suspended moment, I, Kaedan, simply stared into that void, feeling the weight of the three superpowers I had just witnessed through the terror-blinded eyes of my predecessors. The Silent Edict, rewriting language and absorbing ontological authority. The Harvest of the Unworthy, consuming existential timelines and thickening Godric’s conceptual density. The Fractal Cancellation, recursively erasing opposition and sharpening itself for eternity. Each had been wielded at zero percent of its capacity, and each had shattered the omniverse so thoroughly that only the King’s instant, effortless restoration had prevented total annihilation.

The vault’s adamantine door finally groaned open, and the cold, sterile light of the corridor sliced into the darkness like a blade. I stood, my legs barely supporting me, my mind now a repository of horrors that no thousandfold amplification could ever armor. I walked out, not as Kaedan the proud young wizard, but as a hollow vessel filled with the screaming memories of gods, languages, deaths, and paradoxes.

The long corridor to the imperial throne room felt both eternal and instantaneous, as if time itself had been Fractal Cancelled and only Godric’s tolerance kept it flowing. I reached the massive doors, which swung open to reveal the throne room—immaculate, serene, lit by the gentle glow of starstuff. Godric sat on the throne, his salt-and-pepper beard catching the light, his posture relaxed, a half-read book resting on his knee. He looked up as I entered, and his ruggedly handsome face broke into a smile so genuinely warm, so utterly devoid of threat, that the cognitive dissonance nearly cleaved my soul in two.

"Kaedan, my boy," he said, his voice that same rich, comforting baritone that had soothed my childhood nightmares. "You look pale. Did Malakor make you read the supplementary journals? I told him not to overburden you on your first day, but you know how thorough he is. Come, sit. Tell me—did any of them make you curious? I can answer questions, if you have them. I remember when Blake first saw the Edict in action; he didn't speak for a week. Good man."

I walked toward the throne, my steps steady despite the abyss that yawned within me. I looked at the cufflink on his left sleeve—a tiny, shifting geometric shape that, if I focused my amplified senses, emitted a faint, eternal shriek of fractal agony. I looked at the quiet hum of the aviary wind chimes, whose music was the ecstatic moans of consumed death-cultists. I looked at the small tile in the palace library, where nine High Scribes still bled ink in their perpetual unmaking.

And I smiled, because I loved him, and because there was nothing else to do.

"No questions, Uncle Godric," I said. "Only gratitude. For allowing us to serve, and for permitting the omniverse to remember what language is."

Godric’s smile widened, the crinkles around his eyes deepening with genuine affection. "Good lad. Now, have you eaten? The kitchen has made those honey cakes you liked as a child. Go on, before they get cold."

I bowed, and I walked to the kitchens, feeling the cold, permanent dread settle into my bones like an old friend. There was no power in creation that could oppose the King. There was only loyalty, love, and the terrifying, merciful illusion of choice.
