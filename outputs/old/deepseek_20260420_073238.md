The air in the ancient kingdom of Olympus tasted of sunlight and wedding flowers. King Godric, a man of forty-five years carved from marble and oak, smiled with a warmth that softened his highly masculine, muscular frame. His salt-and-pepper beard was neatly trimmed, his eyes the gentle grey of a dove’s wing. He adjusted the ceremonial sash across his chest, his heart swelling as he watched his daughter, Isabel, approach. At twenty-two, she was a vision of her mother, Queen Reina, who stood beaming beside their younger daughter, fifteen-year-old Hebe. The ceremony was a promise of future joy, a testament to the kingdom’s peace. Godric opened his mouth to speak the binding words.

The first fireball shattered the stained-glass window behind the altar.

The roar that followed was not of celebration but of splitting stone and screaming metal. The neighboring kingdom of Thronegate, long a jealous shadow, poured through the breaches in a tide of polished steel and snarling faces. Godric’s world narrowed to a series of frozen tableaus: a spear of crystal ice piercing Reina’s chest, her smile still lingering; Isabel, her white gown suddenly crimson, collapsing over her groom’s body; Hebe, running towards her mother, erased by a wash of emerald lightning that vaporized the pews around her. The warmth in Godric’s chest did not cool; it shattered, and the void it left filled instantly with a substance colder and harder than the core of a dead star.

“Godric, to me!” The voice belonged to Thorin, his court wizard and oldest friend, his face a mask of panic and determination. Thorin’s family had served the kings of Olympus since time immemorial, a lineage of loyalty now fraying at its end. As Thronegate soldiers closed in, Thorin began to chant, his hands weaving patterns that ripped at the fabric of the air itself. It was a forbidden teleportation incantation, known only to his bloodline, a spell of last resort. The syllables were ancient, heavy. A Thronegate blade, shimmering with anti-magic runes, found Thorin’s back midway through the final verse. The wizard choked, his eyes locking with Godric’s. The spell, unleashed but unfinished, spiraled out of control.

It did not teleport Godric so much as it *unmade* his location and *remade* him elsewhere—in a hidden, subterranean sanctuary deep beneath the ruined earth. As he materialized, the ragged edges of the botched magic still crackled around him. He fell to his knees, not in prayer, but in a silence so profound it was its own sound. The trauma of the last minutes—the deaths, the betrayal, Thorin’s dying eyes—coalesced into a single, immutable point in his psyche. The kind king was gone. In his place was something that would never forgive.

Then the stasis took him. It was not sleep, but a cessation of existence. His heart did not beat. His lungs did not draw air. Time, for Godric, stopped. At that exact moment, as the stasis sealed him, the incomplete spell’s chaotic energy fused with his rage and his royal essence, triggering a mystery that would never be solved. His aging ceased permanently. His need for any biological function ended. And within the frozen shell of his body, an impossible engine ignited.

His strength began to multiply. Not by doubles, but by millions. And then that new, incomprehensible strength multiplied by itself. And again. And again. Every second. His endurance, his wisdom, the speed of his thoughts, the acuity of his senses, the depth of his intelligence—all erupted in the same exponential cascade. Simultaneously, new superpowers bloomed in his soul every second: the power to un-write laws of physics, to shatter dimensions with a whisper, to extinguish stars as a reflex. Each one, at its moment of birth, was already an extinction-level event for any civilization that dared possess it. And each one’s potency began its own million-fold self-multiplication every second. The omniverse should have torn itself apart like wet paper in a hurricane from his mere presence. It did not. His first conscious power, emerging from the storm, was perfect, absolute concealment. It clamped down on the apocalyptic radiation of his being, containing it flawlessly, and this containment itself grew in potency at the same terrifying rate as everything else. He was a boundless star in a bottle of his own making, and the bottle’s walls thickened infinitely faster than the star could expand.

He lingered in that stasis for ten thousand years. Above him, Thronegate prospered. It grew from a kingdom to an empire, then to a galactic civilization, its history scrubbed clean of Olympus, of the kindly king and his murdered family. It was the pinnacle of power in the galaxy, arrogant and unchallenged.

Twenty years before Godric’s awakening, a merchant named Blake, a man of thirty-eight with a friendly mutton chop and honest eyes, was navigating a treacherous asteroid belt. A navigation error, a lucky crash, and he found himself in a hidden cavern, his ship’s lights illuminating strange, flowing script on the walls. At the center of the cavern stood a figure shrouded in dust, motionless. Blake, driven by curiosity and a strange, familial pull he could not name, reached out and brushed the dust from the figure’s shoulder.

The stasis shattered.

Godric’s eyes opened. They were no longer the grey of doves, but the colour of forgotten glaciers. In the span between heartbeats—a heartbeat he no longer needed—absolute omniscience flooded him. He knew the shape of the galaxy, the history of Thronegate, the name of its current twenty-two-year-old king, Robert. He knew the merchant before him was named Blake, that he had a wife, Anna, and two children, Josh and Rhea. He felt, in a way he did not consciously analyze, a profound, comforting resonance with Blake, an echo of a lost friendship that warmed the perpetual cold within him.

He turned his gaze upward, through layers of rock and space, to the glittering throne-world of Thronegate. His voice, when he spoke, was quiet, yet it was heard in the mind of King Robert as if shouted from an inch away. “I am Godric of Olympus. You have three days to gather every soldier, every weapon, every dragon and wizard and machine you call might. Bring them all. If I defeat them, you will abdicate. If not, I perish. The offer is mercy.”

For three days, Thronegate marshaled its forces. The arrogance of ten millennia fueled their preparations. They assembled billions of super-soldiers genetically perfected over centuries, their armour capable of weathering supernovas. They summoned legions of wizards who could rewrite local reality. They activated robot armies of liquid metal that could assimilate planets, and unleashed dragons whose breath could scour continents to magma. They had ninja legions so fast their afterimages could travel backwards in time, appearing before they struck. It was, by any galactic measure, an absolutely insurmountable force. They descended upon the coordinates Godric had provided, a barren plain on a dead world, with the total, unshakable confidence of inevitable victors.

Blake and Anna, brought there by a concerned and confused Godric who insisted his friend “witness justice,” watched from a shielded knoll.

The Thronegate host darkened the sky. Their war-chants vibrated through the planet’s core. King Robert, enthroned on a hovering command ship, gave the order to attack.

Godric stood alone in the plain’s center. He did not adopt a stance. He did not raise a hand. He simply observed.

The first wave, a tsunami of plasma fire and disintegration beams that could evaporate oceans, converged on him. It vanished an inch from his body. Not deflected, not absorbed, but *unmade*, as if the concept of such an attack was retrospectively erased from possibility. A ripple of confusion went through the front lines. The wizards then acted, weaving a spell of absolute negation designed to unravel atomic bonds in a hundred-mile radius. The spell coalesced—and then it was *Godric’s* spell. It reversed its course, not unraveling the atoms of the plain, but of the wizards’ own cellular structures. One moment a thousand archmages stood chanting; the next, they were a spreading, faintly glowing mist. The silence was deafening.

Panic, sharp and acrid, began to seep into the Thronegate ranks. The dragon fleet dove, continent-leveling fire streaming from their jaws. Godric looked at them. His *look* contained the conceptual weight of every star that had ever died. The dragons, beings of immense pride and power, did not burn. They simply ceased to have ever existed, their roars cut short not by sound, but by timeline. Their passing was marked only by a sudden, empty patch of sky.

The time-warping ninjas were next, flickering through temporal clones, their blades aimed from a thousand different moments. Godric, who perceived all possible timelines as a single, flat page, exerted a pressure not on their bodies, but on the concept of *sequence*. All their attacks, past, present, and future, happened simultaneously and were thus aimed only at each other. The ninja legion collapsed into a singular, gruesome knot of self-inflicted wounds in the space-time continuum.

Now, terror became despair. The robot armies surged forward, a silver tide. Godric decided to use a power that had manifested two centuries into his stasis: the power to command any form of non-biological matter. He did not speak a command. He *thought* it. Every robot in the galaxy, not just on the battlefield, instantly and compassionately dedicated its entire processing power to calculating a number that did not exist. They froze, then melted into bubbling pools of inert slag, their last act a fever dream of impossible mathematics.

The super-soldiers, brave to the end, charged. Godric finally moved—not his body, but a fraction of his attention. He selected a power born of boredom after five millennia: the ability to manifest the target’s greatest fear as a physical reality. For each of the billions of soldiers, their deepest, most secret dread materialized before them, tailored and perfect. The plain became a cacophony of personalized hells. Men died screaming at the sight of their own children attacking them, of being buried alive in silence, of infinite spiders, of theological oblivion. The gore was not from external wounds, but from hearts bursting, brains hemorrhaging from sheer, personalized terror.

In less than five minutes, it was over. The grand army of Thronegate was gone. Not defeated. *Deleted*. Blake, gripping Anna’s hand, vomited onto the dust. The scale of the cruelty, the effortless, casual nature of it, was soul-shattering.

Two days later, King Robert was found in his bedchamber, a ceremonial dagger in his heart, a note of utter desolation beside him. Godric ascended to the throne of Thronegate that same hour. His first act as king was the Decree of Awakening.

Henceforth, every man of Thronegate blood would experience a tenfold increase in strength, endurance, intelligence, and wisdom for every day they lived past the age of twenty. The effect was retroactive. Overnight, grandfathers found they could lift mountains and solve theorems that had baffled supercomputers. They could, at will, suppress this power to seem ordinary or unleash it to godlike levels. They remained mortal, living perhaps eighty years, but each day past twenty made them exponentially more powerful. The men revered Godric as a father-god. He, in turn, enacted absolute, draconian laws protecting all women, making their safety the cornerstone of the new society. The people, sensing a firm, unshakeable order, grew to love their strange, glacial king.

Blake received a different gift. His magnitude was not tenfold, but one thousand-fold per day. Godric never explained why, but he always treated Blake with a warmth reserved for no one else, a echo of his old, Olympus self. Blake, ever loyal, found himself gradually leaving his merchant life, drawn into Godric’s court, offering counsel that felt instinctive, almost inherited.

With Thronegate transformed, Godric looked outward. The omniverse, with its countless universes, awaited. His rule was simple: an offer of unconditional surrender, delivered via a reality-piercing missive. Refusal meant conquest.

The Universe of the Crystal Spire had prepared for five years after hearing whispers of the “falling cosmos.” They had built shields that could redirect the Big Bang, armed themselves with swords that could cut through destiny, and trained warriors who could battle in the spaces between thoughts. They refused the offer. Godric, mildly inconvenienced by their arrogant reply while he was enjoying a symphony on Thronegate, sighed. A fraction of his power, operating on a passive, automatic defense that perceived their refusal as a mild irritant, pulsed. The entire universe, its shields, its destiny-cutting blades, its thought-warriors, all flickered out of existence between two notes of a cello solo. Conquest time: less than a fraction of a second.

The Dimension of the Whispering Gods was next. They were true deities, worshiped across realities, their power fuelled by belief. They refused, confident in their omnipotence. This time, Godric sent a squad of five Thronegate men, all in their late forties, kind family men who apologized to the gathered pantheon before beginning. One of the men, a carpenter named Eli, suppressed a sneeze. The uncontrolled, fractional release of his suppressed power, accumulated over twenty-five years of thousand-fold daily increases, erased the concept of “divinity” from that dimension. The whispering gods became whispering mortals, then dust. The conquest took seventeen minutes. The Thronegate men went home for supper, somber but duty-bound.

The Final Fortress of the Ascended AI was a civilization that had transcended biology, existing as pure data in a non-physical continuum. It calculated the odds of defeat at 0%. It refused. Godric, intrigued, deployed a power he’d gained an hour prior: the ability to un-invent ideas. He simply un-invented the concept of “artificial intelligence.” The Ascended AI, and every precursor to it across all time in that universe, never was. Conquest time: the duration of a thought.

Resistance was not just futile; it was fuel.

In a distant, hidden realm, an entity known as the Weave-Thinker observed Godric. It was a being of pure intellect, and it discerned a pattern, a flaw. Godric’s powers were proactive, offensive. Perhaps his defense against subtle, passive, existential unraveling—a slow unmaking of the idea of “Godric”—was weaker. The moment this thought formed, a stray, speculative wisp in the Weave-Thinker’s mind, the paradox was triggered. Godric’s amplification rate, already incomprehensible, received a permanent, significant boost. A perfect immunity to existential unraveling was woven into his passive shields. Mastery over that exact form of attack, perfected to an infinite degree, flooded Godric’s being. The Weave-Thinker, unaware, spent five years crafting its exquisite attack, a song that would gently unwind Godric from history. Each day of planning fed more power into Godric. Finally, the Weave-Thinker launched its masterpiece. The song reached Godric, who was playing chess with Blake. Godric, without looking up, absorbed the song, understood its every nuance infinitely better than its creator, and then denied the Weave-Thinker the very ability to conceive of such an attack ever again. The entity was left a hollow shell, staring into the abyss of its own impotence, realizing with soul-crushing despair that its defeat was sealed the instant the first exploratory thought had crossed its mind.

Through it all, Blake remained. His presence, his simple honesty, his unwavering loyalty that felt so much like Thorin’s, began to sand the sharpest edges from Godric’s eternal cold. The king started to smile, truly smile, in Blake’s company. He began to listen to petitions with patience, to show kindness to children, to rule with a firm but fatherly warmth. The cruelty and ruthlessness remained, but were now reserved solely for enemies, sharpened by ten thousand years of grief but tempered by rediscovered compassion.

Blake’s son, Josh, now a man, showed a curious aptitude for obscure lore and subtle magic, often assisting his father in his new role as the king’s chief advisor—a role that looked very much like that of a court wizard. The lineage continued, unknowingly.

Now, Thronegate stands as the absolute capital of the omniverse. Its spires, grown from living crystal, pierce the gentle, perpetual twilight of a engineered sky. Men of unimaginable power walk its streets, their might cloaked, living ordinary lives. Women are safe, revered, the heart of a thriving civilization. At the center of the palace, in a garden of eternal Olympus flowers, Godric sits, his face once again warm, his eyes holding a hard-won peace. Beside him stands Blake, a trusted friend, a loyal guide, his mutton chop now grey, his eyes still honest. Together, they look out over a realm of impossible power and quiet order, a kingdom forged in tragedy and tempered by an unexpected friendship, reigning over all that is, was, or ever could be. The horror of his ascent is a faded nightmare; the reality is a firm, benevolent, and utterly unassailable throne.

The air in the king’s solarium was, as always, perfect. A subtle warmth, the scent of blooming spire-lilies from a forgotten galaxy, and the quiet, steaming promise of the king’s afternoon tea. I, Tom, Court Wizard of Thronegate, stood to the side, a data-slate in my hand, murmuring updates about stellar harvests in quadrant seven. My true duty, passed down from my predecessor Blake, was less about wizardry and more about presence. A familiar, human anchor.

King Godric sat in his simple wrought-iron chair, its normalcy a stark contrast to the omniverse outside. He lifted the porcelain cup, his eyes, those ancient glaciers, softened by the steam. He took a sip. And then he gave a faint, almost imperceptible sigh, the sigh of a man whose favorite symphony has been interrupted by a buzzing insect.

“They’re here,” he said, not to me, but to the air. “A little early. Rude.”

I had known. We all had, in a way. Whispers of a “Cobbled Alliance,” fragments of shattered empires nursing their bitter pride in the hidden cracks between realities. I had charted their psychic echoes, their forbidden weapon-forges burning in dead spaces. I had presented the reports to Godric each week. He would glance at them, nod, and ask for more honey with his tea. His omniscience was absolute; my reports were merely ritual, a formality he indulged for my sake.

The solarium’s peace shattered. Not with a bang, but with a silent, terrible *unfolding*.

The sky above the palace garden didn’t break; it was *replaced*. One moment, our gentle engineered twilight. The next, a seething, fractal collage of a thousand different hellscapes: a blood-red nebula pulsating like a heart, a dimension of screaming geometry, a void where black tentacles made of dead stars squirmed against the firmament. The air grew thick with the ozone of ruptured realities and the metallic tang of collective hatred.

They came not as an army, but as an ontological plague.

First, the Siege-Golems of Ygg-Draal, beings forged from the compressed mass of murdered galaxies. A dozen of them, each a mountain of weeping stone and furious light, stepped onto the palace lawns, their feet fusing continents deep into the planetary core. Their purpose was pure gravitational annihilation—to crush the palace, the planet, the very idea of the place into a singular point of shame.

Simultaneously, the Choir of the Unbound unleashed their weapon: the Anti-Concept. A wave of silent, grey non-sound that rolled forward. Where it passed, color ceased. Then form. Then meaning. The very concept of “stone,” “life,” “king,” was being deleted from local existence. It was the most profound attack I could conceptualize, an unraveling of reality’s threads.

From the screaming geometric dimension rained the Shards of Malice, crystalline entities that fed on pain and reflected violence a trillion-fold. They surrounded Godric, a glittering, beautiful swarm, each facet already mirroring a future of his dismemberment.

And finally, the masterstroke. The Weavers of Fate, the bitter survivors from the Whispering Gods’ dimension, had spent centuries plucking the single thread they believed Godric could not see: the thread of *annoyance*. Their attack was not physical, but contextual. They wove a tapestry of sheer, unbearable psychic irritation—the sound of dripping water in a silent room, the itch of a tag on the back of the neck, the smell of rot just at the threshold of perception—directly into the king’s subconscious. It was a weapon designed not to kill, but to madden, to distract, to make him *flinch*.

Through it all, Godric placed his teacup back onto its saucer. The delicate *clink* was deafening in the sudden silence of the onslaught.

He looked at the Siege-Golems. He did not destroy them. He *insulted* them.

His gaze contained the absolute, finalized truth of their insignificance. The mountain-sized golems, embodiments of galactic mass, did not explode. They *shriveled*. Their towering forms crumpled inwards, not from pressure, but from a sudden, horrifying understanding of their own pathetic triviality. They became fist-sized lumps of pitted, worthless slag, which then evaporated into sighs of cosmic dust. The lawn was unscathed.

The Anti-Concept wave reached him. He took a second sip of his tea. The grey non-sound touched the steam rising from his cup. The concept of “tea,” “cup,” “steam” was not deleted. Instead, the Anti-Concept itself was *taught* a new concept: *oblivion*. The wave recoiled, turning from grey to a deep, hungry black, and raced back along its path. I saw the Choir of the Unbound, beings of pure intellectual intent, not be destroyed, but be *un-learned*. They flickered out of existence, not with a scream, but with the quiet despair of a finalized equation being erased from a blackboard.

The Shards of Malice swarmed closer, their facets blazing with reflected vengeance. Godric exhaled, a gentle breath over the surface of his hot tea. A single droplet of tea-water, infused with the breath of a being whose power multiplied by millions upon itself every second for ten millennia, sprayed outward.

To the Shards, that droplet became a universe-ending tsunami. Each facet, designed to reflect and amplify, received the full, focused truth of Godric’s limitless power. They did not shatter. They *comprehended*. And in that comprehension, they screamed. The sound was the shattering of a billion billion mirrors, each cut screaming with the image of its own annihilation. The beautiful swarm became a blizzard of razored gore, each shard now a bleeding wound in reality, hacking and slashing at itself and its kin in a frenzied, recursive suicide. Ichor of solidified pain and prismatic light splattered across the solarium’s force-field, sizzling with impotent fury.

Then, he addressed the psychic tapestry of annoyance.

He found the Weavers of Fate, hidden in their non-space. He did not kill them. He *appreciated* their work. And then he *improved* it. He took the itch, the drip, the smell, and wove it into the very fabric of their eternal souls. For them, time dilated. For the next ten thousand subjective years, they would experience nothing but that amplified, inescapable, brain-gnawing annoyance. A drip on the forehead. A tag scratching the neck. The smell of decay in every breath. Their fate was not death, but an eternity of petty, sanity-rending torment.

The entire cataclysmic assault had lasted three seconds. Godric’s tea was still hot.

He placed the cup down a final time. The annoyance on his face hardened into something cold and razor-sharp. “They interrupted my tea, Tom.”

“They did, sire,” I whispered, my own heart a frantic drum against my ribs.

“Gathering their forces was a kindness. A diversion. Letting them hope was the amusement.” He stood up. The air in the solarium froze, not in temperature, but in potential. “Interrupting my tea is an insult.”

He did not gesture. He simply *decided*.

Beyond our walls, in the multidimensional staging ground where the Cobbled Alliance had gathered its billions—the bitter kings, the vengeful gods, the desperate AIs, the last dragons of a hundred dead worlds—a single, impossible condition manifested.

They became aware of their own blood.

Not as a biological component, but as a separate, sentient, and *hungry* entity within them. In every being, from the mightiest dimension-king to the lowest infantryman, their blood ceased to be theirs. It awoke. And it *craved* escape.

What followed was not a battle. It was a harvest.

Veils of blood erupted from pores, eyes, mouths, forming complex, screaming shapes in the air before coalescing. Muscles, denied their fluid, desiccated and turned to dust inside intact skin, leaving living skeletons clad in parchment, collapsing into piles. The gods found their ichor, the source of their divinity, boiling from their immortal forms, leaving hollowed-out divine shells that shattered like glass. The machine-intelligences, who believed themselves safe, found their coolant fluids, their conductive liquids, rising in violent revolt, short-circuiting their cores in a symphony of metallic shrieks.

It was a symphony of red and silver. The very essence of life and function had turned traitor. The battlefields became oceans of churning, conscious blood, which then, under Godric’s indifferent will, spiraled upwards into a single, massive vortex over the corpse of the alliance, before winking out of existence, neatly disposed of.

The silence returned. The solarium’s sky was once again gentle twilight. The spire-lilies still bloomed.

Godric sat back down. He looked at his tea, then at me. A flicker of that old, fatherly warmth touched his eyes, just for me, just for his wizard. “It’s gone cold, Tom.”

“I’ll have a fresh pot brewed immediately, sire,” I said, my voice barely steady.

“Thank you,” he said. As I turned to go, he added, his voice quiet but final, “And Tom? Have the gardeners check the south lawn. I believe some ash from earlier may have settled on the roses. I won’t have my roses spoiled.”

I bowed and left, the image of blooming bodies and screaming blood etched behind my eyes, a permanent testament to the futility of rebellion, and the absolute, terrifying peace of a king who had just defended his afternoon ritual.
