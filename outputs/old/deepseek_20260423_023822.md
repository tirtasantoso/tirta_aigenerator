The air in the hidden sanctuary tasted of forgotten centuries, of stone and silence so profound it felt like a physical weight. Blake’s lantern beam cut through the gloom, trembling in his hand. He was a merchant, not an explorer, but a collapsed tunnel in the asteroid belt had revealed this place, and curiosity, that old killer, had won out. The chamber was circular, dominated by a dais upon which a figure sat upon a throne of fused crystal and bone. The man was immaculate, powerfully built, his short hair and neat beard a striking mix of black and silver. He looked like a king carved from marble and left to gather dust for a hundred years. His eyes were closed.

“Hello?” Blake’s voice was swallowed by the room. He took a step closer, his boot scuffing a symbol etched into the floor—a complex, spiraling design that seemed to drink the light. A faint, residual hum vibrated in his teeth. He reached out, not to touch the man, but to brush dust from an armrest.

The moment his fingers grazed the cold crystal, the world stopped.

The figure’s eyes opened. They were not eyes that had been closed for a long sleep. They were eyes that had witnessed the death of a universe ten thousand years ago and had been watching the birth of every star since. They held the chill of absolute zero and the fury of a billion supernovae. Blake stumbled back, a scream trapped in his throat, his very soul shriveling under that gaze. But just as quickly, the pressure vanished, replaced by a focused, terrifying clarity.

The man—Godric—stood. He did not stretch stiff limbs. He simply was, as if the intervening millennia were a single, dismissed breath. He looked at Blake, and in that look, Blake felt his entire life laid bare: his love for Anna, his pride in Josh and Rhea, the honest sweat of his trade, the hidden fear of failure. Godric saw it all, understood it all, in less than an instant.

“You have my thanks,” Godric said. His voice was calm, pleasant even, but it carried the weight of cosmic foundations. He turned his head, not as one looking around a room, but as one surveying the entirety of a spiral galaxy. His gaze settled on a distant point, a world of glittering spires and arrogant power. Thronegate. Memory, not as recollection but as an eternal, bleeding wound, flooded his being. The wedding. Reina’s smile. Isabel’s blush. Hebe’s laughter. The fire. The screams. Thorin’s desperate, severed incantation.

The warmth of that past was ash. What remained was a cold, infinite engine of vengeance.

On the dais, the remains of Thorin’s spell, dormant for epochs, finally completed its purpose. The sanctuary dissolved into light, and Blake found himself standing on a grassy hill under a foreign sun, Godric beside him. The king did not speak to Blake again. He lifted his face to the sky and issued a challenge. It was not a shout. It was a fundamental adjustment to reality, a decree etched into the fabric of local spacetime, heard in the mind of every living being on the planet Thronegate, and specifically, as a gentle whisper inside the skull of its twenty-two-year-old king, Robert.

“I am Godric, last king of Olympus. You have three days to gather every weapon, every soldier, every shred of power your stolen legacy has granted you. I will stand in the Valley of Kings. Bring your might. I will break it without moving a muscle. If I succeed, you will abdicate. If you refuse the challenge, I will unmake your world down to its last memory.”

Then Godric turned to Blake, and the terrifying aspect vanished, replaced by something softer, almost paternal. “You should stay. You and your family. You will be safe. I would have you see this.”

Blake, his mind reeling, could only nod. He sent a frantic, confused message to Anna: *Come to these coordinates. Bring the children. Don’t ask why.*

***

Ten thousand years earlier, the air in Olympus had been perfumed with jasmine and celebration. King Godric, at forty-five, felt a fullness in his heart he thought would never fade. He adjusted his ceremonial sash, smiling as his wife, Reina, fastened a pearl clasp. His daughter Isabel, radiant in her wedding gown, fussed with her veil while young Hebe teased her about her nervousness. The courtyard was a tapestry of color and joy. His best friend, Thorin, the court wizard, gave him a steadying wink. The bond between their families was older than the kingdom itself.

The first fireball did not come from the sky. It erupted from within the crowd, a blossom of green flame that incinerated fifty guests instantly. Then the sky tore open. Thronegate’s assault was not an invasion; it was an extermination. Dragon-riders clad in void-armor rained spells that unraveled stone and flesh. Legions of spectral ninjas flickered into being, their blades moving so fast they cut through moments, leaving victims dying in the past. Godric roared, seizing a sword, but he was a king, not a god. He saw Reina shield Hebe before a beam of black light vaporized them both. He saw Isabel, running towards her fallen betrothed, pierced by a spear of ice that exploded her from within.

He fought, a lion surrounded by wolves, but the tide was absolute. Thorin reached him, face bloody, one arm hanging useless. “The sanctuary! The last spell!” he screamed over the din. He began to chant, fingers weaving patterns that bled light. The air around Godric shimmered. A Thronegate assassin, a man who could step between seconds, materialized behind Thorin. His blade, forged from a dying star’s core, pierced Thorin’s heart. The wizard gasped, but his dying will forced the final syllable into the universe.

The spell was incomplete. Instead of a clean teleportation, it was a catastrophic rupture.

Godric felt the sanctuary’s cool air, saw its dim outlines, and then the world dissolved into a silent, white agony. It was not pain, but transformation. Every cell in his body froze in a state of perfect, eternal stasis. Aging ceased. Biological needs evaporated. His heart no longer needed to beat. His lungs no longer needed air. This was the zeroth second.

Then the amplification began.

In the first second, his strength multiplied by one million. Then by that new total the next second. And again. And again. A geometric explosion of physical potential that would make a supernova feel like a struck match. His endurance, his speed, his senses, his intellect—all embarked on the same endless, exponential journey. In the third second, he could hear the gravitational whispers of distant galaxies. In the fifth, he understood the birth-cry of the omniverse.

Simultaneously, the superpowers awoke. In second one: *Absolute Aura Suppression*. A power to contain the apocalypse he was becoming. It scaled with the rest. In second two: *Conceptual Erasure*. The ability to unmake an idea from all of existence. It scaled. In second three: *Retroactive Causality Manipulation*. It scaled. A new power, each capable of ending a civilization in its infancy, every second. *Spatial Domination*. *Temporal Stillness*. *Biological Ascension*. *Quantum Annihilation*. On and on, an infinite arsenal born every moment, each weapon itself growing infinitely more powerful every second.

And the defense. A paradox woven into his being. He had no weakness. It was an absolute truth. But the arrogance of sentient minds was a universal constant. The moment a being, anywhere, in any universe, even *entertained the notion* that Godric might have a flaw, the universe itself punished their insolence. The rate of his stat multiplication would permanently increase to a new, incomprehensible tier. Immunity to the imagined exploit was forged instantly. Mastery over the proposed attack was granted to Godric before the thought was fully formed in the enemy’s mind. The defense was passive, automatic, and utterly, cosmically cruel.

For ten thousand years, Godric slept and did not sleep. He was a still, silent nucleus in an infinite, silently screaming storm of power. He dreamed of his family’s ashes.

***

On the third day, the Valley of Kings was a bowl of steel and fury. Blake stood with Anna, Josh, and Rhea on the hill Godric had designated, shielded by a faint, shimmering bubble the king had idly conjured. Below, the might of Thronegate was arrayed. Robert, the young king, sat on a hovering throne, face pale but determined. He had believed the challenge a trick, a psychic attack. But here was the man, standing alone in the valley’s center.

The army was a spectacle of nightmare grandeur. Billions of super-soldiers in photon-shift armor stood in perfect, silent ranks, each capable of leveling a city. Behind them floated legions of war-engines: robots that could disassemble matter at a quantum level, siege golems that walked on laws of physics they rewrote with every step. The sky was darkened by dragons the size of mountain ranges, their scales glittering with captive nebulae. Among the legions flitted the Time-Slip Ninjas, so fast they existed in micro-moments, able to deliver a killing blow yesterday.

A grand marshal’s voice boomed across the valley. “Die, relic!”

The attack was not a wave; it was the total, instantaneous application of a galaxy’s military might. Beams of pure entropy, spells that unraveled atomic bonds, temporal loops designed to trap Godric in an infinite death, dragon-fire that could cook planets, ninja strikes delivered a picosecond before they were launched—all converged on the single, unmoving point where Godric stood.

He did not move a muscle.

The entropy beams vanished a foot from his body, not blocked, but *un-conceived*. The unraveling spells found nothing to unravel, for the concept of ‘atomic bond’ ceased to apply to him. The temporal loops snapped, their paradoxes unable to bear the weight of his static existence. The dragon-fire winked out of reality, a candle snuffed by a thought he hadn’t even bothered to think.

Then, Godric *looked* at them.

It was not a physical act. It was the application of a minuscule fraction of a sliver of his power. He used *Spatial Domination*, a power gained in his first minute of stasis, now amplified beyond mortal comprehension for ten thousand years.

The billion super-soldiers simply *folded*. Not their bodies, but the space they occupied. Each man and his armor were compressed into a perfect, two-dimensional square, a screaming portrait the size of a postage stamp, which then fluttered harmlessly to the ground. A billion pops, like a universe of bubble wrap, filled the valley.

The war-engines ceased to be. *Conceptual Erasure* removed the idea of ‘machine’ and ‘artificial intelligence’ from their local reality. They became inert, meaningless sculptures of metal that then dissolved into rust-flakes.

The dragons in the sky experienced *Biological Ascension*. Their cells, overwhelmed by a command to evolve into perfection, did so instantly. They became beautiful, crystalline statues of pure, sentient light, then dissipated into harmless photons, their minds blissfully erased.

The Time-Slip Ninjas presented a unique problem. They were attacking from the past, present, and future. Godric used *Temporal Stillness*. He did not stop time. He made the *concept* of time’s flow apologize for its impudence and stand still for everything except his perception. The ninjas were frozen across the timeline, trapped in every moment they had ever occupied, visible as a ghostly, static smear of a billion identical figures, forever stuck in mid-lunge.

The entire battle, the annihilation of a galactic superpower’s military, took less than a second of subjective time. No effort. No movement. Just the passive, ambient radiation of his being, directed by a whim.

Silence, thick and choking, fell over the valley. The young King Robert vomited over the side of his throne. The remaining auxiliary forces dropped their weapons, the sound like metallic rain.

Blake clutched his family. Anna was sobbing into his shoulder. Josh stared, eyes wide with a horror that would never fully leave him. Rhea hid her face. Blake looked at Godric, who now turned his head and gave him a small, almost apologetic smile.

Two days later, King Robert was found in his chambers. He had used a ceremonial dagger. The note said simply, “I heard their screams in the paper.”

Godric crowned himself king of Thronegate that same hour. His first decree was the Awakening.

It applied to every man of Thronegate blood over the age of twenty. From that day forward, for each day they lived past twenty, their strength, endurance, intelligence, and wisdom increased tenfold. It was retroactive. A fifty-year-old man had lived thirty years past twenty—approximately 10,950 days. His power was multiplied by ten, 10,950 times. The numbers were nonsensical, galactic. But Godric, with a thought, gave them all perfect, instinctive control. They could suppress it, live normally, or access a fraction of this colossal might. They remained mortal, dying around eighty. But for their allotted time, they were demigods.

Blake received a different gift. He felt the surge, the incredible, dizzying expansion of self. But his multiplier was not ten. It was one thousand. Every day. He was thirty-eight. He did not understand why, only that he was now strong enough to crack a planet with a whisper, smart enough to comprehend the blueprints of reality, and wise enough to be terrified of it. He remained, steadfastly, a merchant. A father. A husband.

The men of Thronegate, once arrogant, now worshipped Godric with a fervor born of awe and gratitude. He was their dark father. He enacted absolute, draconian laws for the protection of women and children; a single assault was punished with a fate so creatively horrific it made the old hells seem quaint. Society, paradoxically, became peaceful, prosperous, and fiercely loyal. The shadow of his power was their sun.

***

The expansion began. A universe would receive an offer: surrender and be assimilated into the Thronegate dominion, retaining your culture but acknowledging Godric’s sovereignty. Refusal meant subjugation.

The first to refuse was the Chronarchy of the Eternal Wheel, a universe where beings had mastered time completely. They had learned of Thronegate five years prior through temporal scouts. For five years, they prepared. They built fortresses outside of time, armies that could age an enemy to dust with a glance, paradox weapons designed to erase a target from their own ancestry. They were ready.

Godric was mildly inconvenienced that day. A report from a frontier world was late. In a moment of trivial annoyance, he glanced in the direction of the Chronarchy. The glance carried a fraction of a sliver of a shadow of his power—*Quantum Annihilation*, married with *Conceptual Erasure*.

The Chronarchy, with its five years of preparation, its timeless citadels, and its immortal legions, simply ceased to have ever existed. Not destroyed. *Un-written*. The conquest took less than a Planck instant. Godric did not move from his throne. He returned to reading the late report.

The second universe was the Mycelium Mind, a vast, interconnected fungal intelligence spanning a billion planets. They refused, confident in their collective consciousness and psychic might. Thronegate deployed a squad of five men. All were in their late forties, kind-faced fathers and craftsmen. They apologized to the central fungal node before they began. One of them, a potter named Elian, sighed and *pushed* with his mind, accessing perhaps 0.0001% of his Awakened strength. The psychic push translated as a wave of pure force that sterilized every spore-bearing planet simultaneously, severing the neural network. The Mycelium’s collective scream lasted three seconds. The men were home for dinner, quiet and somber, but duty-bound.

The third was the Pantheon Dimension, a universe ruled by literal, worship-fed gods. They were arrogant, eternal. They saw the offer as a joke. They gathered their divine hosts, weapons that could shatter concepts, shields of pure faith. They would exploit the weakness of this upstart mortal king—surely he was vulnerable to divine will, to the power of belief undone?

The moment the chief god, Arion, conceived the thought that Godric might be weak to faith-negation, the defense paradox triggered. Godric’s stat multiplication rate increased permanently. He became instantly, absolutely immune to any attack based on faith, belief, or divine will. He gained perfect, absolute mastery over the concept of ‘divine will,’ capable of creating or destroying gods with a whim.

Godric, amused, let them proceed. Arion spent a week crafting his grand attack, a spear of ‘Unbelief’ that could erase any deity. He launched it with a universe-shaking roar. The spear struck Godric’s chest and shattered like glass. Godric then looked at Arion and *used the exact same attack*, but with the magnitude of his own, infinite power behind it. The concept of ‘Arion’ was un-believed into oblivion. The other gods, in their despair, realized the truth: their doom was sealed the second their first, arrogant thought had taken form. They had been feeding their own destroyer for days. They were erased in a silent, cosmic sigh.

***

Centuries flowed. Thronegate’s dominion stretched across the omniverse. Blake, aged but vital, his mutton chop now white, stood beside Godric’s throne not as a merchant, but as a steady, grounding presence. He never learned he was Thorin’s last descendant, but he felt a strange, deep kinship with the king, a comfort the omnipotent being found nowhere else. Under Blake’s quiet, loyal influence, the ice around Godric’s heart slowly thawed. The cruelty remained for enemies—absolute, terrifying, final. But for his people, he became what he once was: a fatherly king. He walked the streets of the capital, now a metropolis spanning dimensions, laughing with children, advising craftsmen, a warm, solid presence. The horror of his power was the foundation of their peace, but the face of their rule was one of kindness.

Blake’s son, Josh, now a man with children of his own, showed a peculiar aptitude for stabilizing the complex, reality-bending machinery of the palace. He had a knack for patterns, for soothing erratic energy flows. Blake watched him, a faint, proud smile on his face, a strange sense of déjà vu in his heart.

The final scene was one of terrifying serenity. From a balcony that overlooked a cityscape woven from the stolen glory of a million universes, Godric gazed at the infinite tapestry of his domain. The stars here were arranged in pleasing patterns, their light gentle. The air smelled of jasmine, a scent he had reintroduced.

Blake stood beside him, a trusted friend, a loyal right hand. Below, citizens lived without fear. Women walked safely at any hour. Men of incredible power debated philosophy in cafes, their world-ending strength tucked away like a forgotten umbrella. It was a paradise built upon a mountain of ashes and sealed with absolute, unassailable terror.

Godric placed a hand on Blake’s shoulder. The touch was human, warm. “It is a good kingdom, Blake,” he said, his voice quiet, almost wistful.

“It is, my king,” Blake replied, looking not at the grandeur, but at the faces of the people in the gardens below, seeing their peace.

And above it all, in a silence deeper than the void between universes, Godric’s power continued to multiply, second by endless second, a watchful, infinite engine ensuring that this peace, this hard-won warmth, would never be threatened again. The horror was not gone; it was the bedrock. And on that foundation, they had built something that, against all cosmic odds, resembled home.

The Hall of Accord was a place of quiet majesty, designed not to intimidate but to soothe. Its walls were living wood from the Forest of Whispers, and the light fell in soft dapples, as if filtered through a benevolent canopy. I stood, as I often did, slightly behind and to the left of the throne, which here was a simple chair of aged oak. Godric preferred it that way for these meetings. He believed in setting a tone.

The delegate from the recently surrendered Harmony of Spheres was named Elara. Her people were aesthetes, masters of resonant energy and sonic architecture. They had surrendered willingly, even gratefully, after we presented the terms. Their universe had been on the brink of a civil war over philosophical dissonance; our offer of protection and integration was a lifeline. For three months, the assimilation had been seamless, a textbook example of how this was supposed to work.

Elara was their foremost diplomat, a being of crystalline form and shifting harmonic hues. Her voice was a melody. “King Godric,” she chimed, bowing. “The integration protocols proceed beyond our hopes. Your engineers have helped stabilize our Core Resonance without altering its essential song. We are grateful.”

Godric smiled. It was the warm, fatherly smile he reserved for peaceful occasions. “The song of your people is beautiful, Delegate Elara. We are honored to add its melody to our chorus. Your architects will find our city planners most receptive to your designs.”

The meeting continued, discussing trade routes in metaphysical materials and cultural exchange programs. I mostly listened, occasionally making a note on a data-slate. My role was nebulous—advisor, friend, sometimes a touchstone of normalcy for Godric. I was learning, slowly and awkwardly, the contours of my new existence. The titanic power within me, that thousandfold daily multiplication, was a ghost I kept locked in a deep, silent room in my mind.

Then Elara’s tone shifted. A slight dissonance entered her harmonics, a prideful overtone. “There is one minor point of administrative clarification, Your Majesty. The resource allocation for the new cultural embassies. The forms list a required secondary approval from… a ‘Lord Blake.’” She paused, her crystalline form flickering with a faint, dismissive blue. “Our bureaucratic liaisons were unsure of this title’s provenance. We of the Harmony are accustomed to dealing with chains of command that are… clearly defined. Perhaps this is an archaic holdover? We would humbly suggest streamlining the process directly through your Imperial Ministry.”

The air in the Hall of Accord did not grow cold. The light did not dim. But something changed. It was a change in pressure, not of the atmosphere, but of reality itself. A silence deeper than silence descended, the kind of silence that exists between heartbeats of a dying star.

Godric’s warm smile remained fixed on his face. But his eyes… his eyes shifted from Elara to me, and for a nanosecond, I saw the abyss. Not anger. Something infinitely more terrifying: a perfect, absolute calculation of infinite consequences.

He looked back at Elara. His voice was still polite, still calm. “There is no mistake, Delegate. Blake is my right hand. His approval is my approval. It is not a formality. It is the final word.”

Elara, perhaps emboldened by Godric’s previous gentleness, made her fatal error. A tinkling, melodic laugh escaped her. “With all due respect, Great King, we have studied your glorious hierarchy. The Awakened legions, the sector governors, the cosmic engineers. We see the majesty of the structure. Forgive my bluntness, but we cannot locate *his*… category.” She gestured a crystalline limb toward me. “He seems to be a… personal attachment. A mortal comfort. Surely, for the efficiency of our great merged civilizations, such sentimental bottlenecks can be… optimized?”

The word “bottleneck” hung in the air.

Godric leaned forward, just slightly. The movement was casual. But in that movement, Elara’s entire universe flinched.

“Come here, Delegate Elara,” he said, his voice soft.

She glided forward, her confident harmonics now slightly uncertain.

“Look at Blake,” Godric instructed.

She turned her faceted attention to me. I felt a flush of hot shame, then a cold anger. I wasn’t a bottleneck.

“What do you see?” Godric asked, like a teacher prompting a child.

“I… see a human male of advanced years. I sense… significant latent power, crudely contained. I see a loyal servant,” she said, trying to recover.

“You see a mystery,” Godric corrected, his voice dropping to a whisper that scraped against the bones of creation. “And your first instinct, when faced with a mystery in my court, was not to inquire with humility, but to suggest its *erasure* for your convenience. You called my best friend a ‘sentimental bottleneck.’” He said the phrase slowly, tasting its bitterness.

He stood up. He did not grow in size. Yet, the Hall of Accord, the planet, the galaxy, seemed to shrink to a pinprick behind him. He was all there was.

“Let me clarify your position,” Godric said, and his tone was no longer paternal. It was the voice of tectonic plates grinding, of entropy’s final sigh. “Your Harmony of Spheres exists because I allow it. Your song continues because I find it mildly pleasing. Your people live and breathe and create because I, in my boundless mercy, have decided not to unmake every single note of your existence back to the first silent vibration of your primordial universe. Your surrender was not a treaty between powers. It was a drowning being offered a raft. And you have just spit on the hand that pulled you aboard.”

Elara’s form began to vibrate, a low, terrified hum. “Majesty, I meant no insult! A mere suggestion for efficiency—”

“Silence.” The command did not come from Godric’s mouth. It was implanted directly into the core of her being. Her harmonic voice was cut off mid-chime.

Godric took a step closer. “You questioned Blake’s place. You implied he was less. So let me show you his true place. Not through power. You would not comprehend the numbers. Let me show you through consequence.”

He raised a single finger. He did not point it at Elara. He pointed it at the space beside her.

“This,” Godric said, “is the thread of your existence, Elara of the Harmony. The sum total of your life, your memories, your loves, your achievements.” A single, shimmering strand of light appeared, beautiful and complex, filled with swirling musical notations.

“And this,” he continued, his finger moving a micrometer to the left, “is the thread of Blake’s significance to me.” Another strand appeared. It was not brighter. It was not a strand. It was a cable, a column, a foundational pillar of pure, blinding white that stretched into infinity in both directions, so vast it made the concept of ‘light’ seem like a childish toy. Elara’s life-thread looked like a frail gossamer web beside a galactic supercluster.

“Your insult,” Godric whispered, “was an attempt to pluck at this pillar with your damp, insignificant fingers. Do you see the scale of your arrogance?”

Elara’s crystalline form was cracking, fine fissures spreading from within, not from pressure, but from the sheer ontological weight of the comparison. She was understanding, on a level deeper than thought, her own absolute nothingness.

“Now,” Godric said, his voice returning to a conversational tone that was now the most horrifying sound in all of creation. “Here is what will happen. You will return to your Harmony. You will tell your people of this conversation. You will describe, in exact harmonic detail, the terror you feel now. You will make them understand that Blake’s name is to be spoken with the same reverence as mine, for an insult to him is an insult to the very foundation of my mercy. You will personally oversee the cultural embassies, and you will ensure that every form, every request, every communication is routed through his office with gratitude and speed.”

He leaned in, his eyes holding hers. “And every single day, for the rest of your very, very long life, you will wake up with the memory of this moment. You will feel this terror fresh, as if for the first time. It will be the bass note underlying every song you sing, the silence behind every word you speak. This is not a punishment. This is an education. You sought efficiency? This is the efficient transfer of knowledge: the absolute, non-negotiable hierarchy of this kingdom. My kindness is limitless, but its borders are guarded by infinite fire. You have brushed against the fence.”

He lowered his hand. The visions vanished. The oppressive weight lifted, leaving only the memory of it carved into Elara’s soul.

She collapsed, not into a heap, but into a perfect, silent sphere of dim crystal, all her harmonics stilled, a monument to shock.

“Guards,” Godric said, his voice now warm again, as if ordering tea. “Please help Delegate Elara to her transport. Ensure she has a comfortable journey home.”

The guards, two Awakened men in their fifties with faces like kind uncles, gently lifted the silent crystal sphere and carried it away.

Godric turned to me. The abyss was gone. In its place was a look of slight embarrassment, and deep, unwavering loyalty. “I apologize, my friend. You should not have had to witness that. But some lessons must be taught once, and taught so thoroughly they never need repeating.”

I let out a breath I didn’t know I was holding. My hands were trembling. I had seen him annihilate armies. This was different. This was personal, surgical, and it instilled a deeper dread than any explosion of power. He had not threatened her people. He had not harmed a single cell of her being. He had simply made her *understand*, on a fundamental, unshakable level, her place, and by extension, mine.

“She… she just didn’t know,” I finally managed, my throat dry.

“And now she does,” he said simply, sitting back in his oak chair. He looked at the dappled light, a faint, sad smile on his lips. “Sentimental bottleneck,” he murmured, and the smile turned genuine. “A thousand universes quake at my name, and the only thing that keeps the storm at bay is the friendship of an honest merchant. They will never understand. But they will learn to respect it.”

He placed a hand on my shoulder, the weight of it human, reassuring. “Come, Blake. Let’s walk in the garden. The jasmine is blooming. I find I need the scent after such… administrative unpleasantness.”

And just like that, the King of the Omniverse, the being of infinite terror, was simply my friend again, seeking solace in a simple flower. The lesson was etched into the cosmos. The delegate would carry that existential dread home, and it would ripple through her entire civilization, ensuring a compliance born not of fear of destruction, but of a perfect, awful understanding. My approval would never be questioned again.
