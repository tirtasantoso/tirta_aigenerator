"Impressive," he said. "Though I would have added a secondary stabilization matrix. Stars have a tendency to get cranky when you try to cage them."

I blinked. "You know about containment matrices?"

"I've watched a lot of wizards work." He bit into his own apple with a crunch. "Your great-great-grandfather Leon was particularly gifted with stellar magic. Ask him about it sometime."

"Uncle Godric, Leon died six thousand years ago."

"Did he? Time gets away from me." His smile was easy, unbothered. "My point stands. You're doing well, Gorm. Your father would be proud."

I felt my chest expand. My father had been the court wizard before me, and his father before him, stretching back to Josh, stretching back to Blake himself, the legendary right hand of the king. And now I was carrying that torch, and the king—the immortal, omniscient apex of all creation—was telling me I was doing well. I wanted to bottle the feeling and keep it forever.

"I'm going to protect you," I said, the words spilling out before I could stop them. "That's why I'm working so hard. The court wizard's job is to protect the king from magical threats, and I'm going to be the best one yet. I swear it."

Godric's expression flickered—something too fast for me to catch, a shadow across the sun. Then it was gone, and he was smiling again, and he reached out to ruffle my hair the way he'd done since I was small enough to ride on his shoulders.

"I know you will," he said. "Now eat your pastry before it gets cold."

---

The thing you have to understand is that I had built my entire identity on a foundation of sand. I knew that Godric was powerful. Everyone knew that. The stories of his conquests were embedded in Thronegate's culture like fossils in rock—the annihilation of Robert's army, the subjugation of the omniverse, the countless resistances shattered without him ever lifting a finger. But magic? Magic was different. Magic was the domain of wizards. And Godric, in all the years I'd known him, had never once cast a spell. He didn't carry a focus. He didn't speak incantations. He didn't maintain wards or brew potions or do any of the thousand tiny magical things that wizards do by reflex. When magical problems arose—curses, dimensional breaches, hostile enchantments—he called for me, or for my father before me. And we solved them.

I had concluded, with the unshakeable logic of a twenty-year-old who knew just enough to be dangerous, that Godric kept court wizards because he needed them. His power was physical, strategic, direct. But the arcane arts were a gap in his otherwise perfect arsenal, and my family existed to fill that gap. We were the shield he couldn't raise himself. We were the sword he couldn't wield. We were necessary.

I was so proud of being necessary.

---

The incident that shattered my belief happened three weeks later, in the deep of night, when the outer wards of the palace screamed.

I was awake and moving before my conscious mind caught up, my feet finding the cold stone of the corridor, my hands already shaping a detection spell. The wards were ancient things, layered by forty thousand years of court wizards, each generation adding their own protections to the palace's magical defenses. They didn't scream for nothing. Something had breached the outer perimeter—something vast and magical and utterly hostile.

I reached the throne room in under a minute, my heart hammering, my mana reserves already deploying into a combat configuration. My father had drilled me on emergency response since I was old enough to hold a wand: assess the threat, secure the king, neutralize the attacker. The order was inviolable. The king came first.

Godric was already there. He sat in his usual chair—not the formal throne, which he never used, but the worn leather armchair in the corner by the window, the one with the blanket draped over the back. He was reading a book. He looked up when I burst through the door, and his expression was mild, curious, as if I'd interrupted a particularly engaging chapter.

"Gorm. You're up late."

"Your Majesty, the wards—there's been a breach—" I was already casting, layering a protective dome over him, a sphere of hardened mana that would shrug off a planet-killing strike. "Stay behind me. I'll handle this."

He closed his book. "Gorm."

"Please, Uncle Godric, I need to focus—"

"Gorm." His voice was still warm, but there was something in it that cut through my panic and pinned me in place. "Look at the breach."

I looked. My detection spell was still active, feeding me information about the intruder. And the information was... wrong. Not wrong in the sense of inaccurate; wrong in the sense of impossible. The entity that had breached the wards was a lich-king from the outer necropolises of the Veyros Expanse, a being so ancient that its name had been forgotten before my ancestors were born. It had spent the last hundred thousand years consuming the magical energies of dying universes, growing in power until it was less a creature and more a mobile apocalypse. Its mana signature was so vast that my detection spell couldn't find the edges of it. It was larger than the palace. Larger than the city. Larger than the continent. Its arrival was supposed to be the end of everything.

And it was scared.

The detection spell showed me the lich-king's emotional state as clearly as its magical profile, and the emotional state was terror—pure, absolute, mind-shattering terror. It wasn't attacking. It was fleeing. It had breached Thronegate's outer wards not as an invader but as a refugee, a thing running from something worse, and it had run straight into Godric's presence by accident, and now it was screaming. I couldn't hear the scream with my ears, but my wizard's senses picked it up, a psychic wail that went on and on, the sound of a being that had spent a hundred thousand years as the most feared entity in its universe and had just realized it was a mayfly in the presence of a supernova.

I turned back to Godric. He hadn't moved. He was still sitting in his armchair, the book resting on his knee, his posture relaxed. The protective dome I'd cast around him was still active, a shimmering hemisphere of hardened mana. He reached up and poked it with one finger. It dissolved. Not broke—dissolved, the way sugar dissolves in hot water, its structure simply ceasing to exist at his touch.

"Gorm," he said gently, "I need to show you something."

He stood. The blanket slipped off the back of the chair and he caught it reflexively, folded it, set it aside. Then he turned toward the direction of the breach, and he let go of something—not power, not strength, but restraint. He let a sliver of his magical capacity slip past his absolute concealment. An amount so small that the word "infinitesimal" was an insulting overstatement. A fraction so close to zero percent that the decimal point had more zeros than there were atoms in the omniverse.

I saw it.

I saw it, and my mind did not break, but only because he didn't let it. He held me together with the same absent tenderness he'd used to fold the blanket, a gentle pressure at the edges of my consciousness that kept my thoughts from scattering into the void. What I perceived was not the full scope of his magic—I couldn't have perceived that, any more than a single cell can perceive the full scope of a galaxy—but it was enough. It was more than enough.

Godric's magical capacity was not a number. It was not a measurement. It was a condition of being, an ocean that contained every ocean that had ever existed, and each drop of that ocean was itself an ocean, and each drop of those oceans was an ocean, recursing into a depth that had no bottom. The total magical output of every court wizard who had ever lived—forty thousand years of thousandfold daily amplification, the accumulated arcane might of Blake's entire bloodline—was a single, solitary, subatomic speck against that ocean. The combined magic of the entire omniverse, every spell ever cast, every mana pool ever tapped, every artifact ever forged, every god ever worshipped, every lich-king and archmage and cosmic sorcerer who had ever drawn breath—it was nothing. It was a rounding error. It was the heat from a single match held against the surface of every sun in every universe that had ever burned.

And his magic was still amplifying. Every Planck second—a unit of time so small that the universe's entire lifespan was a lazy yawn by comparison—his mana, his spellcraft, his magical offense, his magical defense, his conceptual magic, his anti-magic, and every other arcane dimension that existed or could exist multiplied by a rate beyond infinity. The Godric of this exact second could erase the Godric of a Planck second ago with a thought that wouldn't even register as effort. And the Godric of the next Planck second would be equally beyond him. Had been amplifying since his stasis ended. Would continue amplifying forever. And the fraction he'd just shown me—the practically-zero-percent he'd revealed—was enough to unmake the entire omniverse. Not just once. Infinite times over. Erase it, restore it, erase it again, in zero seconds, without effort, without movement, without even the satisfaction of having tried.

The lich-king was still screaming. I understood now why it was screaming. It had sensed this. It had sensed the barest, faintest echo of what I was seeing now, and it had run, and it was still running, but there was nowhere to run to because the thing it was running from was everywhere.

Godric's concealment slid back into place. The sliver vanished. He was once again a handsome, forty-five-year-old man with flour on his tunic and a book in his hand. He walked over to me and put his hand on my shoulder.

"The lich-king," he said. "Do you want to watch me handle it, or would you prefer to do it yourself? It would be good practice."

I opened my mouth. No sound came out. My legs were shaking and my eyes were wet and my magical senses had retreated so far into my core that I couldn't feel them at all. I was a twenty-year-old boy standing in the ruins of his own understanding, and the man who had just shattered that understanding was asking me if I wanted practice.

"Your Majesty," I whispered. "Your Majesty, I thought... I thought you needed me."

His face softened. The hand on my shoulder squeezed once, gentle. "I do need you, Gorm. Not for magic. Never for magic. But I need you. The way I needed Blake. The way I needed Tim and Leon and Grim and every court wizard who's ever stood at my shoulder. I need someone to talk to. I need someone who sees me as a person and not a predator. I need family." He paused. "You're family, Gorm. You always have been. Before I even knew what I was—before the stasis, before the amplification—I was a king with a best friend who happened to be a wizard. Thorin. He was the first person I loved outside my blood, and he died saving me. Your bloodline is all I have left of him. I could delete the concept of magic from the omniverse with less effort than it takes to blink, but I don't, because if I did, you wouldn't be a wizard anymore. And I like that you're a wizard. I like watching you invent containment lattices at dawn. I like watching you grow."

The tears were running down my face. I didn't wipe them. "I wanted to protect you."

"You do protect me," he said. "Not from enemies. From loneliness. From boredom. From the weight of being what I am. You and your family—you're the reason I remember how to be warm. That's worth more than any shield."

I stood there in the cold corridor of the palace, the lich-king still screaming somewhere beyond the wards, and I understood that everything I had believed about myself was wrong. I was not necessary in the way I'd thought. I was not a shield. I was not a sword. I was a companion, a friend, a nephew—honored and beloved and entirely superfluous to the defense of the realm. My magic, which I had poured so much pride into, was a candle held against the sun. And the sun loved the candle anyway. Not because it needed the light. Because it liked the company.

---

I asked to handle the lich-king myself.

Godric nodded, unsurprised—he'd probably known I would ask before I did—and settled back into his armchair with his book. I walked out of the throne room, through the outer corridors, past the shattered wards, and into the night air where the lich-king crouched like a cornered animal. Up close, it was a horror. A skeleton wrapped in shadows, its eye sockets burning with the harvested mana of a hundred dead universes, its bony fingers crackling with necrotic lightning that could snuff out a star with a touch. It was one of the most powerful magical entities I had ever encountered. It was, objectively, a cataclysm given form.

And I wasn't afraid of it. Not because I was brave. Because I had just seen what real magic looked like, and this thing—this ancient, universe-eating nightmare—was a candle too. A slightly larger candle than mine, maybe. But a candle.

It lunged. I cast. The fight lasted twelve minutes, and I won—not easily, but completely, my prismatic containment lattice finally finding a target worthy of its theoretical potential. When it was over, the lich-king was trapped in a cage of blue-gold light, its necrotic mana slowly being purified by the lattice's secondary arrays, and I was standing over it with my chest heaving and my hands smoking. I had done it. I had protected the king.

I laughed. The sound was high and wild and half-hysterical. I had protected no one. The king had let me play. And I was so grateful for the chance to play that I could barely breathe.

Back in the throne room, Godric was still reading. He looked up when I entered, and his smile was proud. "Twelve minutes. Not bad. Your containment held perfectly through the third phase—I thought it might waver when the lich tried to phase-shift, but you compensated well."

"You were watching."

"I'm always watching." He closed his book. "Good work, Gorm. You've earned your rest."

I didn't rest. I went to the wizard's tower and sat in the dark and thought about what I'd learned. The horror of it was not that Godric was powerful. I'd always known he was powerful. The horror was the scale. The impossibility of the scale. The fact that every Planck second, the gap between him and everything else grew by an amount that mathematics couldn't describe, and that even if the entire omniverse spent the rest of eternity training and studying and amplifying and sacrificing, they would never close a fraction of a fraction of a fraction of that gap. They couldn't even perceive it.

And yet he was kind. He was genuinely, truly kind. He had let me believe I was protecting him because he didn't want to hurt my feelings. He had folded the blanket and brought me pastries and ruffled my hair and called me family. The most terrifying being in existence, the apex predator of all creation, and he was my uncle. He loved me. And I loved him. And that love didn't cancel the terror. It just lived alongside it.

Somewhere in the omniverse, I knew, someone was already planning their next resistance. Already convincing themselves they'd found a weakness, a loophole, a strategy. The Weakness Paradox was triggering as they thought, feeding Godric's amplification, making him immune to their attack before they'd even finished conceptualizing it. Their fate was sealed. It had been sealed the moment they'd had the thought. And when they finally acted, when they finally launched their rebellion and watched it fail, Godric would do to them what he'd done to every opposer before them. He would be cruel. He would be merciless. He would remind the omniverse why opposing him was the most agonizing and fatal mistake a being could make.

But here, in the palace, with me, he would still be Uncle Godric. He would still ask about my spells and bring me breakfast and tell me stories about my ancestors. He would still be warm.

I pulled a blanket over my shoulders—the same blanket he'd folded earlier, I realized, brought here by some silent servant or passive aura—and I let the fire in the tower's hearth burn low. My magic hummed in my blood, a thousandfold amplified, the inheritance of forty thousand years. It was nothing compared to his. It was laughable.

It was mine. And he was proud of me for having it. And that was enough. It had to be.
