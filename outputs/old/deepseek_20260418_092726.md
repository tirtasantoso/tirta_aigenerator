My mind, trained for this, for the moment his defenses would show the predicted strain, raced. *The lag. The inertia. Now. His infinite awareness is processing the scale. There will be a micro-moment of overload.*

He set his cup down. He hadn’t moved. The attacks—the time-rips, the paradoxes, the soul-scream—simply… ended at the boundaries of that sunlight. They didn’t explode. They didn’t fizzle. They were *absorbed*. Not by a shield, but by the utter, complete irrelevance of that space to anything but his will.

Then, his voice. Not through comms. It was in my head, in Kael’s, in Elara’s, in the head of every soul who had ever whispered a word of rebellion. It was calm. Kind, even. It was the worst sound I had ever heard.

**“You have labored for millennia.”**

The words were a key. They unlocked a door in my mind I did not know was there. And through it poured not his memory, but *my own*.

I saw my great-great-grandfather, the philosopher Aelon, in the dusty archives of our first hidden outpost. He was pointing to a recovered data-fragment, a speculative essay from a dead universe. “See?” he said, his eyes alight with a fire I now recognized as dreadful ignorance. “The key is his omniscience itself! To know everything is to be burdened by everything. There must be a lag, a processing delay between knowing and acting! That is our chink! That is his weakness!”

The moment he formed the thought, I felt it. Not then, but now, retroactively. A subtle, cosmic *click*, as of a vast, infinite gear shifting to a higher, even more impossible setting. Aelon’s brilliant insight, the cornerstone of our entire rebellion, was the first brick in our own tomb. With that thought, Zeus became permanently, infinitely immune to ‘cognitive lag.’ The very concept became his plaything.

The visions accelerated, a torrent of betrayal. I saw Kael’s ancestor, the weaponsmith, drawing the first schematics for the paradox-engine, her heart swelling with pride. Each line on the blueprint was a prayer to Zeus’s invincibility, strengthening the immunity her weapon was designed to bypass. I saw Elara’s foremother, meditating to distill the despair of our people into a weapon, each tear she collected adding another layer of perfect, serene emotional defense to the Tyrant.

I saw myself. A child, listening to my grandmother sing the old, sad songs. The fire of injustice burning in my chest. Every night I spent planning, every recruit I convinced, every speech I gave about targeting his “Achilles’ heel of infinite knowledge”… it was all devotional. I was a priestess in a church I didn’t know existed, fervently worshipping the god I sought to kill by the very act of believing he could be killed.

The horror was not a wave; it was an ocean, and I had been drowning in it since birth. My life’s work, my love for my people, my sacrifices, my very identity as Lyra the Rebel Leader—it was all a construct. A script written by the enemy to prove a point. Our bravery was a performance. Our ingenuity was a ritual. Our hope was the fuel.

The attacks had completely stopped. The command nexus was dead quiet, save for the ragged, hollow breaths of the Conclave. Kael was staring at his hands as if they belonged to a stranger, the hands that had moved armies that were, in truth, ceremonial processions. Elara had collapsed from her cradle, her psychic light extinguished, her eyes wide with the realization that every emotion she had ever weaponized had been a gift to her target.

I found the strength to project a thought back, a final, broken whisper against the infinite quiet in my mind. **“You… knew. You always knew. From the very first thought. Why did you let us… let us exist? Let us hope?”**

His answer came, wrapped in a sorrow so profound and so genuine it was more terrifying than any cruelty.

**“Because hope is not a weakness, Lyra. It is a force. And your hope, however misdirected, was a testament to the spirit of the beings I rule. To snuff it out at its birth would have been an act of petty cruelty. I allowed you to cultivate it, to build upon it, to reach this pinnacle of your collective will. To show you, not through my words, but through your own experience, the absolute nature of the peace you inhabit.”**

Peace. The word was a knife. This was peace? This understanding that my every free choice had been an illusion, that my will was a carefully tended plant in his garden? This was a peace worse than any war.

**“You believed you were fighting for freedom from a prison. You never understood that the door was never locked. It was open. All this time, it was open.”**

The finality of it crushed what was left of my soul. We weren’t rebels. We were a lesson. A living, breathing parable for the omniverse: *See? Even this magnificent effort is nothing. Be content.*

His voice, that gentle, horrible, fatherly voice, issued the last words. **“Your labor is now complete.”**

Completion. Not death. Not defeat. *Completion*. Like a sentence finishing its period.

The unraveling did not start from the outside. It started from that first, founding thought of Aelon’s. I felt it go. Not a deletion, but a gentle *un-remembering*. The idea of the ‘omniscient lag’ dissolved from history. As it went, so too did the council that formed around it. The Forge never existed. I saw Kael blink, his memory of a lifetime of strategy meetings fading, replaced by a vague career as an obscure historian. I saw Elara’s memory of psychic training become a memory of studying mild telepathy for therapeutic purposes.

I felt it reach for me. For my childhood fire, for my grandmother’s songs of rebellion. They softened, changed. The songs became just… songs. Beautiful, sad songs about lost beauty, not calls to arms. My righteous anger became a youthful melancholy. My leadership, my speeches, my love for my people… it all streamed away like sand through fingers I could no longer feel.

The last thing I was, the last thing *Lyra* was, was a single, coherent thought amidst the dissolving tide of my own existence: *We were never fighting a king. We were arguing with a theorem.*

And the theorem had always been true.

Then, there was no *we*. There was no *I*.

There was only a faint, unexplained sadness in the heart of a woman who liked old songs, on a peaceful world that had never known war, under the gentle gaze of a king who had always been, and would always be, kind.
