Blake adjusted the weight of the mountain-core paperweight on his mahogany desk with a single finger, careful not to apply more than a fraction of a trillionth of his strength. To a normal human of the Old World, the small cube would have weighed forty tons. To Blake, at forty years of age, it felt like a speck of dried foam.

He looked out the window of their spire in New Olympus. The city was a sprawling tapestry of white marble and levitating gardens, powered not by burning fuel, but by the ambient "excess" bled off from the Great Restraint. Far above, the sky wasn't blue; it was a shimmering, iridescent gold—the visible edge of Zeus’s passive shield, protecting the world from the vacuum of space and the sheer, crushing pressure of his own existence.

"Lina, darling, you’ve missed the sunset," Blake said, his voice warm and resonant.

Lina stood by the balcony rail, her silhouette sharp against the amber glow. She was thirty-five, the age of the Great Stagnation, though she didn’t possess the Awakening. She looked exactly as her ancestors had: beautiful, fragile, and entirely mortal. She didn’t turn around. Her knuckles were white where they gripped the railing—a railing Blake had reinforced with graviton-steel so she wouldn’t accidentally snap it if she leaned too hard.

"I didn't miss it," she whispered. "I was just wondering if the sun is actually there, or if He just decided we needed light today."

Blake chuckled, walking over to her. His footsteps were silent, his mass perfectly controlled. He wrapped a thick, muscular arm around her shoulders, feeling the delicate heat of her skin. "Does it matter? It’s a beautiful gift. Everything is a gift."

He felt the slight, almost imperceptible flinch in her shoulder. He knew what she was thinking. He knew the stories her grandmother had whispered in the dark—the forbidden tales of the Matriarchs. But Blake felt only a profound, protective pity. He was twenty years into his Awakening. For every day since his twentieth birthday, his strength, speed, and mental processing had multiplied by ten. The math was beyond astronomical; he could think a million years of philosophy in the time it took Lina to blink. He could catch a photon with his teeth. And yet, he was a guttering candle compared to the Sun that sat upon the throne.

"The history books say today is the anniversary," Lina said, her voice trembling. "One hundred and fifty years since the Silence."

Blake kissed the top of her head. "The Uprising, you mean. When the God-King finally showed us the truth of the world. It’s a day of celebration, Lina. It’s the day the chaos ended."

"It’s the day the world became a cage," she hissed.

Blake didn’t get angry. A man doesn’t get angry at a bird for chirping. He simply squeezed her a little tighter, his thumb tracing the line of her jaw. "Let me tell you how it actually happened. Not the myths your sisters whisper. The truth. It’ll help you sleep."

***

One hundred and fifty years ago, the world was a different place. Zeus had ruled for 8,888 years, a figure of mythic stability. He was known as the Eternal Architect, a man who looked thirty-five and possessed a charisma that could end wars with a smile. People knew he was "different." They suspected he was immortal. They saw him walk through fires and heal from wounds that would kill a mortal in seconds. But they thought he was just a man who had transcended—a peak specimen who could be understood, and eventually, surpassed.

The Great Uprising was the culmination of three centuries of clandestine preparation. Billions of women, led by a council of genius-engineers and tactical savants, had built a civilization beneath the civilization. They viewed Zeus’s kingdom as a patronizing stagnation. They saw his favor toward men—the way he encouraged traditional structures—as a slight against half the human race.

They didn't just bring guns. They brought "The Eradicator."

It was a weapon designed to collapse the molecular bonds of any biological entity within a ten-mile radius, fueled by a captured singularity. They had ships that could phase through matter and legions of soldiers in power-armor that could punch through tectonic plates.

When the first shot was fired, Zeus was sitting in a garden in Old Athens, sipping nectar and reading a book of poetry.

The Eradicator beam hit him with the force of a thousand dying suns. The city block evaporated. The atmosphere for fifty miles ignited, turning the sky into a furnace. The rebel commanders cheered in their bunkers, believing they had just erased the "Tyrant."

As the smoke cleared, Zeus was still sitting there. His chair was gone. His book was ash. But he was suspended in mid-air, legs crossed, a look of mild, disappointed amusement on his handsome face. His salt-and-pepper beard wasn't even singed. His muscular chest, bare from the waist up, gleamed like polished bronze.

"That was quite loud," Zeus said. His voice wasn't projected, yet every person on the planet heard it as if he were whispering directly into their ear. "And rather rude. I was just getting to the good part."

He stood up in the vacuum of the blast zone. This was the moment he chose to stop pretending. For 9,500 years, he had been a God-King in secret. At age thirty-five, his body had hit a metaphysical singularity.

Every second, his power multiplied by a thousand, and then by itself.

For nearly ten millennia, that compounding math had been grinding away in the silence of his marrow. Every second, he gained a new way to end the universe. And every second, the "Restraint"—his first and most vital superpower—scaled with him, weaving a cocoon around his soul so that his mere presence didn't shatter the dimensions of the omniverse.

The rebel leaders, panicked, ordered the "God-Slayer" protocol. A trillion nanobots were released into the air, programmed to enter his pores and rewrite his DNA into lead.

In that instant, a young strategist in the bunker had a stray, subconscious thought: *Maybe he has a weakness in his internal organs. Maybe he isn't invulnerable from the inside.*

The Weakness Paradox triggered instantly.

Zeus felt the insult of the thought. In a microsecond, his amplification rate spiked, doubling the already infinite climb. His body didn't just become immune to internal restructuring; he gained absolute mastery over the concept of "Composition." He looked at the cloud of nanobots and smiled.

With a flick of his wrist, the nanobots didn't just stop; they transformed. They became a swarm of sentient, microscopic predators that recognized the genetic signature of every rebel soldier on Earth. They didn't just kill; they began to dismantle the rebels' history, erasing their names from digital records and physical stone as they consumed their flesh.

"You thought I could be unmade from within?" Zeus asked, his voice dripping with paternal warmth. "How creative. In exchange, I shall deny you the concept of 'Inside.' From this moment on, your bodies will be two-dimensional planes of suffering."

Across the globe, millions of women suddenly collapsed as their three-dimensional depth was stripped away by his will. They became living, screaming shadows on the ground, unable to breathe or move, yet kept alive by his power so they could witness his glory.

The remaining rebel fleet, thousands of miles long, dived from the stratosphere. They unleashed "The Chronos-Anchor," a device designed to freeze time around Zeus, locking him in a perpetual "now" where he could be studied and eventually dissected.

Again, the Paradox responded to the thought of "stalling him."

Zeus didn't just resist the time-stop. He accelerated. He moved so fast that the concept of "Speed" became irrelevant. To the observers, he simply appeared in ten thousand places at once. In one location, he was patting a general on the cheek; in another, he was dismantling a dreadnought with his bare teeth; in a third, he was merely laughing.

He appeared in the main rebel bunker, walking through the three-meter-thick blast doors as if they were made of morning mist. The air around him screamed—not from his movement, but because the atoms were trying to get out of his way to avoid being crushed by his aura.

The High Commander, a woman of iron will named Elena, stared at him. She held a detonator for a "Reality-Bomb"—a device that would erase the entire solar system, taking them all with it.

"You won't let us live," she spat, her thumb trembling over the button. "So we will all die together."

Zeus reached out and gently took her hand. His touch was warm, like a father comforting a child after a nightmare. "Elena, you think death is an exit. You think that because I am a king, I must have a limit to my reach."

He squeezed her hand, and the detonator turned into a small, singing bird.

"Every second," Zeus whispered, and Elena felt the existential dread of his words. "I become more than I was. If you detonated that bomb, I would simply gain the power to reconstitute the universe from the memory of its light before the first atom split. I am not part of your reality, Elena. I am the reason reality is allowed to continue."

To demonstrate, Zeus inhaled.

He didn't just breathe air. He inhaled the kinetic energy of the entire rebel army. Every bullet in flight, every missile, every charging soldier—they all suddenly lost their momentum. The energy was pulled into Zeus's lungs, and when he exhaled, he released a "New Superpower": The Breath of Rebirth.

The fire that had consumed the cities didn't just go out; it turned into crystalline flowers. The dead didn't just rise; they were transformed into statues of translucent diamond, frozen in poses of adoration.

But for those who had led the charge, there was no such mercy.

Zeus looked at the High Commander and decided she should see what he saw. He granted her a one-second glimpse of his "Intelligence" stat.

Elena’s eyes burst. Her brain could not contain the complexity of a single nanosecond of Zeus’s thoughts. She saw the birth and death of trillion-year-old galaxies as if they were bubbles in a glass of water. She felt the weight of his "Wisdom," a crushing realization that every rebellion, every thought of freedom, every prayer for a different world, was just a predictable chemical reaction he had already accounted for and dismissed as "cute."

"The Uprising is over," Zeus announced.

He stepped out of the bunker and leaped. He didn't use a ship. He simply pushed off the ground. The shockwave of his jump flattened the mountain range, but the Restraint instantly repaired the tectonic plates before they could cause an earthquake.

He flew into the heart of the rebel fleet. He didn't use weapons. He simply existed. His "Endurance" was so high that the ships colliding with him shattered like glass against a diamond. He drifted through the vacuum, tearing through the metal hulls with his hands, pulling the soldiers out into the cold dark, and then, with a touch, giving them the ability to breathe in space—only so they could watch him tear their world apart.

He manifested a new power: The Symphony of the Patriarch.

A golden light erupted from his chest, sweeping across the globe. It was a conceptual wave. It didn't harm the women, but it rewrote the fundamental "Awakening" of the world. He decided then that the men would be his deputies. He granted every man on Earth the gift of the daily multiplier—a pale, infinitesimal fraction of his own growth, but enough to make them gods compared to what they had been.

"From this day," Zeus’s voice echoed through the stars, "Men shall be the pillars. They shall grow in strength as a testament to my own. And women... women shall be the cherished, the protected, the silent. You have shown that you cannot handle the burden of the infinite. So, I take it from you."

By the end of the day, the billion-strong army was gone. Not just defeated—erased from the possibility of victory. The survivors were returned to their homes, their memories of the battle turned into a source of bone-deep, shivering terror that would be passed down through their bloodlines.

***

Blake pulled back from Lina, his eyes shining with a holy light. He looked at his own hands, capable of shattering stars, and then at her, so small and beautiful.

"The history is brutal, I know," Blake said softly. "But look at what he gave us. A world of perfect order. No more war. No more hunger. He provides everything. The men maintain the world, and we provide for you. Is that so terrible?"

Lina looked down at the city. The lights were twinkling like fallen stars. In every home, a man with the power of a thousand suns was sitting down to dinner with a woman who couldn't lift a heavy crate. It was a world of absolute, unshakable safety, bought with the price of total, cosmic surrender.

"He's still growing, isn't he?" Lina asked, her voice hollow.

"Every second," Blake whispered in awe. "By the time I finished that sentence, his power multiplied by itself thousands of times. He is the beginning and the end. He is the Patriarchy that breathes life into the vacuum."

Lina looked up at the golden sky, the shield that kept the universe from noticing the monster in its midst. She thought, just for a fleeting second, of a world where she could be strong too.

Deep in the heart of the Golden Palace, Zeus sat on his throne. He felt the ripple. A woman, the descendant of Elena, was thinking of "Strength."

The Paradox triggered.

Zeus felt his "Strength" stat leap by a factor of ten-thousand-quadrillion. He gained a new power: The Mandate of Absolute Fragility. With a thought, he reinforced the concept that women's bones would always be as they were—delicate, breakable, needing a man's hand to guide them. He didn't do it out of hate. He did it because the thought of her being strong was an insult to the perfection he had built.

He smiled, a warm, handsome, mid-30s smile. He was a kind god.

Blake felt the shift in the air—a sudden, overwhelming sense of peace and stability. He felt more powerful than he had a moment ago, his own Awakening surging in resonance with his King.

"See?" Blake said, gesturing to the horizon. "Even the air feels more certain tonight. He’s watching over us, Lina. He loves us."

Blake turned Lina away from the balcony and toward the warmth of their home. He felt a profound sense of pride, a reverence that made his very soul ache with joy. He was a man in the kingdom of the only True Man. He was a protector. He was a part of the Great Design.

Outside, the golden sky pulsed once, like a heartbeat. The omniverse trembled, held together by the sheer, terrifying will of the God-King who refused to let his toys break. Blake closed the door, locking it with a bolt that could withstand a nuclear blast, feeling utterly, perfectly safe in the shadow of the Father.
