The marble of the Great Hall in Olympus was not merely white; it possessed a translucent, ethereal quality that seemed to hum with the collective prosperity of a golden age. King Godric stood at the dais, the weight of his ceremonial robes less burdensome than the immense, overflowing pride in his chest. At forty-five, he was a man carved from the very ideals of his people—tall, with a muscularity that spoke of a lifetime of disciplined training, his salt-and-pepper beard neatly trimmed against a jawline that could have been fashioned from granite. His short hair caught the light of the midday suns, and as he looked upon his daughter, Isabel, his eyes were soft, radiating a paternal warmth that defined his reign. This was a day of union, a day where the bloodlines of the heavens would merge under the watchful gaze of a king who loved his subjects as he loved his own kin. Beside him stood Reina, his queen, her grace a mirror to his strength, and fifteen-year-old Hebe, whose youthful laughter was the music that kept the palace alive.

The ceremony had only just begun when the sky did not darken, but rather shattered. The transition from peace to annihilation was instantaneous, a violence so profound it defied the human capacity to process it. The neighboring kingdom of Thronegate, a realm driven by a sudden, inexplicable thirst for absolute hegemony, had not sent a herald or a declaration; they had sent a storm of iron and sorcery. A volley of obsidian bolts, enchanted to pierce the very fabric of divine protection, rained through the vaulted ceiling. Godric saw the first bolt strike Reina, her body collapsing with a sickening wet thud before she could even gasp. Then came the screams. Isabel, dressed in the pristine silks of a bride, was caught in a crossfire of arcane fire that reduced her beauty to charred remains in a heartbeat. Hebe’s cries were silenced by a collapsing pillar, her small form crushed beneath the weight of the very history Godric had spent his life building.

Godric stood frozen, his hands reaching out toward the void where his family had been. The warmth that had fueled his soul for four decades didn't just extinguish; it turned into a freezing, jagged shard of pure, unadulterated malice. In the chaos, his best friend, Thorin, the court wizard whose lineage had served the kings of Olympus since the first stone was laid, surged forward. Thorin’s face was a mask of blood and desperation. He began to chant, his voice cracking as he channeled a forbidden spell known only to the blood of his ancestors—a teleportation ritual intended to bridge the gaps between dimensions, a final fail-safe for a fallen king. The wizard’s lifeblood sprayed across Godric’s face, and as Thorin slumped to the ground, his final syllable was a choked, incomplete gurgle. The world vanished in a scream of white noise. Godric was cast into a hidden sanctuary, a pocket of existence between the folds of reality, where the laws of physics were merely suggestions. As he hit the floor of this timeless vault, the incomplete magic of his dead friend surged through him, locking his body in a state of eternal preservation. In that exact Planck second, the aging process within his cells reached a hard stop. He was forty-five, and he would be forty-five until the last star burned out. The need for oxygen, for the sustenance of bread and water, for the respite of sleep, was stripped away, replaced by a hollow, immortal hunger for vengeance. But this was merely the threshold. The magical feedback from Thorin’s broken incantation began an exponential cascade of power that defied the limits of the omniverse.

The amplification was a silent, terrifying roar within his very essence. Every Planck second—the shortest unit of time measurable by the gods themselves—every single one of Godric’s attributes began to multiply by one thousand and then by itself. His strength, which could already lift the pillars of Olympus, doubled and redoubled until it surpassed the weight of galaxies. His speed accelerated until the concept of distance became a joke, his intelligence expanded until the secrets of every atom in every dimension were laid bare before him like an open book. Simultaneously, the void in his soul began to manifest as apocalyptic superpowers. Every second he remained in stasis, a new ability was forged in the kiln of his rage. One second, he gained the power to snuff out the heat of a billion suns; the next, he could rewrite the genetic code of an entire civilization to turn their blood into molten lead. Each of these powers, too, was caught in the loop of the Planck second amplification, their potency growing at a rate that would have shattered reality had Godric not manifested a concealment aura so perfect it held his own blossoming divinity in a suffocating embrace.

The most terrifying aspect of his new existence was the Weakness Paradox. It was a fundamental law etched into his being: Godric had no flaws. However, the universe was populated by arrogant entities who could not conceive of an absolute. The moment any sentient being even began to harbor the thought that Godric might have a vulnerability—whether it was an emotional tie, a physical limit, or a metaphysical blind spot—the paradox triggered. The rate of his power’s growth would instantly and permanently accelerate to an even more unfathomable degree as a direct punishment for the insolence of the thought. Before the entity could even formulate a plan to exploit this perceived weakness, Godric would gain permanent, perfect immunity to that specific form of attack. They had built Dyson spheres around every star to power a reality-warping shield they believed was impenetrable. In a fraction of a second, the concept of 'shielding' was erased from the Xylos language and physical reality. Their Dyson spheres collapsed inward, turning their own suns into black holes that swallowed their civilization before their processors could register a fault code. The "unspeakable horrors" of the Reach found that their physical forms were as fragile as wet parchment against men whose strength had been multiplied by ten to the power of seven thousand. They had prepared for five years, developing a frequency that they believed would resonate with Godric's physical body and shatter it. Godric, amused by their persistence, appeared in their dimension personally. He stood in the center of their nebulous hive-mind, a solid, muscular man in a sea of light. The Singularity unleashed its frequency. Godric simply breathed in. He inhaled the entire collective consciousness of the universe, consuming their identities, their history, and their power in a single lungful. He then exhaled a sigh, and the universe settled into a quiet, subservient peace. The total time elapsed was less than three seconds.

In the height of this expansion, an entity known as the Void-Eater—a being that had devoured countless realities before the rise of Olympus—decided to intervene. The Void-Eater was a creature of pure entropy, and it believed it had found the ultimate flaw in Godric: his reliance on the merchant Blake. The entity reasoned that if it could erase Blake from the timeline, Godric’s anchor to his renewed humanity would snap, causing his concealment aura to fail and his own power to consume him.

The moment the Void-Eater formulated this thought, the Weakness Paradox shrieked into action. The rate of Godric’s power growth, already hitting numbers that would make infinity look like a rounding error, surged into a new tier of existence. Godric instantly gained mastery over the concept of 'Erasure' and 'Causality.' He watched, with an expression of dark amusement, as the Void-Eater manifested in the throne room, its form a swirling vortex of nothingness. The entity attempted to strike at Blake, who was sitting nearby discussing the grain taxes with the king. The Void-Eater’s attack didn't just fail; it was denied. The entity found that it could no longer remember how to erase things. Worse, it realized that Godric now possessed a version of the Void-Eater's own essence, but amplified by the Planck second loop.

Godric rose from his throne. The Void-Eater tried to flee, but it found that the very idea of 'away' had been mastered by Godric. There was no 'away' from the King of Thronegate. Godric reached out and took hold of the entity’s throat—or the conceptual equivalent. He didn't kill it immediately. He let the entity feel the crushing weight of his omniscience, showing it the exact moment its thought had doomed it. The Void-Eater, a being that had never known fear, spent its final moments in a state of absolute, gibbering despair before Godric folded it into a small, dense sphere of dark matter and used it as a paperweight for Blake’s merchant ledgers.

As the centuries rolled on, the cold, merciless edge of Godric began to soften, though only for those under his banner. The influence of Blake—the honest, short-tempered merchant who never stopped treating Godric like a man instead of a god—wrought a slow redemption. Godric returned to the fatherly, warm figure he had been in Olympus. He walked the streets of his capital, his salt-and-pepper beard a symbol of stability, greeting his subjects by name. He ensured the women of the omniverse were protected by laws that carried the weight of his own absolute power, and he presided over the weddings of his soldiers with the same smile he had once worn for Isabel. To his enemies, he remained a nightmare of gore and inevitability, but to Thronegate, he was the All-Father.

Blake, now in his twilight years at eighty-eight, had transitioned from a merchant to the kingdom’s Grand Vizier, essentially a court wizard in all but name. He sat in the high tower, his son Josh—a man of forty whose strength could move tectonic plates—standing by his side, learning the ancient lore of their people. Blake looked out over a city that spanned a continent, where the lights of a million homes flickered in the dusk of a conquered galaxy. He felt a strange resonance with the old stones of the palace, a sense that he was exactly where his ancestors had always intended him to be.

The final scene was one of quiet, terrifying majesty. Godric stood on the balcony of the highest spire, his muscular frame silhouetted against the swirling colors of a dozen captured nebulae. Blake walked up behind him, his mutton chops now white with age, leaning slightly on a staff made of a material that shouldn't exist. Godric turned, and for a moment, the cosmic predator was gone, replaced by the friend who had lost everything and found a new world to protect. He placed a hand on Blake’s shoulder—a hand that could crush the omniverse, yet held his friend with the tenderness of a brother. Below them, the men of Thronegate, each a god in his own right, bowed in unison as the suns set. The reign of Olympus had fallen, but the era of Godric was eternal, a dark, beautiful peace bought with the blood of the arrogant and maintained by a king who would never again let the sky shatter.

For ten years, I have lived in a house that is half-temple and half-asylum, looking into the eyes of a man who once navigated the stars but now cannot navigate the distance between his bed and the window without collapsing into a fit of soundless weeping.
