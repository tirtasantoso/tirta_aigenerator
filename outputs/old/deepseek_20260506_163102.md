I began to see the truth everywhere. In the way the other courtiers looked at Godric—awe mixed with a constant, low-grade terror that they’d learned to ignore, the way you learn to ignore the hum of an engine. In the way the palace itself seemed alive, subtly rearranging its corridors to please him, because the stones themselves knew who walked upon them. I noticed how every magical spell I cast felt... thin. Hollow. Like a child’s crayon drawing held up against the sun.

One night, I couldn’t sleep. I went to the royal library, searching for something—maybe a record of my ancestors, maybe a diary of Blake’s. I found a leather-bound volume with a lock that opened at my touch. Inside were handwritten notes, some in Blake’s own hand, some in Tim’s, some in Leon’s. They chronicled their service. And each one, at some point, had written something similar:

Blake: *I wish I could protect him, but he needs no protection. He keeps me close because he loves my company, not my spells. I have made peace with that.*

Tim: *The gap between his magic and mine is so wide that calling it a gap is an insult to geometry. But he laughs at my jokes, and that’s enough.*

Leon: *I tremble still, after all these years. Not from fear of his wrath, but from the weight of his kindness. It’s a kindness that could unmake everything by accident.*

I closed the book and sat in the dark for a long time. I wasn’t the first. I wouldn’t be the last. Every wizard in my line had realized this, and every one of them had chosen to stay, to love him, to serve not as protectors but as companions. It was a noble calling. But it was also a horror, because the foundation of our entire identity was a lie.

The next morning, I entered the throne room with a new staff—carved, at Godric’s suggestion, from the dead-universe wood. It channeled magic with a clarity I had never experienced. I could feel my powers, a thousandfold amplified, humming in perfect resonance. A part of me wanted to show off, to cast something magnificent.

Instead, I just stood at his left hand. He was reviewing a trade agreement with a new universe, his brow furrowed in that familiar, thoughtful way. He looked so normal. So human. And I realized that was the most terrifying magic of all: his ability to appear harmless. To appear in need of a wizard.

When the audience was over, he turned to me. “You’ve been quiet today. Everything all right?”

I looked at his kind eyes, his warm smile. And I said, “Yes, Uncle. Everything’s fine.”

He nodded, satisfied. “Good. I was thinking—there’s a pocket dimension that’s been causing trouble. Nothing major, just a few rebel mages. Why don’t you handle it? It’ll be good practice for you.”

I felt the words lodge in my throat. Good practice. Did he really think I needed practice? Or was this another gift, another kindness, a way to let me feel useful? I didn’t know anymore. I would never know, because he would never tell me, and I would never have the courage to ask.

“Of course, Your Majesty,” I said.

“Uncle,” he corrected gently.

“Uncle.”

That evening, I stood on the palace balcony and looked out at the capital, its towers gleaming, its people safe under the umbrella of Godric’s rule. They loved him. They revered him. And they, like me, had no idea how utterly, catastrophically unneeded they were. He could rule the omniverse without a single subject, without a single advisor, without a single court wizard. But he didn’t, because he was lonely. Because he had once been mortal, and something in him still craved the warmth of other souls.

And I, Gorm, descendant of Blake, was that warmth. A candle flame held up to an infinite sun. The sun didn’t need the candle. The sun could accidentally swallow the candle without even noticing. But the sun chose to cup its hands around the tiny flame and call it beautiful.

I should have felt grateful. Instead, I felt the vast, cold expanse of that infinite sun pressing down on my mind, and I knew that I would spend the rest of my life walking a tightrope over that abyss, pretending I wasn’t already falling.

I still love him. That’s the part that keeps me awake. I love my uncle. And I hate, with every fiber of my being, that I know what he is.
