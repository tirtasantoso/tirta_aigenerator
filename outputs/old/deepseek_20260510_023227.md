You could not even properly oppose him, because the act of opposition itself fed the machinery of his perfection. And if you were lucky—very, very lucky—you got to be part of his family, to bask in the warmth of that terrible attention, and to tell yourself that the warmth was worth the price.

He was an object lesson, a footnote in the private chronicles of terrified court wizards, a demonstration that there was no weakness, no gap, no opening. There was only Godric, and the rest of us, and the infinite, unbridgeable distance in between.

The roast was excellent, and the honey rolls did make me want to cry, but I didn’t. I had learned, that afternoon, that some weights are too large for tears. You just carry them. You carry them and you smile and you ask about the crops, because the alternative is to end up like Kael, and there are no second chances in a universe where Godric is the only one who gets to decide what happens next.
