The sun hung like a promise over the spires of Olympus, gilding the marble where King Godric stood. At forty-five, his was a face carved from benevolent strength, a salt-and-pepper beard framing a smile that reached his warm eyes. His muscular build, usually clad in armor, was today draped in ceremonial silks of gold and white. The air smelled of summer blossoms and baking bread, a symphony of laughter and lute strings weaving through the courtyards. His daughter Isabel, a vision of twenty-two years with her mother’s eyes, clutched her betrothed’s hand, her joy a tangible thing. Nearby, his younger daughter Hebe, fifteen and all restless energy, teased the wedding guests. Godric’s wife, Reina, touched his arm, her presence his anchor. He cleared his throat to begin the rites, his voice a familiar, fatherly rumble that quieted the crowd.

The first fireball did not whistle; it *unmade* the air. It struck the central pavilion, and the sound of happiness was replaced by a vacuum of silence before the scream of rending stone. From the western skies, where the peaceful kingdom of Thronegate lay, came a storm of winged shadows and projectile annihilation. Godric’s world fractured into slow, crystalline shards of horror. He saw Reina turn, her smile still half-formed, before a beam of condensed plasma took her and the entire north wing into a mist of atomized marble. Hebe’s name was ash on his tongue as a collapsing arch swallowed her. Isabel, her white gown now scarlet, reached for him across a suddenly vast and burning distance before a Thronegate knight, his armor sleek and alien, swung a sword that ended a thousand years of lineage in a single, casual arc.

A hand gripped his shoulder—Thorin, his friend, his wizard, face pale with terror and fury. “We must go, now!” Thorin yelled over the din of dying Olympus. He began chanting, his hands weaving patterns that bled light into the smoke. It was a forbidden syntax, a spell of last resort known only to the blood of his family. The air around them thickened, reality beginning to peel away. Godric, numb, saw a Thronegate archer take aim. He tried to shout a warning, but his voice was gone. The arrow, etched with anti-magic runes, took Thorin through the throat mid-syllable. The wizard’s eyes widened, not with pain, but with the profound tragedy of an unfinished sentence. The spell, unleashed yet incomplete, collapsed inwards.

It did not teleport Godric so much as it *erased* him from that coordinate of suffering. He fell through a scream of broken magic, through layers of reality that scorched his soul, and landed in a cold, dark place—a forgotten sanctuary carved deep into the bedrock of the world. Agony, loss, and a black, all-consuming fury fused within his chest, crystallizing into something cold and absolute. The kind king was entombed in that moment, and something else began to be born.

As his body struck the stone floor, the misfired remnants of Thorin’s spell, interacting with the raw, unadulterated trauma of a soul destroyed, triggered an anomaly. His aging ceased. The need for breath, for food, for sleep, evaporated like a forgotten dream. And then, in the silent dark, the engine ignited. His strength, the intelligence that had once ruled a peaceful kingdom, his very senses—they began to multiply. A millionfold. And then that new totality multiplied by itself. And again. And again. Not once, but every second, a recursive, exponential explosion of self.

Simultaneously, new realities bloomed within him every heartbeat. A power to unmake gravity. A power to silence thought. A power to transmute time into dust. Each one, at its birth, held the capacity to scour all life from a world. And each one’s potency began its own identical, endless multiplication. The sanctuary, the planet, the universe itself should have vaporized in a blink, but his first conscious power was one of perfect, absolute concealment. An aura of nullification that grew at the same terrifying rate, a dam holding back an infinite ocean. He lay there, trapped in a stasis of body but not of being, as inside the silent shell, a god of infinite depth and fury was being forged, second by endless second, for ten thousand years.

The galaxy had forgotten. On the thriving mega-planet of Thronegate Prime, where cities floated in layered atmospheric rings and history was a curated digital stream, Olympus was a myth less substantial than shadow. It was here, twenty years before the present, that Blake the merchant plied his trade. He was thirty-eight, honest to a fault, with a comforting paunch and mutton chops that gave him a perpetually friendly air. His life was his wife Anna, his son Josh who loved star-charts, and his daughter Rhea who collected oddly shaped stones. During a routine cargo run to a backwater moon, a grav-anomaly forced his freighter down into a deep, uncharted canyon.

Seeking shelter, Blake found a cave that wasn’t a cave. The stone door yielded to a push, as if expecting him. Inside, the air was still and ancient. In a chamber lit by no source stood a figure on a dais, encased in a shimmering cocoon of amber light. Driven by a curiosity he couldn’t name, Blake reached out. His fingers, calloused from handling cargo crates, brushed the surface. The cocoon shattered like glass.

The man who opened his eyes was physically the same as the king who had fallen: muscular, bearded, handsome. But the warmth was gone, replaced by a depth so cold and still it felt gravitational. Those eyes, the color of a starless void, fixed on Blake. In that instant, ten thousand years of compounded omniscience—every fact, every secret, every beating heart in the galaxy—clicked into place within Godric’s mind. He knew the name of Blake’s great-great-grandmother. He knew the exact chemical composition of the tea Anna had drunk that morning. He knew the sprawling, arrogant might of Thronegate, and the boy-king Robert who ruled from his ancestral throne.

Godric’s first words in ten millennia were not of thanks, but of quiet, absolute declaration. “You have my gratitude,” he said to Blake, his voice a low rumble that vibrated in the bones. “Wait here.” He stepped past Blake, out of the sanctuary, and looked up at the sky, seeing not stars but the intricate, fragile web of Thronegate’s power. He issued his challenge not through comms, but by bending the medium of reality itself, a thought that etched itself into the mind of every citizen, from King Robert down to the lowliest street sweeper. A simple statement: *Abdicate, or I will break your army in three days’ time. Gather them.*

***

King Robert, twenty-two and steeped in the invincible legend of his civilization, responded with glorious outrage. The might he assembled above the designated plain was the culmination of ten millennia of unchecked dominance. Billions of super-soldiers in photon-reflective armor stood in perfect, continent-sized phalanxes. Behind them hovered the robot legions, each unit a calculus of perfect slaughter. Magic-wielders summoned storms of raw elemental chaos, and dragons the size of mountain ranges beat wings that stirred hurricanes. There were ninjas among them whose speed allowed them to step a fraction of a second into the past to land killing blows. The air crackled with unleashed potential, a tsunami of force meant to crush a single man.

Blake and Anna watched from a nearby ridge, brought there by a thought from Godric. “He’ll be killed,” Anna whispered, clutching Blake’s arm. Blake, his heart hammering, could only watch.

Godric stood alone in the plain’s center, a speck of black against the glittering, roaring ocean of annihilation. He did not adopt a stance. He simply stood, hands at his sides, as the order to attack was given.

The world exploded towards him.

And then it stopped.

Not metaphorically. Literally. Every projectile, every beam, every spell, every dragon’s breath, every ninja mid-leap—frozen in place. The silence was deafening. Godric had not moved. He had merely *willed* the concept of momentum in his immediate vicinity to cease. Then, with a thought as casual as a man shooing a fly, he *redefined* the projectiles. The billion railgun slugs turned into rose petals that fluttered harmlessly to the ground. The plasma beams became streams of cool, fresh water. The apocalyptic magic spells unraveled into lullabies that whispered across the ranks.

Confusion curdled into dread. The dragon-riders urged their beasts forward. Godric glanced at them. His glance contained the gravitational pull of a collapsing star. The dragons, along with their riders, were compressed instantly into perfect, tiny spheres of carbon the size of marbles, which fell with soft *plinks* onto the grass.

The time-twisting ninjas finally reached him, their blades aiming for vital points from a dozen different micro-second trajectories. Godric allowed the first blade to touch his tunic. It shattered, and the shockwave of that shattering traveled back along the blade, through the arm, and unmade the ninja from the molecular level outwards, a reverse-birth of dust. The others, understanding in a flash of terror, tried to flee back through time. Godric reached into the fourth dimension, plucked the threads of their personal timelines, and tied them into a knot. The ninjas simply ceased to have ever existed.

He then demonstrated a power he had acquired around the year 6,000 of his stasis: emotional transmutation. He targeted the vast army’s collective consciousness and dialed their fear up to its maximum, then multiplied that feeling by a factor of a billion. The effect was not panic, but a catatonic, absolute despair. Soldiers dropped their weapons, weeping uncontrollably, their minds broken by the sheer weight of hopeless horror. The robot armies, detecting the psychological collapse of their commanders, initiated a tactical retreat. Godric sighed, a sound of profound boredom, and snapped his fingers. The snap erased every synthetic intelligence in the galaxy simultaneously, reducing the galaxy’s greatest robot armadas to inert metal.

The battle, if it could be called that, was over in less than a minute. Godric had not taken a single step. He turned and walked back to the ridge where Blake and Anna stood frozen in terror of a different kind. He looked at Blake, and for a fleeting second, the abyss in his eyes softened by a micron. “It is done,” he said.

Two days later, King Robert was found in his throne room, a ceremonial dagger in his hand, the ghost of Godric’s induced despair still etched on his young face. Godric ascended the throne. His first decree was the Awakening.

It was a selective gift, a focused empowerment. From that day forth, every man of Thronegate blood would experience a tenfold increase in strength, endurance, intelligence, and wisdom for every day they lived past the age of twenty. A man of forty, like Blake, had lived twenty years past the threshold—approximately 7,300 days. His power was not tenfold, but ten to the power of 7,300, a number that dwarfed galaxies. And they could control it, could appear as ordinary men. But Blake noticed something. His control was finer, his sense of the power deeper, his multiplicative base felt… vaster. Where others felt a rising tide, he felt an ocean beneath the ocean. He was Godric’s first and only friend, and the king had, without fanfare, gifted him a multiplier of a thousandfold per day. A power that would remain hidden, a silent testament to trust.

Society transformed. The men, becoming demigods through simple longevity, revered Godric as the source of this grace. Crime vanished. Women were protected with a ferocity that became cultural dogma, any threat met with instantaneous and absolute retribution from any man nearby. The people loved him, for he gave them peace, security, and purpose.

That purpose soon turned outward. Thronegate, now a society of latent gods, began to subjugate the omniverse. The protocol was mercy: an offer of unconditional surrender delivered via reality-edict. Refusal meant conquest.

The first universe to refuse was the Chronoarchy, a realm where beings had mastered time as a physical tool. They had prepared for five years, having foreseen Thronegate’s approach in their temporal mirrors. They weaponized causality, creating paradox bombs and armies of themselves from every possible future. A squad of five Thronegate men, all in their late fifties—kindly fathers and grandfathers named Harv, Clem, and others—arrived. The Chronoarchs unleashed their ultimate weapon: a temporal stasis field designed to freeze a target across all timelines. The Thronegate men, suppressing a sigh, simply flexed their strength. The stasis field shattered, and the recoil fractured the timeline of the Chronoarch homeworld into a billion disconnected moments, stranding their civilization in eternal, simultaneous isolation. Conquest time: twenty-two minutes.

The second was the Psyche Nexus, a universe of pure collective consciousness that could weaponized thought, projecting psychic plagues that could drive entire species to madness. They believed no physical force could touch them. Godric, annoyed by their incessant mental buzzing during his contemplation of a new statue for Blake’s garden, glanced in their general direction. The glance carried a superpower he’d gained called ‘Absolute Solipsistic Negation.’ The entire Nexus, an entity spanning a billion minds across a dimension of thought, experienced the sudden, irrevocable belief that only it existed, and that it did not exist. It vanished into a philosophical singularity. Conquest time: the span of a distracted thought.

The third was the Brute Cosmos, a universe of endless, recursive physical escalation, where beings grew stronger with every fight. They welcomed the challenge, having prepared war-worlds and champions who fed on conflict. Five different Thronegate men, including a gentle botanist named Eli, were sent. The Brute champions, planets-sized beings of pure muscle and fury, charged. Eli raised a hand and, with the faintest exertion, imposed the concept of ‘zero’ upon their biological processes. Their strength, their very metabolic energy, was set to null. They became frozen, conscious statues, adrift in the void. Conquest time: seven minutes.

***

In a hidden dimension, the entity known as Xylos the Analyzer, a being who consumed knowledge and vulnerabilities, learned of Godric. Through deep scrutiny, Xylos formed a hypothesis: such concentrated power must have a focusing point, a central core or perhaps a psychic resonator that could be disrupted. The moment this thought coalesced—*Godric has a weakness*—the paradox activated. Across the omniverse, Godric’s already-infinite amplification rate ticked up by an incalculable degree. A perfect immunity to ‘core disruption’ layered itself over his invulnerability like a trillion additional plates of armor over a already indestructible diamond. And mastery over the art of resonant psychic fragmentation flooded Godric’s being, a mastery infinitely superior to Xylos’s nascent theory.

Unaware, Xylos spent years crafting a weapon, the Soul Resonator, designed to find and shatter that theoretical core. He marshaled his forces, launched his attack, and fired the beam of universe-breaking resonance at Godric, who was at that moment sharing a meal with Blake’s family.

The beam struck Godric’s chest and did nothing. Not a flicker. Godric paused, a forkful of Anna’s pie halfway to his mouth. He looked not at the beam, but through the dimensions at Xylos. He then performed the attack Xylos had invented, but with a magnitude a nonillion times greater, refined with nuances Xylos could never comprehend. The attack did not target Xylos. It targeted the very *concept* of analysis and planning against Godric within Xylos’s home dimension, retroactively erasing the capacity for such thoughts from all of its history. Xylos was not killed. He was left as a hollow entity, staring at his weapon, knowing with absolute, desolate certainty that he had been doomed from the very first spark of his ambition, that every second of planning had only fueled the very power he sought to topple. His despair was the final, perfect proof of the paradox.

Through it all, Blake was the constant. To the world, Godric was the unforgiving king, a finality as cold as death to his enemies. To Blake, he was a friend. He laughed at Blake’s simple jokes. He asked after Josh’s studies, Rhea’s stone collection. He gifted Anna a garden that bloomed with flowers from extinct worlds. In Blake’s honest, loyal presence, the frozen core of the king who loved his family began, atom by atom, to thaw. The warmth did not replace the ruthlessness; it coexisted with it, a choice now rather than a wound. For his subjects, he became a fatherly figure, stern but just, his kindness a shield they lived under. For his enemies, the abyss remained.

Blake, his merchant days far behind him, naturally fell into the role of advisor, of mediator, of the one who could interpret the king’s will with empathy. He found he had an uncanny knack for understanding the ancient protocols of the palace, for sensing the flow of magical energies in the throne room. His son Josh, now a man, served as a royal cartographer, but spent his evenings studying obscure cosmological texts, a habit his father encouraged without quite knowing why.

The present-day Thronegate, capital of the Omniverse, is a place of serene, impossible majesty. Its spires, forged from the captured essences of conquered galaxies, pierce a sky where dying universes are reborn as evening stars. In the central palace, on a balcony overlooking infinity, stands Godric. The salt-and-pepper beard is the same, but the eyes, once voids of horror, now hold a placid, paternal depth. Beside him stands Blake, older, his mutton chops gone grey, but his posture straight with a hidden strength known only to two beings in all of existence. He wears the subtle robes of the Royal Advisor, a role that has, over time, seamlessly become that of the Court Wizard. Below, the citizens live without fear, the men humming with quiet, controlled power, the women smiling in absolute safety. Somewhere in the royal archives, Josh pores over a star-chart that looks suspiciously like an ancient, half-remembered spell diagram.

Godric places a hand on Blake’s shoulder, a gesture of absolute trust. “It is peaceful,” Godric says, his voice once more warm, but underpinned by the weight of endless power.

“It is,” Blake agrees, looking out at the kingdom, the omniverse, their legacy. He feels no need for more words. The friend had saved the king, and the king, in turn, had built a heaven upon the ashes of his hell, with the descendant of a wizard who never finished his spell standing faithfully by his side.
