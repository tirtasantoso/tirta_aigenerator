# The Inherited Weight of Absolute Futility

Kaedan's hands trembled as he lifted the first leather-bound volume from the ironwood chest.

The chest itself was ancient, its surface scarred by millennia of handling, its iron bands worn smooth by the touch of countless hands that had come before his. Kaedan was twenty-two years old, the newest court wizard of Thronegate, and the weight of what sat before him was far heavier than any physical object could account for. The morning light of the capital filtered through the high arched windows of the wizard's private study, casting long golden rectangles across the polished mahogany floor. Outside, the streets of the imperial city hummed with the quiet, orderly prosperity that had defined life under Godric's reign for a million years. The scent of fresh bread and blooming jasmine drifted through the open casement, pleasant and completely at odds with the cold dread already coiling in Kaedan's stomach.

His father's voice echoed in his memory, as rough and uncompromising as it had been three days ago when the old wizard had formally passed the mantle. "You will read every journal, Kaedan. Every single one. It is not a suggestion. It is not a tradition I am asking you to honor. It is a requirement. If you cannot stomach what is in those pages, you will still serve. But you will serve with your eyes open."

Kaedan had nodded then, not understanding. He had spent his entire life in the palace, had known Uncle Godric since before he could walk. Godric was warmth itself, a ruggedly handsome man of forty-five with salt-and-pepper hair and a neatly trimmed beard, a man who laughed easily at the dinner table and asked after his studies with genuine, fatherly interest. Godric was the kind of king who remembered every servant's name, who personally ensured that the palace kitchens sent extra bread to new mothers in the capital, who walked among his people with the easy, approachable demeanor of a simple man who happened to wear a crown. Kaedan loved him. Everyone loved him.

But the journals were not about the Godric who laughed at dinner.

The journals were about the Godric who had erased the strongest resistance in omniversal history with less effort than a mortal man might use to brush dust from his sleeve. The Godric whose kindness to his loyal subjects was matched only by the absolute, unflinching cruelty he reserved for anyone who dared to oppose him. The Godric who was, by every measurable and immeasurable standard, the ultimate apex predator of all creation.

Kaedan drew a steadying breath that did nothing to steady him. He opened the first journal.

I am Osvald, forty-fourth court wizard of Thronegate, and I am writing this record because I must. Because the act of writing is the only thing that keeps the screaming inside my skull quiet enough to function.

The marble sits on a small pedestal in Godric's private study now. He keeps it there, among his other curiosities, a tiny sphere of absolute darkness that seems to absorb the light around it. Sometimes, when he is lost in thought, he picks it up and rolls it between his fingers, the way another man might fidget with a coin. And every time he does, I feel the pulse of his amplification ripple outward—another infinitesimal increase in his already incomprehensible power, triggered by the passive resistance of the compressed omniverse pressing against his palm.

The restored reality is perfect. Identical in every detail to what existed before. The capital city bustles with life. The palace servants go about their duties. The Gardener tends his roses. The Sommelier perfects his vintages. Everything is exactly as it was.

But the marble contains the original. The real omniverse. The actual existence that was compressed into a single point of infinite density. What we inhabit now—this restored reality, this perfect copy—is it truly real? Is it the same? Or are we all merely echoes, reflections, a second draft of existence created by a god who found the first draft insufficiently entertaining?

I do not know. I will never know. And the horror of it—the deep, existential, soul-crushing horror—is that it does not matter. The restored reality is functionally identical to the original. No one can tell the difference. No one except Godric, who holds the original in his hand, who rolls it between his fingers like a worry stone, who occasionally glances at it with an expression of mild, nostalgic fondness.

He could do it again. At any moment. For any reason. Or for no reason at all. He could compress all of existence into another marble, and restore it, and compress it again, and restore it, infinitely, forever. And we would never know. We would never be able to tell. We exist because he allows us to exist. We persist because he tolerates our persistence. And our original selves—the real versions of everything we are—are a trophy on his shelf.

The fictional coalition was the last opposition. I do not know this for certain, of course—I am writing this record in the immediate aftermath, and who can say what the distant future holds? But I feel it, deep in my bones. The despair I witnessed in those final moments, the absolute, mind-shattering realization that everything they had, everything they were, everything they had ever been or could ever be, was not merely insufficient, but irrelevant—that despair will echo across the omniverse. It will seep into the consciousness of every being who ever considers opposing Godric. It will whisper to them, in the quiet moments before they form their resistance, that they are not merely doomed to fail. They are doomed to be a disappointment. A letdown. A brief, forgettable interruption to an afternoon tea.

And no one will ever dare to oppose him again.

I am Osvald, descendant of Blake, court wizard to the apex predator of all existence. And I have seen the death of hope. It was smaller than a marble, and it weighed more than the omniverse, and Godric keeps it on his shelf next to a collection of interesting stones he has found on his walks through the palace gardens.

I am going to go lie down now. I do not expect to sleep.

Clive closed the journal with trembling hands.

The study was silent. The arcane lamps had dimmed to their evening glow. Outside the high windows, the eternal afternoon of Thronegate had finally given way to a soft, amber twilight, and the shadows were long across the polished mahogany floor. The ironwood chest sat open beside his chair, its contents—the accumulated trauma of fifty-five generations of court wizards—spread across the low table before him.

He had read the journal of Osvald three times now. Once when he was twenty-two, newly appointed, devouring the contents of the chest in a single night of existential terror. Once when he was forty, after the revelation of the Stasis Event's true origin had left him reeling and desperate for context. And now, at fifty, after the cooking lessons and the gardening lessons and the quiet, terrible realization that Godric's pretense of mortality was the greatest kindness an infinite being could offer.

Each reading revealed new layers of horror. New implications. New reasons to lie awake in the dark hours before dawn.

Osvald had not known. He had written his journal in the immediate aftermath of the fictional coalition's annihilation, and he had speculated—hoped, perhaps—that the despair of that day would echo across the omniverse and prevent future opposition. He could not have known, in that moment, that he was witnessing the final act of resistance. That no being would ever again dare to challenge Godric. That the marble on the pedestal was not merely a trophy, but a gravestone. The end of defiance. The death of the very concept of opposition.

But Clive knew. He knew because he had lived his entire life in an omniverse where no one opposed the throne. He knew because the journals after Osvald's contained no further accounts of massacres—only records of peaceful rule, of the slow, steady expansion of Thronegate's influence, of the quiet, contented existence of a reality that had finally, fully, irrevocably accepted its place. He knew because the very idea of challenging Godric had become, over the eons, not merely impossible, but unthinkable. A category error. A logical absurdity.

And the marble was still there.

Clive had seen it. On his first day as court wizard, thirty years ago, Godric had given him a tour of the private study—the same study where they had shared tea and honey cakes and quiet conversations ever since. And there, on a small pedestal of obsidian, had been a marble of absolute darkness. A tiny sphere that seemed to absorb the light around it, that seemed to pull at the edges of perception, that seemed to hum with a faint, almost inaudible vibration.

"A keepsake," Godric had said, noticing Clive's gaze. "From a very long time ago. A reminder of when opposition still existed. Charming, isn't it?"

Clive had nodded, not understanding. He had not yet read Osvald's journal. He had not yet learned what the marble contained.

Now he knew. The marble contained the original omniverse. The real one. The first draft. Everything that had existed before Godric's disappointed sigh had compressed it into a single point of infinite density. And the restored reality—the universe Clive had been born into, the palace where he had grown up, the throne room where he served, the gardens where the Gardener tended his roses, the kitchen where the Chef prepared her transcendent meals—was a copy. A perfect, indistinguishable, atom-for-atom replica.

Or was it? Did it matter? If no one could tell the difference—if the copy was flawless in every detail, if every soul was restored exactly as it had been, if every memory and emotion and experience was perfectly preserved—then what did "real" even mean?

Clive did not know. The philosophers of Thronegate had debated the question for eons, and no consensus had ever been reached. Some argued that the restored reality was the same reality, that Godric's restoration was not replication but true resurrection, that the continuity of existence was unbroken. Others argued that the original was gone, compressed into a marble on a shelf, and that everyone who lived in the restored reality was a copy, a duplicate, a second iteration of a story whose first edition had been pulped.

Godric never answered the question. When asked—and he had been asked, by court wizards braver or more foolish than Clive—he simply smiled and said that it did not matter. That the restored reality was functionally identical, that no one had lost anything, that the continuity of their existence was as real as they believed it to be.

And the marble remained on its pedestal. A constant, silent reminder that the reality they inhabited was conditional. Provisional. Permitted. That everything they knew, everything they loved, everything they were, existed solely because Godric had decided to restore it after accidentally destroying it with less than zero percent of his strength.

Clive closed his eyes. The dread was a cold, heavy presence in his chest, familiar as an old friend. He had lived with it for thirty years now, and he had learned to carry it, to function despite it, to serve his king with loyalty and love even as the weight of everything he knew pressed down on his soul.

But Osvald's journal had added a new layer to the weight. A new dimension to the dread.

Osvald had witnessed the death of opposition. He had seen the most elaborate, most spectacular, most narrative-perfect coalition in the history of existence be casually, effortlessly, accidentally annihilated. And he had understood, in that moment, that no one would ever try again. That the lesson had been learned. That the futility was absolute.

And he had been right. For all the eons since, across all the googolplexian years that separated Osvald's era from Clive's, there had been zero opposition. Zero resistance. Zero beings who dared to form even the slightest thought of challenging, resisting, threatening, or opposing Godric. The apex predator had become, not merely the ruler of the omniverse, but its fundamental condition. As unquestioned and unquestionable as gravity. As absolute and unassailable as the speed of light.

And the marble remained on its pedestal. The original reality, compressed and preserved, a trophy from the day hope died.

Clive opened his eyes and looked at the journal in his hands. Osvald's handwriting was neat and precise, the letters formed with the careful control of a man who was holding himself together by sheer force of will. The ink was faded in places, the pages worn from the touch of countless hands across countless eons. This journal had been read by every court wizard since Osvald. It had been passed down through the generations, a sacred text of terror, a testament to the absolute futility of defiance.

And now it had been read by Clive. Fiftieth court wizard of Thronegate. The last in a long line of men who had witnessed the impossible and lived to write it down.

He closed the journal and placed it gently back into the ironwood chest. The other journals surrounded it—the stat massacres, the superpower demonstrations, the revelations of the Stasis Event's true origin. The accumulated trauma of fifty-five generations of his ancestors. The complete, exhaustive record of why opposing Godric was not merely impossible, but conceptually meaningless.

And at the center of that record, physically and metaphorically, was the marble. The compressed original. The reality that had been, before a disappointed god had sighed.

Clive stood. His legs were steady, despite everything. He had learned, over thirty years, to carry the weight. To walk beneath it. To serve despite it.

He left the study and walked through the quiet corridors of the palace, past the Gardener's blooming paradise, past the Sommelier's cellar of infinite vintages, past the kitchens where the Head Chef was preparing the evening meal. The servants nodded to him as he passed, their expressions calm and content, their cosmic powers suppressed into the gentle, mundane rhythms of their service. They did not know what the marble contained. They did not know that their original selves were compressed into a trophy on Godric's shelf. They did not need to know. The knowledge was a burden that only the court wizard could carry.

Clive reached the private study at the end of the corridor and paused at the door. It was slightly ajar, and through the gap, he could see Godric sitting in his favorite armchair, a book open in his lap, a cup of jasmine tea steaming on the side table. The fire crackled gently in the hearth. The evening light, filtered through crystalline windows, painted the room in shades of amber and gold.

And on the small obsidian pedestal beside the armchair, the marble sat in silent, infinite compression. Godric, lost in his reading, absently reached out and picked it up. He rolled it between his fingers—once, twice, three times—and then set it back down.

A faint pulse of energy rippled through the room. Another infinitesimal amplification. Another recursive increase in the already infinite power of the apex predator.

Clive watched for a moment longer, then turned and walked away. The dread was heavy in his chest. The love was there too, as it always was, inextricably tangled with the terror. He served the most powerful being in existence. He loved the man who had been his favorite uncle since childhood. And he knew, with the absolute certainty of inherited trauma and personal experience, that nothing in the omniverse would ever challenge that being again.

The marble was a gravestone. The last opposition was long dead. And the rest of existence, in every timeline, in every dimension, in every possible configuration of reality, continued to persist solely because Godric allowed it to persist.

It always had. And it always would.
