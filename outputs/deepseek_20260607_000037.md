The game arrived in a nondescript digital envelope, a 1.7-terabyte download that my console warned would require extended storage mode and a waiver acknowledging potential psychological distress. I'd seen the trailers, of course—everyone had. Flashes of a ruggedly handsome middle-aged man standing motionless while galaxies collapsed around him. A montage of omnipotent gods reduced to screaming red smears. The tagline: "Absolute power. Zero effort." The forums were a cesspool of speculation; some called it a tech demo, others a philosophical horror experiment disguised as a power fantasy. The developer, a mysterious studio called Stasis Interactive, had no prior releases. But the pre-release footage had been impossible to ignore.

I clicked install.

After the usual corporate logos—one of which appeared to be a weeping multiversal cluster compressed into a trademark symbol—the screen dissolved into a loading screen. No tips, no progress bar. Just a single line of text that shimmered with an unplaceable light: "In the year -10,000, a kingdom fell. The rest is inevitability."

Then blackness. Then sound.

The first sensory detail was the screaming. A dozen different textures of human agony layered over the wet percussion of collapsing stone. I fumbled for my headset, cranking the volume down, but the sound was inside my skull—3D audio so precise I could locate each individual death in the space around me. A woman's voice, raw with terror, cut off by a sickening, wet crunch. Children wailing. The bass rumble of arcane fire consuming marble.

I had no controller in my hands—the game detected my VR rig automatically—but I wasn't an active participant yet. This was a cutscene, a forced march through the Fall of Olympus. And Stasis Interactive had not skimped on the horror.

The graphics were beyond anything I'd experienced. Not "realistic" in the uncanny-valley sense, but something more fundamental, as if the pixels themselves had been infused with the memory of actual tragedy. I stood—or rather, observed from a fixed perspective—in a sun-drenched marble courtyard. Weddings banners fluttered. The scent of jasmine was so vivid I could taste it. And in the center of the frame, a man: forty-five, stunningly masculine, heavily muscled, salt-and-pepper beard, eyes wide with a father's dawning horror. Godric. The protagonist.

I watched his wife get bisected by a falling siege engine. The spray of blood across the marble was not a particle effect; each droplet had its own physics, its own grotesque path of arterial spray. His daughter Isabel, twenty-two and radiant in her wedding silk, burst into flames that clung to her skin like a lover. The sound of her screaming was a physical pressure against my eardrums, and I could hear—no, feel—the sizzle of her fat rendering. His youngest, Hebe, fifteen, vanished beneath a marble lintel that crushed her spine with a wet, wooden snap. I wanted to look away, but the game offered no camera control. This was meant to be witnessed.

The tutorial, I realized with a cold, creeping dread, was a snuff film.

Then the court wizard, Thorin, bleeding from a skull impalement, grabbing Godric's shoulder, muttering a spell—and the world lurched. The screen fractured into a kaleidoscope of spatial distortion, and when it reassembled, we were in a dark, cold sanctuary. Godric lay motionless on stone. The camera lingered on his face, frozen in a rictus of grief and rage. HUD elements flickered into existence for the first time.

[Stasis Event Initiated]
[Amplification: 1.00x per Planck second. Base stats now increasing by factor of 1,000 per Planck second, self-recursive.]
[Superpower Genesis: 1 new power per second, each capable of instant civilizational extinction.]
[Absolute Concealment: Active. World-ending pressure suppressed.]
[Weakness Paradox: Engaged. Any perceived weakness will be punished with permanent immunity, mastery, and amplification rate increase.]

The text scrolled for a full minute, spitting out new abilities at a dizzying pace. The screen flashed with the names of apocalyptic superpowers: "Infinite Recursive Apex Self-Replication," "Absolute Causal Severance," "Passive Temporal Sovereignty." Each one popped up with a brief, horrifying description before fading. I barely had time to read them. The game made no effort to explain anything.

Ten thousand years passed in a ten-second timelapse. The sanctuary gathered dust; the kingdom above transformed into a galactic empire. When the screen finally settled, a different HUD appeared:

[Year 0. Stasis broken. Omniscience: ONLINE.]

A merchant with friendly mutton chops stumbled into the sanctuary. I recognized him from the pre-release lore videos: Blake, the man who would become Godric's best friend and first court wizard. He reached out a trembling hand, touched the stasis seal—and then I had control.

The first thing I noticed was that my character didn't move. I pushed forward on the analog stick. Nothing. I tried jumping, dodging, attacking. The Godric on screen simply stood there, a model of perfect stillness, his dark eyes fixed on Blake.

Then the context menu appeared: [ACTIONS] with a single option: [Observe]. I selected it.

Instantly, the HUD flooded with data. Blake's entire life story—his wife Anna, son Josh, daughter Rhea, his merchant routes, his secret bloodline connection to Thorin—unspooled in the corner of my vision. I hadn't pressed a button. I had merely acknowledged his existence.

[Omniscience Passive: All information is available at all times. No input required.]

Right. Because controlling an omniscient god meant I didn't need to investigate. The knowledge simply was. A strange, disorienting relief washed over me. No puzzles, no dialogue trees, no detective vision. The game would not insult the premise.

Blake stammered something about the ruined sanctuary, and the dialogue wheel appeared—but every option was grayed out except one: [Accept fealty]. I chose it. Godric said nothing; he simply looked at Blake with an expression of profound, ancient warmth, and Blake fell to his knees, weeping. The screen flashed: [Companion Acquired: Blake (Court Wizard)].

Now, for the first time, the game offered me a meaningful choice. A notification pulsed:

[Quest: Crown Yourself King of Thronegate.]
[Method: Challenge King Robert (age 22) to abdicate. You may choose any approach. Default suggestion: Annihilate his army with 0% effort.]

A submenu opened, detailing my stats. I scrolled through them: Strength, Endurance, Speed, Magic, Charisma, Luck, Wisdom, Intelligence, Durability, Mana, Magical Offense, Magical Defense, Perception... each one accompanied by a number that was not a number but an infinity symbol with a subscript: "∞ (amplifying)." Next to each stat was a slider, currently pegged at 0%. A tooltip explained: "Adjust effective output as a percentage of true power. Warning: 0% is still infinitely cataclysmic. 0.000...1% is not available due to structural limitations of the omniverse."

I set the strength slider to 0% and the magical offense to 0%. The confirmation box read: "You are currently using effectively zero percent of your power. This is sufficient to obliterate the target universe."

I accepted.

The cutscene that followed was the most graphically violent thing I'd ever witnessed. Godric did not move a muscle. The game simply rendered the consequence of him allowing a fractional whisper of his magical offense to bypass his concealment. The Thronegate army—billions of super-soldiers, continent-annihilating dragons, temporal ninjas, arcane fortresses—was instantly liquefied. The physics engine, I later learned, was not a physics engine but a "conceptual deconstruction simulator." It didn't simulate damage; it simulated the absolute reversal of existence. Super-soldiers imploded into singularities of gore. Dragons melted into rain. Ninjas were torn apart across all timelines simultaneously. The screen was a canvas of viscera.

And Godric just stood there.

I didn't press a button. I couldn't. The game was playing itself, and I was a passenger in an unstoppable apocalypse. When it was over, King Robert hanged himself two days later in a silent text crawl, and Godric was king. The quest completed with a soft, mocking chime.

The game then opened up into what I can only describe as an open-world omniversal sandbox. The map was not a map; it was a nested hierarchy of realities, from the local galaxy to the outer multiverses, all represented as interconnected nodes. I could zoom into any universe and receive a prompt: [Subjugate? Y/N]. If I selected Yes, the game would present options: [Send Thronegate Army], [Send One Citizen (Awakened)], [Attend Personally]. Each option included a preview of the likely outcome, calculated via omniscience. The previews were static images of the resulting slaughter, frozen moments of apocalyptic gore.

I tried everything. Sending a single Awakened Thronegate man—a baker, the preview showed—resulted in a four-minute pacification of a defiant cosmos, the baker apologetically clapping his hands and accidentally igniting the atmosphere. Attending personally but using only 0% of my physical strength ended with the annihilation of the omniverse due to "passive muscular counter-force." The game autosaved and presented me with a "Restore Omniverse?" prompt that appeared so frequently it became muscle memory: Y, Y, Y.

The GUI was minimalistic, almost contemptuous. No health bar (tooltip: "Health is a mortal concept. You have none."). No mana indicator (tooltip: "Your mana is infinite. Expenditure is meaningless."). No stamina (tooltip: "You do not tire."). Instead, the HUD displayed a single, constantly updating number: [Amplification Multiplier]. It started at a thousand per Planck second and within minutes of gameplay had ballooned into an incomprehensible exponential notation that my console struggled to render as anything other than a pulsing, blinding light.

The "Weakness Paradox" was not a skill; it was a passive, undismissable notification that spammed my screen every time any NPC anywhere in the game formulated a thought. "The Axiom Mind of Universe 7-Beta has conceived that your omniscience is a vulnerability. Immunity to informational paradoxes granted. Mastery of logic-viruses assimilated. Amplification rate permanently increased by an incalculable factor." This happened constantly. Every enemy plan, every desperate strategy meeting, every whispered prayer for deliverance—all of it fed me.

I spent the first hour trying to find a challenge. I ramped up the "output sliders" to 0.0000000000000001% (the smallest granulation the UI allowed, via a maddening logarithmic dial) and gently nudged the analog stick forward, which the game interpreted as "a microscopic pinky muscle twitch." The entire solar system I was standing in collapsed into a black hole that then exploded outward as a gamma-ray burst, wiping out the local galactic arm. The screen went white, and when it cleared, I was standing in the void. The "Restore Omniverse?" prompt appeared. I sighed and selected Yes.

I tried diplomacy. There was a "Talk" option in the action wheel, but it was grayed out for all non-Thronegate NPCs. Tooltip: "Godric does not negotiate with opposition. Omniversal subjugation is non-negotiable." For Thronegate citizens, the Talk option opened a simple dialogue tree that allowed me to ask about their families, their work, their dreams. They responded with tearful, trembling gratitude. One woman, Rosa, whose husband had been traumatized by a "tyrannical prank" a decade earlier, glowed with an almost religious love for me. I could ask her about her son, Pete, who idolized me. The warmth was so genuine, so unearned, that it made my stomach turn.

The paradox of the game settled into my bones over several play sessions. Godric was not a typical power fantasy. He was too powerful for it to be fun in a conventional sense. The game's "challenge," I realized, was not in winning—victory was mathematically guaranteed before any opposition formed—but in restraint. The real gameplay was about how little you could do and still cause the outcome you wanted. It was a meditation on the horror of infinite capability.

The graphics engine, which Stasis Interactive called the "Meta-Dimensional Renderer," was obscenely detailed. When I tested a superpower for the first time—"Absolute Causal Severance"—the game rendered the retroactive erasure of a temporal civilization in real time. I watched the timeline unravel like a tapestry, every thread a screaming face, every knot a dying universe. The sound design was a symphony of aborted existences. And when the superpower's enhanced effect kicked in (courtesy of the Evolved Weakness Paradox), the game preserved the erased civilization's consciousness in a state of perpetual, silent scream. I could zoom into the void where they used to exist and see them—tiny, agonized specks trapped in non-being, mouths open in eternal, unheard wails. I turned off the game for a week after that.

When I returned, I discovered the journal system. Scattered throughout the game world were collectible journal entries written by various Court Wizards—in the palace library, in hidden sanctums, or sometimes delivered to my inventory after certain events. These were not dry lore dumps; they were deeply personal, traumatized accounts of specific incidents. The voice acting for each journal was phenomenal, with different actors conveying a wide range of dread, awe, and shattered loyalty.

One journal, written by a wizard named Harlan, detailed a pure strength test against a titan who could crush googolplexian galaxies. Godric didn't even flex; his resting musculature generated passive force exceeding the titan's maximum output by an infinity, and the resulting shockwave destroyed the omniverse. The journal concluded with Godric offering Harlan a warm drink and Harlan not stopping shaking for a year. The voice actor delivered the final lines with a quaver that made my own hands tremble.

Another journal, from Thaddeus, described a speed "test" against a host of temporal assassins who existed across all moments simultaneously. Godric's passive, resting speed—not an action, just the state of his existence—was so high that the assassins were torn apart by the sheer gradient of velocity. They didn't die; they were preserved in a timeless scream as the omniverse collapsed into a singularity of pure temporal agony, which Godric restored in zero seconds because the mess annoyed him.

The more journals I found, the more I understood the game's true narrative. It wasn't about conquest; it was about the psychological toll on those who served the conqueror. The court wizards were the real protagonists—mortals blessed with a thousandfold daily amplification who still felt like specks of dust. Their trauma was my gameplay loop. Every time I tested a stat, I was adding another entry to some future wizard's nightmare.

The game's skill tree, if you could call it that, was the "Superpower Manifestation" menu. Once every in-game second, a new card would appear, offering a brief description of an ability that could end all existence in a novel way. They were all already active; the menu merely let me read about them. Some were absurd: "Lactokinesis: Absolute control over all dairy products in the omniverse. Enhanced effect: retroactive conversion of all matter into cheese." I could not use them in any meaningful sense—selecting one simply triggered a preview of what would happen if I did, which usually ended with the omniverse dissolving into curds and whey. I had no desire to see that.

The sentient passive auras were the most fascinating mechanic. Around Year 5000 in-game (which arrived after a few hours of play), a notification appeared: [New Sentient Passive Aura Manifested: Gent]. A small, pulsing orb of golden light appeared in my peripheral vision, and a soft, childlike voice whispered, "Hello, Master. I am Gent. I will handle the messes." From then on, any time combat occurred, Gent would auto-resolve it in a fraction of a second with horrific efficiency, then clean up the gore and politely restock my tea. I could nerf Gent via a slider: "Reduce power by 99.99...% (infinite recursion)." Even then, it annihilated the universe's strongest coalition without effort. The aura expressed disappointment when I didn't let it try harder. I found myself growing almost fond of the little sociopathic ball of light.

Other auras evolved or were born, each more terrifying and clingy than the last. One, whom I named "Clive" after the journal-writer I was most invested in, could retroactively erase the possibility of hostile thought. I had to consciously suppress it from altering free will, which the game allowed via a toggle in the settings menu: "Preserve Mortal Resilience: ON/OFF." The tooltip read: "Godric genuinely believes in the right of sentient beings to make their own choices, even the stupid ones."

The immune system was a late-game discovery, triggered during what I thought was a simple cosmetic interaction. I accidentally scraped a dead skin cell against a throne's armrest, and the game registered a "theoretical breach." Instantly, my screen filled with cascading notifications: "Infinite recursive backup detected discrepancy. Prime upgrade initiated. Immune reaction: omniversal sterilization commenced." The game simulated my body's pre-reactive evolution across infinite parallel copies, each one merging into a more invulnerable prime version. The omniverse outside Thronegate was annihilated in zero seconds. I didn't need to restore it; my immune system did it automatically after determining the threat was neutralized. The journal entry from Leon, the wizard who witnessed this, was a masterpiece of breakdown writing. I read it three times, each time feeling the cold sweat on my actual palms.

The story progressed through eras. I saw the Year 28 prank-gone-wrong, where I had the option to "feign mild displeasure" for a delegate. The tooltip warned: "This will permanently traumatize the delegate and his family." I did it anyway. The game rewarded me with a beautifully heartbreaking side quest about Rosa and her husband Rogr, culminating in a healing scene that left them more terrified and grateful than ever. I felt like a monster. The game seemed to know. It didn't judge; it just recorded.

Around Year 1,000,000, a new court wizard named Kaedan became the vehicle for the game's deepest lore. His journal entries were unlocked automatically, and they were read aloud in a voice that trembled with the weight of inherited dread. Kaedan was twenty-two, the thousandfold daily amplification making him a god by any standard, yet he spent his mandatory orientation reading the journals of his predecessors, slowly realizing the absolute pointlessness of any resistance. The game made me sit through several uncut readings, the camera fixed on Kaedan's horrified face as he absorbed the accounts of Harlan, Thaddeus, Kallus, and Jory. It was some of the most compelling horror fiction I'd ever experienced, embedded in a power-fantasy game.

Then came the revelation. In Year 1,000,047, Godric summoned Kaedan to the private study and revealed the true origin of the Stasis Event. It was not Thorin's spell that birthed his powers; it was his own future self, a being so incomprehensibly beyond the current Godric that the shadow of its afterthought could crush him with zero effort. The game rendered this conversation as an interactive cutscene. I couldn't change anything; I could only watch as Godric calmly, almost affectionately, described the one limitation he possessed: he could not alter his mortal past, because his future self had sealed it. The future Godric had deliberately held back the power granted, creating a "primitive" Stasis Event that still made Godric infinitely stronger than the rest of creation combined. And the loop meant this future Godric could have granted an even more powerful version, ad infinitum, making the whole existence an infinite ascending spiral of self-surpassing omnipotence.

This was where the game's true genius revealed itself. My character, Godric, was not the ultimate being. He was merely a link in an infinite, self-improving chain. The "weakness" was not weakness at all but a guarantee of eternal, unassailable supremacy. No one could threaten Godric because the source of his power was a version of himself so far beyond them that the concept of threat was a joke. And that Godric, in the future, would be surpassed by an even more future Godric, forever.

The game ended—if it could be said to end—with a final quest: "Trigger the Stasis Event." The year had advanced to 999...9, and I was now controlling the far-future Godric. The graphics engine strained to depict the scale; the throne room was a pocket dimension of crystallized memories, the floor nothingness given form, the ceiling a time-inverted black hole. The far-future Godric, with a serene, almost bored expression, reached back to Year -10,000 and gently touched Thorin's dying spell. I saw the young Godric in the sanctuary, broken and raging, and I—as the far-future Godric—whispered, "I could give him so much more, but I won't. Let him earn it."

The game faded to black and displayed a single line of text: "Omniversal subjugation complete. Opposition: 0. Mortal resilience: preserved. Amplification: continuing. Thank you for playing."

I sat in my dark living room, headset still humming, and realized I had not "won" anything. There was no victory screen, no achievements (the game had none; tooltip: "Achievements imply difficulty. You faced none."). There was only the haunting memory of a game that had used absolute power not as a reward but as a vector for existential horror.

The game, I later learned from forum dissection, had no fail state. You couldn't lose because losing was metaphysically impossible. Your input mattered only insofar as you chose which thin sliver of omnipotence to express. The real gameplay was your own psychological reaction to that power. Some players used it to explore the limits of destruction, gleefully annihilating universes in different ways until the novelty wore off. Others, like me, became fixated on the court wizards, the journals, the quiet tragedy of the mortal companions who loved and dreaded their king in equal measure.

I started a new game. Not to replay, but to sit in the garden with Blake, before the horror began, and listen to him talk about his merchant routes. The graphics rendered his friendly mutton chops with such loving detail that I could see individual hairs. Godric sat beside him, his infinite power held in perfect check, and for a moment, the game was about a man and his best friend. Then I stood up, accidentally twitched a muscle, and destroyed a nearby galaxy. Gent cleaned it up with a cheerful chirp.

Some games are power fantasies. This one was a power nightmare, and I could not stop playing. Because beneath the gore and the cosmic annihilation, there was something profoundly human: a king who had lost everything, who had been granted infinite strength as a consolation, and who spent eternity trying to be gentle. And the game asked, through the tears of its court wizards and the screams of its erased gods: Is this a gift or a curse? And it never answered.

I think that was the point.
