"I apologize, Your Majesty," I squeaked. "It is just... the gravity of the day."

"Ah, yes," Godric said, setting down the shears. He stretched, and the motion caused a chain reaction of meta-dimensional stability checks across the entire omniverse. "The Day of the Gift. The moment I send a little care package to my younger self."

He walked over to a pedestal where a small, swirling orb of light rested. This was the anchor point. The conduit to Year -10,000.

"I’ve been refining the parameters," Godric said casually, poking the orb. "I’ve been calculating the feedback loop. It’s a fascinating little problem. You see, I am currently operating at a level of power that makes my Year 0 self look like a particularly slow amoeba. If I were to simply copy-paste my current physiology and power systems back to that moment, the starting ‘base’ would be so astronomically high that the amplification mechanics would accelerate beyond even my current comprehension."

He chuckled, a sound that made the colors in the room vibrate. "Imagine it. I grant the past version 0.00001% of my current strength. The Stasis Event begins with that as the floor. The past Godric amplifies from that infinitely higher baseline. He becomes King. He rules. And eventually, he becomes *me*. But this new ‘me’—the Future-Future Me—would be exponentially stronger than I am now, because the starting line was moved. And then *that* version would send even more power back..."

He shook his head, grinning. "It’s the ultimate recursive inflation. A power singularity. Within one Planck second of the loop, the power density would exceed the storage capacity of the omniverse. Reality would simply turn into a statue of infinite energy. It would be terribly boring. No stories. No growth. Just... static perfection."

I stared at Him, my mind trying to process the mathematics He was dismissing as "boring." He was talking about rewriting the foundation of existence to such a degree that the concept of "strength" would lose all meaning.

"So," Godric continued, "I have to dial it back. I have to be... gentle. I’m going to give my past self the 'Year 0' package. The standard kit. Infinite amplification, Superpower Genesis, the Weakness Paradox... all the classics."

He looked at me, His eyes twinkling. "It feels almost criminal, really. Holding back so much. Even the 'held back' version I’m about to send is so powerful that it makes the rest of creation combined look like a negative number. My poor past self will think he’s struggling. He’ll think he’s overcoming obstacles. He’ll think he’s growing. He’ll believe in the 'mortal struggle.' It’s adorable, really. He’ll be erasing omniverses with a sneeze, thinking he’s working hard, while I stand here in the future, casually snuffing out suns for a bonsai."

The horror of it settled over me like a shroud. The King I served now was a monster of such magnitude that His " mercy" was a curse of omnipotence. He was deliberately stunting His own past self just to preserve the *aesthetic* of a journey.

Godric turned back to the pedestal. "Gardener!"

The air shimmered, and a being manifested. It was Xorgul, the Devourer of Dimensions. A billion years ago, Xorgul had been a terror that consumed universes to fuel its metamorphosis. Now, Xorgul was wearing a brown apron and holding a watering can made from a collapsed neutron star.

"Yes, Your Majesty?" Xorgul’s voice was a tectonic grind, filled with absolute, groveling terror.

"Water the Timeline-Bonsai," Godric said pleasantly. "And be careful with the hydrogen levels in Sector 7. I don't want the atoms fusing before tea time."

"As you command, Apex One," Xorgul wept, literal black tears of gravitational distortion streaming down its face. The Devourer bowed so low its face touched the floor. It was terrified. Not because Godric had beaten it—Godric never beat it. He had simply existed near it. Xorgul had tried to consume a galaxy Godric was standing on, and the sheer metabolic rejection of Godric’s biology had caused Xorgul to instantly evolve backward into a single-celled organism. Godric had found it amusing, evolved it back forward, and put it in charge of the hydrangeas. The trauma of that reversal, the realization that Godric could edit Xorgul’s evolution like a grocery list, had broken the entity forever.

Godric watched Xorgul work with a satisfied nod. "Good help is so hard to find," He said to me. "But Xorgul has a knack for it. You know, he once tried to calculate the kinetic energy of my little finger. He went mad instantly, of course. His brain melted into the abstract concept of 'zero.' I put it back together, but he’s been much more polite since then."

I looked at Xorgul, the once-great devourer trembling as it watered a galaxy-tree. I looked at Godric, who was inspecting His fingernails.

"And the auras," Godric mused, seemingly talking to Himself. "I need to make sure I include the aura package. Even the nerfed versions."

He glanced at the empty air beside Him. I couldn't see it, but I could *feel* it. His sentient passive auras. They had evolved so far that they no longer had names. They were just layers of existential dread.

"Remember 'Gent'?" Godric asked, chuckling. "My little waste-heat aura? The one that melted an omniverse army by accident? Oh, Clive, you should see Gent now. It’s evolved about... oh, forty trillion times since then. It’s not just sentient anymore. It’s a sub-deity of subservience. If I were to unleash Gent now, it wouldn't just kill the enemy. It would retroactively remove the concept of 'enemy' from the history of the omniverse, rewriting everyone’s childhood memories so we were all always friends. It’s suffocatingly sweet, really. I have to keep it in a phantom cage."

He gestured to the shadows. "And the immune system... oh, the immune system. Back in the day, it used to overreact to scratches. Now? It hunts. It hunts causality itself. If a version of me in a divergent timeline so much as stubs his toe, my immune system over here reaches across the dimensional barrier and erases the chair that caused the stub before it was even built. It’s overkill. It’s paranoia on a cosmic scale. I love it."

Godric sighed, a sound of deep contentment. "And the Weakness Paradox. That’s the crown jewel. It used to trigger when someone thought I had a weakness. Now? It doesn’t need to trigger. It’s just... ambient. The omniverse has learned. Nobody opposes me anymore. Clive, do you know how long it’s been since someone had a rebellious thought?"

I swallowed. "The records say... a googolplexian years, Your Majesty."

"That long?" Godric looked surprised. "Time flies when you’re eternal. But yes. They’ve learned. They’ve all learned. There is no point. I used to hope, you know? Back in the early days, I hoped someone would give me a challenge. I used to let them build weapons, let them gather armies. I thought, 'maybe this one will tickle.' But they never did. They just died. Or they surrendered."

He looked at me, His gaze warm, but the weight behind it was crushing. "I believe in free will, Clive. I really do. That’s why I don’t force them to love me. I let them choose. And they all choose to kneel. Not because I alter their minds, but because they see the math. They look at me, and they see the end of the equation. They realize that fighting me is like trying to drown the ocean by spitting into it. So, they stop. They become gardeners. They become butlers."

"Your tea, Your Majesty," a soft voice whispered.

It was the Butler. A being known as The Prime Logician. It had once been a collective consciousness of a trillion hyper-intelligent AIs that had calculated the Theory of Everything. Then it had met Godric. The sheer complexity of Godric’s existence had caused the Logician’s processors to achieve a state of divine madness. It had dissolved its own hive-mind to become a single, simple servant, obsessed with folding napkins perfectly, because the infinite curves of a napkin were the only thing its shattered mind could handle that wasn't infinitely recursive.

Godric took the cup. "Thank you, Prime."

The Logician bowed, its mechanical joints whirring with terror. "To serve is to exist," it whispered.

Godric took a sip, closing His eyes in pleasure. "Perfect. As always."

Then, His expression shifted. The warm uncle vanished. The Apex Predator returned. He looked down at the swirling orb on the pedestal.

"Enough nostalgia," Godric said, His voice dropping an octave. The gravity in the room doubled. "Time to send the package."

He reached out. He didn't chant. He didn't weave a spell. He simply *touched* the timeline.

I watched through my magically amplified sight. I saw the Year 999...9. I saw the infinity. I saw the power that could delete the omniverse by blinking.

And then I saw Him hold it back.

He compressed His current majesty into a single, microscopic point of intent. He filtered out the Evolved Weakness Paradox. He filtered out the Super-Gent. He filtered out the Temporal Immune System. He filtered out the concepts of power that would have instantly turned His past self into a singularity of madness.

He left only the basics. The "Weakness Paradox 1.0." The "Absolute Concealment Classic." The "Standard Amplification."

It was still enough power to make the fabric of reality scream.

"Go," Godric whispered.

The orb vanished. It shot back across the endless years, piercing the veil of time, racing toward Year -10,000.

The feedback hit us instantly. A shockwave of temporal displacement.

I felt the timeline shift. I felt the history of the universe lock into place. The Fall of Olympus. The Awakening. The Rise of Thronegate. All of it, suddenly fixed, immutable, solid.

"There," Godric said, clapping His hands together. The sound wiped out three nearby star systems. "Done. The loop is closed. My past self has his toys. Now he gets to have his fun."

He turned back to me, the warm, friendly smile returning as if He hadn't just casually re-written the foundation of causality.

"Did you see that, Clive?" He asked, beaming. "I sent him the Weakness Paradox. The original version. Do you know how cute that is? It’s like giving a child a stick and telling him it’s a sword. He’s going to spend ten thousand years thinking he’s earning his power, fighting his little battles, unaware that the stick is actually a nuke. And the best part? He thinks he’s alone."

Godric laughed, walking back to His bonsai tree. He picked up the shears.

"I wonder," He mused, snipping a nebula. "If I had sent the 'Version 2.0' Paradox... the one that triggers on *admiration* instead of *hostility*... would that have been more interesting? No, probably not. Everyone already loves me anyway. Even the ones who are terrified of me."

He looked at Xorgul, who was weeping into the soil. "Look at him. He loves me. He’s terrified, sure, but deep down, he loves that I allow him to exist."

I stood there, clutching my staff, my heart hammering against my ribs like a trapped bird. I looked at the Devourer watering the plants. I looked at the Logician folding napkins. I looked at Godric, pruning a galaxy.

I realized then that there was no horror in opposition. There was no glory in resistance. The horror was in the peace. The horror was that the war was over before it began, and the only thing left for the rest of us was to tend the garden of the monster who ate time itself.

And the most terrifying part of all? As I watched Him stand there, humming a tune from a forgotten kingdom on a dead world, I realized I loved Him too. I was terrified, I was small, I was nothing... but I loved Him.

Because in the 999...9, there was no one else left to love.
