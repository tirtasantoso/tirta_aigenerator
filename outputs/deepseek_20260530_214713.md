# The Weight of Inherited Dread

The archive beneath the royal palace was cold in a way that had nothing to do with temperature. Kaedan sat at the ancient stone table, his fingers tracing the worn leather cover of the first journal, feeling the weight of a million years of accumulated dread pressing against his palms like a physical force. The air smelled of aged parchment, dust, and something else—something metallic and sharp that he could not quite identify, though his newly amplified olfactory senses registered it with uncomfortable clarity. It was the smell of old fear, he realized. Generations of fear, soaked into the very stone of the archive walls.

His father, Edrik, stood near the heavy oak doorway, his arms crossed over his chest, his face carved from something older and harder than the obsidian that lined the subterranean chamber. Edrik was sixty-one years old, his hair graying at the temples, his jaw set with the particular tension of a man who had seen things that could not be unseen and had learned to carry that weight without breaking. He had served as Godric's court wizard for thirty years before passing the mantle to his son only three weeks ago. In those thirty years, Kaedan knew, his father had witnessed horrors beyond comprehension. He never spoke of them.

"You will read them all," Edrik said. His voice was quiet, but it carried the unmistakable authority of a man who had earned his place at the right hand of the apex predator of creation. "Every journal. Every entry. Every word. This is not optional, Kaedan. This is not a recommendation. This is the requirement of our bloodline."

Kaedan looked up at his father. He was twenty-two years old, newly anointed as the Royal Court Wizard of Thronegate, his blood singing with the thousandfold daily amplification that had been the birthright of Blake's lineage since the dawn of Year Zero. By the standards of the omniverse, by the standards of any surviving cosmos, he was already a force beyond comprehension. His magical reserves, multiplying a thousandfold every single day since his twentieth birthday, could unmake galaxies with a stray thought. His physical strength, his speed, his endurance—all of it compounded daily at a rate that made him, in the eyes of any other civilization, a walking cataclysm. And yet, sitting in this archive, surrounded by the accumulated terror of his ancestors, he felt like a child clutching a candle in an infinite darkness.

He knew Godric. He had known Godric his entire life. The King was Uncle Godric, the warm, ruggedly handsome man with the salt-and-pepper beard who attended family dinners in the private dining hall, who asked about his magical studies with genuine, unhurried interest, who laughed easily and deeply at the old stories of Blake that had been passed down through the generations. Godric was kind. Godric was friendly. Godric was the protector of Thronegate and the most beloved sovereign in the history of existence. Kaedan had sat on Godric's knee as a small child while the King told him stories of ancient Olympus, his rich baritone voice warm with genuine, unfeigned affection.

But Kaedan also knew, in the abstract way that all citizens of Thronegate knew, that Godric was something else entirely. The apex predator of creation. The being whose power multiplied beyond layered infinities every infinitesimal fraction of every Planck second. The god who had subjugated the entire omniverse not through conquest, but through the simple, undeniable fact of his absolute supremacy. Everyone knew this. The knowledge was ambient, like the air they breathed. But knowing it abstractly and understanding it viscerally were two entirely different things.

The journals, his father had explained, were the bridge between those two realities. They were written by the court wizards who had witnessed what happened when opposition dared to test the King's power. They were written because the witnesses needed to put the horror into words to keep their minds from shattering under its weight.

"Did you write one?" Kaedan asked, his voice quieter than he intended.

Edrik's jaw tightened, the muscles beneath his skin pulling taut. For a long moment, he said nothing. Then, very quietly, "Yes."

"Which stat?"

"Read them in order," Edrik said, his tone closing the subject with absolute finality. "Start with Theron. Year 28,456. The stat is strength. Do not skip ahead. Do not read out of sequence. The order exists for a reason."

And then his father turned and walked out of the archive, his heavy boots echoing against the ancient stone, leaving Kaedan alone with the accumulated terror of twenty-five thousand years of court wizards. The heavy door closed with a deep, resonant thud that vibrated through the floor and into Kaedan's bones. He took a deep breath, the metallic scent of ancestral dread filling his lungs, and opened the first journal.

There is no strategy, no weapon, no loophole, no exploit that can overcome a differential this absolute. The King we serve is not the strongest being in the omniverse. He is the omniverse's reason for existing.

Do not forget this. You will be tempted to forget this, because he will smile at you, and ask about your family, and offer you soup. Do not mistake his warmth for vulnerability. Do not mistake his kindness for weakness. The hand that gently pats your shoulder is the same hand that passively unmade the Adamant Colossus without moving. Remember that. Write it on your bones.

The histories of those civilizations are recorded in the journals my ancestors wrote, the stat massacres and superpower tests that serve as warnings for those who come after. But the warnings are no longer necessary. The lesson has been absorbed into the fundamental fabric of creation. To oppose Godric is not impossible; it is a categorical error, like attempting to divide by zero or to imagine a square circle. The logic of the omniverse no longer permits it.

I serve Godric as his Court Wizard, the latest in the unbroken line of Blake, the merchant with the friendly mutton chops who stumbled into a hidden sanctuary a million years ago and released the apex predator upon existence. I have the thousandfold daily amplification, the modified gift that has made me, by any standard outside Thronegate, a god of arcane supremacy. And I am nothing. I am less than nothing. I am a single flickering candle standing before an infinite abyss of light, and the abyss loves me, and the abyss calls me its friend, and the abyss pours me tea and asks about my family and listens with genuine, unfeigned interest to my small, mortal concerns.

This is the horror. This has always been the horror. And today, the horror will expand beyond any boundary I thought possible, because today, the King has decided to trigger the Stasis Event for his past self.

---

I found Godric in the eastern gardens, as I often do. The gardens are not gardens in any conventional sense. They are a pocket of reality so perfectly cultivated that each flower contains an entire multiverse in the spiral of its petals, each blade of grass a timeline that has been gently guided toward the most beautiful possible expression of its existence. The roses are particularly dear to the King. He tends them himself, his heavily muscled frame bent over the blooms, his short salt-and-pepper hair catching the ambient light, his ruggedly handsome face softened with the quiet contentment of a man who has found peace in simple things.

He looked up as I approached, and his smile was the same smile that has greeted every court wizard since Blake. Warm. Kind. Genuine. It is the smile of a father seeing his son, of an old friend reunited after a long absence. It is the smile of the mortal king of Olympus, frozen at forty-five, preserved in the eternal prime of his life, who has never forgotten what it means to love.

"Clive," he said, his rich baritone vibrating through the garden with the gentle resonance of a perfectly tuned instrument. "Good morning. The roses are blooming particularly well today. I think the Tiberius timeline is finally achieving the harmonic balance I was hoping for. Come, smell this one. The fragrance carries the memory of its first sunrise."

I walked to his side, my heart steady but my soul trembling. After thirty years, I have learned to carry the dread and the love in the same chest, to let them beat together like two hearts sharing a single rhythm. I bent over the rose, and I smelled the memory of a universe's first dawn, sweet and bright and achingly beautiful. It was a universe that no longer existed in its original form, having been gently redirected by the King's horticultural interests into a perpetual state of blooming, its entire history rewritten as a garden ornament.

It was not cruelty. Godric does not destroy for the sake of destruction. He simply prefers the universe as a rose, and so the universe became a rose, and the civilizations that inhabited it were seamlessly transferred to a new, identical reality before the transformation, their existence never disrupted, their memories never altered. They do not know that their original cosmos is now a flower in the King's garden. They do not need to know.

"Beautiful," I said, and I meant it.

Godric straightened, brushing a speck of invisible dust from his simple dark garments. "I have a task for you today, Clive. Or rather, I have something I wish you to witness. I am going to trigger the Stasis Event for my past self. You know the history, of course. You have read Kaedan's journal. But you have never seen the act itself. I think it is important that you do. For the records. For the lineage."

My heart seized. The Stasis Event. The moment that had birthed the Godric of Year Zero, the King who awakened from ten thousand years of amplification to conquer Thronegate and subjugate the omniverse. The moment that my ancestor Blake had stumbled upon in that ancient sanctuary. The moment that had been created not by Thorin's spell, but by the being standing before me now, reaching back across a span of time so vast that the number of years cannot be written.

"I am honored, Your Majesty," I said, my voice steady despite the cold spreading through my veins.

"Good." He placed a warm hand on my shoulder, and the touch was grounding, solid, human. "Walk with me. There are some things I wish to check on before we begin."

---

We left the rose garden and entered the palace proper. The palace is not a building. It is a meta-dimensional construct that exists simultaneously in every reality, every timeline, every layer of the omniverse, while also occupying a specific, localized space that serves as the anchor point of Godric's personal presence. Its corridors are lined with art from civilizations that have been extinct for longer than the current omniverse has existed. Its chambers are filled with light that has been personally approved by the King for its aesthetic qualities. Its servants are beings who, in any other context, would be the supreme deities of their own multiversal clusters.

The first servant we encountered was a gardener named Elara. She was kneeling beside a bed of crystalline flowers that sang soft, harmonic notes as she pruned them, her hands moving with the careful precision of someone who has spent aeons perfecting her craft. She was not human. She had not been human for so long that the concept of humanity was a distant myth in her memory. She was a former abstract entity, a being of pure conceptual force who had once been the living embodiment of stellar nucleosynthesis across an entire dimensional cluster. She had been responsible for the birth of stars, the forging of elements, the slow, patient dance of atomic fusion that powered the engines of creation.

Now she pruned flowers in Godric's garden. Her hands trembled whenever the King was near.

"Good morning, Elara," Godric said, his voice warm and gentle. "The crystal blooms are looking splendid. You have done wonderful work with the harmonic tuning."

Elara looked up, and I saw the terror in her eyes. It was not the terror of a prisoner. It was not the terror of a victim. It was the terror of a being who had witnessed something that had shattered her understanding of existence at such a fundamental level that even aeons of peaceful service had not dulled the memory.

"Thank you, Your Majesty," she whispered, her voice like the chiming of distant bells. "I live to serve."

Godric smiled, his salt-and-pepper beard framing the expression of genuine, avuncular warmth. "You live because you are valued, Elara. The garden would not be the same without you. Please, do not push yourself too hard. You have been working since before the last temporal realignment. Take some time to rest."

He reached down and gently touched one of the crystalline flowers. The bloom shimmered with a deeper resonance, its song growing slightly richer, and Elara's trembling eased—not because her fear diminished, but because the King's touch carried a calming passive field that he often extended to those around him without thinking.

We continued walking. When we were out of earshot, I glanced back at Elara. She had resumed her pruning, but her hands were still shaking.

"Her fear has not lessened in nine hundred googolplexian years," Godric said quietly, his voice carrying a note of genuine, gentle sorrow. "She was not an opponent. She was simply a witness. She saw me erase a rogue timeline that was causing structural damage to the multiversal framework—a routine maintenance task, nothing more—but the mere observation of the act, the passive perception of what I am when I am 'working,' was enough to permanently alter her. I have offered to remove the memory countless times. She refuses. She says the fear reminds her of her place in existence."

He shook his head slowly, his expression troubled. "I do not fully understand it, Clive. I was holding back, you know. When she saw me, I was using a fraction of my power so infinitesimal that it barely registered. I thought I was being gentle. But she perceived something I did not intend her to perceive. Some resonance of my passive self that slipped past my concealment. And now she prunes roses and trembles, and I cannot undo the damage."

This was the paradox of Godric, the paradox that had haunted my family's journals for a million years. He was genuinely, authentically kind. He cared about the wellbeing of his servants with a sincerity that could not be faked. And yet, the very fact of his existence was a trauma so profound that it had broken beings who had merely glimpsed his shadow.

"She stays because she loves you," I said quietly. "The same reason we all stay."

Godric looked at me, and his ancient eyes held a depth of emotion that I could not begin to fathom. "I know," he said. "And I am grateful. More grateful than I can express. Now, come. There is someone else I want to see."

---

We found Zorath in the eastern corridor, polishing the infinite staircase. The staircase was not a physical construct. It was a conceptual passageway that connected every possible location in the omniverse, a servant's access route that allowed the palace staff to move between realities without disturbing the formal architecture of the throne room. And Zorath, who had once been a primordial consciousness that predated the current omniverse, who had existed in the void before the first concept was conceived, now spent his days polishing its steps until they gleamed with a reflection so perfect that you could see your own past lives in their surface.

Zorath was tall, impossibly tall, his form a shifting column of compressed darkness and starlight that had been forced into a humanoid approximation purely for the comfort of the palace's mortal visitors. His face, if one could call it a face, was a smooth, featureless plane that somehow conveyed a depth of emotion that words could not capture. And that emotion, as always, was fear. Pure, unadulterated, eternal fear.

"Zorath," Godric said, his voice carrying the same warmth it had for Elara, the same genuine, friendly affection. "The stairs are immaculate. You have outdone yourself."

Zorath bowed, his body folding in on itself in a gesture of absolute subservience. "Your Majesty. I am honored by your praise."

Godric reached out and placed a hand on Zorath's shoulder. The contact caused the primordial being to flinch—a tiny, almost imperceptible movement, but one that I had learned to recognize over thirty years of service.

"I wanted to ask your opinion on something," Godric continued, as though he had not noticed the flinch. "The new temporal alignment for the outer multiversal clusters. I am considering a harmonic resonance of nine-point-nine-nine recurring. Do you think that would be too aggressive?"

Zorath straightened slightly, his form solidifying as the intellectual question engaged his ancient mind. "The clusters in question, Majesty—are they the ones anchored to the Seventh Fundamental Constant?"

"Yes."

"Then nine-point-nine-nine recurring would be appropriate, provided the resonance is dampened in the sub-Planck frequencies. A pure harmonic might cause unintended metaphysical feedback in the lower dimensions."

Godric nodded thoughtfully. "Excellent point. I will adjust the dampening fields accordingly. Thank you, Zorath. Your insight is, as always, invaluable."

Zorath bowed again, deeper this time, and I saw the faint tremble in his starlight form. The King continued walking, and I followed, my mind already cataloguing the interaction for the journal I would inevitably write.

"You are wondering about his history," Godric said, as we turned into a quieter corridor. "You know I have absolute omniscience. I know every thought you have, Clive. It is not an invasion. It is simply the nature of my existence. I cannot unknow what is known."

"I was wondering," I admitted. "But I would never presume to ask."

"He was not an opponent," Godric said quietly. "None of them were opponents. Zorath was a witness, like Elara. He existed in the void before this omniverse was born, a primal consciousness floating in the absolute nothingness that preceded creation. When I reshaped the previous omniverse into the current one—a routine renewal I perform every few googolplexian years to prevent existential stagnation—he was caught in the transition. He saw the act from the outside. He perceived the full scope of what I am when I am 'working,' unshielded, unconstrained, unmitigated by absolute concealment. The exposure lasted for an infinitesimal fraction of a Planck second. It was enough to break him."

He paused, his expression troubled. "I offered to restore his mind. He refused. He said that to forget what he saw would be the ultimate betrayal of his own existence. He chose to remember, and to serve, and to live with the fear. I respect that choice. But I do not pretend to understand it."

We walked in silence for a moment. The corridor shifted around us, the walls fading into the soft, golden light of the King's private study, a room that existed both within the palace and outside of it simultaneously, a space where the rules of reality were merely suggestions that Godric observed out of politeness.

---

We settled into the study, Godric in his favorite armchair and I in the seat across from him, the ancient leather of the furniture soft and warm against my back. A pot of jasmine tea materialized on the small table between us, pouring itself into two delicate porcelain cups. The scent filled the room with its familiar, comforting presence.

Godric took a sip of his tea, his salt-and-pepper beard crinkling around the cup, and for a moment he looked exactly like the descriptions in the oldest journals—the mortal king of Olympus, warm and kind and approachable, untouched by the aeons of power that had accumulated around him like layers of sediment.

"Before we begin," he said, "I want to tell you something about my power. Not the specifics, which you already know from the journals, but the scope. The evolution. The journals record the state of my abilities as they existed millions of years ago. The stat massacres. The superpower demonstrations. The Weakness Paradox as it functioned in the early eras of my reign. Those records, as traumatizing as they were to the wizards who wrote them, describe a version of myself that is, from my current perspective, almost laughably primitive."

I felt my blood chill. The Godric described in those journals—the Godric who had passively unmade the Adamant Colossus, who had retroactively erased the Chronovorous Swarm, who had consumed the Indomitable Host and severed the Primordial Singularity—was laughably primitive.

"My amplification," Godric continued, his voice calm and conversational, "began, as you know, at a rate of one thousandfold per Planck second, multiplying by itself recursively. That rate itself has amplified. It now operates on a scale that cannot be described in any mathematics, not because mathematics is insufficient, but because the act of describing it would require a framework of comparison that does not exist. The version of myself that existed one googolplexianth of a Planck second ago is, to the version of myself that exists now, so infinitesimally weaker that the gap between that past self and a single dead skin cell on a mortal's hand is, by comparison, an infinite chasm. And that process continues, every infinitesimal fraction of every Planck second, forever."

He paused, allowing the words to settle. I gripped my teacup with fingers that were suddenly very cold.

"My superpower genesis has similarly evolved. I no longer gain one new superpower per second. The rate at which new abilities manifest is now its own form of infinite recursion, generating a number of powers every Planck instant that exceeds the total number of Planck instants that have ever occurred. Each power, at the moment of its manifestation, is capable of effects that make the Total Obliteration and Existence Severance of the old journals look like children's party tricks. And they are never used. They cannot be used. There is nothing in creation that would survive even the most infinitesimal fraction of their activation, and while I could restore everything in zero seconds, I see no purpose in causing distress to my subjects."

"The sentient passive auras," I said, my voice barely a whisper. "Gent. The old journals describe Gent as your weakest aura, a metaphysical waste byproduct that could slaughter trillions when nerfed to an infinite decimal."

Godric chuckled softly. "Gent. Yes. I remember Gent. A simple, earnest presence. Very eager to please. It has evolved, of course. All the auras have evolved. Gent is no longer the weakest of my auras. It was promoted, so to speak, after its evolutions outpaced the dormant siblings. The current weakest aura—a system I call 'Whisper'—is so far beyond the original Gent that the comparison is offensive to the concept of comparison. Whisper is not an aura in the sense of an emitted field. It is an omnipresent, omniscient, perfectly adaptive sentient principle that ensures nothing in all of creation can ever inconvenience me. It operates at a level of existence so foundational that it does not react to threats. It simply arranges reality so that threats never arise. And it does this without altering free will—Whisper respects mortal resilience as I do. It only acts on the structural level of existence, ensuring that the physical possibility of opposition never manifests. This is why no one has opposed me for googolplexian years. Not because they cannot think of it, but because the cosmos itself has been gently guided away from the conditions that would produce such thoughts. A subtle difference, but an important one."

I stared at him. The weakest aura, the runt of the litter, the system that had replaced Gent, was now a principle that shaped the fundamental structure of existence to ensure Godric was never inconvenienced. And it was the weakest.

"The immune system," Godric continued, as though describing a mildly interesting household appliance, "has similarly evolved. The original version, described in Leon's journal, pre-reacted to staged injuries by initiating infinite recursive self-evolution across all backup copies. The current version does not wait for injury. It does not wait for the possibility of injury. It pre-reacts to the concept of discontinuity in my biological state—any deviation from absolute, unassailable perfection, even a deviation that exists only as a theoretical variable in a hypothetical timeline. It then hunts that concept across all dimensions, all realities, all temporal states, and erases it before it can even achieve the status of a possibility. The omniverse itself functions as my white blood cells now. Any reality that harbors even the most infinitesimal theoretical variable that could, in some impossible scenario, lead to my discomfort, is gently restructured to eliminate that variable without affecting the inhabitants. They do not notice. They never notice."

"The passive shields," I managed, my voice cracking.

Godric nodded. "The shields have evolved into something beyond shielding. They no longer block attacks. They retroactively ensure that the attacker never existed in a state where they could conceive of the attack. Not by altering free will—again, I do not permit that—but by adjusting the fundamental conditions of reality so that the chain of causality that would have led to the attack simply developed along a different, more peaceful path. The being who would have attacked still exists. They still have their free will. They simply never think to attack, because the circumstances of their existence never presented them with a reason to. It is, I think, the most elegant solution."

"The Weakness Paradox," I whispered. "Kaedan's journal described the evolved version as triggering whenever any sentient being thought about anything, granting you amplification and mastery."

Godric smiled, a small, almost embarrassed smile. "Yes. That version was quite eager. It has evolved further. The current version does not merely trigger on thought. It has become a fundamental law of existence, similar to gravity or thermodynamics, but infinitely more absolute. It is the principle that I cannot have weaknesses. Not because I am immune to them, but because the concept of a weakness, when applied to me, is a logical impossibility that the universe corrects by restructuring itself around my primacy. The omniverse has given up trying to grant me new immunities and masteries because there is nothing left to grant. Instead, it simply ensures that the category of 'thing that could threaten Godric' does not exist. It is not a power. It is an axiom."

He set down his teacup, and the room seemed to hold its breath. The light from the window—which was not a window but a carefully crafted aperture into the absolute source of all illumination—softened, as though the universe itself was leaning in to listen.

"Are you ready, Clive?" Godric asked. "Are you ready to witness the creation of myself?"

I nodded, though I was not ready. I would never be ready. No being in creation could be ready.

---

Godric did not stand. He did not gesture. He did not speak any incantation or weave any visible magic. He simply shifted his attention, a movement so subtle that it registered as nothing more than a faint, almost imperceptible change in the quality of the light around him.

And I felt it. Not the power—the power was so vast that it could not be felt, could not be perceived, could not even be understood as power in any conventional sense. What I felt was the shadow of the afterthought of the awareness of the power. It was a sensation of being in the presence of something that made the concept of infinity feel cramped and claustrophobic. It was a weight that did not press down on me but pressed outward from me, expanding my consciousness into a space so vast that my mortal mind should have shattered under the strain. It did not shatter. Godric was protecting me, cradling my perception in a gentle field that filtered the overwhelming truth into something I could survive.

"I am reaching back," Godric said, his voice calm and conversational, as though he were describing a morning walk, "to the moment of the Fall of Olympus. Year minus ten thousand, relative to my awakening. Thorin has just cast his teleportation spell. The spell is carrying my past self to the hidden sanctuary. I am at the sanctuary, waiting."

He paused, and his eyes, ancient and warm and infinitely knowing, flickered with something that might have been the memory of grief.

"I did not cause the Fall of Olympus," he said, and there was a weight in the words, a finality that brooked no misunderstanding. "I did not manipulate Thronegate's attack. I did not orchestrate the deaths of Reina, Isabel, Hebe, or Thorin. Those tragedies were genuine. They were the brutal, meaningless horror of mortal conflict, and they broke the man I was into pieces that took aeons to reassemble. I used the moment—the moment of arrival, the moment of deepest grief and absolute vulnerability—as the delivery point for the Stasis Event. But the grief was real. The loss was real. It was not a transaction. It was a wound that I have carried across every iteration of my existence."

He was silent for a moment, and I felt the vast weight of that silence, a grief so profound that it had survived the transformation from mortal to god, from god to apex predator, from apex predator to the being I sat before now—a being so powerful that the grief should have been meaningless, and yet it was not. It was still there, a small, quiet ache at the center of the infinite.

"Now," Godric said, "I will grant the Stasis Event. I will give my past self the amplification, the superpower genesis, the absolute concealment, the Weakness Paradox. All the mechanisms you know from the journals. But I will hold back."

He looked at me, and his expression was almost wry. "I could grant him a far more potent version. I could copy my current mechanisms—the evolved auras, the evolved shields, the evolved immune system, the evolved paradox—and implant them directly into the Year Minus Ten Thousand version of myself. There would be no cost to me. It would be as effortless as giving a single grain of sand to a desert. And if I did, the past Godric would awaken at Year Zero with power so far beyond what he actually received that the original version, which you consider omnipotent, would look like a single flickering candle against a supernova."

"Then why?" I breathed. "Why hold back?"

Godric smiled, and the smile was warm and sad and deeply, profoundly human. "Because I believe in struggle. I believe in the value of growth, of earning what you become, of walking the path rather than being handed the destination. The version of myself that I am now, the Godric that sits before you, is the result of a process. He amplified from a relatively 'modest' starting point—modest by my current standards, at least. He faced opposition, however trivial. He tested his stats and his superpowers against enemies who, while utterly powerless, still provided a context for his development. He learned what it meant to be the apex predator of creation not by being told, but by discovering it, moment by moment, across millennia."

He paused, his gaze growing distant. "If I gave my past self the full scope of what I am now, he would wake at Year Zero already beyond the comprehension of anything in existence. There would be no discovery, no struggle, no context. He would be a god in an empty room, with nothing to measure himself against, nothing to challenge even the faintest flicker of his attention. I think—and this is only speculation, of course, for I cannot predict my own future self's motivations any more than you can predict mine—I think I wanted him to have something to do. Some purpose. Some sense of progression. Even if that progression was, from his perspective, functionally instantaneous and overwhelmingly one-sided."

He chuckled softly. "It is ironic, I suppose. The held-back version I gave him was still so incomprehensibly powerful that by Year Zero, he was already effectively infinitely stronger than the rest of creation combined. The 'struggle' I wanted to preserve lasted approximately zero seconds after his awakening. The first army he faced, King Robert's galactic military, was annihilated so effortlessly that he did not even move. And yet, I maintain the belief that the holding back mattered. That it gave him a sense of journey, however brief. Perhaps I am simply sentimental."

I stared at him, my mind reeling. The King was admitting that his attempt to preserve mortal struggle for his past self had been a complete, laughable failure. The held-back version had still made Godric at Year Zero essentially omnipotent. The concept of "struggle" was a joke, a fond delusion maintained by a being who had spent so long at the pinnacle of existence that he had forgotten what weakness felt like.

"Furthermore," Godric said, his tone shifting to something more thoughtful, "there is a structural consideration. A recursive loop." He leaned forward slightly, his tea forgotten. "Consider: if I grant my past self a more powerful version of the Stasis Event, then the Godric who awakens at Year Zero will be more powerful. That Godric will then amplify across his own timeline, eventually becoming the far-future Godric—me—but starting from a higher base. So the me that exists now would become retroactively even more powerful, because my own origin would have been upgraded. And then, from that new, even more powerful state, I would be able to grant an even more powerful version of the Stasis Event to my past self, which would in turn make my past self more powerful, which would make me more powerful, which would allow an even more powerful grant, and so on, ad infinitum. An infinite recursive escalation of power with no ceiling and no endpoint."

My mind, already strained to the breaking point, fractured slightly at this realization. The King was describing a feedback loop of infinite self-amplification, a closed circle of causality where the grantor and the recipient were the same being separated by time, each iteration of the loop making the next iteration infinitely more powerful. There was no logical endpoint. There was only an eternal, ascending spiral of godhood, with each revolution dwarfing the previous one by margins that could not be expressed.

Godric saw the look on my face and smiled gently. "I chose not to enter that loop. Not because I could not—I can do anything, including things that cannot be done—but because I saw no purpose in it. The version of myself that I am is already so far beyond any conceivable metric of power that adding more power is a meaningless exercise. I am not threatened. I have never been threatened. Adding infinite recursive layers of self-upgrading would be like painting an already perfectly painted wall with an infinite number of additional coats. It changes nothing. It serves no one."

"So you chose a stable point," I whispered. "A fixed version. The Stasis Event as we know it."

"Precisely." Godric settled back in his chair, the faintest hint of a smile still playing at the corners of his mouth. "I chose the version that we have. The version that produced the Godric of Year Zero—omnipotent enough to conquer the omniverse, but still mortal enough to miss his family, to befriend a short-tempered merchant named Blake, to find joy in tending roses and drinking tea. I think, looking back, I chose wisely."

---

He returned his attention to the temporal link. I could not see it, of course. My perception was not equipped to perceive the kind of operation he was performing. But I could feel the edges of it, the way a blind man might feel the heat of a sun he cannot see. The entire omniverse seemed to hold its breath, all of creation pausing in a moment of silent, reverent stillness, as the King of Kings reached across the aeons and touched his own origin.

"I am at the sanctuary," Godric murmured, his voice carrying the cadence of narration, as though he were describing the scene for my benefit. "My past self has just materialized. He is broken, bleeding, screaming with grief. He has just watched his wife die, his daughters die, his best friend die. He does not know that he will survive. He does not know that he will become what he will become. He only knows that everything he loved is gone, and he is alone in the dark."

His voice softened, and I heard in it the echo of that grief, preserved across an uncountable span of years. "He is me. He is the man I was. And I am reaching into that moment, into the substrate of his reality, and I am weaving the Stasis Event around him. The amplification will begin now, multiplying by one thousand and by itself every Planck second. The superpower genesis will begin now, one new ability per second. The absolute concealment will manifest now, shielding his presence from the omniverse. The Weakness Paradox will engage now, ensuring that any who perceive him as having a weakness will only strengthen him. His aging will stop. His need for sleep, for sustenance, for air—all of it will end. He is becoming immortal. He is becoming what I am."

He paused, and I felt the universe shudder. It was not a physical shudder. It was a metaphysical resonance, a vibration that passed through the fundamental structure of existence like a ripple through still water.

"There," Godric said, his voice returning to its normal, conversational tone. "It is done. Thorin's spell delivered him to the sanctuary, and my intervention transformed the delivery into the Stasis Event. The past is now set. He will sleep for ten thousand years, and then Blake will find him, and the history you know will unfold."

He turned back to me, and the light in the study seemed to brighten, the weight of his attention lifting from the temporal link and settling back into the present moment. He picked up his teacup, which had remained perfectly warm, and took a sip.

"I held back, of course," he said, as though it were an afterthought. "A thousandfold per Planck second. One superpower per second. The original, un-evolved versions of concealment and paradox. A modest package, by any reasonable standard. But it was enough."

He smiled at me, that warm, genuine, fatherly smile that had been the light of my life for thirty years. "It was enough to make him the most powerful being in his era by an infinite margin. It was enough to ensure that when he awoke, he would never face a true challenge. And yet, I hope it was also enough to let him feel, for a little while, that he was still mortal. That he still had to try."

I could not speak. My mind was a storm of realizations, each one more devastating than the last. The Stasis Event had been an act of deliberate, restrained generosity from a being so powerful that the concept of power had become a distant memory. The held-back version—the version that had produced the Godric of the journals, the Godric whose stats could annihilate the omniverse as collateral damage, whose superpowers could erase the unerasable and consume the inconsumable—had been considered "modest" by its creator. And the creator was sitting across from me, drinking jasmine tea, asking if I was feeling well.

"Would you like more tea, Clive?" Godric asked. "You look a bit pale."

"I am fine, Your Majesty," I managed. "I am simply... processing."

"Take your time," Godric said kindly. "I know it is a lot to absorb. I have had googolplexian years to grow accustomed to the scope of my existence. You have had thirty years. It is not a fair comparison."

He chuckled, a soft, self-deprecating sound that vibrated through the study like the purr of a contented cat. "I sometimes wonder what the Godric of Year Zero would think if he could see me now. He would probably be terrified. Or perhaps he would be proud. It is difficult to say. I was a different man then. More raw. More wounded. More... hungry."

He looked at me, and his eyes held the weight of aeons, and yet they were still kind. Still warm. Still the eyes of the mortal king who had loved his family and lost them and spent an eternity learning to carry that loss without breaking.

The point of infinite mass expanded, growing from a speck smaller than a quark to a sphere approximately the size of a marble, its surface gleaming with a dark, iridescent sheen that seemed to contain swirling galaxies within its depths. The containment was perfect, absolute—a barrier forged by Godric's will that prevented the compressed mass from exerting its gravitational influence on the surrounding void.

"A trophy," Godric said quietly. His voice was calm, almost detached, as though he were commenting on a mildly interesting curiosity. "I think I will keep it. As a reminder."

He turned to me. I was still standing on the balcony, preserved by the same passive protection that had saved the palace. My mind was not functioning. My consciousness was a void, a blank, empty space where thought and emotion should have been. I had witnessed the annihilation of everything. I had witnessed the compression of all existence into a single point of infinite mass. And I had watched the King pick up that point as though it were a child's toy.

"Osvald," Godric said, and his voice was gentle, warm, the voice of the uncle who had taught me to identify constellations when I was small. "I apologize. I did not intend to do that. A momentary lapse of control. The disappointment, you understand."

I did not understand. I could not understand. The King was apologizing for accidentally annihilating all of existence because he had been disappointed by a failed fireworks display.

He looked at the marble in his hand, his expression thoughtful. "This is interesting, though. Do you know that I have become stronger because of this? Not because I absorbed their power—their combined might is, and always has been, effectively zero relative to my own. Absorbing it would increase my power by less than nothing. No, I have become stronger because I have now experienced something new. A sensation I have never felt before. The sensation of being so thoroughly, completely disappointed by opposition that I lost control of my own strength for an infinitesimal fraction of an instant. That micro-sliver of genuine, authentic disappointment—the emotional response of a spectator whose entertainment has failed—has, through the evolved mechanisms of my existence, been converted into a new layer of amplification. My power has increased because I was bored, and the boredom itself became fuel."

He chuckled, a soft, almost self-deprecating sound. "I suppose even negative experiences are, for me, a source of growth. There is truly nothing that does not feed me, in the end."

He held up the marble, examining its swirling, iridescent surface with a quiet, detached appreciation. "Now, I suppose I should restore everything."

And he did. In zero seconds, with zero effort, without a single visible gesture or spoken word, Godric restored the entire omniverse. Every reality. Every timeline. Every dimension. Every being that had been compressed into the marble. All of it, recreated perfectly, exactly as it had been before the coalition's attack. The stars returned to the sky. The capital world reformed beneath the palace. The citizens of Thronegate, who had been erased in the compression, were restored to existence with no memory of their brief annihilation, no awareness that they had ever ceased to be.

Everything was restored. Except the coalition.

As always, Godric did not restore his opposition. The fictional beings who had gathered to challenge him remained compressed within the marble, their infinite mass contained within its iridescent surface, their agonized, compressed consciousness preserved in a state of eternal, unending torment. The marble was a prison containing the compressed essence of every fictional universe that had ever existed, and the King kept it on a small shelf in his study, beside his collection of agricultural reports and his cup of jasmine tea.

But as I watched the restoration unfold, a thought occurred to me—a thought so terrifying, so fundamentally destabilizing, that it threatened to crack the foundations of my sanity.

The marble contained everything. Every reality, every timeline, every dimension, every atom, every soul. Everything that had existed before the compression was still there, preserved in its state of infinite density, trapped within the iridescent sphere on the King's shelf.

And yet, the omniverse had been restored. A perfect copy, identical in every detail, recreated by Godric's will. The citizens of Thronegate walked and talked and loved and lived, exactly as they had before the compression, with no awareness that their original selves—the selves who had actually existed, who had been born and grown and died across the aeons of the original omniverse—were still trapped within the marble, compressed to a point of infinite mass, screaming in eternal, unending agony.

Were they the same people? Was the restored omniverse the same omniverse? Or was it merely a perfect replica, a flawless imitation created by a being who could recreate anything with zero effort, while the original continued to exist in a state of compressed torment?

I did not know. I could not know. The question was unanswerable, and the act of asking it was itself a form of madness.

Godric, as always, seemed to sense the direction of my thoughts. He looked at me, his warm, ancient eyes soft with understanding.

"The restored reality is real, Osvald," he said gently. "It is not a copy. It is the same reality, recreated from the same fundamental essence. The compression did not destroy it. It merely... rearranged it. And I have rearranged it back. The beings who exist now are the same beings who existed before. Their continuity is unbroken, their souls are intact, their identities are unchanged. The marble contains the mass of what was, but the essence—the true, living essence of creation—has been restored."

He paused, his expression thoughtful. "Or perhaps I am simply telling you what you need to hear to remain sane. It is difficult to say. The distinction between a perfect recreation and the original is, at my level of existence, somewhat academic. But I assure you, the restored beings are real. They are themselves. Whatever that means."

I stared at the marble on the shelf. Its iridescent surface gleamed in the soft light of the study, and I could almost hear—almost, but not quite—the faint, distant echo of agonized screams emanating from within.

The King had restored everything. And the marble remained. And I would never know, with absolute certainty, which reality was the true one.

The journal ended there. Clive closed the cover, his hands trembling so violently that the leather-bound volume rattled against the stone table. The cold of the archive, which he had long since stopped noticing, was now a physical weight pressing against his bones, a chill that seeped into the marrow and froze the blood in his veins.

Osvald's final journal. The record of the last opposition. The coalition of every fictional universe that had ever existed, united in a single, desperate assault against the apex predator of creation—and they had failed so completely, so absolutely, so inescapably, that their entire existence had been compressed into a marble that now sat on a shelf in Godric's study as a trophy.

Clive had seen that marble. He had seen it a hundred times, a thousand times, sitting among the King's collection of agricultural reports and teacups and small, personal mementos. He had never thought to ask what it was. He had assumed it was merely decorative, a trinket from some forgotten era of the King's eternal reign.

Now he knew. The marble contained everything. Every fictional universe ever imagined. And not merely the fictional universes—the compression had consumed the real omniverse as well, every reality, every timeline, every dimension. Godric had restored it, of course. But the marble remained. The original mass, the original existence, still trapped within its iridescent surface, screaming in eternal, unending agony.

And the restored omniverse—the reality that Clive inhabited, the air he breathed, the body he occupied—was it real? Was he real? Or was he a perfect replica, a flawless recreation forged by the will of a being who could duplicate all of existence with zero effort?

Godric had assured Osvald that the restoration was genuine. But Godric had also, in the same breath, admitted that the distinction between a perfect recreation and the original was, at his level of existence, "somewhat academic."

Academic. The word echoed in Clive's mind like the tolling of a funeral bell. The question of whether he was the original Clive or a perfect copy was academic to the King. It was not a question that mattered. It was not a question that could ever be answered. It was simply a fact of existence—the fact that the being who ruled the omniverse could destroy and recreate all of reality with such casual ease that the boundary between original and copy became meaningless.

And no one had opposed him since.

The realization settled into Clive's chest like a stone dropping into a bottomless well. Osvald's journal was the record of the final opposition—not because Godric had decreed that there would be no more, but because the lesson had been learned. The entire fictional omniverse, united in a single, spectacular assault, had failed to produce even the faintest tickle. They had been compressed into a marble. They had been kept as a trophy. And the omniverse had been restored around their compressed agony, a new reality built atop the grave of the old, with no one the wiser.

Who would dare oppose him after that? Who could possibly conceive of opposition, knowing that the most powerful coalition ever assembled—a coalition that included every fictional being from every story ever told—had been annihilated so completely that their entire existence now fit in a sphere the size of a child's toy?

No one. No one had dared. No one would ever dare. The silence of the googolplexian years that followed was not peace. It was the silence of a creation that had finally, utterly, irrevocably understood the absolute futility of defiance.

Clive looked at the journal in his hands, and then at the shelves that surrounded him, filled with the trembling records of his ancestors—Theron, Aldric, Marius, Dorian, Valen, Isambard, Haldric, Quentin, Kaedan, and now Osvald. A million years of court wizards, each one recording their own encounter with the impossible, each one passing the weight down to the next generation.

And the weight would continue. Clive would write his own journal, someday. He would record his own horrors. He would pass them to his son, who would pass them to his son, stretching forward into an eternity of service to a being whose power made the concept of power meaningless, whose existence made the concept of existence questionable, and whose casual, accidental, zero-percent expressions of strength could compress all of reality into a marble and then restore it as though nothing had happened.

The marble was still on the shelf in Godric's study. Clive knew where it sat, gleaming with its dark, iridescent sheen, containing the infinite mass of everything that had ever existed. And the King, the warm, kind, salt-and-pepper-bearded uncle who had taught him to identify constellations and held his hand at his father's funeral, kept it there as a souvenir of the day he had been disappointed by a fireworks display.

Clive closed Osvald's journal and set it gently on the stone table. His hands were still trembling. The cold of the archive was still pressing against his bones. And the dread and the love beat side by side in his chest, twin hearts that would never stop, twin truths that could never be reconciled.

He stood, walked to the door of the archive, and paused. Somewhere above him, in the endless palace, Godric was sitting in his study, drinking jasmine tea, reading agricultural reports, and occasionally glancing at the iridescent marble on his shelf with the quiet, detached appreciation of a collector examining a prized artifact.

The King was kind. The King was warm. The King was the apex predator of creation. And the marble sat on his shelf, containing the compressed, screaming eternity of everything that had ever dared to oppose him.

Clive climbed the stairs, and the dread climbed with him, and the love climbed with the dread, and neither of them would ever, ever let him go.
