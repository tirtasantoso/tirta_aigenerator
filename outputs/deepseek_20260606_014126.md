"I never understood why you all write these," he said, shaking his head slowly. "I always hold back. I use effectively zero percent of my power. I am very careful. And yet every single one of you acts as though you have witnessed the end of everything."

He looked at me, his brow furrowed with genuine puzzlement. "Honestly, Kaedan, I think you are all overreacting."

I stared at him. The warm, kind, genuinely confused king of the omniverse. The being whose passive, resting strength had annihilated all of existence. The being whose speed had shattered time. The being whose perception could unmake entities by acknowledging them.

"Uncle Godric," I said, my voice hoarse, "Harlan watched your strength destroy the entire omniverse. He wrote that he did not stop shaking for a year."

Godric waved a dismissive hand. "Harlan was always dramatic. It was a fraction of a fraction of my strength. Less than zero percent. I restored everything immediately. There was no lasting harm."

"Varen watched your speed break time itself."

"I was curious. I held back. I always hold back."

"Dorian wrote that the Eternal Agony's screams are still audible in the omniversal background radiation."

Godric paused. He tilted his head, as though listening to something very distant. "Ah. Yes. I suppose they are. I had forgotten about that." He smiled at me, that warm, fatherly smile. "They did try to torture me, Kaedan. I cannot simply let that pass. But I was very restrained. I did not destroy them. I merely reflected their own purpose back at them with some amplification."

"With a googolplexian multiplication. Looped infinitely. Forever."

He shrugged, his broad shoulders rising and falling. "It seemed appropriate."

I looked down at the journals. Theron's final entry was still open, the ink dark and fresh despite the centuries that had passed since it was written. *I serve him. I will always serve him. I am grateful for the privilege. I am also more afraid than I have ever been.*

"Uncle Godric," I said slowly, "I understand now. I understand why we write these. It is not to warn future wizards. It is not to record history. It is because witnessing what you are—even a fraction of a fraction of a fraction of what you are—breaks something inside us. The writing is how we put ourselves back together."

Godric looked at me for a long moment. His expression was unreadable. Not cold, not warm—simply present, observing, knowing. His omniscience processed my words, my emotions, my fear, my loyalty, my love. He understood everything I was feeling. He had understood it before I was born.

"I am sorry," he said finally. "Truly, Kaedan. I do not wish to harm any of you. You are my family. Blake's blood. I would never intentionally cause you distress."

He placed a hand on my shoulder. The touch was warm, grounding, genuinely comforting. I leaned into it despite myself.

"I know, Uncle Godric," I said. "That is what makes it bearable. Knowing that you love us. Knowing that the horror is reserved for those who oppose you."

He nodded slowly. "Never forget that distinction, Kaedan. You are mine. My court wizard. My family. Nothing will ever harm you while I exist."

I believed him. Of course I believed him. I loved him. I would always love him.

And I was more afraid than I had ever been.

Godric squeezed my shoulder gently, then turned and walked toward the door. "Get some rest, Kaedan. We have a council meeting tomorrow. The delegates from the Vespera galaxy are arriving, and I want you by my side."

He paused at the threshold, glancing back over his shoulder. "And Kaedan? When you write your journal—and you will write one, eventually—try not to be too hard on yourself. The others always were."

He smiled again, that warm, genuine, fatherly smile, and then he was gone.

I sat alone in the archive, surrounded by the journals of my ancestors. Each one a scream preserved in ink. Each one a testament to the moment when a man understood that he served a being whose power was not merely overwhelming but definitionally infinite, whose very existence rendered opposition not futile but impossible.

I reached for a blank journal at the end of the stack. The leather was fresh, the pages empty. My journal. Waiting to be filled with the horrors I would one day witness.

I did not start writing yet. There was nothing to record. The horror had not yet manifested in front of my eyes. But I knew—with the absolute certainty of a man who had read the warnings of fifteen generations—that it would. That one day, Godric would be curious about a stat, and I would be there, and I would understand exactly what Harlan and Varen and Dorian and Malen and Theron had understood.

I would understand that victory against Godric was impossible before opposition began. That the war was lost before the first thought of rebellion formed. That the only rational response to his existence was love, and that this rationality was the most terrifying thing of all.

I closed the blank journal and placed it at the end of the row. My addition to the archive. My future trauma, waiting patiently for its moment to arrive.

The candlelight flickered once, then steadied. I sat in the silence of my ancestors' screams and waited for morning.

Because even the strongest, most brilliant, most powerful beings in creation are, from my perspective, made of wet tissue paper and desperate hope."

He sighed, a sound of genuine, weary resignation. "That is the only thing that makes me feel lonely, Kaedan. Not the power. Not the amplification. Not the endless, escalating satisfaction of growth. The fact that I can never use any of it. I am a god trapped in a world of glass figurines, and I have to hold my breath every instant, because if I exhale, everything shatters."

He stood up, brushing off his tunic. "But come. I have kept you long enough. Your father will be wondering where you are. And the council meeting is in an hour."

Kaedan stood on legs that felt like they belonged to someone else. His mind was reeling. The conversation had not gone the way he had expected. He had come seeking to comfort his uncle—to offer sympathy for the loneliness of infinite power—and instead he had discovered that infinite power was not lonely at all. It was ecstasy. It was satisfaction. It was an endless, escalating banquet of pleasure and transcendence.

And the only frustration was that the rest of creation was too weak to handle even the tiniest fraction of what Godric was capable of.

As they walked back toward the palace, Kaedan looked at Godric—at the salt-and-pepper hair, the ruggedly handsome face, the heavily muscled frame draped in simple dark garments—and he understood, with a clarity that froze his blood, that he would never look at his uncle the same way again.

Godric was not a king with immense power. Godric was not a god with infinite strength. Godric was an entity so far beyond the concept of power that the word itself had lost all meaning. He was a being whose new superpowers were born faster than mortals could blink, whose weakest aura could erase everything with less than zero effort, whose every instant was an orgasmic surge of transcendence that made all previous instants look like death.

And he was also the man who had taught Kaedan to skip stones across the palace pond.

The cognitive dissonance would never resolve. Kaedan would live with it for the rest of his life—the knowledge that the warm, loving uncle who attended family dinners and remembered birthdays was also the apex predator whose passive existence could erase the omniverse without noticing.

He would serve him. He would love him. He would write his own journal, one day, and add his scream to the archive of his ancestors.

And he would be more afraid than any being in the omniverse had ever been.
