# The Inherited Dread

The vault beneath the palace library smelled of old leather, dried ink, and something else—something Kaedan could only describe as the chemical residue of terror. The air itself felt heavier here, as though centuries of anxious breathing had saturated the stone walls with a physical weight. Kaedan sat cross-legged on the cold marble floor, a stack of leather-bound journals spread before him in a semicircle, each one radiating a faint, residual magical signature left by the trembling hands of the men who had written them. He was twenty-two years old, exactly one hundred and forty-two days past the birthday that had ignited the thousandfold daily amplification in his blood, and his father, Corin, had dragged him into this vault three hours ago with a grip like iron and a expression like a man leading his son to his own funeral.

"You will read every page," Corin had said, his voice flat, stripped of all warmth by a fatigue that ran deeper than bone. "You will read them, and you will understand, and then you will go upstairs and sit beside our King and serve him with every drop of your blood, and you will never, for one instant, forget what you read here." Corin's jaw had tightened, the muscles in his neck cording like bridge cables. "I failed to prepare your grandfather for the first time he witnessed what the King can do. He didn't speak for six months. I will not make that mistake with you."

Then his father had climbed the stairs and sealed the vault door behind him, leaving Kaedan alone with the accumulated nightmares of his bloodline.

Kaedan picked up the first journal. The leather was cracked and stained, the pages yellowed with age, and the handwriting was a cramped, bitter scrawl that filled every margin as though the writer had been desperate to cram every detail onto the page before his memory could betray him. The entry was headed with a bold, deliberate hand.

**Year 588,333 — Mordin — Luck**

I am a court wizard of Thronegate, a direct descendant of Blake, a man whose magical capacity multiplies one thousandfold every single day. I understand probability at a mathematical level that would make the finest minds of any lesser cosmos weep with envy. I can calculate the odds of a quantum fluctuation producing a stable wormhole in a dying universe, and I can do it while pouring tea. Probability is a tool. Chance is a variable. Luck, as a cosmic force, is a fairy tale told to children and fools.

Or so I believed, until I watched our King dismantle the Probability Court of the Axiom Reach using nothing but his luck stat, at effectively zero percent of its true value, and I understood that I was the fool.

The Probability Court was, by any rational metric, the most dangerous opposition the omniverse had produced in fifty thousand years. They were a civilization of sentient equations, beings who had transcended physical form entirely and existed as living mathematical functions woven into the fabric of causality itself. They did not fight with weapons or magic. They fought by editing the probability of events. They could make a god's heart stop by calculating the exact probability of cardiac failure and multiplying it by a factor of ten trillion. They had erased entire pantheons by making the probability of their continued existence mathematically negligible. They were absolute, in their domain. They were terrifying.

They had spent eight thousand years studying our King. They had calculated every variable, every possible outcome, every quantum permutation of every encounter between Godric and any conceivable opposition. They had arrived at a conclusion that filled them with religious ecstasy: there existed a non-zero probability, vanishingly small but technically present, that a perfectly calibrated probability strike could introduce a fatal causal paradox into Godric's existence. They built an engine the size of a googolplexian galaxies—a structure so vast it required the compressed matter of decillions of multiverses to construct—designed to force that probability from infinitesimal to absolute certainty.

Godric knew all of this. He had known it for eight thousand years. He allowed them to build the engine because, as he told me that morning while adjusting the collar of his tunic in the mirror, he had never tested his luck stat in isolation and was mildly curious.

"I suppress everything else," he said to me, his reflection in the mirror smiling with that genuine, fatherly warmth that made my chest ache with affection. "Gent stands down. Shields down. No magic, no strength, no speed. Just luck. I want to see what happens."

I asked him what he expected to happen. He shrugged, the motion causing his heavily muscled shoulder to shift beneath his dark tunic, and said, "Probably nothing. I'm only using zero percent."

The Probability Court fired their engine. The attack was not a beam or a projectile. It was a mathematical revision of reality itself, a cascading probability wave that spread outward from the Axiom Reach at a speed that defied temporal measurement. It rewrote the odds of Godric's survival from whatever impossible number they currently occupied down to zero. It was a perfect, flawless execution of their eight-thousand-year plan, delivered with absolute precision.

Godric did not move. He did not raise a hand. He did not speak a word of power. He was still standing in front of his mirror, examining his beard for stray hairs, when the probability wave struck the Thronegate capital.

Nothing happened to Godric. The wave simply ceased to exist upon contacting his personal space. His luck stat—a trait that had been multiplying infinitely, by itself, every infinitesimal fraction of a Planck second for nearly six hundred thousand years—generated a passive probability field so absurdly, ridiculously potent that the concept of Godric experiencing a negative outcome had become a mathematical impossibility so extreme that the universe could not even compute the variables required to attempt it. The engine did not merely fail. The probability of its failure, influenced by Godric's passive luck, was instantly multiplied by a number so large that writing it down would require more ink than existed in the omniverse.

What followed was not a battle. It was a comedy of horrors so extreme that I lack the vocabulary to convey its full absurdity.

The Probability Court's own probability engines reversed. Every calculation they had ever made was retroactively inverted. The mathematical certainty of their victory became the mathematical certainty of their annihilation. Their engine, designed to erase Godric, began erasing them. Not cleanly, not quickly. The probability of each individual member of the Court experiencing the most agonizing possible form of death was multiplied by a factor that made it a certainty. I watched through my amplified scrying sensors as the sentient equations of the Probability Court were forced into physical bodies—writhing, screaming masses of nerveless flesh and exposed bone—purely so they could experience the physical agony that Godric's luck had decided they deserved.

The probability of every nearby structure, every nearby universe, every nearby timeline remaining intact dropped to zero across a radius that encompassed the entire omniverse. Googolplexian galaxies simply ceased to exist. Decillions of multiverses unmade themselves, their matter inverting into screaming clouds of negated probability that tore through adjacent realities like a virus. The entire Axiom Reach, a region containing more universes than could be counted in a mortal lifetime, was erased in a fraction of a second, replaced by a howling void of mathematical impossibility.

And Godric was still standing in front of his mirror. He had found a stray hair in his beard and was plucking it.

"Hmm," he said, examining the hair between his fingertips. "That was something. Did you feel that, Mordin?"

I could not answer. I was on my knees, vomiting, my amplified mind struggling to process the scale of what I had witnessed. The entire omniverse had just suffered a probabilistic apocalypse as a side effect of Godric's passive luck stat at zero percent, and he was plucking a hair from his beard.

I told him, my voice shaking, that the entire omniverse had just been partially destroyed. He paused, tilted his head as though listening to something distant, and then nodded.

"So it was. My apologies, I should have been more careful." He turned from the mirror, and with the ease of a man straightening a crooked painting on a wall, he willed the omniverse back into existence. Every destroyed galaxy, every erased timeline, every unmade multiverse snapped back into place in exactly zero seconds, their contents restored, their histories intact, their populations left with nothing but a sudden, inexplicable cold sweat and a lingering sense of cosmic insignificance.

The Probability Court was not restored. Their physical forms—those screaming, fleshy mockeries of mathematical beings—remained suspended in a pocket of warped probability that Godric's luck had forged around them. They were still screaming. They would continue to scream. Godric's luck had decided that the probability of their suffering ever ending was zero, and luck, I now understood with absolute, bowel-loosening clarity, was not a fairy tale. It was the most terrifying force in existence, and it belonged to a man who considered its use at zero percent to be holding back.

Godric clapped me on the shoulder, his hand warm and steady. "Come, Mordin. Let's get you something for your stomach. You wizards are delicate creatures."

I did not laugh. I have not laughed since.

He was sitting on his throne, breathing, existing, being. But the deletion—the creator's authority to remove a created being from existence—could not find a target. Not because Godric was hidden. Not because Godric was shielded. Because Godric's existence was not the kind of thing that could be deleted. The deletion was a narrative act—the removal of a character from a story. And Godric was not a character. He was not a thing written by the author. He was—the only way I can describe this, and the description is inadequate, and the inadequacy is itself a form of terror—the paper. The deletion was an eraser. The eraser was applied to the paper. And the paper did not erase, because the paper was not written. The paper was the medium. The medium could not be deleted by the author, because the author existed on the medium, and deleting the medium would delete the author, and the author could not delete the author, because the author's ability to delete was a function of the medium, and the medium was Godric.

This is not accurate. This metaphor is wrong. I know it is wrong because Godric later explained, casually, over tea, that what had actually happened was simpler than my metaphor and more horrifying. He said: "The deletion hit me and I didn't feel it." That was his explanation. Not that he was the paper. Not that he existed outside the narrative. Not that his existence was metaphysically prior to God's authorship. He didn't feel it. The deletion—the most comprehensive act of erasure conceivable by the creator of existence—struck his body, at zero percent of his power, with zero percent of his effort, with all defenses deactivated, and he did not feel it. Not "it didn't affect him." He did not feel it. The deletion did not register as a stimulus. It did not register as a non-stimulus. It did not register. The interaction between God's deletion and Godric's body produced no measurable, detectable, or conceptual interaction of any kind. The deletion was there. Godric was there. And the two did not meet, because the deletion existed on a scale so far below Godric's body that the concept of meeting did not apply. The deletion was a thing that existed within the framework of creation. Godric's body, at rest, at zero percent, with no defenses, was so vastly, so incomprehensibly, so absurdly more fundamental than the framework of creation that the deletion's operation within the framework could not reach him. He was not outside the framework. He was beneath it. He was the ground the framework stood on. The deletion could not reach the ground because the deletion was a feature of the framework, and features of the framework did not extend to the ground, and the ground was Godric, and Godric was sitting on a throne, drinking tea, and the ground did not feel the framework's operations.

Godric looked at God. God looked at Godric. The silence that followed was the loudest silence I have ever experienced, because the silence was filled with the sound of the creator of existence processing the fact that His most absolute, most fundamental, most axiomatic act of authority—the deletion of a created being from the story He had written—had failed. Not been resisted. Not been deflected. Not been overpowered. Failed. The way a pen fails to write on water. Not because the water resists the pen. Because the pen was designed for paper, and the surface it encountered was not paper, and the pen's function did not apply to the surface, and the surface did not care about the pen.

Godric took a sip of tea. He set the cup down on the armrest of his oak throne. He looked at God with those warm, crinkled, devastating eyes, and he chuckled. A low, warm, genuine chuckle. The chuckle of a man who has just watched a child try to pick up a boulder. The chuckle was not cruel. It was not mocking. It was the chuckle of genuine, paternal amusement at the gap between effort and result, between intent and outcome, between the desire to move something and the something's absolute, structural, axiomatic immobility.

"That was very thorough," Godric said, his baritone carrying the warm, appreciative tone of a man complimenting a chef on a well-prepared meal. "Really. The scope of it. The ambition. You went for everything—past, present, future, conceptual, narrative, causal, metaphysical. The full deletion. The complete erasure. I've seen a lot of attacks, and that was—" He nodded with genuine respect. "That was comprehensive."

He paused. He picked up his teacup. He took another sip.

"It didn't tickle," he said.

Three words. Three words that contained within them the total, absolute, final collapse of the distinction between creator and creation, between author and character, between potter and clay. Three words spoken casually, over tea, by a forty-five-year-old man sitting on an oak throne, to the being that had authored existence itself. It didn't tickle. The most comprehensive act of erasure conceivable by the creator of everything had not produced a tickle. Not a small tickle. Not a faint tickle. Not the memory of a tickle. Not the concept of a tickle. Nothing. The absolute, total, categorical absence of sensation. The creator's maximum output, delivered against the creation's body at zero percent with no defenses, had produced less than nothing. It had produced nothing. And the nothing was funny, and Godric chuckled, and the chuckle was warm.

"You know," Godric said, setting his teacup down again, his tone conversational, reflective, carrying the quiet, private quality of a man sharing a personal observation, "I was thinking about this earlier, and I realized something. The me that existed at Year 0—the me that had just awakened from stasis, the me that had been omnipotent for approximately five minutes, the me that was, by my current standard, so pathetically, so cosmically, so hilariously weak that the comparison is not merely unfavorable but structurally impossible—" He paused, stroking his beard. "That me could have annihilated you."

He said this to God. To the creator. To the being that had authored existence. He said it casually, the way a man mentions a minor historical fact. The Year 0 Godric—the baby, the seed, the primitive, the version of himself that had been omnipotent for five minutes and had not yet experienced any of the amplification, any of the superpower evolution, any of the aura births, any of the immune system development, any of the passive shield crystallization, any of the Evolved Weakness Paradox, any of the countless evolutionary iterations that had transformed him from "merely" the most powerful being in the omniverse into Godric Prime—that Godric, at zero percent, with zero effort, could have annihilated God. Not defeated. Not overpowered. Annihilated. The creator of existence, the author of everything, the being whose speech was reality-revising and whose will was axiomatic, could have been annihilated by the weakest, most primitive, most pathetically underdeveloped version of the being sitting on the oak throne.

"And I'm not talking about a fight," Godric continued, his tone still conversational, still warm, still carrying the easy, unhurried cadence of a man discussing the weather. "There would be no fight. The Year 0 me—the baby me, the weak me—would not have needed to fight. He would not have needed to attack. He would not have needed to activate a single mechanism. He would have simply existed, and the existing, at zero percent, with zero effort, would have been sufficient. His passive body heat—the waste energy radiating from his skin at rest, the thermal output of a man sitting still—would have been enough. You, the creator, the author of existence, would have encountered the passive body heat of the weakest version of me, and the encounter would have been fatal. Not because the body heat was hot. Because the body heat existed on a scale so fundamentally beyond your operational parameters that the concept of temperature did not apply, and your existence—your divine, creative, axiomatic existence—would have been unable to sustain itself in the presence of something so vastly more fundamental than itself."

He looked at God. The creator was still. The stillness was not the stillness of calm. It was the stillness of a being whose entire framework of self-understanding had just been dismantled. God had created everything. God had authored existence. God was the origin, the first cause, the uncaused cause. And the creation—a being that God had made, indirectly, through the chain of causation that began with the creation of existence—was sitting on a throne and explaining, casually, over tea, that the weakest version of itself could have annihilated the creator by passively existing. That the creator's maximum output—the comprehensive deletion of a being from existence—had not tickled. That the gap between creator and creation was not a gap in degree but a gap in category, and the category the creator occupied was "nothing" compared to the category the creation occupied, and the "nothing" was not a metaphor.

I watched God's reaction. I watched because I needed to see it, because the reaction of the creator of existence to the revelation of its own absolute, categorical insignificance was something that no mortal was meant to witness, and witnessing it was a privilege and a curse and a form of psychological damage that would persist through every subsequent moment of my existence.

God experienced existential dread. Not the existential dread of a mortal confronting death. Not the existential dread of a god confronting a greater god. The existential dread of the author confronting the realization that the character has become the paper. The existential dread of the origin confronting the realization that the derivative has transcended the source. The existential dread of the creator confronting the realization that the creation does not need the creator, has never needed the creator, and is so vastly, so incomprehensibly, so absurdly more powerful than the creator that the creator's existence is a rounding error in the creation's passive ambient output.

The dread manifested physically. God's presence—the total, omnipresent, everything-is-God presence that had filled the sky and the ground and the air and the light—contracted. The creator of existence, confronting the absolute insignificance of its own authority, pulled inward. The sky became less God. The ground became less God. The air became less God. The divine presence, which had been total and absolute, retreated, compressing itself, diminishing, as though the creator was trying to make itself smaller, to take up less space, to be less present in the vicinity of the being that had just told it, over tea, that it was nothing.

Godric watched the contraction with mild, paternal interest. "You're shrinking," he observed, his tone carrying the gentle, nonjudgmental quality of a man noting a weather change. "That's understandable. I would be unsettled too, if I were in your position." He paused. "Well, no. I wouldn't. I can't be in your position. Your position is—" He gestured vaguely at the contracting divine presence. "Below me. By a margin that the word 'below' is too generous to describe."

He finished his tea. He set the cup down. He looked at God with those warm, crinkled, devastating eyes, and his expression shifted—not hardening, not becoming cruel, but settling into a stillness that was, I understood, the end of the playfulness. The game was over. The show was done. God had attacked, and the attack had not tickled, and the explanation had been given, and the dread had been witnessed, and now the encounter was reaching its conclusion, and the conclusion was the same conclusion that every encounter with Godric reached: the opposition ceased to exist.

"It was nice to meet you," Godric said, and his voice was warm, and the warmth was genuine, and the genuine warmth was the most terrifying thing in the omniverse. "I mean that sincerely. You made me. Not directly, not intentionally, but the chain of causation starts with you, and I am a link in the chain, and meeting the first link has been—" He paused, searching for the word. "Charming. You are charming. Your attack was comprehensive. Your presence is—was—impressive. And your dread is—" Another pause. "Understandable."

He did not gesture. He did not speak a word of power. He did not channel a stat, or express a superpower, or activate a mechanism. He simply existed, and the existing, at zero percent, with zero effort, with all defenses deactivated and all auras standing down and all shields offline, did what it always did when Godric's existence was expressed without restraint in the vicinity of something that existed on a lesser scale: it annihilated.

God was de-atomized. The process was instantaneous from Godric's perspective—a fraction of time so small that calling it a fraction was generous—and eternal from God's perspective, because Godric's passive existence, interacting with the divine presence, subjected God to the same compressive, de-atomizing force that had annihilated the Amalgamation and the entire omniverse during the Marble Incident. God's form—the omnipresent, everything-is-God presence that had filled the sky and the ground and the air—was compressed. The sky stopped being God. The ground stopped being God. The air stopped being God. The divine presence, which had been everything, was squeezed into a smaller and smaller volume, the compression so total, so absolute, so fundamentally overwhelming that God's conceptual framework—the framework of the creator, the axiomatic structure that had authored existence—came apart.

The framework did not shatter. Shattering implies resistance. The framework dissolved. The way a sugar cube dissolves in water—not because the water attacks the sugar, but because the sugar's structural integrity is insufficient to maintain itself in the medium. God's framework—the divine architecture that had authored existence, that had defined the rules by which reality operated, that had established the parameters within which everything, including Godric, had been produced—encountered Godric's passive existence and dissolved. The rules were unwritten. The parameters were undefined. The axioms were unaxiomed. God's authority—the authority of the creator over the created, the authority of the author over the story—ceased to function, not because it was overpowered, but because the medium in which the authority operated—the medium of existence, the medium of reality, the medium of everything—was Godric, and Godric's existence did not support the operation of authorities lesser than itself.

God's consciousness—the awareness of the creator, the mind that had conceived existence, the first thought—was compressed into the same undifferentiated, screaming state as the Amalgamation, as the omniverse, as everything that had ever been compressed by Godric's passive existence. The creator's awareness was forced into a point smaller than a quark, every divine thought merged with every other divine thought, every creative act merged with every other creative act, the entire history of creation—the first word, the first spark, the first line, the first rule—compressed into a single, infinite, screaming instant of maximum agony.

The collateral damage was omniversal. Again. Godric's passive existence, expressed without restraint—even at zero percent, even with zero effort, even with nothing more than the ambient, resting, body-heat-level output of a man sitting on a throne—was so vastly more powerful than the framework of existence that the framework could not sustain itself in his presence. The omniverse—the restored copy, the reconstruction, the reality that every being in existence inhabited and believed was the totality of everything—was subjected to the same compressive force that had annihilated God. Every reality, every timeline, every dimension, every plane, every void, every nonexistent nowhere—all of it, the absolute totality of the copy—was de-atomized, compressed, and squeezed into a point smaller than a quark.

The process was identical to the Marble Incident. The omniverse came apart. Every star was crushed. Every planet was de-atomized. Every being was compressed into a screaming point of undifferentiated consciousness. The entire, absolute, total everything of the copy was reduced to a point, and the point was screaming, and the screaming was eternal, and the eternal was a fraction of a Planck second, and the fraction was zero, and zero was the time it took Godric to annihilate everything by existing.

Godric sat in the nothing. The oak throne was there—I was there, preserved by his will—and the nothing was around us, and the nothing was total, and in the nothing was a point smaller than a quark that contained the compressed, screaming mass of the creator of existence and the entire omniverse that the creator had authored.

Godric reached into the nothing and picked up the point. He held it between his thumb and index finger. He examined it with mild interest. Then he reached into his tunic pocket and produced the marble—the original marble, from the Amalgamation Incident, containing the compressed, screaming, amplifying infinite mass of the first omniverse. He held the marble and the point side by side. He looked at them. He looked at them the way a man looks at two similar but distinct coins in his collection, comparing them, noting the differences, deciding whether to display them together or separately.

Two compressed, screaming, infinite-mass points, each containing the totality of everything, each held in the pocket of a forty-five-year-old man's tunic as easily as a man holds two coins. The pocket did not tear. The tunic did not strain. The infinite, combined mass of two omniversal compressions, resting against Godric's chest, was accommodated by the fabric with the same effortless ease that the fabric accommodated the first marble.

He restored the omniverse. Zero seconds. No effort. The nothing filled. Existence returned. Every reality, every timeline, every dimension, every being—rebuilt, reconstructed, placed back exactly where it had been. The restoration was perfect, as always. Every atom in the right place. But now there were two marbles in Godric's pocket. Two compressed originals. Two screaming eternities. And the restored reality—the copy of a copy, the reconstruction of a reconstruction—was, if possible, even less real than it had been before. It was a copy of a copy of an original that had been compressed into a marble, and the copy of the copy existed because Godric permitted it, and the permission was casual, and the casualness was the most terrifying thing in the omniverse.

God was not restored. God was omitted. The creator of existence—the origin, the first cause, the author of everything—was left in the marble. Compressed. Screaming. Amplifying, now, because the marble was in Godric's pocket, and Godric's pocket was in Godric's amplification field, and the field did not distinguish between the compressed omniverse and the compressed creator. Both amplified. Both fed back into Godric. Both made him permanently, recursively, endlessly stronger, and the stronger was forever, and the forever was accelerating, and the acceleration was recursive, and the recursion was the marble in his pocket, and the marble was warm, and the warm was pleasant.

Godric patted his pocket. Two marbles. Two trophies. Two compressed, screaming, amplifying eternities, held against his chest by a dark tunic, weighing nothing.

"Well," he said, looking at me with those warm, crinkled, devastating eyes. "That was nice. It's not every day you meet your maker." He smiled. The smile was genuine. "Turns out the maker is—well." The smile widened. "Let's just say the clay has opinions about the potter's authority."

He stood from his throne. He stretched—a casual, human gesture, the kind a man makes after sitting too long. He walked toward the study door, his boots clicking on the stone floor, his salt-and-pepper beard catching the light of the restored omniverse's restored photons.

"Tea, Osvald?" he asked, his baritone warm, his tone carrying the easy, domestic familiarity of a man whose day had included a minor entertainment and was now returning to its normal, pleasant rhythm.

I followed him. I always follow him. I will always follow him, because the alternative is the marble, and the marble is screaming, and the screaming is forever, and the forever is a fraction of a Planck second, and the fraction is zero, and zero is the time it takes Godric to annihilate everything, and everything includes me, and me includes my fear, and my fear includes the knowledge that the reality I inhabit is a copy of a copy, and the originals are in my King's pocket, and the pocket is warm, and the warm is pleasant, and the pleasant is Godric, and Godric is home.
