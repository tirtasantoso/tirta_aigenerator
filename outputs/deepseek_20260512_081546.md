The handwriting was neat, controlled, almost mechanical. And then, without preamble, the neatness shattered.

*On the forty-second day of the Season of High Sun, the King elected to test a superpower he had acquired during the long stasis but had never before expressed. He called it Eternal Severance, though he told me the name was a mortal convenience and that the power's true nature could not be captured in language. He had been aware of the approaching enemy—a gestalt consciousness called the Unity Weavers—since the first stirring of their intent, seven thousand years prior. He had allowed them to prepare, he said, because a power that severs bonds is most honestly witnessed when there are bonds to sever.*

The Unity Weavers, Alistair explained, were not a species in any comprehensible sense. They were a civilization that had evolved beyond individuality, merging their souls into a single, harmonic consciousness that spanned an entire supercluster of galaxies. They called themselves the One-Who-Is-Many, and their driving purpose was to absorb all discrete consciousness into their unity. They viewed individuality as a disease, a fracturing of the sacred whole, and the King—the most singular, the most discrete, the most terribly *individual* being in existence—represented the ultimate heresy. They did not seek to kill him. They sought to dissolve him into their collective, to unmake the self that he was.

They had constructed a weapon for this purpose: the Unity Mandala. It was not a physical object but a hyper-dimensional pattern woven from the entangled soul-threads of every being they had ever assimilated, a lattice of pure, self-replicating connection that could, in theory, bind any entity into the collective. They unleashed it upon the throne room from their hidden cosmic sanctum, the Mandala unfolding into our reality as a vast, shimmering web of golden light that smelled of ozone and community and the cloying sweetness of enforced togetherness.

Alistair wrote: *I felt it touch the edge of my mind, and for a heartbeat I wanted to join it. I wanted to lose myself in the warmth of the One-Who-Is-Many. My wards were meaningless against it; it was not an attack but an invitation, and my soul was reaching toward it before I could even form a protest.*

The King, seated on his throne with a book of Thronegate agricultural projections open on his lap, did not look up. He did not activate his magic. He did not summon Gent. He did not, Alistair specified, employ any of his infinite passive shields. He simply allowed the most infinitesimal, microscopic, functionally nonexistent fraction of the superpower Eternal Severance to manifest—a quantity so minuscule that to express it as a percentage of his total capability would be to write a decimal point followed by an infinite regression of zeroes, with no terminal digit, because the digit would be smaller than the smallest unit of mathematical expression and could never be reached.

The Unity Mandala reached his skin.

And the King's Eternal Severance severed it.

Not in the way that a blade severs a rope. There was no clean cut, no linear separation. The superpower's fundamental nature was the absolute negation of connection as a concept, and it had been amplifying every Planck second for over twelve thousand years, and by now it had evolved far beyond its original parameters. The Weakness Paradox had fed it a thousand times over, and the omniverse, in its groveling inadequacy, had granted it additional effects. When the Mandala touched Godric, the following occurred:

The web of golden light was severed from the concept of wholeness. Every thread that connected the Unity Weavers to one another—every bond of love, of shared thought, of collective identity, of memory, of history, of the very awareness that other souls existed—was violently, instantaneously, and irrevocably cut. But the severance did not stop at the Weavers. The superpower, having been given a target, expanded its definition of connection to include every bond the Weavers had ever forged, every relationship they had ever touched, and every connection that had ever been made in the realms they had assimilated. And then, because the evolved form of the power now carried a secondary effect—the severed bonds would feed the King's own metaphysical integrity—every cut thread was converted into raw, unshaped cohesion and absorbed into Godric's existence, making him more *real*, more *himself*, more indivisibly singular with every connection he erased.

The Unity Weavers experienced this severance as the sudden, total, and eternal absence of every other being they had ever known. The One-Who-Is-Many was torn into trillions of solitary, screaming fragments. Their gestalt consciousness, which had been a single vast harmony, became a storm of isolated voices, each one suddenly, horrifyingly alone. They had no bodies, for they had long since abandoned physical form, but the superpower's evolved third effect—which ensured that severed entities could never again participate in any form of connection, including connection to abstract concepts—forced them into physicality so that they could suffer their isolation in the most visceral way possible. Their soul-fragments congealed into grotesque, solitary bodies: masses of weeping tissue, blind eyes, mouths that screamed without making sound because sound required a medium to travel through, and they were no longer connected to any medium. They could not perceive each other. They could not perceive anything outside their own tormented flesh. Each Weavers' fragment was trapped in a private, silent, lightless hell of endless solitude, and the solitude was so total that they could not even perceive their own screams.

Alistair's handwriting at this point became a jagged, stabbing scrawl. *The King watched them with the mild interest of a man observing an insect under glass. He closed his agricultural report and set it aside. The Weavers' screams were silent to the ear but I could feel them in my soul—trillions of voices shrieking in infinite isolation, each one completely unaware that the others were shrieking too. They did not know that their torment was shared. That was the worst part. Their suffering was multiplied by the fact that each fragment believed itself to be the only being in existence, the only consciousness to have ever existed, the only mind in an infinite void of nothing. And the King had reduced them to this state with a fraction of a superpower that registered as exactly zero percent of his total capacity.*

The collateral damage, Alistair recorded, was catastrophic beyond measure. The severance that had torn the Unity Weavers apart propagated outward along the conceptual vectors of connection, and for a single, horrible instant, every bond in the omniverse was severed. Love. Friendship. Kinship. Alliance. Gravity itself, which is a connection between masses, momentarily failed. Electromagnetism, which is a bond between particles, flickered and died. The nuclear forces, which are the glue of atoms, came undone. Across trillions of galaxies, matter simply dissolved, no longer held together by the bonds that gave it form. Sentient beings across uncountable worlds experienced a microsecond of absolute, soul-shattering isolation as every connection they had ever known was ripped away. The psychological impact was immediate and lethal. Brains, severed from the neural connections that constituted memory and identity, collapsed into undifferentiated biomass. Hearts, severed from their electrical rhythms, stopped. Civilizations, severed from their shared histories, tore themselves apart in an instant of mad, scrambling panic before even their physical forms disintegrated.

The King, with the serene unconcern of a man brushing a stray hair from his sleeve, restored everything in exactly zero seconds. The bonds reformed. The matter coalesced. The dead rose, their memories intact, their relationships re-established, their trauma so thoroughly erased that they did not know they had ever been unmade. The Unity Weavers, however, he did not restore. He gathered their screaming, isolated fragments into a small, crystalline jar, which now sits on a shelf in his private study. Alistair wrote that he had heard the jar hum once, a subaudible vibration of eternal loneliness so profound that it made his teeth ache, and he had never entered the King's study again.

*I am a wizard of Thronegate,* Alistair concluded, *and my magical capacity multiplies a thousandfold every day. I could, with a single spell, sever the bonds of matter across a galaxy. I could unravel the connections that hold a star together. I am, by the standards of the omniverse, a god. And I know, with the absolute certainty of a man who has watched the real God work, that my greatest feat of severing is a blunt, clumsy, pathetic imitation of a power the King tested once, for a moment, and then set aside as a curiosity. He severs to know what severance feels like. I sever because it is all I can do. The gap between us is the gap between a candle and the core of every star that has ever burned, and even that comparison is an insult to the stars.*

---

I set down the silver-bound journal. My hands were shaking so badly that the pages rattled against the bone table. The image of the crystalline jar, humming with the eternal isolation of trillions, would not fade from my mind. I thought of the King's study, which I had visited often as a child, sitting on the warm rug while Uncle Godric read from ancient tomes and occasionally glanced down at me with a smile. I remembered a small, glittering jar on a high shelf, and I had once asked him what was inside. He had said, gently, "Some old guests who overstayed their welcome." I had laughed. He had laughed with me.

I forced my trembling hands to reach for the second volume. The pale, translucent membrane that bound it was warm, and it pulsed under my fingers with a slow, rhythmic beat, as though the cover itself was alive.

Theodric, Court Wizard of the Year Four Hundred and Fifty-Six Thousand, Seven Hundred and Eighty-Nine, did not write like Alistair. His script was loose, almost lyrical, and his sentences ran together in long, breathless paragraphs that read as though he were trying to expel the memory from his mind before it could take root. He began not with the enemy but with a confession.

*I have always been a man of feeling. My father said it was a weakness for a court wizard, but the King never seemed to mind. He said my heart was honest, and that honesty was rarer than power. I loved him for that. I loved him for many things. I still do. But love does not protect a man from the truth, and the truth I witnessed in that chamber has hollowed me out so completely that I sometimes wonder if I am still capable of feeling anything at all.*

The enemy, Theodric wrote, called themselves the Lamenting Ones. They were not warriors or sorcerers in the conventional sense. They were beings of pure, concentrated sorrow, entities that had evolved in a dimension where emotion was the fundamental substance of reality. Their bodies were woven from distilled grief, their voices were the sound of weeping, and their entire civilization was built upon a single, sacred imperative: to spread sorrow to all corners of creation, so that every being might understand the depth of loss that the Lamenting Ones carried within them. They were not malicious, Theodric insisted. They were missionaries. They believed that sorrow was the truest emotion, the only emotion that could bridge the gap between souls, and they sought to gift their perfect, clarifying grief to every conscious being in the omniverse.

And the King, in their eyes, was the ultimate challenge. A being who had lost everything. A being whose wife and daughters had been butchered before his eyes. A being whose best friend had died trying to save him. The Lamenting Ones looked upon Godric's history and saw a well of unprocessed grief so vast, so deep, that if it could only be unlocked, it would be the most beautiful sorrow they had ever witnessed. They believed that the King's cruelty, his mercilessness, his unforgiving nature, were simply defenses against his own pain. If they could breach those defenses and make him *feel* his loss, he would be transformed. He would join them, or dissolve into them, or simply stop his conquest and weep for eternity, which in their theology was the highest state of being.

They spent eighty thousand years composing the Dirge of Eternal Grief, a hyper-conceptual emotional weapon that contained the distilled sorrow of every death, every loss, every heartbreak that had ever occurred in their home dimension. The Dirge was not a sound. It was a direct injection of pure grief into the target's ontological core, bypassing all physical and magical defenses by operating on the fundamental axis of emotion itself. No being that felt could resist it.

The Lamenting Ones projected the Dirge directly into the throne room. Theodric described the sensation as a sudden, crushing weight on his chest, a grief so profound that he collapsed to the floor and began to sob uncontrollably, mourning things he could not name. The guards at the door wept blood. The stone of the pillars wept moisture. The very air seemed to thicken with the oppressive, suffocating presence of loss.

The King sat motionless on his throne. His face showed no reaction. The Dirge washed over him, and he did not flinch. Theodric, through his sobs, saw the King's eyes flicker with something that might have been mild curiosity. And then Godric spoke. "I have a power," he said, as calmly as a man discussing the weather, "that I have never had occasion to test. It is called Agony Sublimation. I believe the time has come."

Theodric wrote: *I will try to describe what happened next, but the words will fail. They must fail. The King allowed the most vanishingly small, virtually nonexistent fraction of the superpower to manifest—a fraction so insignificant that to compare it to the total emotional capacity of the omniverse would be to compare a single atom to an infinity of infinities, and yet that comparison would still overstate the fraction's size. The Dirge of Eternal Grief, which had been pouring into him with the accumulated sorrow of eighty millennia, simply stopped being sorrow.*

The superpower Agony Sublimation, in its original form, had been designed to convert any pain inflicted upon the King into pleasure. But over hundreds of thousands of years, amplified infinitely every Planck second and fed by the evolved Weakness Paradox, it had grown into something far more terrible. Its primary effect now was the complete and total inversion of any negative emotional energy directed at the King into a form of strengthening sustenance. The Dirge's grief did not vanish; it was metabolized. Every ounce of sorrow that the Lamenting Ones poured into Godric was instantly converted into raw, unmediated power, feeding his already infinite stats, adding infinitesimal yet permanent increments to his capacity. The Dirge, instead of breaking him, made him very slightly stronger—so slightly that the increase was undetectable, like adding a grain of sand to a beach that extended past the horizon of all existence.

But the evolved secondary effect was where the true horror lay. The superpower did not merely absorb the negative emotion; it reflected it back upon the sender, but inverted. The Lamenting Ones, who had spent eighty thousand years perfecting the art of sorrow, were suddenly and violently flooded with an emotion they had never experienced: absolute, transcendent, unbearable ecstasy. But it was not pure ecstasy. The superpower wove the ecstasy and the grief together into a self-reinforcing feedback loop, so that the more ecstasy they felt, the more grief it generated, and the more grief it generated, the more ecstasy it produced, spiraling into an infinite recursion of simultaneous pleasure and agony that their emotional architecture had no capacity to process.

The Lamenting Ones, who had existed as shimmering, mournful columns of pale blue light, began to scream. The scream was not a sound of pain, nor a sound of pleasure, but a sound that contained both in equal, impossible measure. Their light-forms twisted, contorted, bent into shapes that hurt to look upon. They began to tear at themselves, trying to rip the feedback loop out of their own essence, but the loop was not a physical thing—it was embedded in the fundamental fabric of their emotional being. They could no more escape it than a mortal could escape their own heartbeat.

Theodric's lyrical prose became jagged, desperate. *They wept and laughed simultaneously, the sounds merging into a wet, choking gargle. Their forms began to sprout physical organs—lungs to sob with, eyes to weep with, mouths to scream with—and the organs were not gentle in their manifestation. Eyes burst from their columns of light with a wet, popping sound, weeping tears of luminescent ichor. Lungs inflated inside their immaterial bodies and then collapsed, over and over, in a parody of hysterical sobbing. They begged for the feedback to stop, but their begging was itself an emotional expression, and the superpower fed on it and reflected it back, magnified. They were trapped in an eternal moment of feeling everything and feeling nothing, of being destroyed and exalted in the same instant, and the loop would never, could never, end.*

But the superpower had a tertiary effect, one that had been granted by the omniverse's overcompensation: the feedback loop was contagious. The emotional energy radiating from the Lamenting Ones propagated outward, and for a single, shattering instant, every sentient being in the omniverse experienced a whisper of that same infinite loop. Across uncountable galaxies, beings were seized by simultaneous waves of religious ecstasy and suicidal despair, laughing and weeping and clawing at their own faces as their emotional centers overloaded. Hearts burst in chests. Minds shattered into irreparable fragments. Civilizations collapsed as their populations were consumed by a plague of feeling that no biology could withstand.

The King, his expression as placid as a lake at dawn, restored the omniverse in exactly zero seconds, erasing the emotional contagion and all its effects, reviving the dead, mending the broken minds. The Lamenting Ones he did not restore. He compressed their screaming, laughing, weeping forms into a single, faint note of sound—a note that oscillated eternally between a sob and a laugh, a tiny, haunting thread of audio that he sometimes, Theodric noted, hums along to when he is working on particularly tedious administrative matters. The note can be heard faintly in the throne room, always on the edge of perception, a constant, background presence of eternal, looping agony. The King finds it relaxing.

*I have tried to write of this with a poet's detachment,* Theodric concluded, *but the poetry breaks in my hands. The King is not cruel for the sake of cruelty. He is cruel because cruelty is a tool, and the tool is used, and then set aside. The Lamenting Ones are not his enemies; they are raw material. Their suffering is not punishment; it is conversion. The superpower he tested that day was not a weapon; it was a digestive process. And I, who have amplified my own small emotional magic a thousandfold every day for sixteen years, am no more capable of understanding his power than a mayfly is capable of understanding the death of galaxies. I serve him still. I love him still. But I hear that note, always, and I know that my love is just another emotional frequency that he could, if he chose, metabolize into strength. He does not, because he loves me. That is the horror. His love is the only reason I am not a screaming note in his private melody.*

---

The translucent cover of Theodric's journal was slick now, not from any moisture the book produced, but from the sweat that had dripped from my palms. I wiped my hands on my tunic and watched the fabric darken. My heart was a frantic, uneven drum in my chest. I looked at the third volume, the one bound in the light-drinking black fabric, and I knew with the certainty of a man who has learned to recognize the abyss that the third would be the worst. The first had shown me the power to sever all connection. The second had shown me the power to convert all feeling. What remained? What aspect of existence could possibly be more fundamental than connection or emotion?

The answer, when I opened the black-bound book and read the opening lines of Court Wizard Castor, was will.

---

Castor served in the Year Seven Hundred and Eighty-Nine Thousand, Twelve. He was, by his own account, a man of few words and fewer illusions. He had served the King for forty-two years when the incident occurred, and his journal opening dispensed entirely with pleasantries.

*The King tests superpowers the way a man might test a new tool. He does not need to test them—his absolute omniscience provides him with perfect knowledge of every superpower's function, its evolution, its every possible application across infinite timelines. He tests them anyway. I have come to believe that the testing is not for him. It is for us. He wants his court wizards to see, to record, to understand. Not to warn us—there is no warning, for there is no threat he cannot unmake—but to anchor us. The terror is the anchor. The weight of it is what keeps us in our place, and our place is at his side, and he wants us there by choice. The choice is real. But the choice is also a gift he could revoke at any moment, and that is what this account is about.*

The enemy was called the Imperium of Absolute Command. They were not a coalition or a gestalt or a collective of emotional entities; they were a rigid, hierarchical empire spanning tens of thousands of galaxies, ruled by a pantheon of beings known as the Voice-Admirals. Their power was simple and terrible: when a Voice-Admiral spoke a command, the command was absolute. It bypassed the will of the listener entirely, rewriting neural pathways, magical alignment, and even conceptual self-identity to enforce obedience. A Voice-Admiral could command a sun to go cold, and the sun would obey. They could command a lover to murder their beloved, and the lover would do it with a smile, their will overwritten so completely that they believed the act was their own desire.

The Imperium had grown fat on conquest. They had never encountered a being who could resist their voice. When Thronegate's expansion reached their borders and the customary offer of surrender was delivered, the Voice-Admirals were genuinely offended. They believed their authority was the supreme law of the omniverse, and they intended to prove it. They did not send fleets. They sent a single Voice-Admiral—the eldest, the most powerful, the one whose commands had silenced galaxies—to the throne room itself. His name, Castor noted, translated roughly to "The Syllable That Ends All Argument." He was a towering figure of obsidian and brass, his throat a resonating chamber that vibrated with the accumulated obedience of trillions of conquered souls. He stood before the throne, opened his reverberating throat, and spoke a single, absolute command:

"Cease."

The command was not directed at a specific aspect of the King. It was directed at the King's entire existence—his motion, his thought, his will, his power, his very presence in reality. The command, in the Imperium's theology, was a perfect, self-executing law. To hear it was to obey it. There was no gap between the word and its effect.

The King did not cease. He remained seated, one leg crossed over the other, his hands folded loosely in his lap. He looked at the Voice-Admiral with an expression that Castor described as "patient." And then he spoke, not to the Admiral, but to Castor, who stood trembling at his side.

"I have a superpower called Authority Negation," the King said. "I acquired it during the stasis, but its evolved form is relatively recent. I have been curious to see it in practice. This seems an ideal opportunity."

Castor wrote: *The King allowed an infinitesimal fraction of Authority Negation to manifest—a fraction so small that I lack the mathematical language to express it, a fraction that registered as exactly zero percent of his total ability, a fraction that, if it were a physical distance, would be smaller than the smallest unit of space, a distance so negligible that it could never be traversed because traversal implies a starting point and an endpoint, and the fraction was a starting point without an endpoint, an endpoint without a starting point, a null-value that was somehow still present. The fraction touched the Voice-Admiral's command, and the command was negated.*

But negation, Castor emphasized, was the wrong word. The superpower did not simply cancel the command. In its evolved form, Authority Negation had acquired three additional effects, each layered upon the original. The first effect: any command or authority directed at the King was not merely nullified; it was appropriated. The authority that the Voice-Admiral had projected was torn from him, seized by the superpower, and transferred to the King, adding to his already infinite dominion. The Voice-Admiral, who had been the absolute master of his reality, was suddenly and forcefully made aware that his authority had been a loan, and the loan had been called in.

The second effect: the superpower did not stop at appropriating the authority of the attacker. It traced the authority back to its source, following the chains of command down through the Imperium's entire hierarchy, and then outward to every being who had ever accepted a command from the Imperium, and then further outward to every being who had ever *conceived* of obedience as a concept. The Voice-Admiral's single, failed command became the vector for a total, omniversal audit of authority, and in the space of a Planck interval—or perhaps less, for Planck intervals were too large to measure the speed of the superpower's propagation—every single entity in the Imperium's domain had their will rewritten. They did not become loyal to the King. They became incapable of disobedience to him, which is a different and far more terrible thing. Their wills were not broken; they were re-framed, so that obedience to Godric was the fundamental axiom of their existence, and any thought of rebellion was a logical impossibility, like a triangle trying to have four sides.

The third effect: now that the superpower had been activated and its evolved form had fully expressed, it permanently altered the King's relationship to command. From that moment forward, Castor wrote, *no command given by any being in the omniverse can take effect unless the King permits it. Every order, every law, every whispered suggestion, every internal resolution of the self—all are contingent on his passive, unspoken assent. He does not micromanage this. He does not need to. The superpower simply exists as a background condition of reality, a new fundamental law, and the law states that all authority flows from him and returns to him. He allows free will to exist because he values it, and because he grants permission for choices to be made. But he could, at any moment, withdraw that permission. The leash is infinite. The leash is also invisible. We only feel it when we consider biting the hand that holds it, and we do not consider that, because the leash ensures we do not consider it. The horror, Castor, is that you do not know if your loyalty is genuine or if it is the leash. I do not know. I cannot know. And yet I love him, and the love feels genuine, and I choose to believe it is. That is the gift he gives us. The gift of believing we are free.*

The Voice-Admiral, in the throne room, experienced the full weight of the superpower's immediate application. His command to cease had been appropriated, inverted, and returned. The King, without moving, without speaking, without exerting even the most microscopic trace of effort, imposed upon the Voice-Admiral a new command: "Obey."

The Voice-Admiral obeyed. But the obedience was not the smooth, frictionless thing he had imposed on others. The superpower's evolved cruelty ensured that the target's original will was preserved, intact and screaming, within the cage of obedience. The Voice-Admiral found himself fully aware, fully in possession of his old desires, his old arrogance, his old burning hatred of the King—and utterly, helplessly compelled to kneel. He knelt. He began to speak a hymn of praise to the King, a hymn he had never learned, that his vocal cords produced without his consent. His eyes, wide with horror, wept tears of brass and blood. His mouth smiled an ecstatic smile. Inside, Castor knew, he was shrieking. The dissonance between his compelled adoration and his true, undiminished hatred was a torture that would never end, because the superpower would never release him.

The Imperium's domain, trillions of worlds, was rewritten in the same instant. But the collateral damage, as with all of the King's tests, spread beyond the target. The superpower's audit of authority propagated across the entire omniverse, and for a single, immeasurable moment, every being in existence felt their will still. They did not lose consciousness. They lost agency. The internal voice that says "I choose" simply fell silent. Birds did not fly. Hearts did not beat. Galaxies did not rotate. The entire omniverse became a frozen tableau, a perfect, motionless diorama of obedience, waiting for a command that never came.

The King restored the omniverse in exactly zero seconds, returning free will to every being, restarting every heart, re-igniting every thought. No one remembered the freeze. No one, except Castor, who had been standing in the presence of the superpower's epicenter and whose memory could not be wiped without also wiping the entire incident. He remembered. He remembered the silence of a cosmos without will, and he wrote that the silence was the most peaceful thing he had ever felt, which was why it was the most terrible.

The Voice-Admiral remained. He kneels now in the throne room's antechamber, a permanent fixture, his mouth forever moving in a whispered hymn of praise, his eyes forever streaming the tears of the hatred he cannot act upon. The King sometimes pauses by him, Castor noted, and pats him on the head, as one would pat a loyal hound. The Voice-Admiral's hatred intensifies each time. The hymn grows louder. The tears flow faster. Nothing else changes.

*I am Castor, and I am the leash's willing wearer. I have served for forty-two years, and I will serve until I die, and I will die loving the hand that holds the leash. The King is not a tyrant. A tyrant imposes his will through fear. The King imposes nothing. He simply is, and his being is the condition under which all other will operates. I choose to serve. I believe I choose. The belief, like the choice, is a gift. I accept it with gratitude. And I tremble, softly, in the stillness of the night, when I remember the silence of the frozen cosmos and the peace it brought. The peace was not his command. It was the absence of all command but his. It was the pure, distilled essence of his supremacy. And I wanted it to last forever. That is the true horror, Kaedan, for whoever reads this. Not that the King can override your will. But that when he does, even for an instant, you will thank him for it.*

---

I closed Castor's journal. The black binding drank the light of the arcane flame, and the flame itself seemed dimmer than before. The silence of the vault was no longer oppressive; it was a reprieve, a small, hollow space in which I could still believe that my thoughts were my own. I sat for a long time, staring at the three volumes, the silver and the translucent and the black. They were not warnings. They were biographies of the abyss. Each wizard had written not to prepare me, but to pass on the weight they carried, so that I would not be crushed by it when I finally saw it for myself. The weight was love and terror in equal measure. The weight was the knowledge that every superpower the King had ever gained—every single one, one per second, for a million years—had evolved into something with effects as profound and as unfair as these. He had never used most of them. He might never use them. The leash ensured that no one would ever give him cause. And yet they existed. The abyss was not a single, gaping hole; it was a sky full of them, each one a superpower, each one waiting, each one amplifying beyond infinity every fraction of a Planck second, forever.

I stood. My legs held. I walked to the adamantine door and pressed my palm to the release. It ground open, and the light of the palace above washed over me, warm and golden and perfectly normal. I could hear, faintly, the distant hum of servants and the soft, reassuring murmur of the ward matrices. The sounds were safe. I clung to their normalcy as I climbed the long, winding stairs to the throne room.

Godric was there, exactly where he had been before my descent, as though no time had passed at all. He turned as I entered, and his ruggedly handsome face broke into the warm, genuine smile he reserved for my bloodline. "Kaedan," he said, and his rich baritone filled the room with affection. "You've been in the vault quite a while. I trust your reading was... informative?"

I looked at the man who had severed connections, sublimated sorrow, and overwritten will, all without effort, all as a casual test of abilities he would likely never need. I looked at the man who carried in his pocket a coin of infinite misfortune, who kept a jar of screaming isolation on his shelf, who hummed along with an eternal note of joy-agony when paperwork bored him. His warmth was real. His love for my family was real. The leash, if it existed, was indistinguishable from the love. And I found, when I searched my own heart, that I did not care about the difference.

"Informative," I said, my voice steady. "Yes, Uncle Godric. Very informative."

He gestured to a chair beside the throne, the chair that had been my father's, and my grandfather's, and Blake's before them. "Good. Then sit. I was just about to review the latest agricultural reports from the Vega sector. Thrilling material. I could use your opinion on the weather-control ordinances."

I sat. The dread was a cold, solid thing in my chest, and I knew it would never leave. But the warmth was there too, real and reassuring, and as I took the proffered report and began to read, I understood something that no journal could have taught me. The horror was the price. The love was the reward. And I, Kaedan, the latest in an unbroken line of court wizards, was exactly where I was meant to be: at the right hand of the apex predator, grateful for the leash I could not feel, terrified of the power I could not fathom, and perfectly, absolutely certain that no force in all of creation would ever, could ever, stand against him.
