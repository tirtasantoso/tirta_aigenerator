**The Journal of Morvyn, Year 80,000 – Strength**

**The Journal of Taric, Year 350,000 – Durability**

*I can’t stop the shaking. It’s been three days—no, wait, four—since the Phantoms came, and my quill still fights the parchment. I’m Taric, court wizard in the year 350,000, and I’m writing this because if I don’t get these images out of my head, I will claw my own eyes out with my bare fingers. The Erasure Phantoms were not creatures in any sense the omniverse understands. **The Journal of Lirion, Year 600,000 – Senses**

*To the future custodian of this archive: I, Lirion of the Two-Hundredth Descendancy, record the following incident with as much dispassionate accuracy as my shattered mental state permits. In the year 600,000, Godric expressed a transient curiosity regarding His passive sensory capabilities, given that His absolute omniscience renders them functionally redundant. For the purposes of empirical observation, He temporarily and voluntarily deactivated His omniscience, desiring to witness how His baseline sense stat—which, I must remind you, multiplies infinitely every Planck second just as His strength does—would handle a threat specifically evolved to evade all conceivable forms of perception.*

*The threat was the Silent Ones, an ancient race that had evolved entirely within the quantum blind spots of existence. They were the ultimate predators of the unobserved: they had no mass, no energy signature, no psychic presence, no magical aura, and they existed only in a state of superpositional non-perception. To observe them was to destroy the observation, for they could not be perceived. Over the course of a million years, they had crafted the Causality Dagger, a weapon that could retroactively delete a target from the timeline, but only if the target never perceived the attack in any frame of reference. The dagger was, by definition, undetectable.*

*Godric sat in the grand astral observatory, His hands resting loosely on the arms of His chair. His eyes were closed. He instructed me to observe the interaction. The Silent Ones, I presume, maneuvered to an optimal attack vector. They struck. And at that exact Planck instant, Godric’s passive sensory field simply… encompassed them. He did not open His eyes. He did not turn His head. He did not even furrow His brow in concentration. His senses, operating on an entirely different order of reality than anything we can conceive, effortlessly perceived them. The act of that perception was not passive reception; it was an active declaration of reality. When His senses ‘touched’ the Silent Ones, they were violently subjected to the forced transition from a state of non-perception to absolute, unavoidable observation.*

*The effects were immediate and catastrophic for the Silent Ones. **The Journal of Fenris, Year 800,000 – Luck**

*In the old tongue of my grandmother’s forgotten world, there was a word: alea. It meant dice, chance, the fickle favor of unseen gods. How quaint that seems now, how utterly, pathetically naive. I am Fenris, court wizard for forty years, and I have learned that there is no such thing as chance. There is no probability, no luck, no statistical uncertainty. There is only Godric’s whim, and any apparent randomness is merely His disinterest in the outcome. But when you actively try to turn probability against Him—ah, then you learn what luck truly means.*

*The Probability Architects were a collective of sentient, hyper-intelligent crystal mathematicians from a universe where mathematics was the sole fundamental force. Their entire consciousness was a running, infinite calculation of all possible futures. They had observed the omniverse, charted the rise of the Godric Reign, and concluded, with flawless mathematical certainty, that Godric’s continued existence was a one-in-an-infinity-layered-infinity statistical aberration—an error in the cosmic algorithm that probability itself, as a self-correcting mechanism, would inevitably rectify. They constructed the Certainty Loom, a reality-editing device that could isolate a single quantum outcome and forcibly collapse the wave function of the entire omniverse to achieve it. They set the outcome to a singular, simple event: “Godric’s heart stops.” The probability became absolute. The waveform of all existence aligned to this single, predetermined result, and they activated the Loom with the calm confidence of mathematicians who have checked their proofs a billion times.*

*Godric was at a formal banquet in the Hall of Eternity, celebrating the seventy-thousandth anniversary of the subjugation of the Luminara Expanse. He was laughing at a mildly inappropriate jest made by my youngest son, who was serving as an apprentice scribe. He was holding a goblet of aged star-wine. The Loom fired. He did not pause in His laughter. He did not even register the event, because His luck did not need to inform Him. His luck is not a passive field like a subtle blessing; it is an active, ravenous, and horrifyingly predatory force that autonomously guards His existence.*

*The moment the Loom tried to force a particular probability, His luck engaged. It did not merely revert the probability. It inverted it with a vengeance that shattered the very concept of favorable odds for anything aligned against Him. The Architects’ bodies, those perfect, eternal crystal lattices, became the epicenter of an infinite cascade of literal, physical misfortune. Their flawless structure spontaneously developed biological terrestrial cancer cells—aggressive, fast-multiplying teratomas that erupted from their facets as pulsating, veined tumors weeping a thick, yellow, gangrenous pus. Their minds, still running their probability equations, encountered a fatal divide-by-zero error: the impossibility of their failure generated a feed-back loop that literally exploded their consciousnesses, but in a way that kept them aware, screaming inside their own shattering psyches. Their crystalline substance began to phase-shift into a corrosive, self-replicating acid that melted them from the inside, turning their intricate internal geometries into a slurry of dissolved mineral and organic rot. *But the Architects’ suffering was merely the center. The wave of inverted luck swept the omniverse like a shockwave of pure, malicious misfortune. In a spiral galaxy controlled by a peaceful federation, the fundamental probability of a heart attack became a solid one hundred percent: every single member of every single species—man, woman, child, infant—felt their cardiac muscle tear itself apart simultaneously, their final moments a panoramic view of their own luck turning their bodies into execution chambers, blood frothing from their lips. On another world, every pedestrian step had a one hundred percent chance of stepping onto a spontaneously generated, venom-soaked spike that would inject a paralytic while the victim remained perfectly conscious, able to feel themselves rotting from the wound outward. The vacuum of space itself seemed to develop a malicious intent: navigation computers failed in the most catastrophically physical ways, guiding ships into black holes that suddenly shifted position, not killing the occupants instantly but keeping them on the event horizon where time dilation stretched their screams into millennial-length dirges. The omniverse became a sadistic casino where every possible roll of the dice came up agony, and the house always won, because the house was Godric’s luck.*

*Godric took a bite of His pastry. He chewed, swallowed, and licked a crumb of sugar from His lip. He looked down the table at me, and His expression was mildly irritated. “How impolite,” He said, referring not to the Architects but to the timing. “They interrupted my dessert.” With exactly the same effort one might use to wave away a fly, He restored the omniverse to its pre-Loom state. The decillions died with their own thoughts bursting from their eye sockets in a spray of neural slurry and calcified idea-stone. The psychic death-scream was a song that made the stars themselves wobble in their orbits.*

*Godric completed His explanation of the knight’s gambit. He moved His piece, then looked up at the tumorous mass now filling half the solar, and His expression shifted into one of mild, apologetic chagrin. “I may have been distracted,” He said, in that rich, warm baritone. Then, in zero seconds, without the faintest effort, He restored the billion multiverses. The dead reappeared, their skulls intact, their thoughts clean. The tumors vanished. The Tyrosians, however, He left in the Concept Sea, their eternal, cancered bodies still screaming as their own minds are perpetually consumed and regenerated to fuel His ever-growing vitality. They are not dead. They are a farm.*

*I excused myself and vomited into a corner. The King patted my back gently, with genuine concern, and asked if I needed a glass of water. I love Him. I will always love Him. And I will never, for a single instant, forget that my own thoughts are permitted to exist only because He has not yet decided they would make better fuel.*

---

I set Orin’s journal down and sat in the silent dark, the three tomes glowing faintly before me. The violet mana in my veins was completely still, as if it too were holding its breath. I had read of Speed that shattered dimensions, of Durability that refused to yield, of Charisma that made the emotionless offer their own hearts. But these superpowers were something else. They were not mere augmentations of a stat. They were entire, self-contained apocalypses, each one layered with recursive, sadistically intelligent cruelties that the Weakness Paradox had woven into their fabric over millions of years. And Godric possessed forty-eight million years’ worth of them. Every second of the Stasis had birthed a new one. Every second since His awakening had done the same.

I thought of the Still Ones, boiling and dying and resurrecting forever, their torment a permanent, infinitesimal trickle of power into His bottomless reservoir. I thought of the Deific Unbound, absorbed into the weeping walls of the east wing, their culture and children immortalized as screaming masonry. I thought of the Thought-Lords, cancered eternally in the Concept Sea, their every idea harvested to fuel the vitality of a god who required no sustenance.

I was His court wizard. I was of the bloodline He loved. He had taught me chess, ruffled my hair, asked with genuine warmth about my studies. And He could, with a fraction of a fraction of a nonexistent fraction of any one of these powers, make me part of an eternal monument of suffering without a second thought if I ever, for a single instant, turned against Him.

The dread was an eternal, frozen sun in my chest. The love was the frozen sun’s only warmth. Both were real, both were absolute, and both were the foundation of my service.

I rose, gathered the three journals, and carried them back up the long, spiral stair into the light of the throne room. Godric was seated upon the dais, His ruggedly handsome face lifted toward the amber glow of the sconces, His salt-and-pepper beard catching the light. He saw me and smiled.

“There you are, Kaedan. More reading? Your father is relentless. Come, sit. Tell me what you have learned.”

I took my seat at His right hand, the dread and the love a perfect, steady balance within me. “I have learned, Uncle Godric, that there is no limit to what You are, and no end to what I owe You.”

He nodded, pleased, and returned His gaze to the light. And I served, as my ancestors had served, knowing that the vaults beneath us were still not the deepest, and that somewhere below, in a darkness that pressed like a grave, there were more journals, waiting.
