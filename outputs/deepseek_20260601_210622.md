Or was I a copy, a perfect recreation conjured into existence by a King who had casually destroyed the original and then decided, on a whim, to bring it back? Did it matter? If the copy was perfect—if every atom, every quantum state, every moment of history was identical to the original—then was there any meaningful difference between the original and the restoration? And if there was no meaningful difference, then what did "real" even mean?

The marble clinked softly in my imagination, and I understood. The marble was the answer. The marble was the proof. The original omniverse—the omniverse that had existed before Godric's accidental strength annihilated it—was compressed within that small, obsidian sphere. It still existed. It had not been destroyed. It had been preserved, in a state of infinite compression, as a trophy. And the omniverse I inhabited—the omniverse I had always inhabited, the omniverse I had known my entire life—was a restoration. A copy. A perfect, indistinguishable duplicate that Godric had conjured into existence because the original had been reduced to a souvenir.

And if he could do it once, he could do it again. At any moment. For any reason. Or for no reason at all. The omniverse existed because he willed it to exist. The omniverse was real because he decided it was real. And if he ever decided otherwise—if he ever grew bored, or disappointed, or simply curious about what would happen—the omniverse would cease to exist, and a new omniverse would take its place, and no one would ever know the difference.

I thought about the beings who served in the palace. Gorthal the Unmaker, whose original self had been annihilated in that ancient moment of accidental strength, who was now a perfect copy tending perfect copies of crystalline roses. Lady Seraphine, whose original tears had been compressed into a point smaller than a quark, who was now a perfect copy weeping perfect copies of ecstatic gratitude. Vorlak the Devourer, whose original hunger had been unmade and replaced, who was now a perfect copy wheeling a perfect copy of a compost cart. All of them copies. All of them restored. All of them completely unaware that their original selves had been reduced to a marble in the King's pocket.

And I—Clive, Court Wizard of Thronegate, descendant of Blake—was I a copy? Had my original self been annihilated in that ancient moment of disappointment? Had I been restored, perfectly, identically, seamlessly, with no memory of my own destruction? Did it matter?

I sat in the dark reading room, the weight of Osvald's journal pressing down upon my consciousness like a mountain of infinite density, and I realized that the question had no answer. Or rather, the answer was yes, and also no, and also both, and also neither. The distinction between original and copy was meaningless in the presence of a being who could destroy and restore the entire omniverse with zero effort. The omniverse was real because Godric said it was real. I was real because Godric said I was real. And if he ever said otherwise, I would not be real anymore, and I would not even know that I had ceased to be.

The last opposition had been defeated. No one had dared to oppose Godric in all the eons since. No one ever would. Because the marble was a reminder, and the reminder was absolute. The King did not merely defeat his enemies. He erased them so completely that even the memory of their existence was compressed into a point smaller than a quark. And if he restored the omniverse after their annihilation, the restored omniverse was a copy, and the enemies were not in it.

I rose from my chair and walked to the window of the reading room. The three moons of the capital world were rising in their synchronized, mathematically perfect orbits. The crystalline roses in the eastern courtyard were glowing softly in the silver moonlight. The rosemary bushes in the herb garden were emitting their faint, golden luminescence. The palace was quiet, peaceful, orderly. The omniverse was exactly as it had always been. Exactly as it would always be.

And somewhere in the pocket of a simple dark tunic, a small obsidian marble contained the compressed infinite mass of the original omniverse, and the heroes of the Final Narrative were still screaming, and the original versions of everyone I had ever known and loved were trapped in an eternal moment of maximum agony, and the King who had done all of this was sitting on his throne, sipping his jasmine tea, humming a soft, melodic tune.

The tea was still warm. It was always warm. And I was real because he said I was real. And that was the most terrifying thought of all.
