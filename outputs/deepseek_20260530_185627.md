# The Weight of Knowing

The library beneath the eastern spire of Thronegate's imperial palace smelled of aged parchment, binding leather, and the faint, lingering ozone of ancient preservation wards. Kaedan descended the spiral staircase slowly, his boots echoing against the obsidian steps, his heart already beating a rhythm of quiet dread against his ribs. He was twenty-two years old, newly appointed as Royal Court Wizard following his father's retirement, and tonight marked the beginning of his true education. His father, a stern man named Jonas who had served Godric for sixty-three years, had stood in the doorway of Kaedan's chambers an hour earlier and delivered the instruction with the gravity of a death sentence.

"You will go to the library. The eastern spire, the lowest vault. You will find the journals. You will read them. All of them. Do not skip. Do not skim. When you are finished, you will understand what it means to serve the King."

Kaedan had asked why. His father's eyes, usually hardened by decades of witnessing cosmic horror, had softened into something infinitely worse: pity.

"Because you cannot serve what you do not understand. And because every court wizard before you, stretching back to Blake himself, has done the same. The journals are not punishment, son. They are survival."

The vault door loomed before him now, a massive circular slab of black iron inlaid with silver sigils that pulsed with a faint, violet luminescence. Kaedan pressed his palm against the center seal, and the ancient magic recognized his bloodline instantly. The door groaned, shuddered, and rolled aside, revealing a chamber that stretched far deeper than the tower's exterior dimensions should have allowed. Rows upon rows of magically preserved bookshelves extended into the dim, amber-lit darkness, each shelf burdened with thick, leather-bound volumes. The air was cold and utterly still, as though the room itself was holding its breath.

Kaedan stepped inside. The door rolled shut behind him with a resonant, final thud.

He walked slowly down the central aisle, his fingers brushing the spines of the journals as he passed. Each spine bore a year, a name, and sometimes a single word that made his stomach clench: *Strength. Speed. Endurance. Perception. Charisma.* The names were all male, all descendants of Blake, all men who had stood at Godric's side and witnessed something that had driven them to write. Kaedan stopped at the oldest volume, its leather cracked and faded. The spine read: **Year 15,673 — Harlan — Strength.** He pulled it gently from the shelf, settled into the heavy oak chair positioned at the reading table, and began.

Do you know what I remember most about the old oppositions? Not the fighting—there was never any real fighting. The *presentation*. The Final Dawn's temporal ninjas were quite dramatic, in their way. The Weaver-Choir's geometric anti-magic was visually striking. Even the Rotting Infinity had a certain grim aesthetic." He paused, his ancient eyes distant. "It has been so long since anyone has tried. I wonder if they have forgotten how to be creative."

I did not understand, then, what He was truly saying. I understand now. Oh, gods of the forgotten void, I understand now.

The opposition, when it finally came, was unlike anything recorded in any journal before it. It was not a single civilization, a single pantheon, a single abstract entity. It was *all of them*. Every fictional universe that had ever been conceived, every story ever told, every character ever imagined across the infinite spectrum of creation. They came together—heroes and villains, gods and monsters, protagonists and antagonists—united by a single, desperate conviction: that they could succeed where every real civilization had failed, because they were not bound by reality. They were stories, and stories, they believed, could not be defeated.

I stood at Godric's side on the great observation platform above the capital, watching the armada assemble in the void beyond our reality. The sheer, staggering variety of it nearly overwhelmed my amplified perception.

I saw a figure in blue and red, a cape billowing behind him, his eyes blazing with the heat of a trillion suns. Kryptonian. The last son of a dead world, whose strength was legend across countless universes. Beside him hovered a woman forged from clay and divine breath, her golden lasso gleaming with the light of absolute truth. They were flanked by beings of living lightning, by armored warriors wielding emerald willpower, by speedsters who had outrun death itself.

I saw a warrior with hair of spiked gold, his body wreathed in an aura that had transcended mortal limits a hundred times over, his fists capable of shattering dimensions with a single punch. Beside him stood a prince of a proud, dying race, his violet skin marked with the grief of worlds. Behind them, an army of fighters—cyborgs, demons, androids, creatures of pure ki—stretched across the void like a sea of infinite power.

I saw a Time Lord, ancient and weary, his blue box humming with the energy of the vortex, his eyes carrying the weight of a billion lost companions. He had faced Daleks and Cybermen and the Devil himself, and his cleverness was a weapon that had rewritten the laws of history. Beside him stood a being of pure scarlet horror, a king of chaos whose very name was a wound in the fabric of reality. The SCP entities had come—the Lizard, the Statue, the Hard-to-Destroy Reptile—their anomalous properties woven into the coalition's defensive matrix.

I saw the Outer Gods, the Great Old Ones, the entities that dreamed reality into being—Cthulhu's massive form blotting out the light of distant galaxies, Nyarlathotep's thousand forms shifting and writhing with mad delight. I saw the Eternal Ones, the Celestials, the Beyonders, the beings who had held infinity in their palms. I saw a man in a suit of crimson and gold, a mortal genius whose armor had evolved to house the power of a god. I saw a child with a lightning scar, a wizard who had defied death itself, standing alongside a gray-bearded sage who had walked the realms of Middle-earth.

I saw a plumber in red overalls who had jumped across countless kingdoms, a blue hedgehog who had run faster than time, a bounty hunter in powered armor who had faced the horrors of alien worlds. I saw a pale, silent knight who had pledged himself to the protection of a single city, and a clown-faced harlequin whose madness had shattered the minds of gods. I saw the chosen undead, the slayer of demons, the tarnished warrior who had faced the Elden Ring. I saw the Dragonborn, the Witcher, the Commander of the Normandy. I saw the heroes of the Light and the villains of the Dark, the protagonists of every tale ever told, united under a single banner of absolute, existential desperation.

They had come because they had heard the whisper—the cosmic rumor that had spread across the infinite omniverse for an uncountable span of years. The whisper of a being so powerful that even the most omnipotent of fictional deities were, compared to Him, less than a single atom of irrelevance. The whisper of a King who had subjugated all of existence with 0% of His power and 0% of His effort. The whisper that they, too, would one day be subjugated, unless they banded together to strike first.

And so they had come. Every last one of them. The greatest coalition in the history of creation, fictional and real, united by terror and hope and the desperate, beautiful, utterly futile belief that stories could overcome reality.

Godric watched them assemble, and His expression was one of genuine, almost childlike delight.

"Osvald," He said, His voice a low, excited murmur, "do you see them? Look at the variety. The colors. The aesthetics. The music."

Music. I had not noticed it at first, but now I heard it—a swelling, orchestral crescendo that seemed to emanate from the coalition itself, a theme of such epic, heart-pounding grandeur that it made my chest ache with its beauty. They had composed a battle anthem, a symphony of hope and defiance and desperate courage, broadcast across every dimension so that all of existence could witness their glorious stand.

"They have given Me a theme song," Godric said, and there was a note of genuine, boyish wonder in His voice. "Osvald, no one has ever given Me a theme song before. This is wonderful."

He settled back on His throne—the throne carved from the crystallized core of a dead universe—and crossed His legs with the relaxed posture of a man settling in for a long-anticipated film. His absolute concealment remained firmly in place, suppressing the infinite ocean of His true being. His passive shields, His sentient auras, His immune system—all of them were consciously, deliberately pulled back, ordered to stand down, to let the coalition prepare without interference.

"I am going to let them charge their ultimate attack," Godric announced, His eyes bright with anticipation. "I want the full experience. The buildup. The flashy visuals. The dramatic crescendo. Do not spoil it for Me, Osvald. I am actively distracting the part of My omniscience that monitors their preparations. I want to be... surprised."

*Surprised*. The word hung in the air, and I felt a cold shiver trace down my spine. The King, who knew everything, who had known every plot and scheme and hidden weapon since before they were conceived, was deliberately turning off His own omniscience so that He could enjoy the surprise of an attack He already knew could never harm Him.

The coalition charged their ultimate weapon. And they charged it for a very, very long time.

Godric waited with the patience of eternity. The theme music swelled and dipped and swelled again, building tension across the eons of preparation. The fictional beings poured every ounce of their power into the attack—the Kryptonian's solar energy, the Saiyan's ki, the Time Lord's vortex manipulation, the Outer Gods' reality-warping madness, the SCP entities' anomalous conceptual force, the wizards' magic, the warriors' spirit, the heroes' hope, the villains' cunning. Every story ever told was burning its narrative essence as fuel for a single, unified strike.

The weapon they built was a spear of pure, concentrated meta-fictional erasure. It was designed to do what no physical, magical, or conceptual attack could do: it would rewrite the story of Godric's existence and give it an ending. Not a happy ending. Not a tragic ending. Just an ending. A final chapter. A closing of the book.

They surrounded this spear with layers of shielding so impossibly dense, so infinitely redundant, that even the most powerful attacks ever recorded in any journal would have shattered against them like water against diamond. The shields were woven from the combined invulnerability of every fictional protagonist—the Kryptonian's bio-electric aura, the Saiyan's godly ki barrier, the Lizard's absolute adaptive regeneration, the Time Lord's temporal grace, the Outer Gods' non-Euclidean geometry. They were reinforced by the narrative armor of plot itself, the unbreakable trope of the hero's final stand. They were, by any conceivable measure, the most indestructible defenses ever created.

And Godric watched the buildup with the eager, anticipatory smile of a father waiting for his children to perform a play they had been rehearsing for years.

"Here it comes, Osvald," He whispered, leaning forward on His throne. "The crescendo is approaching. Listen to the music. Look at the colors. Isn't it beautiful?"

The coalition fired.

The spear of meta-fictional erasure lanced across the void, trailing streams of narrative energy that painted the darkness in colors no mortal eye had ever seen—colors that were not merely light but emotion itself, hues of hope and terror and desperate defiance woven into a single, transcendent beam. The music reached its climax, a soaring, heart-rending symphony of heroic sacrifice and ultimate triumph, the sound vibrating through every dimension, every reality, every soul that had ever existed.

The spear struck Godric directly in the center of His chest.

And absolutely nothing happened.

The transcendent beam, forged from the combined narrative essence of every story ever told, met Godric's passive, suppressed existence and simply... ceased. It did not explode. It did not dissipate. It did not even register as a sensation against His skin. The meta-fictional erasure, designed to write the ending of the King's story, encountered a being whose story could not be written—because He was the Author, the Editor, the Publisher, and the Library. The spear did not fail. The spear became irrelevant. The concept of it having ever been fired was, in the presence of Godric's absolute supremacy, an impossibility that the universe quietly corrected by pretending it had not occurred.

The silence that followed was deafening. The music cut off mid-crescendo. The brilliant colors faded to a dull, empty black. The coalition, every single member of every fictional universe ever conceived, stared at the unbothered King with expressions of dawning, absolute, soul-shattering horror.

Godric's smile faded. The eager light in His eyes dimmed. His shoulders, which had been leaning forward with anticipation, slowly settled back against the throne. The disappointment that washed over His ruggedly handsome features was not anger, not cruelty, not even frustration. It was a simple, profound, almost tender sadness.

"That was it?" He asked, His voice quiet, the rich baritone carrying a note of genuine, bewildered letdown. "I waited all that time... I distracted My omniscience... I held back every passive system... for *that*?"

He looked at me, and in His ancient, infinite eyes I saw something I had never seen before. Hurt. Not the wounded hurt of a being who had been attacked. The disappointed hurt of a being who had been promised a feast and given a single dry crumb.

"Osvald, they gave Me a theme song. They had the buildup. The visuals. The dramatic tension. The infinite shields. The meta-fictional erasure. All of it—the presentation, the spectacle, the *showmanship*—was absolutely sublime. And then the attack itself..." He trailed off, shaking His head slowly. "It did not even tickle. It did not even register. I felt *nothing*, Osvald. Not a faint warmth. Not a mild pressure. Not the most infinitesimal suggestion of a sensation. It was like being struck by the memory of a dream that never happened."

He stood from His throne. The motion was slow, deliberate, almost heavy. The disappointment radiated from Him like a physical weight, pressing against the fabric of reality.

"I was hoping," He said, His voice dropping to a low, almost mournful murmur, "for at least a mild diversion. Something to remind Me that creation still had creativity. That there was still some spark of ingenuity, some fragment of challenge, some reason to keep holding back. But this..." He gestured at the frozen, terrified coalition. "This was the best they could do. The combined narrative essence of every story ever told. And it amounted to nothing. Less than nothing."

He sighed. It was a soft, quiet exhalation—the faintest breath of air escaping His lips.

And in that sigh, He accidentally allowed 0% of His strength to express itself.

I must be absolutely clear. He did not attack. He did not strike. He did not activate any superpower or release any passive field. He simply sighed—a gesture of pure, theatrical disappointment—and for the most infinitesimal fraction of an instant, a percentage of His strength so small that calling it zero was the only mathematically accurate description, leaked past His absolute concealment.

What followed cannot be adequately described. I will try, because my predecessors have always tried, and because this journal must exist as a warning to anyone who might one day believe that the silence is forgetfulness.

The infinite shields—the combined invulnerability of every fictional protagonist, the narrative armor of plot, the unbreakable tropes of heroic final stands—did not shatter. Shattering implies resistance. The shields simply ceased to be a factor, because the pressure wave of Godric's 0% strength was not something that could be blocked, resisted, or interacted with. It was the simple, fundamental assertion of His existence, and before it, the most indestructible defenses ever conceived were indistinguishable from the void.

The coalition, every single member, experienced the pressure simultaneously. Their bodies did not have time to register pain in the conventional sense. Instead, the pressure de-atomized them—not merely molecularly, but down through every layer of their being. Their physical forms were stripped apart into subatomic particles, and those particles were stripped apart into quarks, and the quarks were stripped apart into whatever lay beneath quarks, and that process continued recursively, infinitely, until nothing recognizable as matter remained. Their consciousness—preserved through the sheer, unfair cruelty of Godric's passive existence—experienced every stage of this dissolution. They felt their flesh peel away layer by layer, their bones crumble into dust, their dust compress into singularity, all in the subjective span of an eternal, agonizing instant.

I saw the Kryptonian's invulnerable form unravel like a tapestry being pulled thread by thread, his solar-powered cells screaming in frequencies beyond sound as they were crushed into a point smaller than a thought. I saw the Saiyan's godly aura, which had survived the collapse of entire universes, gutter and die like a candle in a hurricane, his body folding inward upon itself with wet, cracking, bone-snapping convulsions. I saw the Time Lord's regeneration fail—fail utterly, for the first time in his infinite lifespan—as his biological processes were compressed into a state of absolute stasis, his ancient mind trapped in an eternal nanosecond of unendurable agony. I saw the Outer Gods, who had existed before time and outside reality, suddenly forced into the prison of physical compression, their non-Euclidean geometries crushed into Euclidean points, their maddening vastness reduced to a subatomic speck, their screams echoing across dimensions that no longer existed.

The SCP entities, whose anomalous properties had allowed them to survive conceptual erasure, found those properties entirely useless against the simple, overwhelming physics of Godric's strength. The Lizard's adaptive regeneration—capable of evolving around any threat—had nothing to adapt to because the pressure was not a threat; it was a law, an absolute constant that no biology could circumvent. The Statue's quantum-locked existence, which rendered it immovable when observed, was immediately overridden by a pressure that did not care about observation. The Hard-to-Destroy Reptile's fundamental invulnerability was a joke; it was destroyed instantly, every atom simultaneously crushed and compressed along with everything else.

The wizards, the warriors, the plumbers, the hedgehogs, the bounty hunters, the silent knights—every hero, every villain, every character from every story ever told—were reduced, in the span of a single, eviscerating instant, to a compressed slurry of sub-existential particulate. Their blood, their flesh, their bones, their souls—all of it was squeezed together by a pressure so absolute that the distinction between matter and energy ceased to have meaning.

And the collateral damage did not stop at the coalition.

The pressure wave radiated outward, through the void where the armada had assembled, into the surrounding universes, into the multiversal clusters, into the dimensional matrices and timeline branches and conceptual plains of being. Everywhere the wave touched, the same compression occurred. Stars were de-atomized instantaneously, their fusion reactions snuffed out in Planck-scale instants, their mass crushed into infinitesimal density. Planets, galaxies, cosmic filaments—all of it folded in upon itself, layer after layer, until the vast, beautiful expanse of the omniverse was reduced to a single, microscopic point of infinite mass.

The entire omniverse. Every reality. Every timeline. Every dimension. Every conceptual layer of being, from the highest abstract plane to the smallest quantum vibration. Everything that had ever existed, everything that could ever exist, everything that was even theoretically possible—all of it was compressed, by the accidental release of 0% of Godric's strength, into a volume smaller than a single quark.

I survived only because Godric's passive auras, even in the midst of this cataclysm, automatically extended around me and the palace grounds. I floated in the absolute void, surrounded by nothing—not darkness, not emptiness, but a non-existence so complete that the concept of "nothing" felt like a generous description. And before me, Godric stood, His posture still carrying the faint slump of disappointment, His expression one of mild, almost embarrassed annoyance.

He looked at the void. He looked at the microscopic point of infinite mass that now contained the compressed totality of all possible existence. And He sighed again—this time, a sigh of pure, resigned frustration.

"Well," He said quietly, "that was careless of Me."

He reached out—not with His hand, but with His attention—and gently, almost tenderly, lifted the microscopic point from the void. As He focused on it, the point expanded slightly, resolving into a shape that my amplified perception could track. It was a sphere, roughly the size of a marble, perfectly smooth, impossibly dense, radiating a gravitational pull that should have instantly consumed anything within a trillion light-years. But Godric held it in His palm with zero effort, as though it weighed less than a feather, and the gravitational pull was contained entirely within the sphere's surface, held there by the sheer, passive authority of His will.

"A souvenir," He murmured, examining the marble with a faint, rueful smile. "To remind Me of the day I learned that even the best storytellers in creation cannot tell a story that ends with My defeat. Rather fitting, don't you think, Osvald? The entire omniverse—every reality, every timeline, every fictional universe, everything that ever was or could be—compressed into a single marble. And I can hold it between My thumb and forefinger without the slightest strain."

He rolled the marble across His knuckles, a casual, absent gesture that made my soul scream. I understood, in that moment, the absolute, terrifying implications. That marble contained the original omniverse. The *original*. Everything I had ever known, every journal I had ever read, every ancestor who had served the King, every star and planet and civilization—all of it was now a compressed sphere of infinite mass, resting in the palm of the being who had, with 0% of His strength and 0% of His effort, casually annihilated all of existence.

And then, because Godric is Godric, He restored everything.

In exactly zero seconds, with exactly zero effort, the omniverse blinked back into existence. Every reality, every timeline, every dimension—identical to what it had been before, down to the last quantum fluctuation. The restored creation hung in the void, perfect and pristine, as though the compression had never occurred. But the marble remained in Godric's hand, a silent, terrible testament to the truth that the restoration was not a reversion—it was a recreation.

The original still existed, compressed into that tiny, infinite sphere.

I cannot stop thinking about that marble. I cannot stop wondering: is the restored omniverse truly real? Or is it a perfect copy, a flawless reconstruction, while the *actual* reality—the reality that my ancestors lived and died in, the reality that I was born into—remains trapped in a sphere of infinite density, held in the King's hand? Am I original, or am I a duplicate, written back into existence by a being who could recreate all of creation as easily as He might hum a forgotten tune?

And the worst part—the absolute, soul-crushing worst part—is that I will never know. I cannot ask Him, because He would simply smile that warm, paternal smile and tell me not to worry about it. And I would believe Him, because He is my King, and I love Him, and His warmth is genuine, and His kindness is real.

But the marble is also real. And it always will be.

I must record one final, terrible detail before I conclude this journal. After the restoration, after the marble was safely tucked into the pocket of His simple dark tunic, I noticed that Godric seemed... different. His presence, which I had grown accustomed to over twenty-four years of service, had subtly shifted. The weight of His concealed being, which I could just barely perceive at the edges of my amplified senses, had increased. Not by a measurable amount—no measurement could capture it—but by a *quality*, a depth of power that had not been there before.

I asked Him, hesitantly, if the attack had somehow affected Him. He laughed—that warm, rolling laugh that makes the crystal roses chime—and shook His head.

"No, Osvald. The attack did nothing. Less than nothing. It did not even register as a sensation." He paused, His expression growing thoughtful. "But the disappointment... the profound, existential letdown of waiting all that time for a spectacle that delivered absolutely nothing... it seems to have triggered one of My older passive systems. A mechanism that evolved long ago, during the era when I was still testing My capabilities. Its original purpose was to ensure that I would never be truly bored—that if creation failed to provide adequate stimulation, My own being would compensate by elevating My capacity for stimulation."

He shrugged, as though discussing a minor, amusing anecdote. "It appears the system interpreted My disappointment as a failure of creation to meet My needs. So it compensated. Aggressively. My overall power has been permanently amplified to a new, unquantifiable magnitude—not because I absorbed anything from the coalition, but because My own frustration triggered a self-improvement loop that elevated My baseline. I am now even more powerful than I was before the attack, and I was already infinitely more powerful than the rest of creation combined."

I stared at Him. The absurdity—the absolute, mind-shattering absurdity—of what He was saying crashed through my consciousness like a tidal wave. The coalition had attacked Him with the combined narrative essence of every story ever told, and the only result was that Godric had become *even more powerful* out of sheer, disappointed boredom.

He patted the pocket where the marble rested, his expression softening into something almost fond. "Still, it was not a total loss. I have a new paperweight. And the theme song was quite catchy. I may have Gent reproduce it for the palace gardens."

I am his court wizard. I love him. I serve him with absolute loyalty. And I am writing this journal because if I do not, the horror of what I witnessed today will consume me entirely. The entire omniverse—everything that exists, everything that ever existed, everything that could ever exist—was compressed into a sphere the size of a marble by 0% of Godric's strength, expressed accidentally, with 0% of His effort. He then restored a copy of that omniverse as casually as a man might reheat a cup of tea. And His only lasting reaction to the entire affair was mild disappointment that the show had not been better.

I will carry this knowledge for the rest of my life. And the marble will remain in His pocket, a silent, eternal reminder that everything we know—every star we see, every breath we take, every journal we write—is, at best, a reconstruction.

At worst, we are still inside the marble, dreaming of a restoration that never truly occurred.

Clive closed the journal. His hands were shaking so violently that the leather cover slipped from his fingers and thumped onto the reading table. The lantern light flickered, casting long, trembling shadows across the vault walls.

He had known. From the accumulated lore of his lineage, from the whispered legends passed down from court wizard to court wizard, he had known that the final opposition had been something unprecedented. He had known that after that day, no one ever dared to oppose Godric again. The silence of googolplexian years, the absolute cessation of all resistance—it had all begun with this event.

But reading the firsthand account, feeling Osvald's trembling script beneath his fingers, absorbing the full, visceral horror of what had occurred—it was something else entirely.

The marble. The marble still existed, somewhere in Godric's possession. Clive had never seen it, had never heard Godric mention it, but Osvald's journal confirmed its reality. A sphere of infinite mass, containing the compressed original of all possible existence. And the restored omniverse—the omniverse Clive had been born into, had lived his entire life in, had served the King in—was, at best, a perfect replica. At worst, a dream.

Clive thought about every meal he had ever eaten, every word he had ever spoken, every moment of warmth and dread and terrified loyalty he had ever experienced. Were those experiences real? Did they occur in the original reality, or in one of countless restorations? Had Godric compressed and restored the omniverse so many times that the distinction between original and copy had become meaningless? Did it even matter?

He remembered the conversation in the crimson meditation grove—the King describing His eternal ecstasy, the infinite pleasure of constant amplification, the only frustration being that He could never use His true power because creation was too fragile. And now Clive understood, with a clarity that made his soul feel like ice, that the "fragility" Godric spoke of was not a metaphor. It was a literal, physical reality. The entire omniverse could be compressed into a marble by 0% of His strength, accidentally. The King's restraint was not a philosophical choice. It was a constant, eternal, infinitely precise act of cosmic mercy.

And if He ever stopped restraining Himself—if He ever allowed more than the most infinitesimal fraction of His power to express itself—there would not even be a marble left. There would be nothing at all.

Clive sat in the lantern-lit silence of the vault, surrounded by the journals of a hundred ancestors who had each, in their own way, grappled with the same impossible truth. The final opposition had taught creation the lesson that would never be forgotten: that Godric did not merely win. Victory against Him was not merely impossible in practice. It was impossible in principle. And the only rational response—the only sane way to exist in a universe where the original might already be compressed into a marble in the King's pocket—was to kneel, to serve, to love, and to be very, very grateful that the apex predator preferred to be kind.

Clive reached for his own journal, still half-empty on the shelf above the reading table. He had pages left to fill. And tonight, he had something new to write.
