The infinite layered shields that the Armada had constructed—barriers forged from the essence of absolute narrative invulnerability, from the combined protective magics of every fictional universe, from conceptual wards that declared "this shall not pass" in every language ever imagined—those shields were not broken. Breaking is a process. They were simply negated. One moment they existed, their protective auras blazing with the combined might of infinite imagination; the next moment, they were less than nothing, retroactively unwritten from the fabric of reality by a force so overwhelmingly superior that the concept of resistance was rendered mathematically nonsensical.*

*The Fictional Armada themselves did not die quickly. The King's accidental zero-percent strength did not grant them the mercy of instant annihilation. Instead, the force propagated through them layer by agonizing layer, and because the King's omniscience had perceived their attack in all its disappointing inadequacy, his passive cruelty—that ruthless, unforgiving core that never, ever forgives opposition—ensured that their destruction was experienced in excruciating, prolonged detail. The caped crusader, that dark knight of vengeance, felt his body compressed from the inside out as the gravitational shear of Godric's passing strength folded his internal organs into themselves, his armored suit crumpling like wet paper, his bones pulverizing into a fine, calcium-rich mist, his final scream a wet, gurgling choke as his lungs collapsed and his heart burst within his chest. The boy wizard, whose adventures had delighted countless children across countless realities, watched his wand—his beloved, faithful wand—shatter into a million splinters that then reversed direction and embedded themselves in his flesh, each splinter carrying a fragment of the killing force, each one burrowing deeper, turning his body into a pincushion of magical shrapnel, his scar blazing with agony before his skull caved inward under the pressure. The Jedi, the masters of peace and serenity, died screaming as the Force itself—the very energy field they had devoted their lives to—was overwhelmed by Godric's presence and turned against them, crushing their bodies into spheres of compressed meat and bone, their lightsabers imploding into singularities that devoured their screaming faces. The anime warriors, the ones with spiky hair, experienced their transformation sequences in reverse—their power-ups stripped away layer by layer, their auras torn from their bodies, their musculature unraveling, their iconic hair falling out in bloody clumps, their wide eyes rupturing in their sockets. The giant mecha were not crushed; they were folded, like origami, their pilots liquefied inside their cockpits, their screams transmitted across the comms channels before the comms themselves were deleted.*

*And all of this—this unimaginably vast, infinitely varied, agonizingly detailed cascade of destruction—was merely the local consequence. The true horror was the collateral. Because zero percent of Godric's strength, released accidentally, did not discriminate between the Fictional Armada and the rest of the omniverse. The force propagated outward, instantaneously, across all of existence. Every multiverse, every timeline, every reality, every conceptual layer, every hypothetical and actual and possible framework—all of it, the entirety of the omniverse, everything that existed or could ever exist—was caught in the backwash of that infinitesimal flex of strength. And it was not simply destroyed. It was de-atomized and compressed. The sheer, overwhelming pressure of Godric's accidental strength acted as a cosmic trash compactor, folding the infinite mass of the entire omniverse into a space smaller than a quark. I cannot describe the sound, because sound requires a medium, and by the time the compression completed, there was no medium left. But I felt it. I felt the scream of every atom, every particle, every soul, every star, every galaxy, every multiverse being crushed into an infinitely dense point of absolute, silent, mind-shattering nothingness. My own body, I realized later, had been compressed along with everything else. I died. I died in agony so profound that my consciousness was not merely extinguished but stretched across the infinite pressure gradient, experiencing my own atomization across every possible simultaneous instant of the compression. And then, because the King wills it, I did not die. None of us did. The compression contained us, preserved us, held us in a state of eternal, agonizing suspension within the marble-sized singularity that now rested in the King's palm.*

*The King had caught the compressed omniverse. I do not mean that he reached out and grabbed it. I mean that as the compression completed, he—still standing on the balcony, still wearing the faint expression of mild disappointment—simply opened his hand, and the infinitesimal point of infinite mass, the compressed totality of everything that had ever existed, drifted into his palm as though it weighed nothing. Which, to him, it did. He examined it with the casual curiosity of a man inspecting a pebble found on a beach. He turned it over between his thumb and forefinger. He held it up to the light of the now-empty void. Then, with a soft, thoughtful hum, he enlarged it slightly—from smaller than a quark to the size of a small marble—and wrapped it in a subtle, invisible barrier that contained its gravitational pull so that it would not immediately consume the palace, the planet, or the surrounding void. The barrier was, I later understood, the only reason the restored omniverse could exist in the same space as the original without being instantly sucked into the marble and re-compressed. The King had, in effect, created a brand new omniverse—identical in every way to the original—and left the original compressed in his hand as a souvenir.*

*The restoration happened in zero seconds. One moment, there was only void and the King and the marble. The next moment, the omniverse was back. Every galaxy, every planet, every soul, every timeline, every fictional and real and conceptual entity—all restored. The Fictional Armada did not return. As always, the King does not forgive opposition. But the rest of us, the trillions upon trillions of innocent beings who had been collateral damage, were resurrected without a flicker of effort. I found myself standing on the balcony, my robes intact, my body whole, my mind a shattered mosaic of the memories of my own compression-death. The King was beside me, still holding the marble, turning it over in his fingers with an expression of mild, detached curiosity. "Well," he said, his rich baritone carrying the faintest hint of petulance, "that was a letdown. I really thought they'd put on a better show. I even saved a seat." He pocketed the marble—the compressed original omniverse, containing everything—as though it were a spare coin, and walked back into the palace, humming a tune from the aborted orchestral score.*

*The trophy remains on his desk. I see it every day. It is a marble of swirling, iridescent darkness, its surface faintly luminous with the trapped light of a trillion trillion galaxies. It weighs nothing to him. It would weigh infinity to anyone else. And it contains the original omniverse—the one we all lived in, the one that was compressed by his accidental strength. The omniverse we now inhabit is a copy. A perfect, flawless, indistinguishable copy, created by the King with zero effort in zero seconds, so that his subjects would not be inconvenienced by his momentary disappointment. I do not know if this makes the current omniverse less real or more real. I do know that every time I look at that marble, I wonder: am I the original? Or am I a recreation? Does it matter? The King could compress and restore us a billion more times without ever expending more than zero percent of his strength, and we would never know the difference. The only comfort—the only anchor to sanity—is that the King is warm and kind to those he loves. And he does love us. He loves his court wizards, his servants, his subjects. He keeps us not because he needs us, but because he values us. That warmth is genuine. That comfort is real. But the marble is also real. And I will never stop seeing it.*

Clive closed the journal. His hands were steady, but only because the muscles had locked into a rigor of pure, undiluted existential dread. He had known, intellectually, that Godric's power was infinite. He had read the stat journals and the superpower apocalypses. He had witnessed the creation of the Stasis Event and stood beside the King as he contemplated the compression of his younger self's weakness. But this—the marble—was something else entirely. The marble was not a demonstration of power. It was a casual afterthought, a souvenir created by an accidental flex of strength so small it could not be measured, and it contained the entire original omniverse. The omniverse that Clive now inhabited was a copy. A perfect copy, created with zero effort, but a copy nonetheless. And the original was sitting on Godric's desk, next to the inkwell.

Clive had seen that marble a thousand times. He had watched Godric pick it up, toss it idly from hand to hand, roll it across his knuckles like a gambler's coin. He had seen the King tap his fingernail against its surface while thinking, producing a soft, musical click that resonated with the trapped light of a trillion trillion galaxies. And every time, Clive had smiled and nodded and continued his duties, because the marble was just part of the King's collection—a curiosity, a trinket. Now he understood what it truly was. It was the original reality, compressed into a paperweight, and the King handled it with the absent, unthinking ease of a man fidgeting with a pebble.

He looked up from the journal, his gaze drifting to the window. Outside, the golden spires of Thronegate glittered in the eternal afternoon. The restored omniverse spun on, perfectly identical to the original, populated by beings who would never know they had been compressed, atomized, and recreated. The Fictional Armada had been the last opposition ever to rise. After that day, the long silence had begun—not a pause, but an end. No one dared anymore. No one would ever dare again. The lesson had been learned not through punishment, but through the sheer, annihilating weight of absolute demonstration. Godric had not merely defeated the greatest coalition ever assembled. He had compressed the entirety of existence into a bauble and continued his day with mild disappointment.

The dread that settled into Clive's bones was not sharp or sudden. It was the slow, suffocating creep of absolute understanding. He served a being who could, with less than zero effort, compress all of reality into a marble and then recreate it as an afterthought. That being was warm and kind and genuinely loved him. And that dissonance—the warmth and the infinite, casual destruction—was the burden of every court wizard who had ever lived. It was the price of proximity to the apex predator of all existence.

Clive stood, returned the journal to its shelf, and walked slowly toward the throne room. Today, as every day, he would stand beside his King. And somewhere on the desk, next to the inkwell, the marble would be waiting, its swirling darkness a silent reminder that everything—everything—was permitted to exist solely because Godric had not been sufficiently disappointed to keep it compressed.
