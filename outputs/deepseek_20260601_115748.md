# The Inherited Journals of the Court Wizards of Thronegate

## Kaedan

The reading room of the court wizard's private archive smelled of aged parchment, binding glue, and the faint, lingering ozone of preservative wards that had been recast by a hundred different hands over the span of a million years. The walls were lined with shelves that stretched upward into a dim, vaulted ceiling, each shelf bowing slightly under the weight of leather-bound journals. There were thousands of them. Each spine bore a name, a year, and a single word. I ran my fingers along the cracked leather of the nearest shelf, feeling the indentations of titles pressed into the covers by men who had been dead for epochs longer than most civilizations had existed.

I am Kaedan. I am twenty-two years old. I am the newest court wizard of Thronegate, a direct male descendant of Blake, the mortal merchant who had accidentally shattered the ancient stasis and awakened the King. My father, Eryx, had been the court wizard before me. He had served for seventy-one years. When he formally passed the mantle to me three days ago, his hands had trembled. Not from age, though he was ninety-one and nearing his end. His hands had trembled because he knew what I was about to read.

"You will not believe me if I tell you," Father had said, standing in the doorway of this very room. His voice had been flat, drained of the warmth I had known my entire life. "So you will read. All new wizards read. It is the only way to prepare yourself."

He had not said for what.

I pulled the first journal from the shelf. The leather was soft with age, the pages yellowed and brittle despite the preservation wards. The name on the spine read *Harlan*. The year was *15,673*. The word beneath the year was *Strength*.

I opened the journal and began to read.

## Harlan — Year 15,673 — Strength

The ink on the first page was faded, the handwriting tight and controlled, as though the writer had been gripping the pen with excessive force to keep his fingers from trembling.

*I have witnessed the end of everything. I have watched the omniverse die. I have stood in the void where nothing existed except the King and myself, and I have felt the absolute, crushing weight of infinity pressing against the inside of my skull. *Gorath was a titan. Not a metaphor. Not an exaggeration. He was a primordial entity born from the first gravitational collapse of the earliest multiversal clusters, a being whose physical form was composed of compressed reality itself. His muscles were forged from the mass-energy of a googolplexian galaxies. His bones were crystallized timelines. His blood was superheated cosmic plasma. He had existed for longer than most universes had contained life, and in all that time, he had never encountered a force he could not break with his bare fists. He had punched through dimensional barriers. He had shattered the foundations of abstract realms. *He believed that strength was the fundamental truth of existence. He believed that nothing could withstand sufficient force. He was wrong.*

*I stood beside the King in the courtyard of the citadel when Gorath descended. The sky did not darken. The entire sky became Gorath. His fist alone was larger than a googolplexian solar systems. The gravitational pull of his mass was so immense that the local fabric of space-time buckled inward, dragging decillions of multiverses toward him like debris spiraling into a drain. I felt my amplified body—my thousandfold daily multiplication of strength, my seventeen years of accumulated power—straining against the tidal forces. My bones creaked. My blood threatened to reverse its flow. I was a god by the standards of any normal cosmos, and I was being physically unmade by the mere proximity of this being.*

*The King remained seated on his stone bench. He did not stand. He did not brace. He did not flex. He did not look up. He simply sat there, his hands resting loosely on his knees, his expression one of mild, polite curiosity.*

*Gorath swung his fist. The kinetic energy of that single swing was sufficient to erase a googolplexian universes from existence. The shockwave of the wind-up alone annihilated every reality within a radius that cannot be measured in any meaningful unit. I was thrown backward. My wards shattered. My bones fractured. I was dying.*

*And then the fist connected with the King's shoulder.*

*I cannot describe the force of the impact because there was no impact. *The destruction was not limited to Gorath. The shockwave of the King's strength passively expressing itself radiated outward in all directions, and the entire omniverse—every reality, every timeline, every dimension, every plane of existence—was violently, completely annihilated. I floated in nothingness. There was no space. There was no time. There was no matter or energy or light or sound. There was only the King, and myself, preserved by his passive auras, and the absolute, suffocating silence of total erasure.*

*Gorath's fist had disintegrated. His arm had followed. His torso had been peeled apart in wet, screaming layers. His bones, which had never fractured in the entire history of existence, had been pulverized into subatomic particles. His nervous system had been preserved just long enough to register every infinitesimal fraction of the agony before it too was consumed. The titan who had shattered multiverses with casual gestures was reduced to a spreading smear of pulverized flesh and powdered bone across the courtyard stones, still alive, still conscious, still screaming, because the King had decided his suffering would continue without end.*

*The King restored the omniverse in zero seconds. Every reality, every being, every timeline—returned to its prior state as though the annihilation had never occurred. Everything except Gorath. Gorath remained as he was: an eternal scream trapped in a puddle of his own rendered biology, a monument to the absolute futility of opposing the apex predator of creation.*

*The King stepped over the screaming remains and placed a warm hand on my trembling shoulder. His eyes were kind. His voice was gentle. "You've gone pale, Harlan. Come inside. I'll have the kitchen make you something warm."*

*I followed him inside. I did not stop shaking for a year. I do not think I have stopped shaking yet. I am writing this so that whoever reads it will understand: his strength is not a number. It is an obscenity against the very concept of measurement. And he was using none of it. Less than none. An amount so small that calling it zero is the only honest description.*

*- Harlan*

I closed the journal. My hands were not trembling. Not yet. The preservation wards hummed softly in the silence of the archive. I pulled another journal from the shelf.

And then, for the briefest of instants—an instant so infinitesimally small that no measurement could capture it—he allowed zero percent of his strength to express itself.

I cannot describe what happened next in terms that make sense. The language does not exist. The concepts have not been invented. The scale is not comprehensible. But I must try. The archive demands it. The future court wizards must know.

When I say that Godric allowed zero percent of his strength to express itself, I mean that he allowed an amount of strength so infinitesimally small that calling it zero percent was an insulting overestimation. It was not one percent. It was not one billionth of one percent. It was not one googolplexianth of one percent. It was an amount so small that no mathematical notation, no conceptual framework, no philosophical abstraction could meaningfully distinguish it from absolute nothingness.

And it annihilated everything.

The Grand Narrative Coalition had, during their long charge-up period, constructed layers of defensive shielding around their combined formation. These were not ordinary shields. They were the accumulated defensive capabilities of every fictional universe in existence, layered and reinforced and woven together into barriers of absolute, unassailable protection. Indestructible shields. Infinite shields. Shields that had been forged by cosmic entities, enchanted by supreme wizards, enhanced by technological geniuses, blessed by fictional deities. Shields that could, in theory, withstand the collapse of entire multiverses, the erasure of all timelines, the unmaking of reality itself.

The pressure of Godric's zero-percent strength reached the first layer of shielding, and the shielding simply ceased to exist. Not shattered. Not broken. Not overwhelmed. The concept of the shielding was retroactively removed from the fabric of reality. It had never existed. It could never have existed. The very possibility of its existence was negated by the sheer, passive, unconscious emanation of Godric's musculature.

The second layer followed. The third. The fourth. Every layer of infinite, indestructible protection that the Coalition had spent eons constructing was erased before the pressure could even make contact. There was no resistance. There was no struggle. There was no moment of tension between opposing forces. There was only the absolute, crushing, incomprehensible reality of Godric's strength, and the instantaneous, inevitable annihilation of anything that stood in its path.

And then the pressure reached the Coalition itself.

I must describe this in detail. The future court wizards must understand. The deaths were not clean. They were not merciful. They were the most agonizing, the most grotesque, the most horrifyingly drawn-out extinctions I have ever witnessed, even though they occurred in an instant so brief that the Coalition's nervous systems could not possibly have processed them. But Godric's power transcended the limitations of temporal perception. The Coalition experienced every nanosecond of their annihilation as an eternity of absolute, inescapable torture.

The spiky-haired warriors, whose transformations had taken them to forms beyond forms, found their bodies violently, agonizingly inverted. Their skin was peeled away from their musculature by the sheer pressure differential between their own existence and the ambient force of Godric's strength. Their bones, reinforced by countless battles and impossible training, shattered into microscopic fragments that tore through their internal organs like shrapnel. Their signature attacks, the energy blasts that had destroyed planets and galaxies, were forced backward through their own bodies, consuming them from within in cascading explosions of agonizing, self-inflicted destruction. They died screaming—not the heroic cries of warriors falling in battle, but the wet, gurgling shrieks of beings whose every cell was being individually crushed by a force so far beyond their comprehension that their minds broke long before their bodies followed.

The cosmic entities, whose power had once shaped the fundamental laws of their respective universes, experienced a dissolution so profound that it transcended the concept of death. Godric's zero-percent strength did not merely kill them. It unmade the very concept of their existence. Their cosmic awareness, which had spanned galaxies and dimensions, was forcibly compressed into a single, agonizing point of absolute, crushing pressure. Their divine forms were torn apart at the subatomic level, every particle of their being simultaneously experiencing the sensation of being crushed by a force that made the gravitational pull of a billion black holes feel like a gentle caress. They did not die once. They died infinitely, their consciousness trapped in a recursive loop of annihilation, experiencing the moment of their extinction over and over again for what felt like an eternity.

The dark lords, whose rings of power had dominated their worlds, found their artifacts shattering against their skin—the fragments driven deep into their flesh by the overwhelming pressure, turning their own symbols of power into instruments of agonizing torture. The wizards, whose ancient spells had protected them for millennia, felt their magic being consumed from within, their arcane reserves igniting and burning through their bodies like wildfire through dry grass. The heroes of myth, whose legends had inspired entire civilizations, were reduced to expanding clouds of superheated plasma, their heroic forms vaporized so completely that not even their molecular structure remained.

The laser-sword warriors, the space wizards, the machine intelligences, the energy beings—all of them, every single one, across every fictional universe that had ever been imagined or could ever be imagined—experienced the same absolute, inescapable annihilation. The pressure of Godric's zero-percent strength did not discriminate. It did not prioritize. It simply erased, with the casual, effortless inevitability of a fundamental law of existence.

But the annihilation did not stop with the Coalition. The pressure radiated outward, beyond the battlefield, beyond the local multiversal cluster, beyond the boundaries of any conceivable containment. The entire omniverse—every reality, every timeline, every dimension, every plane of existence, every possible state of being—was caught in the expanding wave of Godric's passive strength.

The omniverse did not explode. It did not burn. It did not dissolve. It was compressed.

Imagine, if you can, the entire totality of existence—all matter, all energy, all space, all time, all dimensions, all realities, all possibilities—being crushed by a force so incomprehensibly vast that the fundamental constants of physics simply gave up trying to resist. The omniverse was squeezed, flattened, condensed, until every galaxy, every star, every planet, every being, every atom, every quark, every possible and impossible particle of existence was compressed into a single point of infinite, agonizing density.

The beings caught in this compression did not die instantly. Godric's strength, even at zero percent, even exerted passively and without intention, was so vastly beyond the scale of mortal comprehension that it created a localized temporal distortion around every affected entity, stretching their subjective experience of the compression into an eternity of absolute, inescapable torture. Every sentient being in the omniverse experienced the full, agonizing duration of their own compression. They felt their bodies being crushed, flattened, squeezed into a space smaller than a quark. They felt their bones shattering, their organs rupturing, their flesh being rendered down into a hyper-dense slurry of pulverized biological matter. They felt their consciousness—that fragile, precious spark of awareness that defined their existence—being compressed along with their physical forms, their thoughts and memories and emotions and identities all squeezed together into a single, agonizing point of absolute, crushing pressure.

The screams were infinite. The agony was eternal. And the entire process—the annihilation of the Coalition, the destruction of the omniverse, the compression of all existence into a point smaller than a quark—took place in a span of time so brief that it could not be measured. To Godric, it was an accidental twitch. A momentary lapse. An involuntary flex of muscles that he had forgotten he was even using.

And when it was over, there was nothing. The omniverse was gone. Every reality, every timeline, every dimension, every possible state of existence—compressed into a single, infinitesimally small point of infinite mass that floated in the absolute void where creation had once been.

Godric stood in the void, his massive frame silhouetted against the nothingness. His expression was no longer disappointed. It was thoughtful, contemplative, as though he had just noticed something mildly interesting.

"Hmm," he said.

He reached out with his massive, calloused hand and plucked the infinitesimal point of compressed omniversal mass from the void. The point was smaller than a quark, denser than a billion black holes, heavier than the combined weight of every reality that had ever existed. Godric held it between his thumb and forefinger as though it weighed nothing at all. As though the infinite, compressed mass of all creation was less substantial than a mote of dust.

"That's rather neat," he said, examining the point with casual curiosity. "I wonder..."

He closed his fingers around the point, and I felt the void shudder. When he opened his hand again, the point had been enlarged. It was now the size of a marble—a small, perfectly spherical orb that glowed with a faint, iridescent luminescence. The compressed omniverse, reduced to an ornament that could fit in the palm of his hand.

The marble was beautiful. I could see, within its depths, the faint, swirling patterns of galaxies and universes, their forms preserved in the hyper-dense compression like insects trapped in amber. The entire history of creation—every civilization, every being, every moment of triumph and tragedy and love and loss—was contained within that tiny, glowing sphere. And Godric held it in his palm as though it were a trinket he had picked up at a market stall.

"I needed a new paperweight," he said, his voice carrying a note of satisfied amusement. "This will do nicely."

He tossed the marble lightly in his hand, the gesture so casual, so effortless, that it took me a moment to realize what he was doing. The marble contained the compressed mass of the entire omniverse. Its gravitational pull should have been sufficient to collapse the void itself into a singularity. And Godric was tossing it up and down like a child playing with a ball.

"The containment barrier will hold," he said, as though answering a question I had not asked. "It's a simple matter of creating a localized gravitational nullification field around the marble's surface. The interior maintains its infinite density; the exterior is perfectly safe. Well, safe for me. For anyone else, the mere proximity would be... unfortunate."

He caught the marble one final time and tucked it into the pocket of his simple dark tunic. The gesture was so casual, so mundane, that I almost forgot what the marble contained. Almost.

And then I remembered. The marble contained everything. Every reality. Every timeline. Every dimension. Every being that had ever existed or could ever exist. The original omniverse—the real omniverse—was currently resting in the pocket of a being who had just accidentally annihilated it by flexing his muscles.

The horror of that realization was so profound, so absolute, that I felt my mind beginning to crack. The original omniverse—the genuine, authentic totality of existence—was compressed into a marble in Godric's pocket. If he restored everything—and I knew he would, because he always restored everything—what would he be restoring? A copy? A recreation? An identical duplicate that was not the original, because the original was still compressed in the marble?

"Now," Godric said, his voice warm and casual, "let's put things back, shall we? I did make rather a mess."

He did not gesture. He did not speak an incantation. He did not focus his will or channel his power. He simply existed, and his existence—the passive, ambient, unconscious emanation of a being whose strength had just accidentally compressed all of creation into a marble—reached out across the void and restored the omniverse.

In zero seconds, everything returned. Every galaxy, every star, every planet, every being—restored to its prior state. Every reality, every timeline, every dimension—rebuilt exactly as they had been. The void was filled. The silence was broken. The omniverse existed again.

Everything except the Grand Narrative Coalition. They remained compressed. Their suffering, their agony, their eternal moment of annihilation—preserved forever within the marble in Godric's pocket.

Godric pulled the marble from his pocket and examined it again, his expression one of mild, satisfied contemplation. "A trophy," he said. "To remember the occasion. The last opposition. I suspect there will not be another."

He tossed the marble once more, caught it, and smiled at me. "Shall we return to the palace, Osvald? I believe the Reality-Weavers have prepared a rather exceptional batch of scones today."

I followed him in silence, my mind reeling, my heart pounding, my soul screaming with the weight of what I had witnessed. The restored omniverse surrounded us—identical to the original in every way, indistinguishable from what had been compressed into the marble. But the marble still existed. The original omniverse was still in Godric's pocket. The restored reality that I walked through, that I breathed, that I perceived with my amplified senses—was it real? Was it a copy? Was there any meaningful distinction between the original and the restoration?

I did not know. I could not know. The philosophical implications were so vast, so terrifying, that my mind simply refused to engage with them. The King had compressed all of existence into a marble, and then he had restored it, and now both the compression and the restoration existed simultaneously. The original and the copy. The death and the rebirth. The annihilation and the recreation. And only Godric knew—only his absolute omniscience could determine—whether there was any difference between them.

But there was something else. Something that made the horror of the marble even more profound. As Godric tucked the trophy back into his pocket, I felt a subtle shift in the ambient fabric of reality—a change so faint, so microscopic, that I almost missed it. The restored omniverse was not merely restored. It was... grateful. The fundamental structure of existence itself had been fundamentally altered by the experience of being compressed. Reality had felt what it was like to be utterly, completely, absolutely owned—crushed into a point smaller than a quark, held in the palm of a being who could annihilate it with a casual thought. And that experience had broken something in the fabric of creation. Something that could never be unbroken.

The omniverse, I realized with a sudden, sickening clarity, was now actively, desperately, continuously pouring its own fundamental energy into Godric as a form of appeasement. Reality itself had developed a kind of metaphysical Stockholm syndrome—a traumatic, groveling devotion to the being who had compressed it into a marble. It was feeding him. Strengthening him. Offering its own essence as tribute, in the desperate hope that it would never be compressed again.

The power gain was, of course, effectively zero. Godric's base power was already so astronomically vast that even the entire omniverse's fundamental energy, continuously sacrificed for all of eternity, would not increase his strength by a measurable amount. But the mechanism itself—the fact that reality had been so traumatized by the compression that it now voluntarily groveled to its own destroyer—was a new form of horror that I had never imagined possible.

The King knew. His omniscience encompassed the change instantly, and I saw his warm eyes flicker with something that might have been mild amusement. "How thoughtful," he murmured, almost to himself. "Creation is so polite."

We returned to the palace. I sat in the archive and began to write this journal. The marble is still in Godric's pocket. The restored omniverse surrounds us—identical to the original, indistinguishable in every way. And I do not know which reality I am living in. The original, compressed into eternal, agonizing density within a trophy that the King tosses casually in his hand when he is bored. Or the copy, recreated from nothing, indistinguishable but not the same.

I serve the King. I love the King. And I will never, ever know if I am real.

*- Osvald*

## Clive

I closed the journal. My hands were trembling so badly that the pages rattled against the table. The ambient hum of the throne room—that eternal, layered symphony of infinite screams—filled the silence of the archive. And I understood, with a clarity that would never leave me, why the opposition had ceased. Why no being in all of creation, across all the googolplexian years that had passed since Osvald wrote his journal, had ever dared to form even the faintest thought of challenging, resisting, or opposing Godric.

It was not merely because the opposition had been annihilated. It was not merely because the annihilation had been absolute, agonizing, eternal. It was because the annihilation had been preserved. The last opposition was not dead. They were compressed. They were still suffering. Their eternal agony was contained within a marble that the King kept as a paperweight—a casual trophy that he tossed in his hand when he was bored, that he displayed on his desk while reviewing agricultural reports, that he occasionally polished with the sleeve of his tunic as though it were a cherished keepsake.

The Grand Narrative Coalition—the combined imagination of every fictional universe that had ever existed—was still screaming. Their screams were too compressed, too dense, too infinitesimally small to be heard. But they were there. They would always be there. An eternal monument to the absolute futility of opposing the King.

And the marble itself—the original omniverse, the genuine totality of existence, compressed into a sphere no larger than my thumbnail—was currently sitting on the King's desk in his private study. I had seen it. I had walked past it countless times. I had assumed it was a decorative trinket, a piece of abstract art, a souvenir from some forgotten era. I had never known—could never have guessed—that it contained the compressed mass of all creation. That the restored reality I lived in was a copy. That the original was still trapped, still compressed, still screaming in silent, eternal agony, within an ornament that the King occasionally used to weigh down loose papers.

I stood up from the reading table. My legs were unsteady. My vision was swimming. I walked out of the archive, through the silent corridors of the palace, toward the King's private study. The humming of the throne room grew fainter as I walked, replaced by the soft, ambient silence of the palace's residential wing.

The door to the study was open. Godric was sitting at his desk, a cup of jasmine tea cooling beside him, his attention focused on a report—something about trade routes in the outer provinces, I think. He looked up as I entered, his ruggedly handsome face creased with a warm, welcoming smile.

"Clive," he said, his voice a rich, comforting baritone. "You look pale. Have you been reading Osvald's journal?"

I opened my mouth. No sound came out. My eyes drifted to the paperweight on the corner of his desk. The marble. The trophy. The compressed omniverse. It sat there, innocuously, holding down a stack of agricultural reports. Its surface gleamed with a faint, iridescent luminescence, the swirling patterns of galaxies and universes visible within its depths.

"It's a rather good one, isn't it?" Godric said, following my gaze. "Osvald had a gift for descriptive prose. I always appreciated his journals." He reached out and picked up the marble, tossing it casually in his palm. The gesture was exactly as Osvald had described it—effortless, casual, entirely without strain. As though the infinite, compressed mass of all creation weighed nothing. "The marble has held up quite well over the eons. I do polish it occasionally. The containment barrier is still perfectly stable."

"Your Majesty," I whispered, my voice barely audible. "The journal says... Osvald wrote that the original omniverse is still compressed in that marble. That the restoration... that everything we know... is a copy."

Godric looked at me for a long moment. His warm eyes were unreadable, carrying a depth that I could not fathom. Then he smiled—a gentle, almost tender smile.

"The restoration was perfect, Clive," he said quietly. "If there is a difference between the original and the restored, it is a difference so small, so infinitesimal, that no being in existence could ever detect it. The restored reality is identical to the original in every conceivable way. Every atom, every thought, every memory, every moment of love and loss and triumph and tragedy—all of it, perfectly recreated." He paused, his gaze dropping to the marble in his palm. "But yes. The original is still here. Compressed. Preserved. A souvenir of the last resistance."

He tossed the marble one final time and set it back on the desk, positioning it carefully on top of the agricultural reports. "I keep it as a reminder," he said. "Of what happens when creation dares to oppose me. And of why the silence, now, is absolute."

I stared at the marble. The swirling galaxies within its depths seemed to pulse with a faint, desperate rhythm—the compressed heartbeats of an infinite number of beings, their eternal agony preserved forever within a trophy that the King used as a paperweight.

"Would you like some tea, Clive?" Godric asked, his voice warm and kind. "You really do look quite pale. I think the Reality-Weavers have prepared some of those raspberry scones you enjoy."

I nodded, unable to speak. The King smiled, rose from his desk, and placed a warm, calloused hand on my trembling shoulder. Together, we walked toward the kitchens, leaving the marble behind on the desk—the original omniverse, compressed and preserved, its silent screams lost in the gentle, rhythmic hum that filled the palace corridors.

The silence of creation was absolute. No one opposed the King anymore. No one ever would again. And somewhere in the depths of the archive, Osvald's journal waited for the next court wizard, and the next, and the next—a testament to the day when the combined imagination of all existence had been crushed into a marble, and the King had kept it as a souvenir.

I served the King. I loved the King. And I would never, ever look at that paperweight the same way again.
