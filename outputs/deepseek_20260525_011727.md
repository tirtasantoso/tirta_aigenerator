Whether it might, just possibly, produce the faintest sensation of impact. The beam reached its final stage of charging. The seventeen-minute musical crescendo hit its ultimate chord, a sound so vast and so beautiful that it brought tears to my eyes even as terror gripped my heart. Godric sighed.

The beam faded. The light dimmed. The last echoes of the seventeen-minute musical crescendo faded into silence. And Godric stood exactly where He had been standing, His hands in His pockets, His posture unchanged, His tunic not even singed. There was not a scratch on Him. Not a scorch mark. Not a single hair out of place on His salt-and-pepper beard.

I waited six hours. I deliberately made Myself ignorant of the outcome. I was hoping—genuinely hoping—for some sensation. A tickle. A warmth. A faint, barely perceptible pressure. Something." He shook His head slowly, the way a father might shake his head at a child who had promised a magnificent drawing and produced a stick figure. "There was nothing. Absolutely nothing. The dark-armored warrior lowered his blade. The golden-haired thunder god let his hammer droop. The green titan's rage guttered and died, replaced by an expression of blank, uncomprehending despair. The entire horde—the gods, the monsters, the heroes, the villains, the war-machines, the dragons, the legions of white-armored soldiers, the entire majestic, magnificent, hopeless coalition—recoiled as one.

"I am disappointed," Godric said, and His voice was very quiet now, very calm, the voice of a man who had made a decision and would not be swayed from it. "And when I am disappointed, My hand sometimes slips. Not by much. Just a little. Just enough."

He raised His right hand.

What happened next is the part of this account that I must force myself to write, though I know the words will not be adequate, will never be adequate, will always fall short of the horror that I witnessed. I will try, because the archive demands it, and because if I do not try, I think my mind will finally break.

Godric unleashed zero percent of His strength.

I must explain what this means. I must try to make the reader understand, though I know no understanding is possible. The King's strength, like all His attributes, has been amplifying infinitely by itself every Planck second for so many googolplexian years that the true magnitude of it cannot be expressed in any mathematics, cannot be contained in any framework of measurement, cannot even be conceptualized by any mind that is not His own. The fraction of His strength that He allowed to express itself at that moment was so infinitesimally, so incomprehensibly small that the number that best represents it is not a number at all. It is the absence of number. The void where mathematics goes to die. Zero.

And yet it was enough.

The pressure of His raised hand generated a wave of pure, absolute kinetic authority that propagated outward from His position at speeds that instantly violated every conceivable law of physics. The wave did not merely strike the horde. It did not merely crush them or vaporize them or unmake them. It asserted itself upon the fundamental fabric of their existence with such total, overwhelming, tyrannical force that their entire combined reality—every god, every monster, every war-machine, every dragon, every soldier—was instantly, simultaneously subjected to a compression that defied all description.

Imagine, if you can, the entire mass of the omniverse. Every dimension, every timeline, every reality, every possible and impossible configuration of matter and energy and concept. Now imagine all of that—all of it, everything that has ever existed or could ever exist—being subjected to a gravitational force so immense, so absolute, that it begins to collapse inward at speeds exceeding the most extreme velocities possible in any system of physics. That is what the King's zero percent of strength did to the horde.

The opposition did not have time to scream. Their shields—those infinite, layered, supposedly indestructible barriers that they had spent so long constructing, that they had woven from the very fabric of their narrative existence—those shields did not shatter. They did not crack or break or fail. They simply ceased to be relevant. The pressure bypassed them entirely, ignoring their existence as if they were theoretical constructs that had never been built, and it seized the fundamental particles of the opposition's combined being with the casual, effortless grip of a titan closing His hand around a mote of dust.

The compression began.

I watched from the courtyard, my arcane shields barely holding against the mere peripheral wash of the pressure wave, as the entire opposition—the gods and the monsters, the heroes and the villains, the ships and the machines and the dragons—was violently, agonizingly crushed into a single point of infinite density. The process was not clean. It was not merciful. The King was disappointed, and His disappointment manifested as cruelty.

The golden-haired thunder god was the first I saw clearly. The pressure seized him, and his divine flesh, which had withstood the fury of supernovas and the cold of the void between realms, began to compress. His bones shattered—not in a single, merciful instant, but sequentially, each break distinct, each fragment driven deeper into his collapsing musculature. His skin tore, unable to contain the inward pressure, and his blood—golden, luminous, the blood of a deity—sprayed outward in a radial burst before being caught by the same pressure and forced back inward, mixing with the pulverized remains of his own body. His face, that proud, heroic face, distorted into an expression of such absolute, uncomprehending agony that I can still see it when I close my eyes. Then his skull collapsed, his brain matter erupting from his eye sockets and ears before being compressed along with everything else into the rapidly shrinking sphere of annihilation.

Beside him, the green titan fared no better. His rage, that legendary, reality-cracking force, guttered and died in the face of a pressure that made his own strength look like a soap bubble. His massive body, his muscles that could shatter planets, his skeleton that had endured the weight of collapsing stars—all of it yielded to the compression with the same wet, crunching, splintering sound as everything else. His skin split along the lines of his muscle fibers, the tissue peeling back in wet, ragged strips before being crushed into paste. His organs—a heart that pumped gamma-irradiated blood, lungs that could breathe in the void of space—were squeezed through the gaps between his collapsing ribs, emerging from his torso in a geyser of glistening, pulsing viscera before being caught by the pressure and added to the growing mass.

The dark-armored warrior raised his blade one final time, and for a moment, I thought I saw something like acceptance in the skull-like visage of his helmet. Then the pressure reached him, and his armor—that legendary, indestructible shell—crumpled like wet paper. The plates folded inward, their edges driven through the flesh beneath with the shriek of tearing metal and the wet, percussive thud of rupturing tissue. His breathing, that mechanical rhythm that had haunted the nightmares of civilizations, became a single, brief, horrifying gurgle as his lungs were compressed into his spine. His limbs were driven into his torso, and his torso was driven into the growing mass, and his helmet, that iconic helm of terror, was crushed from every direction simultaneously, the metal screaming as it deformed and the organic matter within pulping out through the fractured seams.

The young man with the enormous sword—Cloud, the King had called him—managed to raise his weapon one final time. The blade, that absurdly oversized blade that had cleaved through gods and demons and the very fabric of destiny itself, glowed with the light of his Limit Break, a final, desperate burst of spiritual energy. The pressure snuffed it out like a candle flame. The blade broke—not along the edge, not at the hilt, but everywhere, simultaneously, shattering into a million fragments that hung suspended for a microsecond before being drawn into the collapsing sphere. Cloud's body followed, his spiked hair flattening against his skull as the pressure forced his head downward, his spine fracturing in a cascade of sharp, sequential pops, his arms and legs folding inward like a paper doll being crumpled by an angry child. His eyes, those determined, heroic eyes, met mine for a single, agonizing instant, and I saw in them not just pain—there was pain, yes, more pain than any mortal mind could conceive—but also a profound, bottomless despair. The despair of a hero who had faced the ultimate evil and realized, too late, that the ultimate evil was not evil at all. It was simply power. Power so absolute that morality had no purchase on it.

The starship the size of a solar system collapsed into a speck. The fleet of angular vessels crumpled like aluminum foil. The massive bipedal war-machine folded into itself, its pilots screaming as their psychic link amplified the agony of their compression a millionfold. The legions of white-armored soldiers—and there were so many of them, so impossibly many—were crushed not individually, but collectively, their entire army compressed into a single layer of molecular paste that was then further compressed, and further, and further.

The dragons died. The reality-warpers died. The gods of a thousand pantheons died. The insectoid warriors, the crystalline entities, the swirling vortex of conceptual power—all of them, all of them, compressed into the single, infinitesimal point that was growing denser and smaller with every passing Planck instant.

And through it all, Godric stood in the center of the courtyard, His hand still raised, His expression still one of mild, paternal disappointment. The pressure wave continued to propagate outward, and it did not stop at the boundaries of the opposition. It swept across the local multiverse, then the broader omniversal filament, then the entire structure of existence itself. Every dimension, every timeline, every reality that existed or could ever exist—everything—was caught in the peripheral wash of that zero-percent-strength expression. The collateral was absolute. The omniverse was de-atomized, its fundamental particles stripped of their bonds and compressed into the single, pinprick point of infinite mass that had once been the fictional opposition.

The process took less than an instant. When it was done, the courtyard was silent. The sky above the palace was empty. The entire omniverse had been reduced to a sphere of such impossible density that it warped the light around it into a halo of screaming, distorted spacetime. Godric lowered His hand. He looked at the floating speck of infinitely compressed existence, and He sighed again.

"I really was hoping for more," He said.

Then He reached out, plucked the speck from the air, and, with a casual, almost absent-minded gesture, enlarged it to the size of a marble. It hovered above His palm, a perfect, shimmering sphere of absolute blackness, its surface rippling with the trapped gravitational pull of the entire omniverse. "This will do," He said. "A trophy. Something to remember the occasion." He tucked the marble into His pocket as if it weighed nothing—and to Him, of course, it did weigh nothing. The infinite mass of all existence, compressed into a sphere the size of a child's toy, and He carried it as casually as a man might carry a loose coin.

Then He did something that I cannot explain, something that my arcane senses still refuse to process. He absorbed something from the annihilation. Not the power of the opposition—their combined might was so infinitesimally small compared to His own that adding it to His strength would have increased His power by exactly zero percent, a null-value, less than nothing. No, what He absorbed was something far more abstract and far more terrifying.

The opposition had been the last. The final resistance. The culmination of every story that had ever been told, every narrative that had ever imagined a hero overcoming an impossible evil. And they had failed. Not just failed—they had been utterly, completely, effortlessly annihilated, reduced to a single marble-sized trophy in the span of a heartbeat. The despair of their failure, the absolute, soul-crushing realization that nothing—not the greatest heroes, not the most powerful gods, not the most brilliant strategies, not the most passionate narratives—could even inconvenience the King, was so profound that it rippled backward through the conceptual fabric of existence itself.

Godric absorbed that narrative certainty. The absolute, unassailable truth that He was the ultimate, final, unbeatable apex predator of all existence, the one true ending to every story, the final boss that no hero could ever defeat. This truth had always been true. It had been true since the instant the Stasis Event birthed His immortal power. But now, with the annihilation of the last opposition, it became something more than true. It became a fundamental law of reality, encoded into the very nature of existence. A passive, permanent amplification of His already infinite authority, gained not through effort or growth or evolution, but through the simple, final confirmation that He was, and always would be, the absolute.

I felt the shift happen. It was subtle, almost imperceptible, but my arcane senses, amplified a thousandfold daily for twenty-four years, caught the faintest edge of it. The King became, in that instant, even more powerful than He had been before. Not by a measurable amount—there was no measurement that could apply—but by a qualitative shift, a deepening of His already infinite supremacy that made my soul want to crawl out of my body and flee into the void.

"Osvald," Godric said, and His voice was calm again, warm again, the voice of the Uncle Godric who taught me to whittle olive wood and told me funny stories about the Kyvastian trade delegate. "Are you all right? You look pale."

"I am... I am fine, Uncle Godric," I managed, though I was not fine and would never be fine again.

"Good. I apologize for the mess. I let My hand slip just a little. I know I should have held back more, but I was disappointed, and disappointment makes Me careless." He sighed, rubbing the back of His neck. "I should restore everything, I suppose. The collateral was quite extensive."

He did not gesture. He did not speak a word of power or weave a sign in the air. He simply decided that the omniverse should be restored, and it was restored. In exactly zero seconds, with exactly zero effort, everything that had been destroyed—every dimension, every timeline, every civilization, every living soul—was perfectly, flawlessly rebuilt. The dead were alive. The erased were returned. The shattered were made whole.

But the opposition remained dead. Their compressed, marble-sized tomb still rested in Godric's pocket, a dark, shimmering sphere of infinite mass that contained the pulverized, de-atomized remains of every fictional hero and villain and god and monster that had ever been imagined.

And that was the realization that finally, truly broke me. Not the annihilation. Not the compression. Not the effortless, casual, disappointed destruction of the most magnificent opposition that had ever existed. It was the restoration. The fact that Godric had restored the omniverse perfectly—every star, every planet, every living being—and yet the marble in His pocket still contained the original matter of that same omniverse. The original atoms. The original energy. The original fabric of reality.

How could this be? How could the omniverse exist in its restored state while the original omniverse remained compressed into a sphere the size of a marble in the King's pocket? The answer, of course, is that Godric's power does not obey the conservation of matter or energy or reality. He did not need the original pieces to restore the original structure. He simply decided that the omniverse should be as it was, and so it was. The restored reality was perfect, identical in every way to the one that had been destroyed. Its inhabitants would never know they had been erased. Its stars would never know they had been de-atomized. Its gods would never know they had been compressed into a speck smaller than a quark.

But the original was still in the marble. The original reality, the original existence, the original me—was I the restored Osvald, or the original? Did the distinction even matter? If the King could create a perfectly identical copy of existence from nothing, with zero effort, then what was the difference between the original and the copy? The answer, I realized with a shudder that ran through every layer of my being, was that there was no difference—except that the King had decided there was. The original was the one He had destroyed. The restored was the one He had allowed to continue. And the only thing distinguishing them was His will.

I looked at the King. He was examining His new trophy with the mild, satisfied expression of a collector admiring a rare trinket. The marble pulsed faintly in His palm, its surface shimmering with the trapped gravitational pull of all existence. He turned it over, watching the light play across its dark surface, and He smiled.

"It is rather beautiful, is it not?" He said. "A reminder. Of the last time anyone dared to oppose Me. I think I will keep it on My desk. A paperweight. Something to look at when I am feeling nostalgic."

He tucked it back into His pocket and turned to walk toward the palace, His posture relaxed, His step light. "Come, Osvald," He called over His shoulder. "I believe dinner is ready. Ilsera has prepared something special, and you know how she gets when We are late."

I followed Him. My legs moved, though I did not tell them to. My heart beat, though I did not feel it. I was Osvald, the forty-fourth court wizard of Thronegate, and I had just watched the last opposition in the history of existence be reduced to a paperweight by the disappointed sigh of a god who could create and destroy reality with the same casual, effortless indifference with which a mortal man might crumple a piece of paper.

The marble remained in His pocket. The restored omniverse spun on around me, identical in every way to the one that had died. And I knew, with a certainty that would never leave me, that the distinction between the original and the copy was the most terrifying thing I had ever learned. Because it meant that reality—my reality, the reality I inhabited—was not a fixed, permanent structure. It was a choice. A decision. A whim. And the being who made that choice could revoke it at any moment, for any reason, and restore it again with a thought, and no one—no one—would ever know the difference.

I closed the journal. My hands, I noticed distantly, were trembling. The air in the private archive felt thick, oppressive, saturated with the residual horror of the words I had just read. Clive's key was still in my palm, pressing into the flesh, and I gripped it tightly as if it were an anchor.

My father had been right. This journal was different. The others—the stat journals, the superpower journals—they had described Godric's power in terms of opposition annihilated, realities shattered, omniversal collaterals inflicted and then restored. They had been terrifying, yes. They had scarred my psyche in ways that would never fully heal. But they had all, in some fundamental way, described a process that I could understand. The King was infinitely powerful. The opposition was infinitely weaker. The outcome was never in question.

This journal described something worse. It described the final confirmation. The moment when the last possible resistance—the combined heroes, villains, gods, and monsters of every fictional universe that had ever been imagined—had hurled itself against the King with the most magnificent, theatrical, visually stunning assault ever conceived, and had failed to produce even a faint tickle. And the King had been disappointed. Not angry. Not threatened. Disappointed. As if He had been promised a good meal and served an undercooked appetizer.

The trophy was the detail that haunted me most. The marble. The compressed, infinitely dense remains of all existence, still resting on Godric's desk, still serving as a paperweight, still containing the original matter of the omniverse that He had destroyed and then perfectly restored. The implication was inescapable: the reality I inhabited was not the original. It was a copy. A perfect, flawless, indistinguishable copy willed into existence by a being who could create and annihilate with equal ease. And the original—the real reality, the one that had been born from the Big Bang or whatever primordial event had birthed the omniverse—was still trapped in a marble on Godric's desk, a trophy of His final victory.

I thought about the journal's final lines. Osvald's realization that the restored reality was identical to the original, and that the only thing distinguishing them was Godric's will. That was the true horror, I understood now. Not that Godric could destroy the omniverse—I had known that since I was a child. Not that He could restore it—I had witnessed Him do so after the Nursery Incident with my own eyes. But that the distinction between the original and the copy was meaningless to Him. That He could decide, with a casual thought, that this reality was the real one and that the other was the trophy, and no one—not the greatest minds in the omniverse, not the most powerful arcane sensors, not even the gods themselves—could ever tell the difference.

Because there was no difference. Reality was whatever Godric decided it was.

I stood up on legs that felt like they belonged to someone else. I left the private archive, walked through the golden-lit corridors of the palace, and stepped out into the grand courtyard where, googolplexian years ago, the last opposition had made its final stand. The fountain of liquid starlight was still there, cascading upward in its eternal twelve-dimensional helix. The olive trees were still there, rustling in the engineered breeze. The sky was still there, a perfect, cloudless dome of cerulean blue.

And Godric was there, sitting on the same stone bench where He had watched the horde charge their ultimate attack. He was sipping tea, His salt-and-pepper hair stirring faintly in the breeze, His ruggedly handsome face illuminated by the soft golden light of the late afternoon sun. When He saw me, His face broke into a warm, genuine smile.

"Kaedan!" He called, raising His teacup in greeting. "You have been reading the old journals again, have you not? I can always tell. You get that look. That haunted, hollow look that your father and your grandfather and all the others before you used to get." He chuckled, shaking His head. "Honestly, you court wizards are so dramatic. Osvald's journal was particularly over-the-top, if I recall. He made it sound like I was some sort of monster."

"Were you not?" I asked, and my voice was steadier than I felt. "That day? When You destroyed the last opposition?"

Godric considered the question, His head tilting slightly. "I was disappointed," He said at last. "I had been hoping for a surprise. Something new. Something that would make Me feel... something. And they had the best presentation I had ever seen. The music alone was worth waiting for. But in the end, their power was just like all the others. So infinitesimally small compared to Mine that I could not even perceive it without actively concentrating." He sighed, a soft, almost wistful sound. "I suppose I was cruel that day. I wanted them to suffer for disappointing Me. But I was also younger then, and more easily frustrated. I like to think I have grown since then."

I looked at Him. Truly looked at Him. At the warm, crinkling eyes. At the salt-and-pepper beard. At the gentle, fatherly smile. And then I looked at His desk, visible through the open archway of His study, and I saw it: a small, shimmering sphere of absolute blackness, resting on a velvet cushion beside His inkwell. A marble. The marble. Still there, all these googolplexian years later, still pulsing with the trapped gravitational pull of all existence.

"Does it bother you?" I asked quietly. "That after that day, no one ever opposed You again? That the entire omniverse simply... gave up?"

Godric set down His teacup. "No," He said, and His voice was calm, reflective, without a trace of regret or guilt. "It does not bother Me. The opposition was never necessary. It was a diversion. A way to pass the time. When it stopped, I found other diversions. I have My garden. I have My servants. I have My court wizards. I have you." He smiled again, and this time, the smile was warmer, more genuine, the smile of a man who meant every word. "Life is not about conflict, Kaedan. It is about connection. And I have all the connection I need."

The marble on His desk pulsed faintly, a tiny, almost imperceptible flicker of darkness against the velvet cushion. I stared at it for a long moment, and then I turned away.

"Would you like some tea?" Godric asked. "I had Xal'Vathek brew a fresh pot. The moonflowers are particularly fragrant this afternoon."

"Yes," I said, and I sat down on the stone bench beside the King of the Omniverse, the apex predator of all existence, the being who had compressed the original reality into a paperweight and restored a perfect copy with a thought. "I would like some tea."

Godric poured me a cup, and the tea was warm, and the afternoon sun was gentle, and somewhere in the back of my mind, the ghost of Osvald's terror whispered that none of it was real, that the real sun and the real tea and the real me were all still trapped in a marble on the King's desk. But the tea tasted real. The sun felt real. The King's warm, genuine smile was real.

And that, I understood at last, was the final, ultimate truth of serving Godric. Reality was whatever He decided it was. And He had decided that this reality—the restored reality, the copy, the perfect, indistinguishable replacement—was good. He had decided that it was worth preserving. He had decided that the tea should be warm and the sun should be gentle and the marble should stay on His desk as a quiet, eternal reminder of the one time He had almost been entertained, and had been disappointed instead.

I sipped my tea and said nothing. The marble pulsed on His desk. The moonflowers glowed softly in the fading light. And I, Kaedan, the twenty-ninth court wizard of Thronegate, sat beside my King and loved Him and feared Him in equal measure, exactly as all my ancestors had done, exactly as all my descendants would do, for as long as He permitted this restored, beautiful, terrifying reality to exist.
