# The Ledgers of Annihilation

## Year 1,000,000

The weight of the leather-bound tome pressed against my palms like a slab of cold iron. My father had placed it in my hands not ten minutes ago, his weathered fingers trembling against the worn cover before he turned and left the chamber without a single word. I had expected a ceremony. I had expected a speech, perhaps some words of encouragement or warning. Instead, I received only silence and the soft click of the heavy oak door closing behind him. The silence was worse.

My name is Kaedan. I am twenty-two years old, and I am the newest Royal Court Wizard of Thronegate. The thousandfold daily amplification of the Awakening has been coursing through my veins for two years now, and I have already grown accustomed to the dizzying, terrifying sensation of my magical capacity multiplying beyond all mortal comprehension with every passing sunrise. My bloodline stretches back across a million years, an unbroken chain of wizards descending from Blake himself, the legendary best friend of our sovereign. I have heard the stories. Every child of my lineage hears the stories. But stories whispered by the hearth during winter evenings are not the same as the written records left by the men who witnessed the impossible with their own eyes.

The tome before me was the first of many. Its leather was ancient, preserved by enchantments so old that I could taste the residual mana clinging to the binding like old smoke. The title was burned into the cover in faded gold leaf: *The Ledgers of Annihilation – Volume I: Year 1 to Year 100,000*. I knew there were nine more volumes in the restricted archives beneath the palace, each one cataloging a hundred thousand years of witnessed horrors. The reading was mandatory for all new court wizards. My father had completed his reading fifty years ago, when he was my age, and he has never once spoken of what he learned within these pages. Now I understood why.

I opened the tome. The first entry was dated Year 50,000, written in a cramped, frantic hand that spoke of sleepless nights and trembling fingers. It was the journal of Joren, the High Court Wizard of that era, and it detailed an incident that I had never heard mentioned in any of the stories passed down through my family. The ink was faded but perfectly legible, and as my eyes traced the words, the chamber around me seemed to grow colder.

*Year 50,000 – The Incident of the Unyielding Fist*

*Recorded by Joren, High Court Wizard of Thronegate, descendant of Blake, for the mandatory education of all future wizards who will serve our eternal King. May these words prepare you for what you will inevitably witness. May they shatter your arrogance before the universe does it for you.*

*I have served Godric for thirty-one years. I am fifty-one years old, and my magical capacity has multiplied a thousandfold every single day since my twentieth birthday. I can unmake galaxies with a thought. I can rewrite the fundamental laws of thermodynamics across entire universal clusters. By any conceivable metric, I am a being of supreme, apocalyptic power. And yesterday, I learned that my power is less than nothing compared to an infinitesimal fraction of our sovereign's physical strength.*

*The opposition called themselves the Adamant Compact. They were a coalition of seven hundred thousand universes, each one populated by beings who had evolved under the crushing gravity of supermassive black holes. Their warriors were forged from condensed neutronium, their bones unbreakable by any conventional force. They had spent two hundred thousand years preparing for their assault on Thronegate, converting entire galactic clusters into a single, unified fortress of hyper-dense armor and conceptual reinforcement. Their shields were woven from the fundamental strong nuclear force itself, layered infinitely across dimensional boundaries. Their champions were beings of such immense physical density that their mere footsteps collapsed local spacetime into screaming singularities. They believed—truly, genuinely believed—that their physical durability could withstand anything. They had even developed a theory: if they could simply endure Godric's initial attack, if they could survive the first blow, they might find an opening, a weakness, a flaw in his invulnerability.*

*They did not understand. None of them ever understand.*

*Godric allowed them to gather. He allowed them to fortify. He permitted their existence for two hundred millennia because he was, in his own words, "curious about the tensile strength of my knuckles." He wanted to test his physical strength against the most durable opposition the omniverse could assemble. He did not plan to use any other aspect of his power. No magic. No speed. No durability. No passive auras. No immune system. No Weakness Paradox. No Gent. No shields. He simply wanted to throw a single punch.*

*When the Adamant Compact finally launched their assault, they did so with absolute confidence. Their fortress-armada materialized in the void beyond Thronegate's outer dimensional boundary, a construct so vast and dense that its gravitational pull began to warp the fabric of our entire multiverse. Trillions of neutronium warriors stood in perfect formation, their bodies gleaming with the cold light of collapsed stars. Their champion stepped forward, a being named Ur-Daekan, who claimed to possess a body so durable that even conceptual erasure could not scratch his skin. He issued a challenge.*

*Godric stood on the balcony of the grand observatory, his hands resting casually on the obsidian rail. He was wearing a simple dark tunic, his salt-and-pepper hair rustling gently in the artificial breeze. He looked at the armada with an expression of mild, detached curiosity. "They certainly are dense, aren't they?" he remarked to me, his tone conversational, as if he were commenting on the weather. Then he lifted his right hand.*

*I must pause here to explain something that you, as a newly awakened court wizard, must understand before you proceed. Godric's physical strength multiplies infinitely every fraction of a Planck second. This has been occurring for fifty thousand years. The mathematical framework required to describe his current strength does not exist. It is beyond layered infinities, beyond recursive eternities, beyond any concept of measurement that mortal or immortal minds have ever conceived. When I watched him curl his fingers into a fist, I understood that the motion he was making was purely theatrical. He did not need to make a fist. He did not need to move at all. But he enjoys the aesthetic of physical combat. It reminds him of his mortal days in Olympus.*

*What followed cannot be adequately described. I will try. I will fail. Read my failure and understand the truth.*

*Godric threw a single punch. He did not move from the balcony. He did not extend his arm toward the armada. He simply punched the air in front of him with zero apparent effort, a casual, almost lazy motion that would not have disturbed a candle flame. The infinitesimal fraction of his physical strength that he allowed to bypass his absolute concealment was so small that no mathematical notation exists to express it. It was not one percent of his power. It was not 0.000001 percent. It was a percentage so close to absolute zero that the universe itself could not distinguish it from nothingness.*

*The effect was total, instantaneous, and catastrophic. The shockwave of that single, effortless punch propagated across dimensional boundaries with such force that the Adamant Compact's fortress-armada did not merely shatter. It was unmade. The neutronium warriors, beings of such immense durability that they could withstand the heart of a collapsing universe, were instantly reduced to a state of sub-quantum dissolution. Their bodies did not break. Breaking implies fragments. There were no fragments. Every atom in their bodies was simultaneously and perfectly separated from every other atom, scattered across an infinite number of dimensional planes. They screamed, I think. The sound did not have time to reach us.*

*But the collateral damage was far, far worse. The shockwave did not stop at the armada. It propagated outward, a wave of pure kinetic annihilation that tore through the fabric of reality itself. The seven hundred thousand universes that comprised the Adamant Compact were not merely destroyed. They were retroactively erased. The shockwave moved backward through time, forward through time, and sideways through every possible timeline, ensuring that the Compact had never successfully assembled in any version of reality. The void left behind was so absolute that light itself forgot those universes had ever existed.*

*Further still. The shockwave continued, bleeding into the structures of neighboring multiverses. Thirty-two thousand additional universes—completely uninvolved, populated by peaceful civilizations who had never heard of Thronegate or the Compact—were instantly annihilated as a side effect of Godric's casual punch. They did not suffer. There was no time for suffering. One moment they existed, and the next they were simply not. Their absence was so complete that it created a wound in the causal fabric of the omniverse, a scar of nothingness that screamed in the void.*

*Godric lowered his hand. He looked at the emptiness where the armada had been, and he frowned. "Ah," he said, his voice tinged with mild annoyance. "I overdid it again."*

*Then, without ceremony, without effort, without even a gesture, he restored everything. The seven hundred thousand universes of the Adamant Compact rematerialized exactly as they had been before the assault, their trillions of warriors intact, their memories of the annihilation perfectly preserved. The thirty-two thousand collateral universes were also restored, their populations blinking in confusion, unaware that they had briefly ceased to exist. Godric had killed them all with a casual punch, and then he had brought them back in zero seconds, purely because he disliked the mess.*

*I stood on that balcony, my body trembling so violently that I could barely remain upright. The gap between my power and his was not merely infinite. It was an infinity of infinities, a chasm so vast that the word "gap" is a cruel joke. I was nothing. The entire omniverse was nothing. And Godric had not even noticed the exertion.*

*He turned to me, his warm smile returning, and clapped a hand on my shoulder. "Well, Joren," he said cheerfully, "that was a decent test of my knuckles. I think my strength has held up rather well over the years. What do you think?"*

*I could not answer. My throat had closed entirely. I simply nodded, my eyes fixed on the void that had briefly consumed thirty-two thousand universes, and I understood for the first time the absolute, soul-crushing horror of serving an apex predator who could unmake all of creation with a single, effortless punch.*

*— Written on the third day following the incident, with hands that still shake and a mind that still screams.*

*Year 80,000 – The Incident of the Unbroken Body*

*Recorded by Aldric, High Court Wizard of Thronegate, descendant of Blake, for the mandatory education of all future wizards. I have read Joren's account of the Unyielding Fist. I laughed when I first read it, in the arrogance of my youth. I am not laughing now.*

*The opposition called themselves the Final Eschaton. They were not warriors or gods or conceptual entities. They were a coalition of weaponized realities—entire universes that had been converted into singular, devastating attacks. Their strategy was elegant in its simplicity: if they could not match Godric's offensive power, they would simply bypass it entirely. They would not fight him. They would not engage his strength or his magic or his speed. They would target his body directly with attacks that ignored physical durability, that struck at the conceptual level of existence itself. They had spent four hundred thousand years converting their universes into weapons of absolute annihilation. The first universe had been compressed into a lance of pure ontological negation, a spear that could erase the very concept of durability from any target. The second universe had been forged into a blade of retroactive death, a weapon that killed its target before the blow was struck, rendering durability irrelevant. The third had been shaped into a curse of infinite recursion, a self-replicating attack that would multiply infinitely until the target's durability was overwhelmed.*

*Godric allowed them to complete their preparations. He allowed them to exist because he wanted, in his own words, to "see if my skin has any ticklish spots." He intended to test his durability. Nothing else.*

*When the Final Eschaton launched their assault, they did so with mathematical precision. The three converted universes struck simultaneously, their attacks coordinated to bypass physical, temporal, and conceptual defenses. The lance of ontological negation struck first, aimed directly at Godric's chest. The blade of retroactive death struck second, killing him before the first blow landed. The curse of infinite recursion struck third, ensuring that even if he survived the initial assault, the infinite multiplication would eventually overwhelm his durability.*

*None of them worked. None of them even registered.*

*Godric stood on a floating platform above the capital, his hands clasped behind his back, his posture relaxed. The three universe-weapons struck his body with the combined force of all possible annihilation, and they simply stopped. The lance of negation shattered against his skin like a soap bubble hitting a mountain. The blade of retroactive death found no past version of Godric to kill because Godric had no past he was not already occupying. The curse of infinite recursion multiplied infinitely, yes, but Godric's durability multiplied infinitely faster—every Planck second, his durability increased by an amount that rendered the previous Planck second's durability utterly insignificant by comparison. The curse was trying to fill an ocean with a thimble, and the ocean was growing faster than the thimble could move.*

*The collateral damage was immediate and incomprehensible. The shattered lance of negation bounced off Godric's skin and ricocheted into the surrounding omniverse. The force of the rebound was amplified by the perfect invulnerability of his body—the attack was not merely deflected, it was reflected with infinite force. The ontological negation that had been intended for Godric was instead cast across the dimensional boundaries like a scythe of absolute erasure. Entire clusters of innocent multiverses were instantly blotted from existence, their populations never having even known they were in danger. The blade of retroactive death, unable to find a past version of Godric, lashed outward and killed the past of everything else within its range. Thirty million years of cosmic history across thousands of universes was simply deleted, creating a paradoxical wound in causality that screamed with the agony of broken time.*

*Godric stood at the center of this apocalypse, completely untouched, completely unmoved, completely uninterested. He was looking at his fingernails. "My durability seems to have held up," he remarked to me, his tone casual. "I was hoping for a bit more of a challenge, honestly. Perhaps next time."*

*Then, with a thought, he restored everything. The erased universes returned. The deleted timelines rewove themselves. The paradoxical wounds healed instantly. The Final Eschaton had spent four hundred thousand years preparing the most devastating attacks in the history of the omniverse, and Godric's skin had not even registered their existence. His durability had not been tested. It had not been challenged. It had not even been noticed.*

*I returned to my chambers that night and vomited into my washbasin for three hours. The knowledge that our sovereign's body is so perfectly, so absurdly invulnerable that universe-level conceptual attacks cannot even tickle him is a weight that my mind cannot carry. I am writing this entry with a hand that still shakes, not from fear of what Godric might do to his enemies, but from the crushing awareness of my own absolute insignificance. I am a gnat standing on the shoulder of an infinite being, and I have just watched that being ignore a supernova.*

*— Recorded immediately following the incident, in a state of profound existential collapse.*

They would kill Thorin before the teleportation spell. They would poison the water his ancestors drank. They would unmake him retroactively, erasing his existence from the timeline so thoroughly that he would never have become the apex predator.*

The dead civilizations returned to life. The Chronos Concordance, however, was not restored. Godric had deemed them unworthy of continued existence, and so they remained erased, their entire civilization reduced to a footnote in a history that no longer included them.*

*I am writing this entry with hands that cannot stop trembling. I watched Godric outrun time itself. I watched him exist in ten thousand moments simultaneously, and I understood that his speed was not merely physical motion—it was the absolute negation of any limitation on his presence. He is everywhere he wishes to be, always, and no force in the omniverse can prevent it. The Chronos Concordance had spent three hundred thousand years preparing their attack, and it failed because Godric could simply be wherever they were, whenever they were, with zero effort and zero delay. *Year 500,000 – The Incident of the Unbound Senses*

*Recorded by Hadvar, High Court Wizard of Thronegate, descendant of Blake. I have read the accounts of my predecessors. I believed their words were hyperbole. I was wrong.*

*The opposition called themselves the Silent Ones. They were a civilization that had evolved in a universe with no electromagnetic radiation—no light, no heat, no visible spectrum. They perceived reality through senses entirely alien to our own, and they had spent millions of years perfecting the art of absolute stealth. They could move through any dimension without leaving a trace. They could exist in the gaps between thoughts, the spaces between moments. Even my thousandfold amplified perception, honed over sixty-eight years of daily multiplication, could not detect them. They were, for all practical purposes, nonexistent until they chose to strike.*

*Their strategy was simple: if Godric could not perceive them, he could not defend against them. They had studied his omniscience and concluded—erroneously, catastrophically—that his knowledge was limited to things that existed in some form of observable reality. They would strike him with weapons forged from the concept of imperceptibility, blades that could not be seen or felt or anticipated. Even if he was invulnerable, they reasoned, he could not defend against an attack he did not know was coming.*

*Godric allowed them to approach. He allowed them to believe their stealth was effective because he was curious, in his own words, "whether my senses could pick up something that didn't technically exist." He intended to test his senses. Nothing else.*

*Year 700,000 – The Incident of the Unbeatable Odds*

*Recorded by Orlen, High Court Wizard of Thronegate, descendant of Blake. I have read all previous entries. I have cataloged them, analyzed them, and prepared myself accordingly. None of that preparation was sufficient.*

They could adjust probability curves across infinite dimensional scales. They could ensure that any attack against them had exactly 0% chance of success. They could guarantee that their own attacks had exactly 100% chance of landing. They had never lost a conflict, not because they were stronger or faster or smarter, but because the mathematics of chance itself favored them with absolute certainty.*

*Their strategy against Godric was elegant. They did not attempt to overpower him or outsmart him or bypass his defenses. They simply adjusted the probability of his existence to 0%. If they could not kill him through force, they would unmake him through mathematical inevitability. They had spent seven hundred thousand years constructing their probability engine, a device that could rewrite the fundamental odds of reality itself. The device could guarantee any outcome they desired, and what they desired was Godric's absolute nonexistence.*

*Godric allowed them to build their engine. He allowed them to deploy it because he was curious, in his own words, "whether my luck stat could overcome absolute mathematical certainty." He intended to test his luck. Nothing else.*

*The Certainty Collective activated their engine on the first day of the Year 700,000. The device pulsed once, a wave of probability manipulation that swept across the omniverse, rewriting the fundamental mathematics of chance. The probability of Godric's continued existence was adjusted to exactly 0.000000... repeating to infinity. It was absolute. It was certain. It was mathematically irrefutable.*

*And nothing happened.*

*Godric's luck had been multiplying infinitely every Planck second for seven hundred thousand years. Luck is not probability. Luck is not chance. Luck is the universe's desperate, groveling need to favor a particular outcome regardless of mathematical impossibility. Godric's luck is so overwhelming that the universe itself will violate its own fundamental laws to ensure his convenience. When the Certainty Collective adjusted his existence probability to zero, Godric's luck simply adjusted it back to infinity. The probability engine registered his nonexistence as a mathematical certainty, and his luck countermanded that certainty with a force that shattered the concept of probability itself.*

Instead of adjusting Godric's existence probability to zero, it began adjusting the existence probability of everything else. The Certainty Collective watched in horror as their own probability of existence was recalibrated to zero by their own machine, which had been hijacked by the sheer, overwhelming force of Godric's luck. Their trillions of members, their millions of fortress-worlds, their entire civilization—all of it was mathematically designated as impossible, and the universe, eager to please Godric, moved to correct the discrepancy.*

*The Certainty Collective ceased to exist. They were not killed. They were not destroyed. They were simply deemed impossible by the fundamental mathematics of reality, and reality itself erased them to resolve the contradiction. Their worlds vanished. Their memories were deleted from the cosmic record. Their history was unwritten. They had attempted to use absolute certainty against Godric, and his luck had turned their certainty into annihilation.*

*The collateral damage was catastrophic. The probability engine's inversion did not stop at the Certainty Collective. It cascaded outward, destabilizing the fundamental mathematics of chance across infinite universes. Probability itself began to break down. Events that should have been certain became impossible. Events that should have been impossible became inevitable. Suns spontaneously extinguished. Planets randomly reversed their orbits. Living beings found themselves teleported across cosmic distances with no cause. The fabric of reality groaned under the weight of broken mathematics.*

*Godric stood in the center of this chaos, his luck passively protecting him from all of it, and he sighed. "Luck is such a fickle thing," he said, more to himself than to me. Then, with a thought, he restored the fundamental laws of probability across the omniverse. The suns relit. The orbits corrected. The teleported beings returned to their homes. The Certainty Collective, however, was not restored. Godric had found their certainty amusing but ultimately tedious, and he chose to leave them erased.*

I have no peers. I have no rivals. I have no one who can tell me when I am being unreasonable, or reckless, or cruel, or simply wrong—except my court wizards. You are not merely my arcane advisors, Jim. You are my conscience. My restraint. My connection to the mortal perspective that I lost ten thousand years before your ancestors first crawled out of the primordial soup. It is a heavy burden. Are you prepared to carry it?"

Jim was silent for a long moment. His eyes—the same gray-green as Blake's, I noted, still appearing in the bloodline after all these millennia—met mine with a directness that few beings in the omniverse could manage. "I am, Uncle Godric. I've been preparing my whole life. Even if I didn't always know what I was preparing for."

"Good," I said. "Then let us begin."

---

The first hidden task of a court wizard, I explained as we walked through the eastern corridors of the palace, was the management of my boredom. This was not a small task. It was not an occasional task. It was the fundamental, underlying responsibility that underpinned every other duty a court wizard performed.

"I am immortal," I said, my boots making no sound on the polished obsidian floor. "I do not age. I do not sleep. I do not require sustenance or rest or recreation. I exist, continuously and eternally, with absolute omniscience that shows me every detail of every moment of every reality in the omniverse. I have been doing this for one and a half million years. Boredom is not an inconvenience. It is an existential threat."

Jim walked at my left hand, his father at my right. The morning light filtered through the crystal windows, casting prismatic patterns across the floor. "An existential threat," Jim repeated. "To you, Uncle Godric? I don't understand. What could threaten you?"

"Nothing threatens Me directly," I said. "But My boredom threatens everything else. When I am bored, I seek entertainment. When I seek entertainment, I allow rebellions to form. I permit enemies to gather. I test My stats and My superpowers against whatever opposition the omniverse can assemble. And every single time, the collateral damage is catastrophic. Universes annihilated. Multiverses erased. Entire civilizations reduced to screaming, eternal torment as a side effect of My curiosity. My court wizards have recorded these incidents in the Ledgers for a million years, and every entry begins the same way: an opposition formed because I was bored, and I allowed it because I wanted to see what would happen."

Tom spoke for the first time since we had entered the corridor. "The task of a court wizard is to prevent those incidents whenever possible. Not by fighting our King's enemies—that is laughably unnecessary. But by keeping our King engaged. Interested. Distracted from the tedium of eternity by things that do not require the annihilation of civilizations."

"Exactly," I said. "Tom has spent sixty-two years doing this. He brings Me books from the far corners of the omniverse—not just reports or analyses, but novels, poetry, philosophy. Things that are genuinely creative. Things that surprise Me. He engages Me in conversation about topics that I have not already fully comprehended. He suggests new hobbies, new projects, new avenues of exploration. And when all else fails, he tells Me when I am about to do something catastrophically stupid and advises Me to exercise restraint."

We entered the grand hall, where the obsidian throne sat in its eternal silence, surrounded by the adamantine pillars engraved with the names of annihilated civilizations. I did not sit on the throne. I rarely sat on the throne anymore. It was too formal, too ceremonial, too much a reminder of the tyrant I had briefly pretended to be in Year 28—a prank gone wrong that had permanently traumatized a diplomat named Rogr and taught me the terrible weight of even my mildest expressions. Instead, I summoned three comfortable chairs from the ether, arranging them in a loose circle before the cold fireplace. We sat, the three of us, like family members gathering for a morning chat.

"The second hidden task," I continued, "is the management of My temper. I am not, by nature, an angry being. The rage I felt after the Fall of Olympus cooled long ago, replaced by a cold, hollow void that I have spent a million years learning to fill with warmth again. But I am capable of anger, and My anger is apocalyptic. Even a mild flicker of annoyance, if I fail to properly restrain My absolute concealment, can permanently traumatize a mortal mind. Tom has witnessed this."

Tom's jaw tightened. "I have, my King. The incident with the diplomat Rogr, which I was not alive to witness, but which I have read about in the historical records. The incident with the Tantrum of Ten Ticks, which I did witness. The incident with—"

"Enough," I said, raising a hand. "Jim does not need the full catalogue. The point is that My emotional state is not merely personal. It is geopolitical. Cosmological. When I am displeased, the omniverse trembles. Your job, Jim, is to recognize the signs of My displeasure before it reaches a threshold that might cause collateral damage, and to address the source of that displeasure before it escalates."

Jim leaned forward in his chair, his expression intent. "What are the signs, Uncle Godric? How do I know when you're... displeased?"

I considered the question. "The ambient mana in your immediate vicinity will begin to darken. The temperature will drop—not physically, but perceptually. The light will seem dimmer, the shadows longer. You will feel a pressure at the base of your skull, a sense of impending dread that you cannot explain. These are the passive emanations of My presence seeping through the absolute concealment. They are dangerous. If you feel them, you must act quickly. Distract Me. Engage Me. Remind Me of My own restraint." I paused, allowing a faint, self-deprecating smile. "Scold Me, if necessary. Blake was a master of it. He once threatened to strangle Me with his bare hands if I so much as furrowed My brow at a delegation. He was joking. Mostly."

Jim absorbed this information with the same focused intensity I had seen in his father, and his grandfather, and all the generations of Blake's lineage that had served me. "And if distraction doesn't work? If you're too far gone for conversation or scolding?"

"Then you escalate," Tom said, his voice carrying the weight of experience. "You remind Him of the consequences. Not the consequences to Himself—there are none—but the consequences to others. You remind Him that if He loses control, even briefly, innocent beings will die. Universes will be annihilated. The omniverse will tremble. And He will have to restore it all afterward, which He finds tedious. The threat of tedium is, in my experience, the most effective deterrent."

"Indeed," I said. "Restoration is tedious. I can do it instantly and effortlessly, but the principle of the thing annoys Me. I dislike having to clean up My own messes. Tom has used this against Me many times. He is very good at it."

Tom's expression remained carefully neutral, but I detected the faintest glimmer of pride in his eyes. "I have had sixty-two years of practice, my King."

We continued the morning in this manner—conversation interspersed with instruction, Jim asking questions and absorbing the answers with the sharp, amplified intelligence that would, in time, make him a formidable court wizard. I explained the third hidden task: the management of my loneliness. The fourth: the maintenance of my connection to mortal perspective, which required court wizards to share their own lives with me, their own struggles and joys and fears, so that I would not forget what it was like to be finite. The fifth: the delicate art of telling me when I was wrong, and the even more delicate art of surviving that telling.

But the true lesson of the day, the one that would define Jim's understanding of his role, came in the afternoon.

---

It began with a minor annoyance. A trivial thing. A report from the outer territories indicating that a newly subjugated universe was experiencing administrative difficulties—nothing catastrophic, nothing unusual, just the standard friction of integrating a resistant population into the Thronegate empire. I had seen this report a million times before. I would see it a million times again. It was, in the grand scope of my existence, less than nothing. An atom of an atom of an atom of irrelevance.

And yet, on this particular afternoon, it irritated me.

I did not know why. I did not need to know why. My emotional state was, as I had explained to Jim, not always rational. A million years of immortality had not erased the fundamental architecture of my mortal psychology—it had merely overlaid it with infinite power and absolute omniscience. I could still be irritated by small things. I could still be annoyed by repetition. I could still, despite everything I had become, feel the faint, prickling heat of displeasure when confronted with a problem that I considered beneath my attention.

The ambient mana in the grand hall began to darken.

Tom noticed immediately. His sixty-two years of experience had attuned him to the subtlest shifts in my presence, and the dimming of the arcane sconces was, for him, equivalent to a screaming siren. He straightened in his chair, his eyes flicking toward the scrying matrix that displayed the offending report. "My King," he said carefully, "the administrative situation in Universe 774-Beta is well within standard parameters. The governor is handling it. There is no need for your direct intervention."

"I am aware," I said, and my voice was flatter than it had been a moment before.

Jim noticed the change too. His young face, so eager and attentive, flickered with the first shadow of unease. He glanced at his father, then back at me. "Uncle Godric? Is something wrong?"

"No," I said. Then, more honestly: "Yes. A minor irritation. It will pass."

But the ambient mana continued to darken. The shadows in the corners of the grand hall grew longer, deeper, more substantial. The temperature did not drop—I was still maintaining my absolute concealment—but the perceptual sensation of cold began to creep into the edges of the room. Jim shivered involuntarily. Tom's jaw tightened.

"My King," Tom said, his voice now carrying the firm, almost scolding tone that he had perfected over six decades, "the irritation is not minor. I can feel it. The boy can feel it. You are displeased, and your displeasure is beginning to manifest. I strongly advise you to either address the source of the irritation or find a distraction before the bleed-through exceeds acceptable thresholds."

I looked at Tom. My expression was not angry—I was not angry—but it was not warm either. It was the flat, hollow expression of a being who had been doing this for a very long time and who was, in this particular moment, tired of doing it. "And if I choose not to?"

"Then I will remind you," Tom said, his voice steady despite the pressure building in the room, "of the Tantrum of Ten Ticks. Year 1,200,000. You were annoyed by a mathematical paradox in a subjugated universe's tax code. A tax code, my King. You allowed your annoyance to bleed through your concealment by a fraction so small it defied measurement, and the resulting metaphysical pressure annihilated seventeen thousand inhabited worlds. You restored them immediately, but the trauma of the annihilation persisted in the survivors' memories because you forgot to include the memory wipe in the restoration. You were annoyed by a tax code, and seventeen thousand worlds now have a cultural mythology about the day the sky went black and every living thing on their planets died. The day is called the Black Breath in their dominant religion. They worship you as a god of death and rebirth. You find this deeply embarrassing. I am reminding you of this because I know you do not wish to repeat it."

The silence that followed was absolute. Tom's words hung in the air like a physical presence, their weight pressing against the darkening mana. Jim stared at his father with an expression of pure, unadulterated awe—the awe of a young man who had just watched his father scold the apex predator of the omniverse and survive.

I stared at Tom for a long moment. The ambient mana continued to flicker at the edges of darkness. The shadows stretched. The cold pressed.

And then I laughed.

It was not a warm laugh. It was not the genuine, rough laughter of the morning garden. It was a sharp, surprised laugh—the laugh of a being who had just been caught in a moment of pettiness and who recognized, with rueful self-awareness, the absurdity of his own irritation. The ambient mana brightened. The shadows retreated. The cold dissipated. The grand hall returned to its usual warm amber glow, and Tom released a breath he had been holding for a very long time.

"Well played," I said. "You have been saving the Black Breath example for a special occasion."

Tom's expression remained carefully neutral, but I could see the relief in his eyes. "I have, my King. I am glad it was effective."

"Effective and humiliating," I said. "Which was, I suspect, the point." I turned to Jim, who was still staring at his father with an expression of reverent disbelief. "Do you see now, Jim? Do you understand what the hidden task truly entails? Your father just prevented a cosmological catastrophe by reminding Me of a previous cosmological catastrophe that I found embarrassing. He did this with words. With memory. With the accumulated wisdom of sixty-two years of managing My eccentricities. This is the job. This is the responsibility. This is what it means to be a court wizard to the apex predator of the omniverse."

Jim swallowed hard. His voice, when he spoke, was steadier than I expected. "I understand, Uncle Godric. I think. But I have a question."

"Ask."

"If Dad had been wrong? If the embarrassment hadn't been enough to snap you out of it? What would have happened?"

The question was a good one. A brave one. I met Jim's eyes and answered him honestly, because honesty was the only gift I could offer in return for his courage.

"Then I would have annihilated Universe 774-Beta," I said. "Not intentionally. Not maliciously. Simply as a passive expression of momentary irritation, a bleed-through of my true presence that would have erased the universe and everything in it before I consciously registered the act. And then I would have restored it, perfectly and instantly, because I do not actually wish to destroy universes over administrative difficulties. I am not a monster, Jim. I am a being with infinite power and the emotional regulation of a grieving mortal king who never fully recovered from watching his family die. Those two things are not always compatible. Your father's job—and soon, your job—is to manage the incompatibility."

---

The rest of the afternoon passed without incident. Tom continued Jim's instruction in the more mundane responsibilities of a court wizard—the arcane wards, the scrying matrices, the protocols for communicating with the outer territories. Jim listened and learned and asked questions, his young mind absorbing information with the amplified efficiency of the Awakening. I observed from my chair by the cold fireplace, offering occasional comments and corrections, but mostly simply watching. I enjoyed watching my court wizards work. It reminded me that the omniverse was not merely a tapestry of mathematical certainties, a closed system that I had long since fully comprehended. It was populated by beings who thought and felt and chose, who grew and learned and made mistakes, who carried within them the spark of something that I had lost long ago and could only experience vicariously through their eyes.

In the evening, after Tom had retired to his chambers and Jim had been shown to the quarters that would be his for the remainder of his training, I sat alone in the garden. The Olympian jasmine swayed in the artificial breeze. The fountain trickled in patterns that were now contemplative, subdued. The stars were emerging in the simulated sky above, each one a pinpoint of light that represented a real star in a real universe somewhere in my empire. I could have named every one of them. I could have described every planet orbiting every star, every civilization on every planet, every individual being in every civilization. I had known them all for longer than they had existed, and I would continue to know them long after they were gone.

The door to the garden opened. Jim stepped through, his young face hesitant, his hands clasped nervously before him. "Uncle Godric? May I speak with you?"

I gestured to the stone bench beside me. "Sit."

He sat. For a long moment, he said nothing, and I said nothing, and the silence between us was comfortable rather than tense. The fountain trickled. The jasmine swayed. The stars burned.

"What my father did this afternoon," Jim said finally. "Scolding you. Reminding you of your own embarrassment. It seemed... dangerous. Even knowing that you love us, that you value our bloodline, it seemed like a terrible risk. How does he do it? How do any of my ancestors do it, across all these millennia, without being destroyed?"

"He is destroyed," I said quietly. "Every time. In small ways. The terror of that moment—the moment when my displeasure is bleeding through and he does not know if his words will be enough to pull me back—erodes something in him. Erodes something in all of them. Your ancestors have written about it in the Ledgers. The cold dread. The hollow exhaustion. The slow, grinding awareness that they are nothing, that their power is a joke, that their existence is permitted solely by my whimsical mercy. They love me, and they serve me, and they are destroyed by me, over and over, in increments so small that they do not notice until they are old and gray and wondering where their youth went."

Jim was silent. The fountain trickled. The jasmine swayed.

"But they continue," I said. "They continue because they believe that I am worth serving. They believe that the omniverse I have built—the empire of order and protection and prosperity—is worth preserving. They believe that their sacrifice, their slow erosion, their incremental destruction, is a fair price for the security of infinite realities. And they believe that I love them."

I turned my head to look at Jim directly. "They are correct. I do love them. I loved Blake, my first court wizard, with a depth that I have never fully recovered from. I loved his son, and his son's son, and every descendant who has served me since. I love your father. And I love you, Jim, though we have only just begun to know each other as sovereign and wizard rather than as uncle and nephew. This love is not a mask. It is not a manipulation. It is the most genuine thing remaining in my existence. But it does not make me safe. It does not make my displeasure harmless. It does not make my power less infinite or my annoyance less catastrophic. It only makes the destruction more painful—for me, as well as for you."

Jim met my eyes. His gaze was steady, his expression composed, but I could see the weight of my words settling into his young bones. The same weight that his father carried. The same weight that every court wizard had carried since Blake first stumbled into the sanctuary and shattered my stasis.

"Uncle Godric," he said, "I am afraid. I have been afraid my whole life, even when I didn't know what I was afraid of. I grew up in the palace. I played in these halls. I sat on your knee and listened to your stories. And underneath all of it, there was always this... pressure. This vast, silent awareness that the man I called Uncle could unmake me with a thought. I thought that becoming a court wizard would make the fear go away. That understanding my role would give me control over it." He paused, a faint, rueful smile touching his lips. "Instead, I am learning that the fear is the job. That managing the fear is the hidden task beneath all the other hidden tasks. And that no matter how long I serve, no matter how skilled I become, the fear will never fully go away. Will it?"

"No," I said. "It will not. But it will become familiar. It will become a companion rather than an enemy. And in time, if you are strong—and I believe you are—it will become the foundation of a courage that no external threat can ever shake. Because if you can stand in the presence of the apex predator and scold Him for His pettiness, there is nothing else in the omniverse that can frighten you."

Jim was silent for a long moment. Then, slowly, his smile widened. "That's... actually kind of inspiring, Uncle Godric."

"I have my moments."

He laughed—a genuine laugh, warm and young and unbroken. The sound echoed across the garden, mingling with the trickle of the fountain and the faint rustle of jasmine in the artificial breeze. It was a good sound. A sound that I had not realized I needed to hear.

"Tomorrow," I said, "your father will teach you about the arcane protocols of the inner palace. The day after, you will begin your study of the Ledgers. You will read about the things I have done—the universes I have annihilated, the civilizations I have erased, the eternal torments I have inflicted on those who opposed Me. It will be difficult. It will challenge your love for Me in ways that you cannot yet imagine. But I want you to remember this moment. This conversation. This garden. I want you to remember that beneath the infinite power and the absolute omniscience and the cosmic horror, there is still a man who once loved his family more than anything in existence, and who lost them, and who has spent a million years trying to fill that loss with the descendants of his best friend. I am not a good man, Jim. I stopped being a good man a very long time ago. But I am trying. And you—your father, your ancestors, your descendants—are the reason I continue to try."

Jim rose from the bench. His eyes were wet, I noticed, though his expression remained composed. "Uncle Godric," he said, "I am honored to serve you. Not because I must. Not because the bloodline demands it. But because I want to. Because I love you. And because I believe—I genuinely believe—that you are worth serving."

I did not respond immediately. I could not. Something had closed in my throat, a sensation I had not felt in a very long time—the ghost of tears that could no longer fall, the echo of a sorrow that could no longer be expressed. When I spoke, my voice was rougher than usual.

"Thank you, Jim. Now go. Your father will be wondering where you are. And tell him—" I paused, considering my words. "Tell him that he has raised a fine son. A fine court wizard. A worthy heir to Blake's legacy."

Jim bowed—a formal gesture, but his eyes still held that familiar, irreverent warmth—and left the garden. I sat alone on the stone bench as the stars wheeled overhead and the fountain trickled and the jasmine swayed. The omniverse spread before my omniscient awareness, infinite and intricate and painfully, beautifully predictable. I had seen it all before. I would see it all again. But tonight, in this small garden on the eastern promontory of the eternal palace, I had seen something new: the face of a young man who was afraid and determined and brave, who loved me despite knowing what I was, and who had chosen, freely and fully, to be my conscience.

It was, I reflected, a good day.

And tomorrow—tomorrow I would test my magical offense against a particularly annoying coalition of reality-benders in the outer multiverses, and Jim would witness his first incident worthy of the Ledgers. The terror would return. The dread would settle into his bones. The slow erosion would begin. But tonight, there was only the garden and the fountain and the memory of a young man's laugh.

I closed my eyes and let the jasmine-scented breeze wash over me, and for a single, fleeting moment, I allowed myself to feel something that I had not felt since the Fall of Olympus: hope.
