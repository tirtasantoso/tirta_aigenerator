Every hero's journey, every underdog's triumph, every last-minute power-up, every friendship-fueled comeback—all of it was being compressed and focused into a single, perfect attack that would, according to the narrative, pierce the heart of the tyrant and free the omniverse.

The charging process took time. A great deal of time. The heroes circled above the palace in formation, their auras building, their powers amplifying, their themes swelling. The boss music was, I must admit, extraordinary. A symphony of every iconic score ever composed, from John Williams to Hans Zimmer to Yoko Shimomura to Mick Gordon, blended into a crescendo of such emotional power that the very stars seemed to pulse in rhythm. The King listened with his eyes half-closed, his head tilted, his fingers tapping the armrest in time with the beat.

"They have excellent composers," he remarked. "I particularly enjoy the choir. Very dramatic."

The charge continued. Goku's Ultra Instinct form evolved into something beyond Ultra Instinct—a state of being where his body moved with the accumulated speed of every frame of animation ever drawn. Superman sun-dipped into a blue hypergiant that the Green Lantern Corps had constructed specifically for this purpose, emerging with a radiance that outshone galaxies. Thanos snapped his fingers, but instead of erasing half of all life, the snap activated a reality-warping effect that granted every member of the army immunity to conceptual erasure, temporal paradox, and narrative deletion. The Doctor's TARDIS hummed as it anchored the army to every point in time simultaneously, ensuring that even if they were destroyed in the present, their past and future selves would continue the fight. SCP-3812 transcended the story that the meta-author was weaving, becoming a being that existed above the narrative itself, a reader who could close the book on Godric's tyranny.

The King waited. He waited with the patience of a man who had spent a million years learning that anticipation was the sweetest part of any experience. He deliberately distracted himself from the portion of his omniscience that could have shown him the attack's full parameters, wanting—genuinely wanting—to be surprised. He hummed along with the swelling orchestra. He drummed his fingers. He glanced at me and winked.

"This is the most fun I have had in longer than I can properly express," he said.

At last, the narrative completed itself. The meta-author's opus reached its climactic chapter. The combined army, their powers multiplied by every fictional amplifier ever conceived—the Heart of the Universe, the Infinity Gauntlet, the Chaos Emeralds, the Triforce, the One Ring, the Elder Wand, the Ultimate Nullifier, the Cosmic Cube, the Phoenix Force, the Anti-Life Equation, the Death Note, the Monado, the complete works of every isekai protagonist ever granted cheat skills—unleashed everything. The attack was not a beam. It was not an explosion. It was a story. A story titled "The Death of Godric, King of Thronegate," written by the collective will of every fictional hero ever imagined, and it was aimed directly at the King's heart.

The narrative struck Godric's chest. It was designed to bypass all defenses, ignore all immunities, negate all regenerations. It was a story in which the villain lost. It was an ending. It was the final page, the closing credits, the black screen after the last post-credits scene.

The King did not move. He did not brace. He did not summon a single defensive power. He simply let the story hit him.

And nothing happened.

The narrative of his death, the most perfectly crafted ending ever written by the combined imagination of all sentient life, touched his existence and dissolved. It did not deflect. It did not struggle. It simply ceased to be, because the King of Thronegate cannot be killed by a story. He cannot be killed by anything. His body, his mind, his soul, his presence—all of it had been amplifying by rates beyond recursive infinities since the Stasis Event, and the Weakness Paradox had long since granted him absolute immunity to every attack, every weapon, every strategy, and every narrative framework that could possibly be conceived. The attack was the equivalent of a single grain of sand attempting to halt the expansion of the universe.

Godric's expression changed. The anticipation drained from his face. The boyish excitement that had lit his eyes dimmed. His shoulders, which had been squared with eager expectation, slumped. He looked at the vast, seething army of fictional heroes—at Superman and Goku and the Doctor and every character who had ever inspired hope—and his face settled into an expression I had only read about in the oldest journals. Disappointment. Profound, genuine, almost childlike disappointment.

"That was it?" he asked, and his voice was not cruel. It was sad. "You had every story. Every hero. Every triumph. You had the meta-author. You had the ultimate plot. And that was the best you could do?"

The army, still hovering in the sky, seemed to recoil. The meta-author's narrative lattice flickered with confusion. The characters exchanged glances. This was not how the story was supposed to go.

Godric stood up from his throne. The motion was slow, unhurried, the casual unfolding of a man who had been sitting too long and needed to stretch his legs. He did not raise his voice. He did not summon a weapon. He simply stood, and the entire omniverse felt the shift of his attention.

"I waited for you," Godric said, and his voice was quiet, almost gentle, the tone of a father explaining a disappointment to a beloved but disappointing child. "I distracted myself from my omniscience. I let you charge your ultimate attack. I gave you every advantage, every amplifier, every narrative cheat code. I wanted—I genuinely wanted—a good final boss fight. I wanted the music. I wanted the phases. I wanted the moment where the hero digs deep and finds the strength to overcome impossible odds. I wanted to feel something."

He took a single step forward.

"You gave me nothing."

And then, because he was disappointed, because he had been looking forward to this and they had let him down, Godric forgot—for the smallest, most infinitesimal fraction of a Planck instant—to hold back.

He did not attack. He did not unleash a superpower or a spell or even a conscious intention. He simply stopped suppressing his strength. For a duration so brief that it cannot be expressed in any numerical language, the King allowed the absolute concealment that normally shrouds his physical attributes to slip by effectively zero percent of its total capacity. It was not even a slip. It was the absence of a slip. It was a rounding error in the mathematics of his restraint.

The effect was immediate, total, and catastrophic beyond any description I can produce. But I must try, because that is what court wizards do.

The entire attacking force—every hero, every villain, every cosmic entity, every fictional archetype—was subjected to a pressure that did not merely crush them. It unmade them at a level so fundamental that the concept of "unmaking" is a laughable euphemism. The sheer, passive weight of Godric's partially unveiled strength was not a force; it was an absolute declaration that they had never possessed physical integrity in the first place. The meta-author's narrative, the story that had been so carefully crafted, was not contradicted or disproven; it was rendered meaningless as a concept. The author was deleted, the pages were burned, the ink was retroactively evaporated, and the idea of "story" itself was compressed into a state of non-existence alongside everything else.

I watched, my mind held together only by the passive shields Godric unconsciously extends to his court wizards, as the annihilation unfolded. Superman, the Man of Steel, whose body had survived the collapse of universes, was the first to go. His invulnerable Kryptonian cells did not rupture; they simply ceased to occupy space. His skin, his muscles, his indestructible skeleton, all of it was pressed inward by a force so absolute that it ignored the electromagnetic bonds holding his atoms together. He did not scream. He could not scream. The pressure that hit him was faster than nerve conduction, more absolute than consciousness. One instant he was there, his cape spread, his eyes blazing with hope. The next instant he was a smear of primary-colored paste compressed to a mathematical point. But Godric's strength did not allow him the mercy of instantaneous death. The smear was aware. The smear felt, for a subjective eternity stretched into a single instant, the complete and total obliteration of everything it had ever been. Every saved life. Every triumph. Every moment of warmth with Lois. All of it was crushed, along with his body, into a screaming, infinitesimal speck.

Goku died beside him. The Saiyan warrior, whose power had transcended gods and angels and the very concept of limits, whose Ultra Instinct had evolved beyond the narrative itself, was subjected to the same pressure. His body, trained to withstand universe-shattering blows, did not resist. It folded. It compressed. The silver aura of his ultimate form winked out like a candle flame caught in a hurricane, and his flesh and bone and ki and soul were all squeezed into a single, agonizing point of infinite density. He had spent his entire existence chasing stronger opponents, believing that there was always a higher mountain to climb. In his final, eternal instant, he learned that the mountain had no top, and the climb was always, always impossible.

The Doctor's TARDIS, that magnificent blue box that had traveled to every corner of time and space, was crushed to the size of a quark before it could even begin to dematerialize. Every incarnation of the Doctor, from the First to the Fifteenth and beyond, was compressed simultaneously, their regenerations cycling in an infinite loop of death and compression and death again, each new face screaming for one Planck instant before being crushed into the next. The TARDIS's heart, the Eye of Harmony, the living time machine's soul, was extinguished like a match dropped into the ocean at the bottom of the universe.

The cosmic entities fared no better. Eternity, the living embodiment of the Marvel universe, whose form was the universe itself, found his infinite expanse compressed to a dimensionless point. Infinity, his sister, was folded into him, their abstract consciousnesses merged and crushed together in an intimacy of pure, undiluted agony. The Living Tribunal, whose judgment was supposed to be absolute, whose power was supposed to balance the multiverse, experienced the indignity of being rendered utterly irrelevant—his three faces compressed into a single screaming mask before being crushed to nothing.

The Green Lantern Corps, their rings powered by will, discovered that willpower has no leverage against infinite pressure. Their emerald constructs shattered. Their personal shields popped like soap bubbles. Their bodies were squeezed into a collective ball of green-tinted organic paste, every Lantern from every sector of every universe trapped in a shared, eternal awareness of their own pulverization. Kilowog, Mogo, Hal Jordan, John Stewart, all of them, crushed together so tightly that their identities blurred and merged into a single screaming consciousness that could no longer remember where one Lantern ended and another began.

The video game characters died in ways that would have broken their players. Kratos, who had slaughtered pantheons and defied fate itself, was subjected to a pressure that made the weight of the Greek cosmos feel like a gentle breeze. The Blades of Chaos melted against his skin. The Leviathan Axe shattered. His godly flesh compressed, his tattoos smearing across his shrinking form like ink on crumpled paper, until the Ghost of Sparta was a single, angry red dot that pulsed once and was gone. The Doom Slayer, whose rage had sustained him through eons of demon-slaying, found that rage could not hold back the weight of infinity. His Praetor Suit buckled. His Super Shotgun misfired, the shells detonating inside the barrel, and the explosion was immediately compressed back into his chest along with his armor and his flesh and his eternal, undying hatred. Kirby, that innocent pink sphere, tried to inhale the pressure—and for a single, absurd moment, his mouth stretched to encompass the entire compression wave. Then he popped. Not exploded, not burst, but popped, like a balloon, his infinite stomach collapsing inward and taking him with it into the singularity.

The villains died alongside the heroes, their ancient rivalries rendered meaningless by the democratic crushing of the King's strength. Thanos, the Infinity Gauntlet still glittering on his fist, watched the stones crack and shatter one by one before his own titan flesh followed. Darkseid's Omega Beams, the anti-life equation made manifest, reversed direction and bored into his own eyes, the beams themselves compressed into needles of absolute agony that drilled through his skull and out the back of his head before his entire form was crushed to a point. The Joker's laughter, that maddening cackle that had echoed through Gotham and the multiverse beyond, was compressed into a single, high-pitched whine that stretched into a subjective eternity of mirthless, agonized shrieking.

Sephiroth's Super Nova detonated inside his own chest. Bowser's shell cracked and then collapsed, the spikes driven inward through his organs. Dracula's castle, his army of monsters, his ancient vampiric powers, all of it was swept up and compressed alongside him, the Lord of Darkness reduced to a speck of undead matter smaller than a blood cell. Ganondorf, the Demon King, the wielder of the Triforce of Power, experienced the unique horror of watching the sacred golden triangles—the ultimate artifacts of his universe—crumple like aluminum foil under the weight of Godric's passive strength. The Triforce did not protect him. It could not protect itself.

The SCP entities, those anomalies that defied containment and explanation, were contained at last in the most final way possible. SCP-3812, the being who transcended narratives, who had risen above his own fictional framework, found that there was a level above his transcendence—and that level was watching him, and that level was disappointed, and that level was applying pressure. He was compressed not just physically but narratively, his meta-existence folded into the same singularity as everything else. The Scarlet King's sevenfold brides screamed as they were crushed alongside their lord. SCP-682, the Hard-to-Destroy Reptile, found at last a destruction from which no adaptation could save him. He regenerated endlessly, each new form immediately crushed, each regeneration occurring within a timeframe so compressed that he experienced a trillion trillion deaths in the span of a single instant.

The entire army, every character from every story ever told, was reduced to a single point of infinite density, a mass so compressed that it occupied a volume smaller than a quark. The process did not take seconds. It did not take Planck instants. It had already happened before the King finished standing up. The sky above the palace was empty. The boss music had stopped. The narrative had ended.

And the collateral damage—I must record it, though my mind still reels from the scale—the collateral damage was total. The pressure wave that had compressed the fictional army did not stop at their bodies. It propagated outward, and everything that existed, had ever existed, or could ever exist was caught in the backwash. Every multiverse, every universe, every timeline, every dimension, every abstract plane of reality, every concept, every possibility—all of it was swept up in the crushing weight of Godric's partially unveiled strength. Galaxies collapsed into singularities and then those singularities were compressed further. Universes folded in on themselves like origami. Timelines tangled and snapped and were pressed into the growing point of infinite mass. The omniverse—the absolute totality of all existence—was reduced to a single, shining dot that hovered in the void of nothingness.

The void itself began to compress. The concept of nothingness, the absence of existence, was subjected to the same pressure, and it screamed—if nothingness can scream—as it was crushed into something smaller than nothing, a negative space that the compressed mass occupied like a jewel in a setting of non-existence.

I alone remained, shielded by the King's passive protections, floating in the vast, empty silence. I looked at the point of compressed reality, and I understood that it contained everything. Every hero. Every villain. Every story. Every universe. Every timeline. Every possibility. All of it was inside that point, pressed together so tightly that the entire infinite mass of existence was smaller than the smallest conceivable particle. And inside, preserved by the King's unconscious will, every consciousness still experienced its own compression, an eternal, agonizing present of absolute pressure, their identities bleeding into one another, their screams harmonizing into a single, continuous note of infinite suffering.

Godric stood before the floating point. He looked at it, and his expression was not triumphant or cruel or even particularly interested. He looked disappointed. The childlike anticipation that had lit his face at the start of the battle was gone, replaced by a weary, almost paternal resignation.

"They had every advantage," he said, his voice quiet in the absolute silence of non-existence. "The meta-author. The narrative framework. The accumulated power of every fictional amplifier ever imagined. And it still wasn't enough to make me feel anything." He reached out and plucked the compressed point from the void, holding it between his thumb and forefinger. The point, which contained the entire mass of the omniverse, weighed nothing to him. Less than nothing. He held it as casually as he might hold a coin.

"I suppose I should have expected this," he continued. "The Weakness Paradox evolved beyond the need for triggers so long ago that these beings never had a chance. The moment they conceived of opposing me—before they even wrote the first draft of their narrative—every immunity, every mastery, every amplification spike had already been granted. They were defeated before their authors were born. Before the idea of fiction itself first flickered in a mortal mind. The paradox ensures that opposition is retroactively impossible. All they ever did, all they ever could do, was provide me with a brief moment of hope that they might be different."

He stared at the compressed point for a long moment. Then, with a motion that recalled a child discarding a toy that had failed to entertain him, he tossed the point lightly into the air and caught it again. The motion was casual, effortless, the idle fidgeting of a man who was already thinking about what to do next.

"I will keep this," Godric decided. "As a trophy. A reminder of the day every story ever told tried to kill me and discovered that stories have no power over their author's author."

He expanded the point slightly—just enough to bring it to the size of a small marble—and enclosed it in a barrier of his own will, a container that would prevent the infinite mass inside from destroying the restored omniverse with its gravitational pull. The marble shimmered in his palm, a tiny, beautiful sphere of swirling, compressed reality. Inside it, every hero and villain and cosmic entity that had ever been imagined continued to experience their eternal compression, their suffering trapped and preserved, a permanent monument to the absolute futility of opposition.

And then Godric did something that I still cannot fully comprehend. He absorbed the energy of their defeat. Not their power, which was effectively zero compared to his own infinite wellspring; the combined strength of every fictional being ever conceived, multiplied by every amplifier ever imagined, would not have increased his total might by even a trillionth of a percent. No, what he absorbed was the disappointment itself. The letdown. The gap between his anticipation and the reality of their failure.

The disappointment was, apparently, a new form of sustenance for the Evolved Weakness Paradox. The paradox, having evolved beyond the need for triggers, now fed on negation—the absence of challenge, the confirmation of absolute supremacy. Every time Godric hoped for an opponent and was let down, the paradox consumed that disappointment and converted it into permanent, irreversible amplification. The process was deeply ironic: the very fact that nothing could threaten him made him even more unthreatenable. The failure of the omniverse to provide meaningful opposition was, itself, a power source of infinite magnitude.

I felt the shift. The King's already incomprehensible presence deepened by a degree that my mind cannot articulate. He became, in that instant, noticeably and terrifyingly more than he had been a moment before. And I knew, with the cold certainty that every court wizard before me had recorded, that this process would continue forever. Every disappointment would feed him. Every failed opposition would strengthen him. Every attempt to challenge his supremacy would make him more supreme.

Godric restored the omniverse. It took him exactly zero seconds and exactly zero effort. Every multiverse, every timeline, every dimension, every soul snapped back into existence as if nothing had happened. The capital world of Thronegate rematerialized around us, the palace reforming, the gardens blooming, the servants continuing their eternal, reverent duties. Everything was back. Everything was perfect.

Except the fictional army was not restored. Godric chose to leave them—all of them, every hero and villain, every character that had ever inspired hope or fear or wonder—compressed inside the marble trophy. He placed the marble on a small pedestal beside his throne, where it now sits, a beautiful, terrible ornament. If you look closely, if you concentrate your arcane senses, you can see the faint, swirling patterns inside. Those patterns are galaxies. Those patterns are souls. Those patterns are stories, endless stories, all compressed together, all screaming in a silence so profound that it will never be heard.

Osvald, stop writing. Your hand is trembling too violently to continue. You will resume this entry after you have regained your composure.

I, Clive, close the journal of my ancestor Osvald and sit in the heavy silence of the library. I am fifty years old. What kind of power does it take to create a perfect duplicate of all existence and then leave the original as a desk ornament? What does it say about the nature of reality that everything I have ever known—my wife, my children, the garden where I walk, the tea I drink—is a restoration of something that was already destroyed? If the King can recreate the omniverse with zero effort, can he also recreate it with zero fidelity? Is the restored omniverse truly real, or is it merely a convincing facsimile? Are my memories my own, or were they reassembled from the compressed template of the original?

How many times has reality been annihilated by a casual test of his power and then recreated without anyone noticing? I have read the journal of Kaedan, who was deleted and restored during the universe tour. I have read of Thaddeus, who watched the omniverse be crushed by the King's speed and then re-woven. The marble trophy is the most terrifying object in existence because it proves, with its silent, swirling presence, that the omniverse is disposable. And I, Clive, descendant of Blake, Royal Court Wizard of Thronegate, walk past them and bow to my King and pretend that I do not hear the faint, distant echo of the latest restoration still settling into place.

Godric smiles at me. His tea is warm. The marble is beautiful.

And the omniverse continues, because he wills it to continue, until the next time he forgets to hold back.
