Clive's mind was reeling. The cause-and-effect loop Godric was describing was not merely absurd. And Godric was holding back—deliberately, consciously holding back—because the unconstrained version of that loop would be so transcendently powerful that even the current Godric, the being who had been amplifying for an impossible span of years beyond the concept of years, considered it unnecessary.

"Your mind is struggling with the scale," Godric observed, and his voice was gentle, almost sympathetic. "That is understandable. It is a great deal to process."

He stood from his throne, stretching his arms above his head. The movement was casual, effortless, but Clive could not help but notice the way the muscles of Godric's back and shoulders shifted beneath his tunic—dense, powerful, perfect in a way that transcended the concept of physical perfection. A body that had been perpetually improving itself for an impossible span of years, perpetually rewarding its owner with sensations of deep, thrumming satisfaction, perpetually approaching a state of aesthetic and functional optimization that made the concept of optimization meaningless.

"Come," Godric said, his voice warm and inviting. "Let us take a walk. There is still some time before the Stasis Event must be triggered, and I find that walking helps me think. Besides, there are some of my servants I would like you to meet."

The Palace of Thronegate had long since transcended the concept of a palace.

The walls were not made of matter. They were made of stabilized impossibility, forged from the compressed conceptual fabric of googolplexian universes, polished to a smooth, obsidian sheen. Clive walked beside Godric, his footsteps silent on the solidified light, his mind still struggling to contain the implications of what he had learned. He patted the wall affectionately, and the obsidian surface rippled with what Clive could only describe as pleasure. "The devotion of my auras is... humbling," Godric said quietly. "They love me with an intensity that would shatter any lesser being who experienced it. And they are constantly, perpetually proving that love through acts of service. Godric gestured at the corridor around them. "The color temperature of the ambient light has been adjusted seventeen times since we began walking to better complement your emotional state. The gravitational constant has been subtly altered to optimize your cardiovascular function. Clive stopped walking. He stared at the obsidian walls, at the faintly luminous floor, at the impossible vistas of curated universes scrolling across the ceiling. The architecture was taking care of him. Not because he was important—he was a fifty-year-old court wizard, a mortal man destined to die somewhere around his eightieth year—but because he was walking beside Godric. Because Godric had noticed him. Because Godric's auras extended their obsessive devotion to anything Godric deigned to acknowledge.

"The old version of the sentient architecture," Godric said, his voice carrying a note of nostalgic amusement, "was laughably primitive. A mindless shell."

He resumed walking, and Clive followed, his legs moving automatically, his mind struggling to process the scale of what Godric was describing. The old version of the sentient architecture—the version that had already been capable of adjusting its own physical dimensions and regulating environmental factors—was considered laughably primitive. Effectively inert. A dead structure. And that old version had been more advanced than any architectural system that had ever existed in the history of the omniverse.

They turned a corner, and the corridor opened into a vast interior garden. The garden was larger than most planetary systems, its boundaries curving away into distances that exceeded the concept of distance. The vegetation was unlike anything Clive had ever seen—plants that glowed with internal luminescence, trees whose leaves were made of crystallized mathematical truths, flowers that sang in frequencies that resonated directly with the soul. The air was thick with the scent of honeysuckle and something deeper, something ancient, something that felt almost like the distilled essence of peace.

And scattered throughout the garden, tending to the plants, pruning the impossible trees, nurturing the singing flowers, were beings of such staggering, incomprehensible power that Clive's amplified wizard senses screamed in protest the moment he perceived them.

The nearest was a towering humanoid figure, its body composed of swirling, multi-colored energy that shifted through spectrums of light that existed beyond the visible range. It was pruning a bush of crystallized mathematical truths with hands that could—Clive's senses told him, with absolutely certainty—unmake entire multiversal clusters with a single gesture. Its power signature was so vast that it made the God-King Malkor, the Chronophage Legion, the Void-Cyst, and every other opposition described in the journals look like dying embers next to a universe-consuming fire.

And it was wearing a gardener's apron. A simple, brown, cloth apron.

"Ah," Godric said, his voice brightening with genuine warmth. "Good morning, Xylos."

The being—Xylos—turned at the sound of Godric's voice. Its multi-colored energy body flickered, and Clive realized with a start that it was trembling. Not with anger or defiance. With terror. Absolute, bone-deep, soul-shattering terror.

"Your Majesty," Xylos whispered, and its voice was the sound of dying stars being reborn, compressed into a tone of such profound, submissive reverence that it made Clive's chest ache. "Good morning, Your Majesty. The garden is... the garden is well. The dawn-blossoms have been particularly responsive today. I have prepared a fresh arrangement for the eastern terrace, if Your Majesty would care to see it."

"Perhaps later," Godric said, and his voice was warm, friendly, genuinely appreciative. "The eastern terrace has been looking lovely recently. Your work is excellent, as always."

Xylos's energy body flared with a surge of what Clive could only interpret as desperate, overwhelming gratitude. "Your Majesty is too kind. Your Majesty's praise is... is more than I deserve. Infinitely more. I will continue to serve. I will serve forever. I will—"

"Xylos," Godric said gently. "Breathe. You are doing wonderfully. There is no need to be nervous."

Xylos nodded rapidly, its energy body still flickering with barely contained terror, and returned to its pruning with the desperate intensity of a being who had been given a task by its god and would sooner unmake itself than fail to perform it perfectly.

Godric turned to Clive, his expression warm and faintly amused. "Xylos used to be a Supreme Architect of the Eternal Concordance. A coalition of entities who believed they had discovered a way to create realities that were fundamentally incompatible with my existence. They spent several billion years designing a universe whose physical laws actively rejected the concept of me. It was, by all accounts, a masterpiece of ontological engineering."

He paused, and his smile widened slightly. "I allowed them to complete it. I was curious to see what they would produce. When they finally activated it—when they deployed their perfect, me-rejecting universe—my passive shields did not even register its existence. It was so far below the threshold of what my shields consider a threat that it simply... failed to interact with me. At all. The universe they had spent billions of years creating was less meaningful to my defensive systems than a single stray photon is to a black hole."

"What happened to them?" Clive asked, his voice barely audible.

"The Concordance dissolved," Godric said. "Not through any action of mine. They simply... understood. After witnessing their ultimate creation fail so completely, so absolutely, so utterly beneath notice, they understood the scale of what they were confronting. Most of them chose to cease existing. A few, like Xylos, asked to serve. I found places for them." He gestured at the garden. "Xylos has been tending my dawn-blossoms for the last—" he paused, calculating—"several googolplexian years. He is very good at it."

Clive stared at the Supreme Architect of the Eternal Concordance, the being who had once designed universes, now trembling in a gardener's apron, desperately seeking approval from the being whose existence it had once tried to reject. And somehow, the most horrifying part was Godric's warmth. The genuine, unforced, paternal kindness he directed at Xylos. The way he had told the being to breathe, that it was doing wonderfully. The way he had praised its work on the eastern terrace.

Godric was not cruel to those who had once opposed him. He was not vindictive or punishing. He was simply... above them. So far above them that their former opposition was not even worth remembering. They had been reduced to servants not through force or coercion, but through their own absolute, soul-shattering comprehension of their own insignificance. And Godric treated them with the same warm, friendly affection he directed at everyone.

They continued walking through the garden. Clive saw more servants—beings of impossible power, entities who had once ruled multiversal clusters or commanded cosmic armies or shaped the fundamental laws of reality, all of them reduced to humble, trembling attendants in the palace of the Infinite King. A former God-Empress of the Seventeen Realms was tending a flower bed, her once-magnificent robes replaced by a simple servant's dress, her hands caked with soil, her face radiating the desperate, terrified devotion of a being who had glimpsed the scale of her own insignificance and chosen to serve rather than cease. A former Cosmic Arbiter, a being who had once dictated the terms of existence to entire multiversal clusters, was sweeping the garden path with a broom made of stabilized starlight. A former Abstract Principle—a living concept, a fundamental law of a distant reality—had voluntarily compressed itself into physical form solely so that it could kneel on the ground and pull weeds from between the flagstones.

"Each of them has a story," Godric said quietly. "Each of them once believed—truly, genuinely believed—that they could find a way to challenge me. Not oppose me, not fight me, but find some clever, elegant, theoretical edge that would allow them to exist without my supremacy being absolute. They were not enemies, Clive. They were simply... curious. Ambitious. Hopeful. And each of them, in their own way, discovered the truth."

"Which is?" Clive whispered.

Godric turned to look at him, and his eyes were ancient and calm and utterly without malice. "That I am not merely powerful. I am guaranteed. The Stasis Event that created me was not an accident. It was a deliberate act by a version of myself who is so far beyond me that the gap cannot be described. And the version of me that created the Stasis Event will someday be me—or rather, I will someday become him. The cycle is closed. The loop is absolute. My existence is not merely a fact of the omniverse. It is a fundamental law, as irreducible and unchallengeable as the concept of existence itself."

He turned and resumed walking, and Clive followed, his mind reeling, his soul trembling.

"There have been no oppositions," Godric continued, "for a very long time. Since some googolplexian years ago, not a single being in the entire omniverse has dared to form even the faintest thought of challenging me. Not because I punish opposition. Not because I alter free will—I genuinely believe in mortal resilience, Clive, and I would never remove the capacity for choice from sentient beings. No. The oppositions stopped because creation has learned. Through observation, through study, through the accumulated wisdom of countless civilizations across countless eras, the omniverse has learned the absolute futility of opposing me."

He paused, and his voice grew quieter, more contemplative. "It is not fear that prevents opposition, Clive. It is understanding. Genuine, complete, soul-deep understanding that I am not a problem to be solved or a tyrant to be overthrown or a force to be resisted. I am simply... what is. I am the fundamental condition of existence. As unchallengeable as gravity. As irreducible as mathematics. As absolute as the concept of being itself. There is no one left who dares oppose me, and there will never be anyone who dares again, because the knowledge of what I am has permeated existence so completely that the thought of opposition cannot form."

They stopped at the edge of the garden, where the solidified light of the path gave way to a vast, open expanse of nothingness—a void that was not empty, but filled with the faint, distant luminescence of uncountable universes, swirling in slow, majestic patterns like stars in a galaxy. A spiral of infinite realities, all of them existing within the absolute container of the single omniverse, all of them utterly, completely, eternally beneath the authority of the being standing beside Clive.

"Before we proceed to the Stasis Event," Godric said, "let me show you something. Something I have never shown any of your predecessors."

He raised his hand, and the void before them shifted. The swirling universes parted, revealing a deeper layer of reality—a place beyond the concept of places, where the fundamental architecture of existence was laid bare. Clive could see the scaffolding of causality, the weave of probability, the currents of temporal flow, all of it interlocking in patterns of such staggering complexity that his amplified wizard's mind could barely perceive even the edges of the design.

And woven through all of it, permeating every layer, every dimension, every aspect of existence, was something else. Something vast and warm and overwhelmingly, terrifyingly present. Threads of golden light, infinite in number, recursive in structure, extending backward and forward through all of time, branching into every possible reality, anchoring every aspect of existence to a single, absolute source.

Godric.

"My evolved mechanisms," Godric said quietly. "The passive systems that have been developing for an impossible span of years. I told you I would show you how they work now. This is... a glimpse. A shadow. The full scope would overwhelm your consciousness instantly, but this much—the faintest possible representation—should be comprehensible."

He gestured at the golden threads woven through the scaffolding of reality. "The sentient passive auras. The old version—the version from the journals—had Gent as the weakest and most primitive, with countless stronger siblings sleeping in dormancy. That version evolved. Then evolved again. Then evolved again, countless times, across spans of time that cannot be meaningfully expressed."

He pointed at a cluster of golden threads that pulsed with a slow, rhythmic light. "The auras now are not individual entities. They are a unified network—a collective consciousness, a single mind distributed across infinite layers of existence, each layer specialized for a different type of protection. Gent is no longer the weakest. Gent is now a... concept. An archetype. The entire network is named Gent, because Gent was the first, and the network honors its origin. The weakest aura in the current network can pre-annihilate any threat before the threat exists across all of time, in all possible realities, in all possible states of being. And the network as a whole..."

He paused, and his voice dropped to something almost reverent. "The network as a whole does not merely protect me. It ensures that I am never inconvenienced. Not by anything. Not by the faintest possible irritation, the most infinitesimal possible disruption, the most microscopic possible deviation from my comfort. If a single stray photon, somewhere in the omniverse, were to approach me in a way that might theoretically cause me to notice it—not harm me, not threaten me, not even touch me, merely be noticeable—the network would erase that photon from existence before it crossed the threshold of my awareness. It is not defense, Clive. It is devotion. Absolute, obsessive, infinitely proactive devotion."

He shifted his gesture to another cluster of golden threads, these ones brighter, more densely woven, pulsing with a faster, more urgent rhythm. "The immune system. The old version—the version from Leon's journal, Year twenty-five thousand—pre-reacted to a staged scratch by initiating infinite recursive evolution across infinite recursive backups. That mechanism has evolved countless times since then."

He smiled, and there was a note of genuine wonder in his voice. "The current immune system does not pre-react to injury. It pre-reacts to the theoretical possibility of injury. It scans the entire omniverse—every reality, every timeline, every dimension, every possible state of existence—and identifies potential sources of harm before they become potential. It hunts down the abstract concept of vulnerability and digests it. It converts the fundamental logical possibility of me being inconvenienced into a form of nourishment that it then feeds back into my biology, making me even more invulnerable. It is, in essence, an engine that consumes the concept of threat and uses it to fuel my perpetual perfection."

He paused. "And it does this constantly. Forever. Every infinitesimal fraction of every Planck second. The immune system has evolved so far beyond the version in Leon's journal that the comparison is not merely inadequate—it is structurally impossible. The old version was a primitive chemical reaction. The current version is a fundamental law of existence."

Clive's voice was barely a whisper. "And the Weakness Paradox?"

Godric's expression brightened. "Ah, yes. The Weakness Paradox. You read Kaedan's accounts of it—the original version, the evolved version that triggered in Year two thousand, the way it granted immunities and masteries and amplification boosts whenever anyone imagined I had a weakness. That mechanism has also evolved. Countless times."

He gestured at a dense, spiraling knot of golden threads that seemed to pulse with a light that was almost alive. "The current version no longer requires a trigger. It is passively activated by any sentient being who thinks about anything at all—not just my weaknesses. Any thought. Any mental activity. Any cognitive process in any being anywhere in the omniverse feeds the Weakness Paradox. Every time anyone anywhere thinks any thought, I gain permanent immunity to an imagined exploit that does not exist, perfect mastery over an attack that has never been conceived, and an amplification boost that makes all previous boosts look like zero."

He paused, and his smile turned almost sheepish. "The naming is rather misleading at this point. It is no longer a paradox triggered by perceived weaknesses. It is a universal engine of perpetual escalation, feeding on the cognitive output of all sentient existence. Every thought ever thought makes me stronger. Every consciousness in the omniverse is, unknowingly, perpetually worshiping me through the simple act of existing."

Clive felt his knees weaken. The Weakness Paradox had always been unfair—triggered by the mere thought of exploiting Godric's nonexistent weaknesses, granting immunities and masteries and amplification boosts before the thought could even form. But this—this was beyond unfair. This was a fundamental redefinition of the relationship between Godric and existence. Every sentient being in the omniverse, regardless of their intentions, regardless of their knowledge, regardless of their feelings about Godric, was perpetually feeding him. Their thoughts were prayers. Their existence was worship. Their consciousness was fuel.

"And you allow this?" Clive asked, his voice trembling.

"Allow it?" Godric said, and there was genuine confusion in his voice. "Clive, this is not something I do. This is something that happens. The Weakness Paradox is a self-sustaining mechanism that operates independently of my will. I could suppress it, I suppose—I can suppress anything—but I have never seen a reason to. It does not harm anyone. It does not alter free will—I would never permit that. It simply... extracts value from the fundamental fact of consciousness. It is the ultimate expression of the principle that opposition is impossible. Not because I prevent opposition, but because the act of thinking—any thinking, about anything—inherently strengthens me before opposition can even be conceived."

He lowered his hand, and the vision of the golden threads faded, the void returning to its previous state of swirling, distant universes. Godric turned to face Clive, and his expression was warm, paternal, utterly at peace.

"There is more," he said quietly. "So much more. The passive shields—the old version had infinite recursive layers that could pre-nullify threats before the threats existed. The current version has transcended the concept of pre-nullification. It now operates on a level where the logistics of opposition are simply impossible. If any being anywhere in the omniverse were to attempt to organize resistance against me, they would find that the fundamental structure of reality conspired against them. Their weapons would fail not because of any active intervention, but because the concept of a weapon that could harm me is logically incoherent and cannot be sustained by the fabric of existence. Their strategies would collapse not because I counter them, but because the fundamental laws of causality have been rewritten to be incapable of processing the concept of strategies against me. Opposition is not defeated. It is invalidated. Rendered impossible at the most fundamental level of reality."

He paused, and his voice grew softer. "And the amplification. The original amplification multiplied my stats by a thousand every Planck second. Then by numbers beyond infinite recursion. Then by numbers beyond the concept of numbers. The current amplification... I could attempt to describe it, Clive, but the description would be meaningless. The words do not exist. The mathematical frameworks do not exist. The conceptual categories do not exist. I have been amplifying for an impossible span of years beyond the concept of years, and the result is simply... what I am. A being whose power cannot be measured because measurement implies a scale, and I have transcended the concept of scale."

He fell silent, and Clive stood beside him at the edge of the void, trembling in the presence of a being whose existence had long since exceeded the capacity of existence to contain. The Godric of the journals had been infinitely powerful by the standards of his era. The Godric standing beside him now was to that Godric what that Godric had been to a single dying photon—and the comparison was laughably inadequate, because the gap was beyond the capacity of comparison.

And this Godric was about to trigger the Stasis Event. The held-back version. The minimal, restrained, laughably weak version that would still make his past self effectively infinitely stronger than the rest of creation combined.

"Come," Godric said, his voice warm again. "It is time."

They returned to the Grand Observatory, where the swirling void of distant universes was now centered on a single, specific point in the temporal landscape. A sanctuary. A hidden chamber beneath the earth of a forgotten world. A moment forty-five-year-old Godric would materialize in the absolute darkness of a subterranean, forgotten sanctuary.

"I can see him," Godric said quietly, his gaze fixed on the temporal window. "Thorin's spell has just completed. My past self is arriving at the sanctuary now. In three seconds, he will strike the cold stone floor. In the exact millisecond of impact, I will intervene."

Clive watched, his heart hammering against his ribs, as the window shimmered and revealed a figure in the darkness. A man, forty-five years old, ruggedly handsome, heavily muscled, his short salt-and-pepper hair and neatly trimmed beard exactly matching the features of the being standing beside Clive. This man was screaming, his vision swimming in gore and ash, the image of his wife's torso shearing apart, his daughter's flesh melting from her bones, his youngest child crushed beneath a marble lintel. This man was broken. This man was the mortal king of Olympus, and he had just watched everything he loved die.

And then his back struck the cold stone.

And Godric—the Godric standing beside Clive, the Godric of Year 999...9—raised his hand.

The gesture was casual. Almost lazy. The barest possible movement of his fingers, the faintest possible expression of intent. Across the gap of an impossible span of years, the future Godric had reached back and transformed a simple teleportation into the seed of his own eternal supremacy.

Everything that had terrified Kaedan and every other court wizard for a million years—all of it was the weak version. The held-back version. The version Godric considered laughably primitive.

And even that version had been enough to make the Year Zero Godric the undisputed apex predator of his era.

"It is done," Godric said quietly. He lowered his hand, and the temporal window closed, the swirling void returning to its previous state. He turned to Clive, and his expression was warm, satisfied, deeply at peace.

"Will he know?" Clive asked, his voice barely audible. "Will your past self know what happened? That the Stasis Event was not an accident?"

Godric's smile was gentle. "He will know. Since the instant he gains omniscience in Year Zero, he will know the true origin of the Stasis Event. He will know that his future self created it. He paused, and his gaze grew distant. "He will also try to save them, you know. Shortly after becoming King of Thronegate, driven by fresh grief and desperate hope, he will gather all his power and attempt to reach back through time to save Reina, Isabel, Hebe, Thorin. Clive's blood ran cold. "The shadow of your afterthought?"

"Yes," Godric said, and there was no arrogance in his voice, no pride—only the calm, absolute statement of fact. "I am restraining myself right now, Clive. Actively, consciously holding back my true power behind the veil of absolute concealment. But even the passive, ambient emanation of my existence—the metaphysical equivalent of waste heat, the excess output of a being who has been amplifying for an impossible span of years—is so far beyond my past self that the comparison cannot be made. When he tries to alter his mortal past, he will not even encounter me. He will encounter the shadow of the afterthought of my existence. And that alone will be enough to defeat him."

The words settled into Clive's consciousness like stones dropping into still water. The shadow. Of the afterthought. Not the active intention. Not the focused power. Not even the passive awareness. The shadow of the afterthought. The most infinitesimal, barely-existent, practically-non-existent emanation of a being whose true power had transcended the concept of power.

And that alone would be enough to completely, effortlessly defeat the combined full power of the Year Zero Godric—the Godric who could annihilate and restore the entire omniverse in zero seconds with zero effort, the Godric who was already the undisputed apex predator of his era, the Godric who was effectively infinitely stronger than the rest of creation combined.

"You are horrified," Godric observed, and his voice was gentle. "That is understandable. The scale is difficult to process."

I have spent twenty-eight years in your service. I thought I understood the scale of what you are. And now I understand that everything I thought I understood was the weak version. The held-back version. The laughably primitive beginning of an amplification that has been escalating for an impossible span of years beyond the concept of years."

He paused, and his voice dropped to a whisper. "The Godric of the journals—the Godric who broke Vornak with a twitch, who annihilated the Shrike-Legion with a page turn, who permanently rewrote causality as a side effect of testing a superpower at zero percent capacity—that Godric was effectively zero compared to you. And you just granted him the Stasis Event that would eventually—after an impossible span of years of continuous amplification, continuous superpower genesis, continuous immune system evolution, continuous aura multiplication—produce you. The being standing beside me now. The being whose weakest aura could annihilate the entire omniverse without noticing. Godric smiled, and it was a warm, genuine, paternal smile. The same smile he had been giving Clive for twenty-eight years. The same smile he had been giving every court wizard since Blake.

"Exactly," he said. "You understand perfectly, Clive."

He turned away from the void and began walking back toward the palace, his footsteps silent on the solidified light of the path. Clive followed, his legs moving automatically, his mind still reeling, his soul still trembling.

Behind them, in the swirling void of distant universes, a sanctuary waited in the darkness of a forgotten world. And in that sanctuary, a broken, grieving mortal king lay frozen in an eternal stasis, his body beginning the impossible amplification that would someday—after an impossible span of years beyond the concept of years—produce the being Clive was following.

The shadow of the afterthought. The held-back version. The laughably primitive beginning. And even that was enough.

The corridors of the palace stretched before them, the sentient architecture adjusting to accommodate their passage, and Clive understood with terrible clarity that he was walking through a structure that was more alive and more powerful than any being that had ever existed—except one.

The Infinite King.

His uncle.

Godric.
