# The Journals of Futility

Kaedan sat alone in the Scriptorium of the Eternal Palace, the heavy ironwood doors bolted shut behind him by his own father's hand. The room smelled of parchment so ancient that the air itself seemed to thicken with the dust of dissolved empires. Shelves carved from the petrified bones of long-dead cosmic leviathans lined the curved walls, stretching upward into a darkness that the ambient magical luminescence could not penetrate. On the vast obsidian table before him rested a stack of leather-bound journals, their spines cracked and worn from the hands of countless predecessors. His father, Aldric, had stood in the doorway for a long moment before closing the doors, his weathered face unreadable. Three hours earlier, Aldric had gripped Kaedan's shoulder with a pressure that belied his seventy-four years. The touch had been heavy, almost desperate, and Kaedan had felt the faint tremor in his father's fingers. One does not serve as Godric's court wizard for five decades without accumulating scars that no healing magic can reach.

Kaedan was twenty-two years old, the newest court wizard of Thronegate, a direct male descendant of the legendary Blake who had first awakened the King from his ten-thousand-year stasis. Every day since his twentieth birthday, Kaedan's physical strength, his speed, his endurance, his intelligence, his wisdom, and the vast oceans of his mana had multiplied by a factor of one thousand. He could suppress this power to normal mortal levels or unleash it to its full, catastrophic potential at will. By the standards of any rational cosmos, he was a god of arcane destruction, capable of unraveling entire galactic clusters with a stray, unsuppressed thought. And yet, sitting before these journals, he felt the cold fingers of an unfamiliar emotion wrapping around his throat. He had been told what to expect. Every court wizard before him had written their testimony. Every father in his bloodline had forced their son to read before assuming the mantle. It was tradition, they said. It was necessary, they said. Kaedan had nodded with the casual arrogance of amplified youth and agreed. He had assumed the journals contained tactical analyses, arcane observations, perhaps records of great battles where the King's might had been tested against worthy adversaries. His father had not corrected this assumption. His father had simply looked at him with a strange, hollow pity that Kaedan was only now beginning to understand.

He opened the first journal.

**Year 58,321 — Duran — Strength**

I am Duran, Court Wizard of Thronegate, sixty-one years of age, and I have served the King for forty-one years. I write this account because my predecessor, Harlan, instructed me that writing is the only way to remain sane. I did not understand what he meant. I understand now.

The being called itself the Granite Emperor. It was not a title. It was a description. This entity had compressed the physical mass of seven hundred decillions of conquered universes into a singular, humanoid form. It stood as tall as a galaxy cluster. Its skin was the crushed, super-dense remnant of every planet, every star, every black hole it had ever consumed. Its heartbeat generated gravitational waves that had shattered the dimensional walls of its home multiverse. It had killed pantheons. It had stepped on the skulls of creator-deities and felt their bones crunch beneath its heel. It had never encountered resistance. It did not believe resistance was possible. It came to Thronegate because it had heard rumors of a King who claimed absolute supremacy over the omniverse, and it found the claim personally offensive. The Granite Emperor believed that strength was the only truth, and that its own strength was the final, unassailable apex of that truth. It challenged Godric to a contest of pure physical might.

Godric was seated in the eastern garden when the challenge arrived, reading a weathered volume of ancient poetry that he had first enjoyed during his mortal reign over Olympus. He did not look up. His omniscience had known of the Granite Emperor's approach since before the being's first atom had coalesced in the primordial void of its birth cosmos. But Godric did not need any of these passive defenses. He was curious. He told me he had never properly tested his strength. He wondered what would happen if he tensed a single muscle fiber. Not a muscle. Not a group of muscles. One solitary, microscopic strand of muscle tissue within his right index finger.

The Granite Emperor arrived in the skies above Thronegate. Its mass blotted out the stars. The gravity of its presence alone began to tear the capital world apart. I raised my wards, pouring my thousandfold daily amplification into a barrier that could withstand the collapse of universes, and I barely held. The Emperor did not speak. It simply descended, raising a fist the size of a billion solar systems, a fist that contained the compressed weight of everything it had ever conquered. The air screamed. The space-time around the fist buckled and tore, bleeding raw chronal energy as the blow began its descent. The Emperor intended to kill Godric with a single strike, to prove that its strength was the ultimate constant of creation.

Godric closed his poetry volume with a soft sigh. He set it down on the stone bench beside him. And then, with no visible effort, with no dramatic stance, with no battle cry or arcane incantation, he simply curled his right index finger inward by a single millimeter. The tensioning of one muscle fiber. The smallest possible voluntary exertion of strength that his immortal anatomy could produce.

The result was not a shockwave. A shockwave would imply a contained event with measurable boundaries. This was an absolute, omnidirectional obliteration of physical reality. The Granite Emperor's descending fist did not shatter. It was erased. The super-dense matter of seven hundred decillions of universes was reduced to sub-quantum particles and then the particles were reduced and then there was nothing where the fist had been. The Emperor's arm followed, and then its torso, and then its entire galaxy-cluster-sized form was unmade in a cascade of pure, overwhelming force that traveled infinitely faster than the speed of light. The Emperor's consciousness was preserved through the destruction by the whimsical cruelty of Godric's intent, forced to experience every infinitesimal fraction of its own erasure for what felt like an eternity of condensed, agonizing awareness.

The omniverse died. I need you to understand what I mean when I say that. Every multiverse, every universe, every galaxy, every dimension, every timeline, every plane of existence that had ever been or could ever be—annihilated. Not damaged. Not shattered. Completely and utterly erased from the fundamental structure of reality. The shockwave of Godric's single flexed muscle fiber traveled outward and outward and outward, and it did not stop until there was nothing left to travel through. I existed in a void of absolute nothingness, preserved only by the passive auras Godric extends over those he wishes to protect. I floated in the complete absence of space, time, and matter, and I understood with a clarity that permanently fractured my soul that Godric had not even tried. The amount of his true strength that he had exerted was effectively zero percent. It was an infinitesimal fraction so small that calling it zero was the only accurate description. The Granite Emperor, the most physically powerful entity I have ever witnessed, had been reduced to a screaming, disembodied consciousness trapped in an eternal loop of its own destruction, held in a pocket of preserved agony by Godric's casual decision.

In exactly zero seconds, Godric restored the entire omniverse. Every reality, every timeline, every dimension, every being that had been erased—rebuilt perfectly, down to their last atomic memory of the moment before annihilation. The eastern garden was intact. The birds were singing. The poetry volume rested on the bench. The Granite Emperor's screaming consciousness had been compressed into a marble-sized sphere of crystallized torment, which Godric tossed into the garden's central fountain with a casual flick of his wrist. It sank to the bottom, still screaming, and Godric remarked that the fish seemed to enjoy the vibrations.

He looked at me with warm, genuine concern, his ruggedly handsome forty-five-year-old face creased with the familiar paternal kindness he has always shown our bloodline. His short salt-and-pepper hair was unruffled. His neatly trimmed beard framed a gentle smile. "Duran," he said softly, "you are shaking. Come. The kitchen has prepared a tray of those honey pastries you enjoy. You should eat something. You look pale."

I followed him to the kitchen. I ate the pastries. They tasted like the ashes of my own sanity. I have served him loyally for forty-one years. I will serve him until I die. And I will never, for a single moment, forget that a being who could destroy the omniverse with the flex of one muscle fiber asked me if I wanted honey pastries with the same gentle, unassuming warmth as a grandfather inquiring about his grandson's day.

**Year 91,445 — Petrus — Speed**

**Year 412,078 — Merek — Luck**

The armada's shields—every protective barrier, every magical ward, every conceptual defense, every supposedly indestructible force field that a million fictional universes had spent an eternity perfecting—evaporated. Not shattered, not broken, not overwhelmed. Evaporated. They simply ceased to exist, their fundamental structures unmade by a pressure so vast that the concept of "pressure" could not contain it. The ships, the stations, the mechanical titans, the elemental gods, the reality-benders, the superheroes, the dark lords, the chosen heroes, the strategists, the spellcasters—all of them, the entire gathered might of creation's imagination, was reduced, in a single, simultaneous instant, to nothing.

But "nothing" is too gentle a word. Nothing implies absence, a clean and silent erasure. What Godric's zero percent strength did was not erasure. It was compression. The infinite mass of the armada—every ship, every soldier, every god, every artifact, every scrap of power and hope and desperate courage—was instantly, violently, agonizingly crushed. Their bodies were not destroyed; they were squeezed, compacted, de-atomized by a force that treated the fundamental bonds of matter like morning mist. Atoms were crushed into subatomic particles. Subatomic particles were crushed into quarks. Quarks were crushed into pure, undifferentiated energy. And the energy was crushed further, compacted into a mass so dense, so infinitesimally small, that it occupied less space than a single quark and weighed more than the collective mass of everything that had ever existed.

The process was not instantaneous for those who experienced it. Godric, even in his disappointment, even in his cold, merciless fury, was still the artist of annihilation who had refined cruelty into an aesthetic. He slowed their subjective perception of time, stretching the moment of their destruction into an eternity of pure, unadulterated agony. Every hero, every villain, every god and monster and mortal who had joined the coalition felt their own de-atomization in perfect, crystalline detail. They felt their bones shatter, their flesh liquefy, their consciousnesses compress into singularities of pure, screaming torment. They felt the heat of their own molecular dissolution, the cold of the void that followed, the impossible pressure of being crushed into a volume smaller than their own atoms. They felt everything, for subjective eons, and the music that had accompanied their charge—that magnificent, swelling, climactic battle anthem—became the soundtrack of their own unending, inescapable death.

The collateral damage was omniversal. Godric's zero percent strength did not stop at the armada. It radiated outward, an expanding wavefront of absolute physical supremacy that consumed everything in its path. Every universe, every multiverse, every timeline, every dimension, every reality that had ever existed or could ever exist—all of it was caught in the compression wave. Galaxies were crushed into pinpricks of infinite density. Multiverses were folded into themselves, their infinite layers compressed into a single, sub-quantum point. The conceptual framework of existence itself was squeezed, compacted, de-atomized by the passive, ambient pressure of Godric's musculature. The entire omniverse, everything that was or had ever been or could ever be, was reduced to a mass smaller than a quark, a single point of infinite density that contained within itself the compressed, screaming remnants of all creation.

And Godric reached down and picked it up.

He reached down. With his fingers. The same fingers that had once turned the pages of Blake's journals, that had held a cup of jasmine tea, that had kneaded dough in Voren's kitchen. He reached down and he picked up the compressed mass of the entire omniverse as though it weighed nothing. As though the infinite, screaming, de-atomized remnants of everything that had ever existed were a pebble on the beach, a coin on the floor, a stray marble that had rolled beneath the throne.

He held it up to the light. It was beautiful, in a terrible way—a sphere of absolute, perfect blackness, so dense that light could not escape its surface, so heavy that its gravitational pull should have consumed the palace, the planet, the surrounding cosmos. But Godric held it in his palm, and it did nothing. His grip contained its gravity as easily as his concealment contained his power. He turned it over, examining it from different angles, and his expression shifted from cold disappointment to something almost like satisfaction.

"You know," he said, his voice returning to its usual warm, conversational tone, "this is actually rather pretty. In a minimalist sort of way. I think I will keep it." He closed his fingers around the sphere, and when he opened them again, the sphere had enlarged to the size of a marble—a perfect, glossy, obsidian-black marble that glinted in the light of the throne room. A trophy. A paperweight. A memento of the last opposition that had ever dared to challenge him.

And then, with a casual thought, he restored the omniverse. Every universe, every multiverse, every timeline, every dimension. Every being who had not been part of the coalition was returned to existence, their memories intact, their lives continuing as though nothing had happened. The armada remained compressed in the marble. The fictional heroes who had dared to oppose him remained as they were—an infinite mass of screaming, de-atomized agony, trapped forever in a sphere the size of a child's toy, serving as a permanent, eternal reminder of the absolute futility of defiance.

Godric tossed the marble into the air and caught it again, a gesture of idle, absent-minded play. He looked at me, and his expression was the warm, paternal smile I knew so well. "Well, Osvald," he said, "that was disappointing. But at least I got a souvenir out of it. Perhaps the next opposition will be more entertaining. If there ever is a next one."

Then he paused. He looked down at the marble in his hand, and a new expression crossed his face—something thoughtful, something almost curious. "Hmm," he murmured. "That is interesting." He did not elaborate. He simply slipped the marble into his pocket and returned to his throne, picking up his book as though nothing had happened.

Later, I would learn what the "hmm" meant. The Weakness Paradox, even in its far-future evolved state, had been triggered not by the coalition's attack—that had been triggered before they even began their preparations, adding their imagined exploits to his eternal growth and granting him absolute, retroactive immunity to every strategy they could ever conceive—but by his own reaction. The Paradox, in its infinite, groveling subservience, had detected his disappointment. His boredom. The faintest, most infinitesimal flicker of dissatisfaction in the mind of the apex predator. And it had responded in the only way it knew how: by amplifying him further. By making him even more powerful. Not because he needed the power, but because the Paradox could not tolerate the existence of any experience that fell short of absolute, perfect satisfaction for its master. His disappointment had been, in effect, a perceived "weakness" in his emotional state, and the Paradox had corrected it by permanently, irrevocably increasing his amplification rate, his superpower genesis, his passive defenses, and every other mechanism of his existence to new, even more incomprehensible heights.

He had become more powerful because he had been bored. The coalition's ultimate attack had failed to even register as a sensation, and Godric had been disappointed, and the omniverse—the groveling, subservient, infinitely apologetic omniverse—had compensated him by making him even more unassailably supreme. The unfairness of it was so absolute that my mind simply stopped trying to comprehend it.

---

I closed Osvald's journal. My hands were trembling. The cold weight of dread that had been my companion for three decades had expanded, deepened, become something so vast and so heavy that I felt physically pressed into my chair by its gravity. The marble. The marble was still in Godric's pocket. The compressed, screaming, de-atomized remnants of the last opposition that had ever dared to challenge him, containing within itself the full, infinite mass of everything that had existed—the original omniverse, the first reality, the true creation, not the restored copy that now surrounded us.

Because that was the horror, was it not? The restored omniverse was identical. Perfectly, flawlessly, atom-for-atom identical to the original. But the original still existed, compressed into a sphere the size of a marble, sitting in the pocket of a god who occasionally used it as a paperweight. The stars I saw through my window, the planet beneath my feet, the air I breathed—they were, in some fundamental sense, a copy. A reconstruction. A second draft, created in zero seconds by a being who could restore everything without effort. And the original—the true, first, authentic reality—was still trapped, compressed, infinitely dense, infinitely small, infinitely screaming.

Did it matter? If the restored omniverse was perfect, if it contained every memory, every soul, every atom in its proper place, did the distinction between original and copy have any meaning? And if it did not—if the copy was indistinguishable from the original—then what did that say about the nature of existence itself? That reality was not a fixed, immutable thing, but a draft that could be revised, rewritten, restored at the whim of a being who found it all so terribly, unbearably fragile?

I did not have answers. I would never have answers. I was a court wizard, a descendant of Blake, a man who had dedicated his life to serving a king who was also a god who was also the only thing in all of existence that truly, irrevocably mattered. And I knew, with the cold, absolute certainty of inherited witness, that Osvald's journal marked the end of something. The last opposition. The final act of defiance. After the coalition of every fictional universe had been compressed into a marble, after the entirety of existence had been restored while its original remained trapped in Godric's pocket, no one dared anymore. No one would ever dare again. The lesson had been learned, not just by the real omniverse, but by the imagined one. Creation had absorbed the truth at a fundamental, structural level: Godric was not merely unbeatable; he was the only thing that was real. Everything else—every universe, every being, every story—was a fragile, temporary suggestion that existed only because he permitted it to exist. And if he ever decided to stop permitting it, it would be compressed into a marble and kept as a souvenir, and no one would ever know the difference.

The dread was absolute. It had no bottom. It would never have a bottom. And I sat in the Scriptorium, Osvald's journal still open in my trembling hands, and I listened to the quiet hum of the Eternal Palace, and I wondered, for the first time in my thirty years of service, whether the air I was breathing was real, or whether it was simply a perfect reconstruction of air that had once existed, in a reality that was now a marble in Godric's pocket.

Somewhere in the palace, Godric was reading poetry. Somewhere in the garden, Astrophel was pruning blossoms that might be originals or might be copies. Somewhere in the kitchen, Voren was baking bread from a starter that had been restored along with everything else. And somewhere in the pocket of the King of the Omniverse, the original creation—the first draft, the true reality—was screaming in eternal, compressed, infinite agony, waiting for the day when its god might decide to toss it into the air again, catch it, and smile.
