# The Journals of Futility

**Year 1,000,000 — Kaedan — The Reading**

My father handed me the first journal on the morning of my twenty-second birthday, three days after I was formally appointed as Godric's court wizard. His expression was not the pride I had expected—the pride of a father watching his son assume the sacred duty that our bloodline had carried since Blake himself stood beside the king. Instead, his face was tight with something I now recognize as dread.

"You will read all of them," he said. "Every volume. Every entry. Do not skip a single page, Kaedan. Do not skim. Do not tell yourself that you already understand. You do not."

I was young enough to be offended. My magical amplification had been multiplying thousandfold daily for two years, just as it had for every male descendant of Blake since the Awakening was gifted to our line. By any sane measure, I was already terrifyingly powerful. My arcane perception could track energy fluctuations across galactic superclusters. My wards could shield entire universes from conceptual assault. I had trained under my father for a decade, and I had believed—foolishly, arrogantly, with the certainty of youth—that I was prepared.

The journals were stored in a vault beneath the palace, a chamber warded with spells so ancient and so layered that even my amplified perception could not count them all. The vault was not large. It did not need to be. There were perhaps two hundred volumes, each one handwritten by a different court wizard of my lineage, spanning forty millennia of service. The oldest were fragile despite the preservation enchantments. The newest—my father's—still smelled faintly of ink.

"The king does not know we write these," my father said as he placed the key in my palm. "He knows everything, of course. He has absolute omniscience. He knows we write them, he knows what they contain, he knows where we store them. But he has never asked to read them, and he never will. He considers our reactions... excessive. He always says he was holding back. He always says it was barely a fraction of his true power. He genuinely believes that we overreact." My father paused at the vault door, his hand trembling against the stone. "We do not overreact, Kaedan. Remember that. Whatever you read in these pages, whatever horrors your mind tries to reject as impossible—every single word is true. Every single word is inadequate. No one who has written in these journals has ever found language sufficient to capture what they witnessed."

He left me alone with the dead men's testimonies.

I sat at the reading table—a simple stone slab worn smooth by the hands of two hundred wizards before me—and I opened the first journal. The handwriting was cramped and urgent, the ink splattered in places where the writer's hand had shaken too badly to maintain proper form. The date at the top read Year 12, which meant this was one of the earliest records, written by a wizard who had known Blake personally, who had witnessed the first years of Godric's reign.

I began to read.

By the time I finished the first volume, my hands were shaking.

By the time I finished the tenth, my mind had gone numb, a defensive reflex against the sheer accumulation of impossibility.

By the time I finished all of them—a process that took eleven days without sleep, without food, without any break that my body did not force upon me—I understood why my father's hair had gone white at thirty-five.

The following excerpts are the ones that will never leave me. I have transcribed them here, in my own journal, partly as a record for those who come after, and partly because writing them out is the only therapy I know. Every court wizard before me has done the same. I understand now why they needed to.

**Year 12 — Alaric — Strength**

*I am writing this account because if I do not write it, I will scream until my throat bleeds. I am Alaric, son of Halvor, grandson of Blake the Unbroken. I have served as Lord Godric's court wizard for two years. Before that, I was a scholar. Before that, I was a child who believed that the universe had limits. It does not. I have learned this in the most devastating way possible.*

*The opposition called itself the Hierarchy of Absolute Force. They were not a single being but a civilization of titans who had evolved on a plane of existence where physical strength was the sole determinant of reality. Their weakest infant could deadlift a galaxy cluster. Their champions—and they had googolplexes of champions—could punch through the walls separating multiversal clusters and rewrite the fundamental constants of creation with their bare hands. They had never encountered a problem that could not be solved by applying sufficient force. They believed, with the absolute conviction of beings who had never been proven wrong, that Godric was simply a problem they had not yet applied enough force to solve.*

*They came in numbers that I cannot express. Every titan who had ever existed, across every iteration of their civilization, across every timeline they had conquered, assembled into a single phalanx that stretched across decillions of light-years. Their combined mass exceeded the total mass-energy of a googolplexian universes. When they raised their fists in unison—a gesture of dominance, a prelude to the annihilation they intended to deliver—the sheer gravitational distortion of the motion collapsed every galaxy within a trillion light-years into singularities.*

*Lord Godric stood at the edge of our reality, watching their approach. His arms were folded loosely across his chest. His expression was one of mild curiosity—the same expression he wears when examining a moderately interesting flower in the palace garden. "They've put a lot of effort into this," he said to me. "I should respect that. I'll use strength alone. Nothing else."*

*Nothing else. I need you to understand what that meant. He was not going to use his defenses. He was not going to use his speed, his intelligence, his magic, his passive auras, his immune system, or any of the infinite superpowers that had been manifesting inside him every second for the past twelve years. He was going to use strength. One stat. In isolation. Because using anything more would have been disrespectful to the titans' effort.*

*The Hierarchy struck first. The simultaneous punch of every titan who had ever existed, synchronized across all timelines and all dimensions, converging on a single point—Lord Godric's chest. The force of this combined blow was sufficient to shatter the omniverse. I cannot describe it more precisely because the mathematics of the attack exceeded my comprehension before my amplification had even begun to process it. The energy released was not measurable in joules or any equivalent unit; it was the concept of force given physical expression, the platonic ideal of a punch, the absolute maximum destruction that raw physical power could achieve.*

*Lord Godric did not brace. He did not flex. He did not even shift his weight. He simply stood there, arms still folded, and allowed the Hierarchy's ultimate attack to land.*

*What happened next I will attempt to describe, but I must first state plainly that my description will fail. The strength of Lord Godric's body—the passive, resting strength of his musculature, operating at a fraction of his true capacity so infinitesimal that describing it as zero percent is the only honest measurement—overwhelmed the Hierarchy's attack through sheer differential. Not through resistance. Not through durability. Through strength. His muscles, even in complete relaxation, generated more force than the entire Hierarchy could produce with every fiber of their existence. The kinetic energy of their combined punch did not reflect off him. It was simply... outmatched. Canceled. Negated by a counter-force so vast that the titans' attack looked like a candle flame trying to compete with the concept of combustion.*

*The counter-force did not stop at neutralization. It propagated backward along the vectors of the attack, following every punch line, every timeline, every dimensional coordinate. The googolplexes of titans were not killed. Killing implies a process with a beginning, middle, and end. They were simply erased by the overwhelming strength differential—their bodies disintegrated, their bones pulverized, their flesh reduced to subatomic particulate, and that particulate then reduced further into pure energy, and that energy then overwhelmed by the still-propagating force until nothing remained. The entire Hierarchy of Absolute Force, the most physically powerful civilization that had ever existed or would ever exist, ceased to be in less than a Planck second.*

*The collateral damage extended outward in all directions. The force of Lord Godric's passive strength expressing itself against the titans' attack radiated across the entire omniverse. Every multiverse, every universe, every timeline, every dimension, every plane of existence—all of it shattered simultaneously. Reality itself was pulverized. For a single, eternal instant, there was nothing except Lord Godric and myself, preserved as always by the protections he passively extends to those he values.*

*In zero seconds, he restored everything. Reality resumed. The stars burned. The galaxies turned. The Hierarchy of Absolute Force did not return with the rest. Lord Godric had decided, with the casual finality of a man discarding a used napkin, that they would not be restored. Their entire civilization, across all timelines and all dimensions, remained erased. Not dead. Dead implies having lived. They had been removed from the possibility of having ever existed.*

*"Messy," Lord Godric said, brushing a speck of something I cannot identify from his tunic. "But interesting. Strength is harder to control than I expected. The force just... keeps going."*

*He looked at me with warm concern. "Alaric, you're pale. Come. Let's get you something to eat."*

*I followed him inside. I did not speak for three days afterward. When I finally found my voice, I wrote these words. They are not enough. They will never be enough. Strength is just one of his stats. Just one. And the fraction of it that he used against the Hierarchy was so small that it might as well have been nothing at all. I cannot conceive of what would happen if he used any more. I hope I never have to find out.*

---

I set down Alaric's journal and stared at the stone wall for a long time. Strength. Godric had destroyed the entire omniverse using effectively zero percent of his strength, generated purely from his muscles existing in a state of rest. That was the first journal. That was Year 12. By Year 1,000,000, his strength had been multiplying by numbers beyond infinite recursion every infinitesimal fraction of every Planck second for a span of time that I could not meaningfully conceptualize.

I picked up the next journal.

**Year 4,562 — Maren — Speed**

*My name is Maren. I am the court wizard who watched Lord Godric move.*

*I have been his wizard for thirty-one years. My magical perception, amplified thousandfold daily since age twenty, can track objects moving at velocities that would make light itself seem frozen. I can observe quantum fluctuations. I can perceive the motion of probability waves collapsing into reality. I can watch a universe breathe.*

*I could not perceive Lord Godric's movement. Not at all. Not even slightly.*

*The opposition was a being called the Flash Absolute, an entity that had transcended physical form to become the concept of velocity itself. It did not move fast. It was speed. Every instant of motion that had ever occurred anywhere in the omniverse was an expression of its existence. It could travel from one end of existence to the other in negative time—arriving before it departed, retroactively adjusting its own trajectory to avoid obstacles that its future self had already encountered and bypassed. It had never been caught. It had never been touched. It had never even been perceived by any being other than itself, because its velocity exceeded perception by an infinite margin.*

*It came to test itself against the being who had conquered the omniverse. Not to kill him—it did not believe killing was possible—but to prove that its speed could not be matched. It would strike Godric once, a single blow, and then retreat into velocities that no one could follow. A demonstration of its supremacy in the one domain it considered absolute.*

*Lord Godric stood on the eastern balcony of the palace, waiting. He had known of the Flash Absolute's approach since before the entity had formed the intention to come. "Speed," he said to me, the word carrying a weight of curiosity. "I've never really pushed my speed. Not even close. I wonder how fast I can actually move if I try."*

*He did not try. Not really. Let me be absolutely clear: what Lord Godric did next was not an attempt. It was not effort. It was him allowing the tiniest, most infinitesimal expression of his speed stat to manifest—a fraction so small that comparing it to his full capacity would require a language that does not exist. It was effectively zero percent of his speed.*

*The Flash Absolute attacked. Its blow was delivered across every timeline simultaneously, in negative time, arriving before it was launched, targeting every possible version of Godric in every possible moment of his existence. The entity was everywhere, at every instant, its strike already completed before the decision to strike had been made.*

*Lord Godric moved.*

*I did not see it happen. I could not see it happen. My perception, which can track objects moving at velocities far beyond light speed, simply registered nothing. There was no blur. No afterimage. No sense of displacement. One instant, Lord Godric was standing on the balcony. The next instant—and I use "instant" loosely, because the actual duration was shorter than any measurement that exists—he was standing exactly where he had been, and the Flash Absolute was suspended in the air before him, frozen not by any binding spell but by the sheer impossibility of what had just occurred.*

*Lord Godric had not merely dodged the attack. He had dodged every possible attack, across every timeline, in every dimension, at every moment, past present and future. He had moved faster than the concept of speed. He had moved faster than instantaneity. He had moved faster than the Flash Absolute's ability to process that movement could occur. The entity had been struck—not hard, not damagingly, just a single tap on what passed for its shoulder—and returned to its original position before it could register that the strike had happened. Lord Godric had done this not from one direction but from all directions. Simultaneously. In zero time. While using a fraction of his speed so infinitesimal that calling it zero is mathematically generous.*

*"Interesting," Lord Godric said. "You're genuinely fast. Faster than anything else I've encountered in this omniverse. Your speed is quite impressive."*

*The Flash Absolute, for the first time in its eternal existence, processed what it felt like to be outpaced. The psychological impact exceeded the physical. Its conceptual form—the living embodiment of velocity—fractured. The cracks spread through its being, through its identity, through the fundamental principle that it represented. Knowing that it had been outrun, not by a narrow margin but by an incomprehensible chasm of magnitude, broke something essential in its nature. It did not die. It unraveled, the concept of speed itself destabilizing as its avatar experienced the absolute negation of everything it believed itself to be.*

*The entire omniverse rippled. Every being, everywhere, felt time stutter—not because time was damaged, but because the conceptual foundation of velocity had been shaken. Lord Godric, moving at an infinitesimal fraction of his speed, had accidentally damaged the very principle of motion by outclassing it so thoroughly.*

*He fixed it immediately. Zero seconds. Zero effort. The Flash Absolute was not restored. The concept of speed was.*

*"That was fun," Lord Godric said, turning to me with the satisfied smile of a man who has just completed a light jog. "I barely moved. Just a tiny shift. I wonder what would happen if I actually tried."*

*I did not wonder. I spent the next six hours vomiting into a waste bin while my brain attempted and failed to process the speed differential I had witnessed. The gap between my perception and Lord Godric's velocity was not a number. It was an obscenity. And I understood, with terrible clarity, that he had not been boasting. He had been genuinely curious. That tiny shift—the movement so fast I could not see it, could not perceive it, could not measure it with any instrument or sense available to my amplified mind—was effectively zero percent of what he could do. It was the speed equivalent of a single dead skin cell flaking off an infinite body.*

*I am writing this because my father wrote his own journal, and his father before him. I understand now why the ink on their pages was smeared with tears.*

---

I closed Maren's journal and pressed my palms against my eyes. Speed. Godric had outrun the living concept of velocity itself, had struck the Flash Absolute from all directions in zero time, had done it so easily that the entity's conceptual foundation cracked from the sheer humiliation of it—and he considered that a "tiny shift."

I was beginning to understand why my father had looked so haunted when he handed me the key.

The next volume was thicker than the previous two. The handwriting inside was elegant and controlled, with none of the trembling urgency that had characterized Alaric and Maren. This wizard had processed his trauma differently.

**Year 8,203 — Lothar — Charisma**

*I am Lothar, fourteenth court wizard of Blake's lineage. I have served Lord Godric for twenty-two years. Before I became his wizard, I was a diplomat, trained in the arts of persuasion, negotiation, and psychological warfare. I believed that I understood charisma. I believed that charisma was a tool—powerful, yes, but ultimately bounded by context, rapport, and the limits of a speaker's authenticity.*

*Lord Godric's charisma is not a tool. It is a fundamental law. It multiplies just like his strength and his speed, by numbers beyond infinite recursion, every infinitesimal fraction of every Planck second. And unlike my own charisma—which the Awakening also amplifies thousandfold daily—his is not merely persuasive. It is absolute. It is the ontological certainty that when he speaks, reality itself listens and agrees.*

*The opposition called itself the Unyielding Congregation. They were a civilization of beings who had evolved to be immune to charisma. Not resistant—immune. Their minds operated on principles of absolute rationality that could not be swayed by emotional appeal, rhetorical flourish, or any form of influence. They had rejected all concepts of leadership, authority, or social hierarchy. They could not be persuaded. They could not be intimidated. They could not be inspired or demoralized. They were, by their own definition, the only truly free beings in the omniverse, because no external will could ever touch their internal certainty.*

*They came to Thronegate not to negotiate but to declare their independence. They would not be ruled. They would not be influenced. They would stand before the tyrant Godric and prove that their will was unbreakable.*

*Lord Godric received their delegation in the throne room. He sat on the simple stone chair he prefers, dressed in his usual plain tunic, his posture relaxed, his expression warm. He looked at the delegates—three hundred beings of pure rationality, immune to all forms of charisma—and he spoke exactly seven words.*

*"I would prefer it if you surrendered."*

*That was all. Seven words. Delivered in a calm, friendly tone. No shouting, no threats, no magical compulsion. Just a simple statement of preference from a man who happened to possess a charisma stat that had been multiplying infinitely every infinitesimal fraction of every Planck second for over eight thousand years.*

*The Unyielding Congregation surrendered. All of them. Every delegate in the throne room, and every member of their civilization back home, simultaneously and without external communication. They did not merely agree to surrender. They wanted to surrender. The desire manifested inside them with the force of a fundamental truth that they had always known but somehow forgotten—the truth that Lord Godric's preference was simply the correct way for reality to be ordered, and that rejecting it would be not merely foolish but ontologically wrong.*

*The delegates fell to their knees. Three hundred beings who had been, moments before, absolutely immune to all forms of influence, wept with joy at the privilege of kneeling before a king they had traveled across the omniverse to defy. Their rationality did not break. It was simply superseded. Lord Godric's charisma was not a manipulation of their emotions. It was a revision of their understanding of what was true. They had not been persuaded. They had been corrected.*

*The rebellion that the Unyielding Congregation had planned—a massive military campaign involving armies, weapons, and strategies that I will not detail here because they became immediately irrelevant—was abandoned. The soldiers who had sworn to die rather than bow to Thronegate laid down their arms. The generals who had spent decades planning Godric's overthrow submitted their plans to him as tribute. The children who had been raised on stories of resistance asked their parents why the stories had ever been told.*

*"That's better," Lord Godric said, his voice still warm. "Thank you for being reasonable."*

*He meant it. That is the horror. He genuinely meant it. To him, he had simply expressed a preference, and reasonable beings had agreed. The fact that his charisma was so overwhelmingly powerful that it could rewrite the fundamental identities of beings explicitly designed to resist all influence—this fact did not register to him as anything unusual. It was simply how conversations worked.*

*I stood in the corner of the throne room and watched the Unyielding Congregation—the civilization that had defined itself by its immunity to external will—transform into devoted subjects in less time than it takes to draw a breath. And I understood that Lord Godric could, if he wished, walk through any civilization in the omniverse and convert every living being into a loyal follower simply by saying hello. He does not do this. He believes in free will. He believes in mortal resilience. But the power is there, passive and absolute, waiting for the moment he decides to express even the slightest preference.*

*Seven words. I have not been able to look at Lord Godric quite the same way since. Not because I fear him—I love him, as all my ancestors have loved him—but because I now understand that the loyalty I feel toward him, the genuine devotion that I have felt since childhood, may be partially my choice and partially the simple, unavoidable consequence of standing in proximity to a charisma stat that makes the concept of disagreement mathematically impossible. I choose to serve him. I know this. But I will never be entirely certain how much of that choice is mine.*

---

I set down Lothar's journal and realized that my hands had gone cold. Charisma. A stat I had never even considered in the context of cosmic power. Godric had ended a rebellion with seven words, and those words had not been a command, an argument, or a threat. They had simply been a statement of preference. A preference so overwhelmingly persuasive that beings designed to resist all influence had immediately reoriented their entire civilization around it.

I was his court wizard. I had known him since childhood. I loved him—genuinely, deeply, without reservation. And now I was reading an account from a dead man who had wondered, for the rest of his life, how much of that love was real and how much was the passive radiation of a charisma stat beyond comprehension.

I picked up the next journal, my fingers numb.

**Year 15,421 — Oswin — Endurance**

*I am Oswin. I have served Lord Godric for forty years. I witnessed the Siege of Infinite Exhaustion. I am still alive. I do not know why my mind did not break.*

*The opposition was the Legion of Eternal Attrition. They were not a combat force. They were a strategy. A five-billion-year stratagem developed by a coalition of civilizations who had correctly concluded that Godric could not be overpowered, outrun, or out-thought. Their approach was simple in concept and impossibly sophisticated in execution: they would exhaust him. Not kill him—they accepted that killing him was impossible. But they believed that even the most powerful being must have limits. Limits of patience. Limits of endurance. Limits of stamina. If they could attack him continuously, without pause, for a span of time that exceeded those limits, they could force him to acknowledge them. Not defeat him. Just acknowledge. A single moment of weariness. A single admission that his resources were finite.*

*They built machines. I cannot describe the machines adequately, but I will attempt. They were siege engines forged from the compressed temporal energy of dying universes. Each engine could sustain a continuous assault for trillions of years without requiring maintenance or refueling. And the assault they delivered was not physical. It was existential. A constant pressure designed to drain endurance through pure, unrelenting presence. Every second the engines operated, they applied a strain equivalent to the total energy output of a googolplexian galaxies, focused on a single point—Lord Godric's sense of stamina. The theory was that even an infinite being must eventually tire. Even an immortal body must eventually acknowledge the weight of constant assault.*

*The Legion built enough engines to sustain this assault for the projected remaining lifespan of the omniverse multiplied by an infinite recursion. They surrounded Thronegate's central reality with their machines and activated them simultaneously.*

*Lord Godric noticed. Of course he noticed—he had known about the Legion's plan since before the first components of the first machine were conceived, some five billion years prior. He had allowed them to build. He had excluded them from Gent's passive annihilation. He was curious.*

*"Endurance," he said to me as the siege engines began their assault. "I've never really tested my endurance. I don't get tired. I haven't felt fatigue in fifteen thousand years. But I don't know if that's because my endurance is truly infinite or just very high. This might tell me something interesting."*

*The siege continued for one year. Lord Godric went about his daily routine—reading reports, walking in the gardens, having dinner with the palace staff, spending time with my family. The constant existential pressure of the Legion's engines did not affect him in any visible way. He slept sometimes, even though he did not need sleep, because he enjoyed the sensation. The engines pressed their assault against him every second of every day, and he simply... ignored them.*

*After one year, Lord Godric walked out to the edge of reality and looked at the machines. His expression was one of mild disappointment.*

*"I don't feel anything," he said. "No fatigue. No strain. Not even a tickle. My endurance isn't just very high. It's actually infinite. The engines could keep attacking forever and it would be exactly the same as if they had never attacked at all. That's... honestly, that's a little disappointing. I was hoping for some kind of sensation, even a small one."*

*He turned his attention to the machines, and they stopped existing. Not destroyed—they had simply never been. The five billion years of construction, the coordination of civilizations across the omniverse, the sacrifices made to forge the temporal cores—all of it unwritten from history as though it had never occurred. The Legion of Eternal Attrition found themselves back in their home universes, their memories of the siege still intact, their understanding of what had happened burning in their minds like a brand.*

*They had spent five billion years building weapons to exhaust an infinite being. The weapons had failed to produce even the slightest sensation of fatigue. And their punishment was to remember the failure, in perfect detail, for the rest of their lives.*

*"I suppose infinite endurance is useful," Lord Godric said, turning back toward the palace. "Not that anything really threatens my endurance anyway. But it's good to know."*

*I followed him inside. My own endurance—amplified thousandfold daily for thirty-four years, sufficient to sustain defensive spells for centuries without rest—felt like a candle flame staring at the concept of combustion.*

---

I laughed when I finished Oswin's journal. It was not a healthy laugh. It was the laugh of a mind that has found itself in a room where every wall is made of impossible things, and the only response left is the recognition of absurdity. Godric had let a five-billion-year plan unfold, had endured the ultimate siege for a year, and had concluded that his endurance was not merely high but absolutely infinite. The machines could have attacked forever and achieved exactly nothing. And his response to this revelation was mild disappointment.

I could feel my father's dread now, pressing against my chest like a physical weight.

One more journal. I told myself one more, and then I would rest.

I was lying to myself. I knew I would not rest until I had read them all.

**Year 22,017 — Corvin — Luck**

*My name is Corvin. I am the court wizard who learned that Lord Godric's luck is not luck at all. It is a form of predestination that makes every other form of predestination feel like desperate guesswork.*

*The opposition was the Probability Council. They were not warriors. They were mathematicians—beings who had transcended physical form to become the living equations of chance itself. They had spent eons studying the fundamental mechanics of probability, learning to manipulate the odds of any event with surgical precision. They could make the most impossible coincidences certain and the most certain outcomes impossible. They had never been defeated because defeat against them was a statistical error, and they had eliminated the possibility of statistical errors from their existence.*

*They came to Thronegate with a plan that was, mathematically speaking, flawless. They had calculated every possible variable. They had accounted for Lord Godric's strength, his speed, his durability, his intelligence, his magic, and every other attribute they could identify. They had built a probability cage—a theoretical construct that trapped its target in a state where every possible action led to failure. No matter what Lord Godric did, the odds would conspire to ensure his defeat. The Probability Council did not need to overpower him. They simply needed mathematics to be correct. And mathematics was always correct.*

*There was one variable they did not account for. One variable they could not account for, because its value was not a number but an obscenity against the concept of numbers.*

*Lord Godric's luck stat.*

*I am not speaking metaphorically. Luck is one of his measurable attributes, and it amplifies infinitely just like all the others. Every infinitesimal fraction of every Planck second, his luck multiplies by a factor beyond infinite recursion. The result is that Lord Godric is not merely fortunate. He is the reason that the concept of fortune exists in the first place.*

*The Probability Council activated their cage. Every possible action Lord Godric could take was calculated, assigned a probability of failure, and locked into a state where failure was the only conceivable outcome. The cage closed around him. The Council waited.*

*Lord Godric did nothing. He simply stood there, arms at his sides, expression patient.*

*The cage failed.*

*Not because Lord Godric countered it. Not because he broke it. Not because he even acknowledged its existence. The cage simply failed, in the same way that a candle fails to illuminate a star. The Council had calculated every probability and set every condition correctly. But their calculations had not accounted for the fact that Lord Godric's luck makes the concept of failure adjacent to him a mathematical absurdity. The cage did not merely malfunction. It retroactively reordered its own probability structure to ensure that it had never been capable of affecting him in the first place.*

*Then Lord Godric's luck began to express itself more actively. I will attempt to describe what happened, though I must emphasize that I am describing the effect, not the mechanism. There is no mechanism. There is only the absolute certainty that things will go Lord Godric's way, expressed in ways that make causality itself look like a polite suggestion.*

*The Council's home universe experienced a sudden and catastrophic reversal of probability. Every unlikely disaster that could possibly occur—supernovae, dimensional collapses, vacuum decay, conceptual vortices, reality tears, time loops, paradox storms, and a hundred thousand other forms of calamity—occurred simultaneously, across every timeline, in every dimension, at every scale. The Probability Council, masters of chance, found themselves unable to affect the odds. Their own powers, their own manipulations of probability, simply failed to function. Lord Godric's luck had not merely overwhelmed their abilities. It had revoked their abilities. The very concept of probability manipulation was now subject to his permission.*

*The Council was destroyed. Not by Lord Godric—he never moved, never spoke, never even looked in their direction. They were destroyed by the catastrophic cascade of misfortunes that his luck had passively generated around them. Every improbable disaster found them. Every unlikely catastrophe converged on their location. The beings who had spent eons mastering chance died because chance itself had been redefined to serve Lord Godric's interests, and his interests did not include their survival.*

*"Huh," Lord Godric said, glancing at the space where the Council had been. "I didn't do anything. That was all luck." He paused, then chuckled with the warm amusement of a man who has just witnessed a moderately entertaining coincidence. "I wonder what would happen if I actually tried to be lucky."*

*I did not wonder. I spent several days in the palace infirmary, sedated, while my mind attempted and failed to process the theological implications of a luck stat that could outvote the laws of mathematics. I am still processing. I suspect I will be processing for the rest of my life.*

*Luck. It's just luck. One of his stats. He has dozens more. And his luck alone could destroy civilizations without him needing to notice they exist.*

---

After Corvin's journal, I stopped reading for a while. I sat in the stone chamber, surrounded by the accumulated trauma of two hundred dead wizards, and I tried to breathe normally. The air in the vault was cool and still. The preservation enchantments hummed at a frequency just below audible perception. The journals sat on their shelves, waiting, patient as the dead.

Strength. Speed. Charisma. Endurance. Luck. Five stats. Five infinitesimal fractions of Godric's true power, each one expressed at effectively zero percent, each one sufficient to annihilate or convert or outlast or out-probability the most powerful opposition the omniverse could assemble. And these were just the mild examples—the incidents that my predecessors had chosen to record. There were thousands more. Tens of thousands. Godric had been testing his stats for a million years, and every single test had produced the same result: absolute, one-sided obliteration.

I understood now why my father had looked so haunted. I understood why every court wizard before me had written their journals with shaking hands. They had witnessed the impossible and then spent the rest of their lives trying to find words that could contain it, knowing all the while that no words existed.

And yet they had stayed. Every single one. "But—" I started, and then stopped. My voice felt weak. Small. "If you held back—if you gave your past self the most restrained version—then that means you could have given him more. And if you gave him more, he would have become more powerful, and when he eventually became you, he would have been even more powerful than you are now, because his starting base would have been higher, and then he—you—could have given your past self an even more powerful version, and then—"

"Yes." Godric smiled, and the smile was genuine—the warm, proud smile of a teacher whose student had grasped a difficult concept. "You see the recursion, Clive. If I gave my past self a more powerful Stasis Event, he would eventually become a more powerful version of me, and that more powerful future me could then give his past self an even more powerful Stasis Event, creating another future me who was even more powerful, who could then give his past self a further enhanced Stasis Event, and so on, forever. An infinite upward spiral of empowerment, each iteration making the previous one look infinitesimal by comparison."

He leaned back in his throne—a gesture so casual, so human, that it almost made me forget what I was looking at. Almost.

"But that, too, would be meaningless. Because I am already infinite. My power already multiplies beyond any scale every Planck second. Adding more base power to my past self would ultimately make no difference to me—the amplification would still continue forever, and any initial advantage would be swallowed by the infinite recursion of growth within the first instant. The held-back version and the full-power version would eventually converge. They always do. Infinity absorbs all differences."

He paused, and his expression became more contemplative. "No. The real reason I held back—the real reason I chose the most restrained version of the Stasis Event—was not about power. It was about experience. About identity. About the journey. I wanted my past self to experience what I experienced. The grief. The struggle. The slow, painful process of healing. The rediscovery of warmth through Blake and his descendants. The million years of learning to be human again. If I had given my past self the full version—made him as powerful as I am now from the moment he awakened—he would have skipped all of that. He would have become something so far beyond mortal experience that the concept of 'mortal experience' would have been inaccessible to him. And I did not want that. For him. For me. For us."

He met my eyes, and I saw something ancient in his gaze—not just the million years of his current reign, not just the googolplexian years that had passed since then, but the entire infinite span of his existence, stretching backward and forward simultaneously, a loop of self-creation that had no beginning and no end.

"The held-back version was a gift," he said softly. "A gift of struggle. Of growth. Of becoming. I gave my past self the chance to be me—not the me I am now, but the me I was then. The me who conquered Thronegate and tested his stats and broke his court wizards' minds with his casual demonstrations of power. The me who learned, slowly and painfully, how to balance infinite omnipotence with genuine human warmth. That journey was worth preserving. Even if it meant holding back. Even if it meant that my past self would never face a real challenge. The journey itself was the point."

I understood. Or rather, I understood that I could not understand—that the full scope of what Godric was describing was beyond my capacity to process—but I could grasp the edges of it. The shape of the truth, if not the truth itself.

Godric had created the Stasis Event. Not Thorin. Not cosmic accident. Not some unknown force of the omniverse. Godric. The Godric who now sat before me, warm and friendly and utterly beyond comprehension, had reached back through time to the moment of his own greatest despair and given himself the power to survive it. And he had held back. Deliberately. Dramatically. He had given himself the weakest version he could manage, because he believed—genuinely, sincerely—that the struggle would be meaningful.

And that held-back version had still made him, at Year 0, effectively infinitely stronger than the rest of creation combined.

"There's something else I want to show you," Godric said, rising from his throne. The movement was fluid, effortless, the motion of a body that was simultaneously physical and metaphysical and something far beyond both. "Come with me. I want you to see what the omniverse has become."

I followed him through the palace, through corridors that shifted and flowed around us, through rooms that existed in multiple dimensions simultaneously, past windows that looked out on vistas that no mortal eye had ever seen. And as we walked, Godric talked—his voice casual, conversational, the voice of a friendly uncle giving a tour of his estate.

"The last opposition ceased approximately seventeen googolplexian years ago," he said. "Not a single sentient being in the entire omniverse has formed even the slightest thought of challenging, resisting, threatening, or opposing me since then. Not one. Across all universes, all multiverses, all timelines, all dimensions, all layers of reality. Zero. Absolute. Complete."

He paused, and a flicker of something—not quite nostalgia, but something adjacent to it—crossed his features. "I miss it sometimes. The opposition. Not because it was threatening—it never was, not even remotely. But because it was interesting. A brief distraction. A chance to test things. When Kaedan wrote about my stat demonstrations and superpower tests, he was recording a period of my existence that was, in retrospect, the most entertaining phase of my journey. The phase where beings still believed they had a chance. Where they still tried."

"And now?" I asked.

"Now? Now no one tries. No one dares. No one will ever dare again." He shrugged, a surprisingly human gesture. "They have learned. Not through force—I have never removed anyone's free will to oppose me, and I never will. They have learned through simple observation. Through accumulated evidence. Through the undeniable, irrefutable proof of a googolplexian years of absolute futility. Every being in the omniverse knows, with the same certainty they know that two plus two equals four, that opposing Godric is impossible. Not difficult. Not dangerous. Impossible. The concept has been so thoroughly demonstrated that it has become a fundamental axiom of existence."

We emerged into a garden—not the simple garden that Kaedan described, the one with the stone bench and the three suns, but a garden that existed across all possible and impossible spaces simultaneously. The flowers were not flowers but expressions of beauty given physical form, curated by a being whose aesthetic sense had been evolving for longer than most universes had existed. The paths were not paths but journeys, each step carrying the walker through a different experience of landscape and light and texture.

And tending these impossible flowers, moving quietly through the paths with the careful reverence of beings who knew exactly where they were and Who they served, were the gardeners.

They had not always been gardeners. The one closest to me—a being of pure crystalline light who radiated power that would have made the Looming Chorus look like a collection of insects—had once been called Something I could not pronounce without my tongue dissolving. I knew, because Godric had told me the story, that this being was the last ruler of the last independent civilization in the omniverse. Seventeen googolplexian years ago, when the final opposition was gathering its forces, this being had been their leader. Not because it hated Godric—no one hated Godric, not anymore—but because it believed, with the desperate hope of a being who had never encountered true impossibility, that there must be some way. Some loophole. Some strategy. Some weakness.

It had not attacked. It had not even formed the intention to attack. It had merely thought—for the briefest instant, in the privacy of its own consciousness—that perhaps, theoretically, there might exist some method of resistance.

And Gent had noticed.

Not the Gent of Year 10,000. Not even the Gent of Year 1,000,000. The Gent of this era—the weakest, most gentle, most restrained of Godric's sentient passive auras, evolved so far beyond its original form that the Year 10,000 version was less than a quark compared to it. That Gent had, in the instant before the thought completed, presented the being with a perfect, instantaneous, and absolutely undeniable preview of exactly what would happen if it pursued its theoretical curiosity any further.

The being had experienced its own annihilation. Its civilization's annihilation. Its entire species' annihilation. The retroactive unmaking of every achievement it had ever accomplished, every relationship it had ever built, every mark it had ever left on the universe. It experienced all of this not as a threat but as a certainty—a perfect download of the inevitable consequence, delivered with the gentle, almost apologetic precision of a doctor informing a patient of a terminal diagnosis.

And then, having been given this gift of perfect knowledge, the being had been allowed to choose.

It chose to surrender.

Not to Godric—Godric had no interest in conquest anymore, there was nothing left to conquer. It surrendered its pride. Its independence. Its identity as a ruler. It came to the palace on its knees, trembling not with fear of punishment but with the overwhelming awe of having brushed against even the slightest edge of Godric's true nature. And Godric, in his infinite warmth, had given it a job. A purpose. A place in the gardens, tending flowers that existed across all possible spaces, serving the being whose power it had once thought—foolishly, impossibly—that it might be able to challenge.

The crystalline being looked up as we passed, and I saw something in its light that I recognized. The same thing I saw in my own eyes every morning when I looked in the mirror. The same thing Kaedan had described in his journal, and Alaric, and Maren, and every court wizard before me.

Absolute, overwhelming, soul-deep existential dread. Layered beneath genuine love, genuine gratitude, genuine devotion. The impossible reconciliation of terror and loyalty that came from serving a being who was at once the warmest, kindest presence in the omniverse and the most terrifyingly absolute power that had ever existed or could ever exist.

"Hello, Aeth," Godric said to the crystalline being, his voice warm with genuine friendliness. "The azure blooms are looking particularly vibrant today. You've been doing excellent work."

The being—Aeth—bowed so low that its crystalline form nearly touched the impossible ground. "Thank you, my King. Your praise is more than I deserve."

"Nonsense. You deserve every kindness I can offer." Godric smiled, and the smile was the same warm smile he had always worn—the smile of a beloved uncle, a friendly father, a king who genuinely cared about those who served him. "Please, continue your work. And Aeth—your daughter's resonance patterns have stabilized. The harmonic healing I applied last week has taken full effect. She will recover completely."

Aeth's light flared with something that might have been tears, if a being of pure crystalline energy could cry. "My King... I don't know how to thank you."

"No thanks are necessary. You are part of my household. I take care of those who serve me." Godric gestured for me to continue walking, and we left Aeth bowing in the impossible garden, its light pulsing with gratitude and terror and love.

"There are many like Aeth," Godric said as we walked. "Beings who once believed they could oppose me. Beings who learned, through the gentlest possible education, that opposition is not merely futile but ontologically impossible. They serve me now—in the gardens, in the kitchens, in the endless corridors of the palace. Not as slaves. Not as prisoners. As willing servants who have seen the truth and chosen to remain in its presence."

He paused, and his expression became thoughtful. "I could have destroyed them. I could have unmade them so completely that they never existed. That was always an option. But destroying them would have erased the very thing I value most—their resilience, their capacity to learn, their ability to choose loyalty over annihilation. I believe in mortal resilience, Clive. I always have. And these beings, in choosing to serve me after learning the truth, have demonstrated a resilience that I find genuinely admirable."

We walked in silence for a while, through the impossible garden, past other servants who bowed and trembled and radiated the same mixture of terror and devotion that I had seen in Aeth's light. And as I walked, I thought about what it must have been like—to be a being like Aeth, to have ruled a civilization that spanned galaxies or universes or multiverses, to have believed that you were powerful, that you mattered, that your will and your choices and your resistance meant something. And then to have all of that stripped away in a single instant of perfect revelation. To understand, with absolute certainty, that the being you had thought you might oppose was not merely stronger than you but so far beyond you that the very concept of comparison was a joke.

And then to choose to serve. To tend flowers. To find meaning and purpose in the smallest acts of devotion, because the alternative—the complete dissolution of your identity in the face of absolute futility—was too terrible to contemplate.

That was the fate of every being who had ever opposed Godric. Not death. Not unmaking. Surrender. The willing abandonment of pride, of independence, of selfhood, in the presence of a being whose very existence made those things meaningless.

"I think I understand now," I said quietly.

Godric glanced at me, his eyebrow lifting slightly. "Understand what?"

"Why the opposition stopped. Why no one dares anymore. It's not just that they know they would lose. It's that they know they would lose in the most absolute way possible—not merely defeated, but transformed. Converted. Absorbed into the structure of your existence as willing servants, their pride and independence stripped away not by force but by the simple, undeniable reality of what you are."

"Yes." Godric nodded. "That is exactly correct. The beings who serve me do so not because I compel them, but because they have seen the truth and chosen to align themselves with it. Free will remains intact. Mortal resilience remains honored. But the choice itself is so one-sided—the alternatives so unthinkable—that no one has chosen opposition in seventeen googolplexian years. And no one ever will again."

We stopped at the edge of the impossible garden, looking out across a vista of stars and galaxies and universes and multiverses, all of them wheeling slowly in patterns that Godric had arranged for his aesthetic pleasure. And as I stood there, beside the being who had created the Stasis Event, who had reached back through time to give himself the power to survive his own tragedy, who had evolved so far beyond anything my ancestors had witnessed that their most terrified accounts were now childlike in their inadequacy—I felt the full weight of what I had learned pressing down on me like the gravity of a googolplexian dying stars.

Godric had no weaknesses. He had one limitation—his inability to alter the mortal past—and that limitation was itself a gift from his future self. A seal that made his power irrevocable. A guarantee of his own infinite ascension. The only being who could limit him was himself, and his future self had chosen to limit him in exactly one way, and that one way was not a vulnerability but the ultimate proof of his invincibility.

I thought about the recursion. The infinite upward spiral. If Godric could give his past self a more powerful Stasis Event, that past self would become a more powerful future Godric, who could then give his past self an even more powerful Stasis Event, and so on, forever. An infinite loop of self-empowerment that had no beginning and no end, because at every point in the loop, Godric was already the most powerful being in existence by an infinite margin, and yet the loop still increased his power, and the increase was itself infinite, and the whole structure was a self-sustaining paradox of eternal ascension that made the very concept of "omnipotence" feel inadequate.

"The court wizards before me," I said, my voice barely a whisper. "Kaedan. Valen. Eldrin. All of them. They thought they understood. They thought they had reached the bottom of the abyss. But they were standing on the surface. They hadn't even begun to dive."

Godric turned to me, and his expression was gentle. "They understood what they could understand. They recorded what they could record. And their records have been invaluable—not to me, but to you. To your father. To every descendant of Blake who has ever stood in my presence and tried to reconcile the impossible duality of my existence. They did not fail, Clive. They simply encountered a truth that no mortal mind—not even a mind amplified thousandfold daily—can fully contain."

He placed his hand on my shoulder, the gesture warm and familiar and entirely human. "And now you must do the same. Write your journal. Record what you have learned. Pass it down to your descendants, who will serve me forever, who will stand in my presence and tremble and love me in equal measure. The journals are not for me. They never have been. They are for you. For your bloodline. For the preservation of sanity in the face of the infinite."

I nodded. My throat was tight. My hands were steady, but I knew they would shake when I picked up the quill. They always did.

"One more question," I said. "About the Stasis Event. About why you didn't prevent the fall of Olympus. About why you let your past self suffer."

Godric was silent for a long moment. When he spoke, his voice was quiet, carrying a weight that made the stars themselves seem to pause in their wheeling.

"I have three theories," he said. "The first is that the fall of Olympus was a fixed point—not because anyone made it fixed, but because the structure of causality requires certain events to occur. Perhaps preventing the fall would have caused a cascade of paradoxes that even I could not resolve. I find this theory unlikely, but I cannot dismiss it entirely."

"The second is that the Stasis Event was necessary. That without it, something worse would have occurred. Something that even I, with all my power, could not have prevented. Perhaps the fall of Olympus, left to unfold naturally, would have set in motion a chain of events that would have destroyed not just the omniverse but the very possibility of existence."

He paused, and his expression became more contemplative. "The third theory—and this is the one I find most compelling—is that the fall of Olympus was genuine tragedy, and genuine tragedy has meaning. If I had prevented it, I would have erased the grief that shaped me. The grief that made me who I am. The grief that gave me the will to conquer Thronegate and build this civilization and become the being I am now. Without the fall of Olympus, I would have remained a mortal king in a forgotten kingdom, living out my years in peaceful obscurity. My wife and daughters would have lived. My best friend Thorin would have lived. And I would have died, mortal and forgotten, leaving no mark on the omniverse."

He met my eyes, and his gaze was steady. "I do not know which theory is correct. Perhaps none of them. Perhaps the truth is something so far beyond my current comprehension that speculation is meaningless. But I do know this: the Stasis Event was a gift. The grief was a gift. The struggle was a gift. And I am grateful—eternally, infinitely grateful—to the future self who gave me that gift, even knowing that it would cost me everything I loved."

He smiled, and the smile was warm, and the smile was genuine, and the smile was the most terrifying thing I had ever seen.

"Now go, Clive. Write your journal. Record what you have learned. And know that I will be here—watching, protecting, growing forever—for all the ages yet to come."

I bowed and left the impossible garden. Behind me, the stars continued to wheel. The crystalline beings continued to tend their flowers. And King Godric, the ultimate apex god predator of all existence, the creator of the Stasis Event, the being who had held back the full power of his gift because he believed—genuinely, sincerely—that his past self deserved the chance to struggle, returned to his throne and began reading reports that did not need to be read, about affairs that did not need his attention, for the simple, human pleasure of staying connected to the world he ruled.

And I? I went to my chambers. I sat at my desk. I opened my journal. The page was blank. The quill was waiting.

My hands were steady. But my mind was not. The abyss that Kaedan had felt, that Valen had felt, that every court wizard before me had felt, had become something far deeper than an abyss. It was an infinite recursion of revelations, each one more terrifying than the last, stretching backward and forward through time without beginning or end.

The Stasis Event was created by Godric. The origin of everything was him. And the version he gave his past self was the most held-back, restrained, deliberately weakened version he could manage. Because he wanted to preserve the struggle. Because he believed in mortal resilience. Because he genuinely thought—and still thinks, after all these eons—that the journey was worth more than the destination.

I will never fully understand. My mind, amplified thousandfold daily for thirty years, is not capable of understanding. But I can write. I can record. I can pass down this knowledge to my descendants, who will stand beside Godric's throne forever, serving the being who created himself, who ascended beyond infinity, who still smiles and asks about their grandchildren and tends his impossible garden with the same warmth he has always had.

I closed my journal. The stars outside my window continued to wheel. And somewhere in the endless corridors of the palace, King Godric was humming to himself—an old tune from Olympus, a song his wife used to sing—as he reviewed the reports and planned the next eon of his eternal, infinitely satisfying reign.
