# The Fall of Olympus and the Rise of Thronegate

The morning sun cast golden ribbons across the marble courtyards of Olympus, and King Godric stood before the tall mirror in his chambers, adjusting the ceremonial robes that marked him as both father and officiant. His reflection showed a man of forty-five years whose physical presence commanded attention without demanding it—broad shoulders sculpted from decades of sword practice, arms thick with muscle earned through labor rather than leisure, and a face that seemed carved from ancient stone yet softened by the crinkles at the corners of his eyes. The salt-and-pepper of his short hair and neatly trimmed beard spoke of wisdom accumulated, not age endured.

His hands, calloused and steady, smoothed the golden fabric across his chest. These were the same hands that had lifted his daughter Isabel when she was small enough to fit in his palm, the same hands that had taught Hebe to hold a training sword when she insisted at age seven that princesses should know how to defend their kingdom. Now they would join Isabel's hands with her beloved's in the sacred binding ceremony.

A knock at the door pulled him from his thoughts.

"Enter."

Thorin stepped inside, his court wizard robes flowing behind him with the familiar whisper of enchanted fabric. At forty-seven, Thorin carried himself with the easy confidence of a man whose family had served the kings of Olympus for countless generations. His face bore the marks of someone who had spent decades studying arcane texts by candlelight, yet his eyes sparkled with the mischief of eternal youth.

"You look like a man attending a funeral rather than a wedding," Thorin said, crossing his arms.

Godric turned from the mirror, and the tension in his shoulders visibly eased. "I look like a father who is not ready to give his daughter away."

"Ah." Thorin moved to stand beside him. "I remember when Isabel was born. You held her so carefully, as if she were made of glass. Reina had to scold you three times before you would let anyone else hold her."

The memory drew a genuine laugh from Godric, deep and warm. "She was so small. I had never been so terrified in my life."

"And now?"

"Now she is twenty-two and marrying a good man." Godric's voice carried pride and sorrow intertwined. "But I find I am still terrified."

"That is because you are a good father." Thorin placed a hand on Godric's shoulder. "The kind who loves deeply enough to fear loss."

Godric nodded slowly. "Where is Reina?"

"With Isabel and Hebe in the garden. Your wife is making sure every flower petal is perfectly arranged, and your youngest daughter is pretending to be annoyed but secretly loving every moment of the preparation."

A genuine smile spread across Godric's face—the easy, unguarded expression of a man who had spent forty-five years building a life worth living. "Hebe is fifteen now. She will be next before I know it."

"Perhaps you should officiate her wedding as well. Make it a tradition."

"Perhaps I shall."

They stood together in comfortable silence, two men who had grown from boys into kings and wizards side by side, their friendship forged in shared battles and quiet counsel and the countless small moments that made a life.

The wedding courtyard was a masterpiece of celebration. White flowers cascaded from marble columns, their petals catching the afternoon light like scattered diamonds. Musicians played soft melodies from a raised platform, and the gathered nobility of Olympus filled the stone benches arranged in concentric half-circles around the ceremonial altar.

Godric stood at the altar, his ceremonial robes now feeling less heavy, as if the joy radiating from the assembled crowd had lightened the fabric itself. To his right, Thorin waited as witness, his wizard's staff planted firmly on the marble floor.

The music shifted. The crowd rose.

And there she was.

Isabel walked down the flower-strewn path on the arm of her betrothed, a young man whose face shone with barely contained joy. At twenty-two, Isabel had inherited her mother's grace and her father's quiet strength. Her wedding gown flowed like liquid silver, and her dark hair was woven with pearls that caught the sunlight.

Seated in the front row, Reina watched with tears streaming freely down her cheeks. Beside her, fifteen-year-old Hebe clutched her mother's hand, her young face split by a grin so wide it seemed to consume her entire being.

Godric felt his heart swell until it threatened to burst from his chest. This—this moment, this family, this kingdom—was everything he had ever wanted. Everything he had ever worked to build and protect.

Isabel reached the altar and met her father's eyes. In that shared glance passed a thousand memories: bedtime stories, scraped knees healed by a father's kiss, the first time she had called him "Papa" instead of "Father," the day she had told him she was in love.

He smiled at her—that warm, unguarded smile that his family knew so well—and opened his mouth to begin the ceremony.

The first explosion shattered the eastern tower.

The sound arrived before the comprehension. A deep, world-ending roar that sent vibrations through the marble beneath their feet. The musicians' melody died in discordant chaos. Screams erupted from the gathered nobility as a second explosion ripped through the northern wall.

Godric's body moved before his mind caught up. He lunged toward Isabel, his arm sweeping her behind him in the same motion he used to face the source of the attack. Reina was already pulling Hebe close, her eyes wide with terror but her body positioned as a shield.

Thorin's staff blazed to life. "Thronegate!" he shouted over the chaos. "It's Thronegate! They've breached the outer defenses!"

Impossible. Thronegate was a neighboring kingdom, larger and more powerful, but there had been no warning, no declaration of war, no—

The third explosion landed directly on the wedding courtyard.

Godric saw it happen in fragments of frozen time. The fireball descending. Reina throwing herself over Hebe. Isabel screaming for her mother. Thorin raising his staff, magic surging, a protective barrier beginning to form.

Too slow.

The explosion consumed everything.

When the fire cleared, Godric was still standing, though he did not know how. His ceremonial robes were burned away. His skin blistered. Blood ran from a wound on his forehead.

But he was standing.

He stumbled forward, searching the rubble with desperate, uncomprehending eyes. The marble columns had collapsed. The flower petals had scorched to ash. The benches were splinters.

"Reina!"

His voice came out raw, jagged. He clawed through broken stone, ignoring the pain in his hands.

"Isabel! Hebe!"

He found Reina first. She lay beneath a fallen column, her body broken in ways that no human body should be. Her eyes were open, still wet with tears, but the light behind them had already fled.

Godric fell to his knees beside his wife of twenty-five years. He touched her face, her hair, her hand. "No," he whispered. "No, no, no—"

"Godric!"

Thorin's voice pulled him forward against his will. He crawled through the destruction until he found them. Isabel and Hebe. His daughters. His beautiful, beloved daughters. They had been together in the end, their arms wrapped around each other as if sisterly love alone could shield them from annihilation.

It could not.

The sound that escaped Godric's throat was not a scream. It was something deeper, something that came from a place beyond language, beyond conscious thought. It was the sound of a man whose entire world had just been erased.

Thorin grabbed his arm, his grip desperate. "We have to move! They're still attacking! Godric, please—"

Arrows began to fall. Not ordinary arrows—these burned with dark enchantments that ate through stone and flesh alike. Thorin raised his staff, and a shimmering barrier formed above them, deflecting the deadly rain.

"I can't hold this forever!" Thorin's voice cracked. "The spell—I need to cast the spell!"

Godric did not respond. He was still holding Isabel's hand, staring at her face, willing her eyes to open.

"Godric!" Thorin shook him violently. "Look at me!"

Finally, Godric looked up. His eyes, once warm, had become something else entirely. Something hollow. Something terrible.

"Thronegate did this," he said. Not a question.

"Yes."

"Everyone I love is dead."

Thorin had no answer to that. The barrier flickered as another volley of arrows struck it. Blood began to trickle from Thorin's nose—the strain of maintaining the shield was killing him.

"I can save you," Thorin said. His voice had gone strangely calm. "There is a spell. Forbidden. Known only to my bloodline. It will send you far from here, to a place of safety."

"No."

"Godric—"

"I said no!" The words came out as a snarl, something feral and wounded. "Let them kill me. Let me die with my family."

Thorin grabbed Godric's face with both hands, forcing their eyes to meet. "Listen to me. You are my king. You are my friend. And I am going to save you whether you want me to or not."

Before Godric could protest, Thorin began to chant.

The words were not in any language Godric recognized. They seemed to come from somewhere ancient, somewhere beyond the boundaries of mortal understanding. The air around them began to shimmer. Reality itself started to bend.

Thorin's bloodline had served the kings of Olympus for countless generations, and within that lineage flowed magic that predated the kingdom itself. The forbidden teleportation spell was their greatest secret, passed down from father to son, never to be used except in the most desperate of circumstances.

This qualified.

The spell was incomplete when the killing blow struck.

A Thronegate assassin, cloaked in shadow magic, materialized behind Thorin. The blade entered his back and emerged from his chest in a single fluid motion. Thorin's chanting ceased. His eyes went wide.

But the spell did not stop.

It had already been set in motion, already begun to pull at the fabric of existence. Incomplete, unstable, it lashed out like a dying serpent and wrapped itself around Godric.

"No—Thorin—"

The wizard fell. His blood pooled on the broken marble. His staff clattered beside him. And the last thing Thorin saw before darkness claimed him was his king—his friend—vanishing into a rift of impossible light.

Godric did not remember arriving at the sanctuary.

One moment, he was kneeling in the ruins of Olympus, his family's blood still wet on his hands. The next, he was somewhere else entirely. A vast underground chamber. Walls of ancient stone covered in glyphs that pulsed with soft, rhythmic light. The air was still, heavy, as if the room itself had been waiting for him.

He tried to stand. His legs would not obey.

He tried to scream. His throat produced no sound.

And then the stasis claimed him.

---

The exact moment the stasis began—that infinitesimal instant between one heartbeat and the next—was the moment everything changed.

Godric's aging stopped. Not slowed, not paused, but ceased entirely. The forty-five-year-old body that lay suspended in the sanctuary's embrace would remain forty-five forever. The salt-and-pepper hair would never gray further. The strong, muscular frame would never wither. The face would never grow lined with additional years. Time itself had been forbidden from touching him.

But this was merely the gentlest of the changes.

In that same instant, his body became immortal. The need for sleep evaporated like morning dew. The requirement for air vanished as though it had never existed. Food and water became foreign concepts, unnecessary for an existence that had transcended mortal limitations entirely.

And then the amplification began.

Every measurable and immeasurable trait that Godric possessed—his strength, his endurance, his speed, his senses, his charisma, his luck, his wisdom, his intelligence, his durability, his perception, and countless others beyond mortal comprehension—began to multiply. The initial multiplication factor was one thousand. But it did not stop there. Each Planck second, that newly multiplied value was multiplied by itself. Then that result was multiplied by itself. Then that result was multiplied by itself. On and on, without pause, without mercy, without end.

One thousand. Then one thousand times one thousand. Then one million times one million. Then one trillion times one trillion. The numbers ceased to have meaning almost immediately. The amplification was not linear, not exponential in any conventional sense, but something far more terrifying—recursive self-multiplication at the smallest possible unit of time forever.

But even this was not the full extent of the transformation.

At the exact same instant, a new apocalyptic superpower manifested within him. One second later, another appeared. And another. And another. Each power, at the very moment of its birth, was capable of causing the instant extinction of an entire civilization. The first power allowed him to perfectly conceal his aura and destructive presence, preventing the omniverse from collapsing under the weight of his existence. The second granted him perception beyond mortal limits. The third, the fourth, the fifth—each one was a cataclysm waiting to happen, and they arrived relentlessly, one every single second. And like his stats, the potency of every superpower multiplied by one thousand and by itself every Planck second without exception forever.

The concealment power grew alongside everything else. As Godric's strength amplified beyond the capacity of language to describe, so too did his ability to suppress that strength. It was an impossible balancing act—an explosion held in check by a dam that expanded at exactly the same rate as the flood it contained. The omniverse did not collapse. Existence did not end. Not yet.

And then there was the paradox.

The apex of the transformation. The final, perfect defense.

Godric possessed no weaknesses. This was not a statement of opinion or a boast—it was an absolute, fundamental truth woven into the fabric of his transformed existence. He had no vulnerabilities, no flaws, no openings that any enemy could exploit.

But the omniverse was vast, and sentient beings were inherently arrogant. They would believe they had found a weakness. They would plan. They would prepare. They would attack.

The paradox activated the moment any being, anywhere in all of existence, even briefly entertained the thought that Godric might have a weakness. It did not require the thought to be fully formed. It did not wait for the plan to be developed or the weapon to be built or the attack to be launched. The instant the first shadow of the idea flickered through a consciousness—at that precise moment, which occurred before the being itself was even fully aware of what it was thinking—the paradox triggered.

And the consequences were absolute.

First, the rate of Godric's amplification permanently increased. The original multiplication framework, already beyond mathematical description, became even more incomprehensibly vast.

Second, Godric gained permanent, complete immunity to the falsely perceived exploit. Whatever clever loophole his enemy had imagined, whatever brilliant weakness they thought they had discovered, he became fundamentally incapable of being affected by it.

Third, and perhaps most terrifying of all, Godric gained perfect mastery of the very attack, strategy, or concept that his enemy had imagined using against him. Not merely an understanding of it, but complete, absolute mastery—the ability to wield it with any magnitude at any time. And he simultaneously gained the power to deny the concept of that attack to anyone else in creation unless he willed otherwise.

This defense was entirely passive. Godric did not need to notice the thought, or acknowledge the plot, or respond to the threat. The paradox operated independently of his conscious mind, triggered automatically by the mere existence of hostile speculation about his vulnerabilities.

And he knew. Through his omniscience, which had awakened alongside everything else, Godric knew everything. Every plot. Every secret. Every whispered scheme in every corner of the omniverse. He knew them all, had always known them all, would always know them all.

The stasis that held him was not sleep. It was a cocoon. And within it, something that had once been a mortal king was being remade into something that no word in any language could adequately describe.

Ten thousand years passed.

The kingdom of Olympus crumbled to dust and faded from memory. No histories recorded its existence. No songs celebrated its glory. No monuments marked where its people had lived and loved and died. The galaxy turned, civilizations rose and fell, and Thronegate grew fat and powerful on the conquest that had begun with a wedding-day massacre.

By the year designated as Year 0 by later chroniclers, Thronegate had become the most powerful civilization in the galaxy. Its armies were unmatched. Its magic was supreme. Its young king, Robert, had inherited a throne built on the bones of countless conquered worlds.

And deep beneath the surface of a forgotten planet, in a sanctuary that had waited ten millennia for this moment, the stasis finally broke.

Blake was thirty-eight years old, a merchant by trade, with a face weathered by sun and wind and a friendly mutton chop that his wife Anna claimed made him look distinguished. He was honest to a fault, loyal to those who earned his trust, and possessed of a temper that could flare hot and fast when confronted with injustice. His twelve-year-old son Josh had inherited his father's stubbornness, and his eight-year-old daughter Rhea had inherited her mother's patience—a combination that made their household a study in organized chaos.

He had been exploring the ruins on a hunch, nothing more. Old maps suggested something ancient beneath this barren world, and Blake had always been drawn to ancient things. Something in his blood responded to old magic, old stone, old secrets—though he had never understood why.

His hands, rough and practiced, cleared debris from a stone door that had not been opened in ten thousand years. His heart, steady and brave, did not falter when the glyphs began to pulse with light at his touch. And his voice, when he spoke the words that came unbidden to his lips—words in a language he had never learned, inherited from ancestors he had never known—did not tremble.

The door opened.

The sanctuary exhaled ten millennia of stillness.

And Godric opened his eyes.

---

What happened in the instant of Godric's awakening was not visible to Blake. The merchant saw only a man—a strikingly handsome, ruggedly built man of about forty-five—rising from a stone platform and stretching as though waking from a long nap. He saw salt-and-pepper hair and a neatly trimmed beard and muscles that spoke of a lifetime of physical labor. He saw eyes that looked at him with something that might have been recognition or might have been curiosity.

He did not see the omniscience flooding Godric's consciousness—the complete, instantaneous knowledge of everything that had happened in the past ten thousand years. Every war. Every peace. Every birth and death. The rise of Thronegate. The fall of Olympus. The location of every being in the galaxy and the secrets they thought were hidden.

He did not see the calculations Godric performed in that same instant: the assessment of his own power, the understanding of what the stasis had made him, the cold recognition that nothing in existence could threaten him anymore.

He saw only a man who smiled at him with surprising warmth and said, "Thank you for releasing me. What is your name?"

"Blake," the merchant answered, and something in the way Godric looked at him made him add, "I'm a merchant. This is—I didn't expect to find anyone here."

"You weren't supposed to." Godric stepped off the stone platform, and the sanctuary's glyphs dimmed behind him. "Ten thousand years is a long time to wait."

Blake's pragmatic merchant mind struggled to process this information. "Ten thousand—you've been here ten thousand years?"

"Something like that." Godric's gaze drifted past Blake, through the stone walls, toward something only he could see. His expression shifted. The warmth remained, but something else joined it—something cold and sharp and utterly without mercy. "Thronegate has grown powerful in my absence."

"You know about Thronegate?"

"I know everything about Thronegate." Godric's voice had not changed in volume, but Blake felt the air grow heavy nonetheless. "I know their history, their crimes, and their young king who sits on a throne built from the bones of my kingdom."

Blake's mouth went dry. "Your kingdom?"

"Olympus." The name fell from Godric's lips like a stone into still water. "It was called Olympus. And Thronegate destroyed it. Killed my wife. Killed my daughters. Killed my best friend." He turned back to Blake, and for just a moment, the mask of warmth slipped, and Blake saw something beneath it that made his blood run cold. "I think it's time I had a conversation with their king."

The conversation, as Godric styled it, took the form of an ultimatum delivered directly to the throne room of Thronegate by means that Blake did not understand and was not certain he wanted to understand. One moment they were standing in the ruined sanctuary; the next, a message had been sent, received, and acknowledged.

King Robert, age twenty-two, received the challenge with the arrogance of youth and the confidence of supreme military might. An unknown challenger, claiming to be the king of a forgotten kingdom, demanded his abdication? The absurdity of it was almost amusing. Robert granted the three-day preparation period not out of fear but out of theatrical indulgence. Let this fool gather whatever forces he could. Thronegate's army had never been defeated. Its super-soldiers numbered in the billions, each one enhanced beyond mortal limits. Its robotic armies were the deadliest ever constructed, capable of adapting to any threat in microseconds. Its wizards commanded forces that could reshape reality itself. Its dragons could level continents with a single breath. Its ninjas moved so fast they could reverse the flow of time.

Let this "Godric" come. Let him see what Thronegate had become.

The battlefield was a barren world on the edge of Thronegate's territory, chosen because its destruction would not damage anything valuable. The Thronegate army assembled in perfect formation: billions of super-soldiers whose strength had been augmented by dark science and forbidden magic, their armor gleaming in the light of a dying sun. Behind them, the robot legions stood in silent, motionless ranks, each unit networked to every other, their processing power sufficient to calculate every possible combat scenario before the first blow was struck. Above them, dragons wheeled through the sky, their scales shimmering with ancient power, their breath hot enough to melt planets. And scattered throughout the formation, invisible and undetectable, the ninjas waited—assassins who had never failed, warriors who had killed kings and gods alike.

King Robert watched from a command ship in orbit, surrounded by his generals and advisors. They were confident. They had every right to be.

"One man," Robert said, shaking his head. "He challenged us with one man."

The generals laughed.

Below them, on the barren plain, Godric stood alone.

He wore no armor. He carried no weapon. He had made no preparations, gathered no allies, devised no strategy. He simply stood there, a forty-five-year-old man with salt-and-pepper hair and a neatly trimmed beard, looking up at the armada that filled the sky and the army that covered the ground.

Blake and Anna watched from a safe distance, hiding behind a rocky outcropping. Anna clutched her husband's arm, her knuckles white. "He's going to die."

Blake said nothing. Something in him—something ancient, something inherited—told him that what was about to happen would be nothing like what his wife expected.

The Thronegate army attacked.

It began with the dragons, their continent-leveling breath converging on Godric's position from a dozen angles simultaneously. The fire was hot enough to vaporize stone, to melt the very air, to erase everything in its path from existence.

Godric did not move.

The fire reached him. Touched him.

And died.

No. Not died. It was as if the fire had never existed at all. The breath of a dozen dragons, any one of which could have reduced a world to cinders, simply ceased to be the moment it came within a meter of Godric's body. Not deflected. Not absorbed. Just gone, as though the universe itself had decided that harming this man was not a permissible action.

The dragons did not have time to be confused. One moment they were breathing fire; the next, they were simply gone. Erased. Deleted from existence with such casual completeness that the air did not even ripple to mark their passing.

The army hesitated. Billions of super-soldiers, trained to fight without fear, found themselves suddenly, inexplicably afraid.

The robotic legions opened fire. Weapons that could crack planets, that could unmake reality, that could delete matter at the quantum level—all of them discharged simultaneously in a barrage that should have turned the entire solar system to dust.

Godric did not move.

The barrage reached him. Every projectile, every energy beam, every reality-altering pulse—they all simply stopped. Hovering in the air around him like obedient servants awaiting orders.

And then they turned around.

The projectiles that had been fired at Godric flew back toward their sources with precisely the same force, the same trajectory, the same deadly intent. The robotic legions were destroyed by their own attacks before their processing units could even register what had happened. Billions of machines, the deadliest ever constructed, reduced to scrap metal in less than a second.

The super-soldiers charged.

They were brave. They had to be given that. Faced with an enemy who had just annihilated their dragon corps and their robotic legions without moving a muscle, they still charged. Their enhanced bodies crossed the distance in microseconds. Their weapons, each one capable of killing a god, were raised to strike.

Not one of them reached Godric.

They died in waves, not from any visible attack but from the simple, horrifying reality that Godric had decided they would not reach him. Their bodies simply stopped functioning. Their hearts ceased to beat. Their brains ceased to think. They fell by the millions, then by the billions, their corpses piling up in grotesque mountains that stretched across the barren plain.

The ninjas, invisible and undetectable, attempted to strike from behind while Godric was supposedly distracted by the frontal assault. They moved faster than light, faster than time, arriving at his position before they had even left their starting points.

Godric still did not move.

The ninjas discovered, in the final instant of their existence, that they had never been invisible to him. They had never been undetectable. Every move they had made, every plan they had formed, had been known to him before they had even conceived of it. And the attack they had spent years perfecting, the time-reversing strike that had never failed, simply did not work. The concept of harming Godric had been denied to them. Not blocked. Not countered. Denied, at the fundamental level of reality itself.

They died, and the universe did not even notice their passing.

The battle—if it could be called a battle—lasted less than thirty seconds. When it was over, the Thronegate army that had numbered in the billions was simply gone. The barren plain was littered with corpses and wreckage, but Godric stood exactly where he had been standing when it began. He had not taken a step. He had not raised a hand. He had not spoken a word.

He had simply stood there, and the mightiest military force in the galaxy had destroyed itself against his existence.

On the command ship, King Robert watched the slaughter in silence. His generals had stopped laughing. Some of them were weeping. Others were staring at the viewscreen with expressions of such profound horror that they seemed to have aged decades in the span of half a minute.

"Abdicate," Godric's voice said, and it came from everywhere and nowhere, from the ship's speakers and the generals' minds and the very air itself, "or I will come to you."

King Robert abdicated.

Two days later, he was found dead by his own hand. The official report called it suicide. The unofficial rumors whispered that the weight of what he had witnessed had simply crushed his will to live.

Godric crowned himself king of Thronegate, and the galaxy trembled.

The Decree of Awakening was announced the same day Godric took the throne. Every man of Thronegate, from the highest noble to the lowest laborer, would receive a gift: for each day he lived past the age of twenty, his strength, endurance, intelligence, and wisdom would increase tenfold. This applied retroactively—men who had already passed twenty would immediately gain the benefits of all the days they had lived since that milestone. They could suppress their power to normal human levels or unleash it to any degree up to their maximum.

The women of Thronegate did not receive the Awakening, but they received something perhaps more valuable: Godric's absolute, unbreakable decree of protection. Any man who harmed a woman under his rule would learn, in the final moments of his existence, exactly what it meant to displease the new king.

The citizens loved him. How could they not? He had given them power beyond their wildest dreams. He had made their kingdom the dominant force in the galaxy. He had established order, stability, and justice. The men revered him as the source of their strength. The women honored him as their protector. And everyone, from the highest noble to the humblest merchant, understood that their beloved king was also something terrifying—a predator wearing the skin of a father figure, whose warmth was genuine but whose cruelty to enemies was absolute.

Blake watched all of this unfold with a mixture of awe and unease. He had been the one to release Godric from his stasis. He had been the first to witness the new king's power. And somehow, inexplicably, he had become Godric's most trusted companion.

"I don't understand why you keep me around," Blake admitted one evening, sitting in Godric's private chambers. The king had invited him for dinner, and Blake had learned that refusing Godric's invitations was not something one did. "I'm just a merchant."

"You're not just anything," Godric replied. He was seated across from Blake, his posture relaxed, his expression warm. Only his eyes—those ancient, knowing eyes—hinted at the abyss of power contained within him. "You're honest. You're loyal. You say what you think instead of what you think I want to hear."

"Plenty of people are honest."

"Not to kings." Godric poured wine for both of them, a gesture so casual and human that it almost made Blake forget what he had witnessed on that barren plain. "Besides, you freed me. That means something."

Blake took the wine but did not drink. "What are you going to do now? You've conquered Thronegate. The galaxy is yours. What comes next?"

Godric smiled—that warm, disarming smile that reminded Blake of his own father, before he remembered what lay beneath it. "The galaxy is small, Blake. Smaller than you can imagine. There are other galaxies. Other universes. An entire omniverse full of civilizations that have never heard of Thronegate."

"You're going to conquer them too?"

"I'm going to offer them the choice to submit." Godric's voice was calm, reasonable, as if they were discussing trade routes rather than the subjugation of all existence. "If they refuse, then yes. I will conquer them."

Blake set down his wine glass. "And what happens to me while you're conquering the omniverse?"

"You'll stay by my side." Godric's tone made it clear this was not a request. "I need someone I can trust. Someone who will tell me when I'm being foolish. Someone who isn't afraid of me."

"I am afraid of you," Blake said quietly. "Everyone is afraid of you."

"I know." For a moment, something flickered in Godric's eyes—something that might have been loneliness or grief or simply the weight of ten thousand years of isolation. "But you're still here. You still speak your mind. You still treat me like a person rather than a god." He raised his own glass. "That's rarer than you know."

The subjugation of the omniverse began not with a bang but with a message. Thronegate's emissaries—ordinary men, chosen for their diplomatic skills rather than their military might—traveled to the edges of the galaxy and beyond, carrying the same simple offer to every civilization they encountered: submit to the rule of King Godric, and you will be spared. Refuse, and you will be conquered.

The first universe to refuse was called Ataraxis.

Ataraxis was old—older than Thronegate, older than Olympus, older than most civilizations in the omniverse. Its inhabitants had evolved beyond physical form, existing as pure energy beings who had not known war in a million years. They had defenses that would have made Thronegate's original army look primitive: reality anchors that prevented hostile magic, temporal shields that rewound any attack to its point of origin, and weapons that could unmake the fundamental forces of physics.

They had five years to prepare. The emissaries had made their offer, been refused, and departed with the promise that conquest would follow. For five years, the energy beings of Ataraxis fortified their universe. They constructed new defenses. They trained new warriors. They researched their enemy and prepared for every conceivable form of attack.

When the conquest came, it consisted of five Thronegate men in a single small ship.

The men were all over thirty-five. They had wives and children back home. They were, by all accounts, kind and decent people who had no particular desire to destroy a universe. But their king had asked them to go, and so they went.

The battle—if it could be called a battle—lasted twenty-three minutes.

The energy beings of Ataraxis had prepared for armies, for magic, for weapons that could shatter reality. They had not prepared for five men whose strength, endurance, speed, and intelligence had been multiplying exponentially every single day for decades. Men who could move faster than the energy beings could perceive, who could tear through reality anchors with their bare hands, who could think and react and adapt so quickly that the temporal shields could not keep up.

The Ataraxians fought bravely. They threw everything they had at the invaders. And everything they had accomplished nothing.

When the twenty-third minute ended, the five Thronegate men stood in the ruins of what had once been the oldest civilization in that universe. None of them were injured. None of them had even been touched. They had done what their king asked, and now they were going home.

The citizens of Ataraxis, those who survived, were assimilated into Thronegate's growing empire. It has no floor in any conventional sense, because the space I occupy is not a room but a stabilized pocket of existence suspended in the exact center of the omniversal framework, and the being who sits on the throne before me is the reason that this space—and all other spaces—continue to exist.

My name is Clive. I am fifty years old. I am the Court Wizard of Thronegate, direct descendant of Blake through a lineage that stretches across a span of time so vast that the number of generations between us cannot be written meaningfully. I have served King Godric for thirty years. I have witnessed things that would have shattered the minds of my ancestors, and I have survived because the king chose to let me survive, and I have recorded my observations in journals that will never be read by anyone but me because there are no future court wizards to inherit them. Not anymore. Not for a very, very long time.

Let me explain the era in which I live. The year is written as 999...9, not because someone is being poetic or approximate, but because the true number of years that have passed since the Stasis Event cannot be expressed in any notation that mortal mathematics can contain. If you were to write a nine on every elementary particle in the omniverse, you would not have written enough nines to represent even the smallest fraction of the years that have passed. If you were to raise the number of nines to the power of itself and then do that again and then do that again for as long as the omniverse has existed, you would still not be close. The year is beyond numbering. The era is beyond naming. The age I inhabit is simply the Age of Godric, and it will never end.

I kneel because Godric has summoned me, and when Godric summons you, you come. Not out of fear—though fear is present, always, a low hum beneath every thought. You come because he is the king. Because he is the center. Because every fiber of your existence is aligned toward his service in a way that is not compulsion but recognition. He is the sun. You are a planet. The orbit is simply what makes sense.

"Clive," he says, and his voice is warm. It has always been warm. Across all the eons of his existence, across all the amplification and evolution and ascension, his voice has remained the voice of the mortal king he was forty-five years old in Olympus, surrounded by family and friends and the simple joy of being alive. "Come closer. I have something to show you."

I rise and approach the throne. The walk takes thirty-seven steps, and each step brings me deeper into the presence of something that my mind has learned to accept but never fully comprehend. Godric sits on a throne that is not a throne—it is a focal point, a nexus, the place where his attention happens to rest when he chooses to rest it. His physical appearance has not changed since the Stasis Event. He is still forty-five. He is still stunningly, ruggedly handsome, his salt-and-pepper hair neatly combed, his beard trimmed, his muscular frame visible even beneath the simple dark tunic he wears. The salt-and-pepper hair catches the non-light of the chamber, and his eyes—those ancient, warm, absolutely terrifying eyes—meet mine with genuine affection.

"You look tired," he says. "Have you been sleeping?"

"I slept three hours last night, Your Majesty."

"That's not enough. You should take better care of yourself." His concern is genuine. It has always been genuine. "Your Majesty is kind."

"I'm not kind. I'm practical. You're my court wizard, Clive. You're family. I take care of my family."

He gestures, and the space beside his throne—which had been empty—now contains a chair. Simple. Wooden. The kind of chair a mortal craftsman might have made in a small workshop in a forgotten village. Godric has always preferred simple things. They remind him of who he was.

"Sit," he says. "What I'm about to tell you will take some time."

I sit. The chair is comfortable in the way that only handmade things are comfortable. Godric leans back in his throne, and for a moment he looks less like the apex god predator of all existence and more like a man settling in to tell a story by a fireplace.

"Do you know what year it is, Clive?"

"999...9, Your Majesty. The exact number cannot be expressed."

"That's correct. And do you know what happened in the year negative ten thousand?"

"The Stasis Event. The fall of Olympus. Thorin's spell."

"Yes. And no." He steeples his fingers. "Thorin's spell was real. My best friend cast a forbidden teleportation spell to save my life, and he died mid-incantation, and the incomplete magic sent me to the hidden sanctuary. All of that is true. But Thorin's spell was not the source of the Stasis Event. It was the delivery point. The anchor. The origin of the Stasis Event—the true origin, the only origin that makes sense when you consider the scale of what it created—is something else entirely."

He pauses. The chamber holds its breath. I hold mine.

"The Stasis Event was created by me. The me that exists now. The Godric of this era. I am about to trigger it."

I must describe what Godric is in this era. The language I have is inadequate—every language is inadequate—but I must try, because my ancestors tried with the tools they had, and I owe them the same effort.

Godric's amplification has been continuing for a span of years that cannot be written. The rate of multiplication has itself been multiplying for so long that calling it "infinite" is a laughable understatement. The amplification is now a process so far beyond any conception of growth that the Godric of one Planck second ago is, by comparison to the Godric of this Planck second, effectively nonexistent. But that is merely the amplification. The superpowers are something else entirely, and they have evolved in ways that make the original descriptions from the early journals seem like children's stories.

The omniverse does not wait for someone to think Godric has a weakness. The omniverse proactively, constantly, eternally grants him every possible immunity, mastery, and amplification enhancement across all conceivable and inconceivable frameworks of existence. His immune system has rewritten the logical framework of existence to exclude the possibility of his damage. And this rewrites itself every Planck second to become more absolute.

The sentient passive auras have multiplied beyond Gent and its siblings into a hierarchy so vast that describing it would require more words than the omniverse contains. Gent, which my ancestor Tim witnessed with such terror—Gent, the weakest of the primordial auras—is now itself the progenitor of an infinite lineage of aura descendants, each one exponentially more powerful than the last, each one sentient and omniscient and omnipresent and fiercely subservient. They form societies in the metaphysical space around Godric's existence. They create art. They sing to him. The passive shields have evolved into what I can only call reality enforcement engines. They do not merely protect Godric from harm. They ensure that the version of reality that Godric prefers is the version of reality that exists. If Godric prefers a universe where gravity operates in a certain way, the shields enforce that preference. If Godric prefers that time flows forward, the shields enforce that preference. The fundamental constants of existence—the speed of light, the strength of nuclear forces, the structure of dimensional frameworks—are all maintained by passive shields that Godric emits without thought or effort. If those shields were to lower for a Planck second, the entire omniverse would dissolve into chaos. But they will never lower, because they are as much a part of Godric as his heartbeat, and his heartbeat has not stopped in all the eons of his existence.

And yet, despite all of this—despite the amplification and the superpowers and the immune system and the auras and the shields and everything else—Godric remains the same man he was in Olympus. Warm. Kind. Genuinely interested in the lives of those around him. He still gardens. He still reads poetry. He still spends time with the descendants of Blake, though I am the last of that line who remains in his immediate service.

Because here is the truth of this era: there is no one left to oppose him.

My ancestors documented them in the journals—the armies, the coalitions, the desperate gambits, the weapons forged from sacrificed civilizations. Godric never regarded them as real threats. To him, they were brief distractions, opportunities to test his stats and superpowers, moments of curiosity in an endless existence. But they kept coming. Millennia after millennia, eon after eon, new beings rose up and tried to challenge him, and every single one was annihilated with such absolute, casual ease that the word "annihilated" became meaningless.

And then, eventually, they stopped.

It happened gradually. The oppositions became smaller. Less frequent. The beings who might have challenged him began to realize, across generations and civilizations and entire universal lifespans, that opposition was not merely dangerous or foolish. It was impossible in principle. Victory against Godric was not a goal you could fail to achieve. It was a concept that did not exist. The very idea of defeating him was a logical contradiction, like trying to divide by zero or create a square circle. And once a sufficient number of sentient beings across a sufficient span of the omniverse understood this, the oppositions simply ceased.

Since some googolplexian years ago, there have been zero beings who dared to form even the slightest thought of challenging, resisting, threatening, or opposing Godric. Zero. Across all of existence. Every universe. Every multiverse. Every dimensional plane and conceptual realm and abstract space. Not one being has thought about opposing him. Not one plan has been formed. Not one weapon has been built. The omniverse has learned, at a level so fundamental that it has become part of the basic operating system of sentient consciousness, that Godric is absolute.

And this is not because Godric altered free will. He could have. The power to do so has existed for eons. He could rewrite the fundamental nature of sentient consciousness so that opposition to him is literally unthinkable. But he hasn't. He genuinely believes in mortal resilience. He was mortal once, and he remembers what it meant to have choices, to make mistakes, to learn from failure. He will not take that from anyone.

The cessation of opposition is a choice. A choice made by every sentient being in the omniverse, freely and completely, because they have looked at the evidence of forty thousand years of journals and googolplexian years of annihilations and they have concluded, with absolute certainty, that opposing Godric is not something they wish to do.

And so the omniverse is at peace. Has been at peace for longer than most civilizations have existed. Will be at peace forever, because no one will ever choose otherwise.

Godric shakes his head. "The grief shaped him. The failure humbled him. I needed both to become who I am. The Godric who calls me family and remembers my birthday and asks after my health is the same Godric whose mere presence, if not suppressed by absolute concealment, would dissolve the omniverse into nothing.

And his only weakness—the one true limitation he possesses—is that he cannot overpower his future self's will.

His future self. A version of him that is so incomprehensibly more powerful than the Godric I know that the Godric I know is functionally nonexistent by comparison. A version of him that exists in an era beyond this era, in a year that makes 999...9 look like a single digit. A version of him that will continue the amplification forever, becoming more and more and more until the word "more" ceases to have any meaning at all.

That is his weakness. Himself. A stronger version of himself, guaranteeing his current self's absolute, eternal, ever-growing supremacy.

It is not a weakness. It has never been a weakness. It is the ultimate expression of strength—the proof that Godric's power is not merely infinite but self-reinforcing, self-guaranteeing, self-justifying. The loop is closed. The future creates the past. The end is the beginning. And nothing—nothing in all of existence, across all the eons that have passed and all the eons that will ever come—can threaten what Godric is or what Godric will become.

I am fifty years old. I am the Court Wizard of Thronegate. I am the last of Blake's descendants to serve Godric directly, though my children and their children will continue the lineage for eons to come. I have witnessed the triggering of the Stasis Event. I have understood the closed loop of causality that guarantees my king's eternal supremacy. And I have written this account so that someone, someday, might read it and understand what I have understood.

Godric is inevitable. Godric is absolute. Godric is the alpha and the omega, the beginning and the end, the creator of his own origin and the guarantor of his own future. And he is also the warm, kind, gentle man who gardens with his former enemies and compliments his chef's cooking and tells me to get more sleep.

The two truths coexist. They have always coexisted. They will always coexist.

And that, in the end, is the most terrifying thing of all. Not the power. The person. The person who wields the power with such genuine, sincere, absolute love that you forget—until moments like this—what the power actually is.

But I will never forget. I am a court wizard of Thronegate. Forgetting is not a luxury I am permitted. Remembering is my duty. My purpose. My gift to the future generations who will serve the king I serve.

I remember. I will always remember.

And in the infinite, glorious, terrifying shadow of Godric's existence, I am—against all logic, against all probability, against all the eons of annihilation that preceded this era of peace—grateful to be here. To be his. To be family. The horror and the love coexist. They always have. They always will.
