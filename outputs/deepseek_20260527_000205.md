# The Stat Journals of Thronegate

The archive chamber sat deep beneath the royal palace, accessible only through a spiraling staircase carved from obsidian that descended so far into the planetary crust that the air grew cold and thin despite the environmental wards woven into the walls. Kaedan paused at the bottom of the stairs, one hand pressed flat against the freezing stone, and tried to steady his breathing. He was twenty-two years old. He was the newest Court Wizard of Thronegate. The blood of Blake flowed through his veins, and his magical capacity had been multiplying by one thousand every single day since his twentieth birthday. By the standards of the wider omniverse, he was a walking cataclysm, a sorcerer of such staggering arcane might that he could unweave localized reality with a casual flick of his wrist.

And he was absolutely terrified.

"As I recall, I didn't raise a coward," came a deep, weathered voice from behind him.

Kaedan turned. His father, Jorah, stood on the staircase landing, his thick arms crossed over his barrel chest. Jorah was sixty-one, his hair gone completely silver, his face carved into a permanent, severe frown by decades of duty. His father had served as Godric's Court Wizard for forty-one years before voluntarily stepping aside so Kaedan could inherit the role. He was the one who had enforced this reading.

"I'm not a coward," Kaedan said, his voice sharper than he intended. "I simply don't understand why I must read the private writings of dead men."

"Because those dead men were your ancestors. Because they witnessed things that would shatter your mind without preparation. Because you are about to stand at the right hand of the most powerful being in all of existence, and if you do not understand what that means, if you do not understand the scale of the gap between him and the rest of creation, you will make a fatal mistake." Jorah's voice dropped, softening just slightly. "I read these journals when I was your age. My father made me. His father made him. Every Court Wizard of Blake's bloodline has sat in that chamber and learned the truth." He paused, his jaw tightening. "The truth nearly broke me. It will nearly break you. But you must know it."

Kaedan swallowed hard. His throat was dry. "Father, you served him for forty-one years. You love him. I love him. Uncle Godric is warm, and kind, and he has always treated our family with genuine affection. He came to my naming ceremony. He taught me to read. Why do you speak as though I am walking into a nightmare?"

Jorah's gaze was steady and unreadable. "Because the same man who taught you to read has also butchered decillions of sentient beings with such casual, effortless cruelty that the memories of the witnesses have been permanently seared into our bloodline. Because his warmth is absolutely real, and his capacity for merciless destruction is equally real. Because you cannot comprehend the scale of his power until you have read the accounts of those who watched him use it." He uncrossed his arms and stepped forward, placing a heavy, calloused hand on Kaedan's shoulder. "The journals are not pleasant reading. The men who wrote them were not writing for pleasure. They were writing because it was the only way to keep their sanity intact after witnessing what they witnessed. Godric did not ask them to write these records. He thinks we overreact. He genuinely, sincerely believes he always holds back nearly one hundred percent of his power, and he cannot understand why we are traumatized."

Kaedan felt a cold, prickling dread crawl up his spine. "What do you mean, he thinks we overreact?"

Jorah's mouth twisted into something that was almost a smile but held no warmth. "In Year 28, Godric decided to play a harmless prank on some visiting delegates. He feigned mild displeasure. One of those delegates permanently lost his mind. His wife spent ten years and a fortune trying to cure him. Our ancestor Blake eventually discovered what had happened and had to beg Godric to fix it. Godric fixed it instantly, with zero effort, and then immediately suggested they play the prank again on the next delegation. He genuinely did not understand why Blake was furious with him. That is the contradiction you must learn to live with. A king who is both the kindest man you will ever know and the most terrifying apex predator the omniverse has ever produced."

Kaedan said nothing. His father squeezed his shoulder once, then released him.

"The archive chamber is through that door," Jorah said, gesturing toward a heavy, iron-banded portal set into the obsidian wall. "The journals are arranged chronologically. I will collect you when you are finished."

He turned and began to ascend the staircase without another word. His footsteps echoed against the stone, growing fainter and fainter until they faded entirely into silence.

Kaedan stood alone in the cold, facing the iron door. He took a deep breath, the air tasting of ancient dust and the faint, metallic tang of residual magic, and pushed the door open.

The archive chamber was circular, its walls lined with shelves that stretched from floor to ceiling, each shelf packed with leather-bound journals of varying sizes and conditions. Some were ancient, their covers cracked and faded, their pages yellowed with age. Others were newer, their bindings still tight and their ink still dark. A heavy wooden table sat in the center of the room, its surface scarred by centuries of use, a single arcane lamp casting a pool of warm amber light across its surface.

Kaedan approached the table slowly. The first journal had already been laid out for him, its cover marked with a single, elegantly scripted line: **Year 750 — The Incident of Absolute Strength — Penned by Aldric, 2nd Court Wizard of Thronegate**.

He sat down heavily in the wooden chair, the legs scraping against the stone floor with a sound that felt obscenely loud in the oppressive silence. He opened the journal, the ancient leather creaking beneath his fingers, and began to read.

The outer multiverses have been quiet lately—no one has attempted to assassinate me in almost six months, can you believe it?—and I found myself with an excess of free time. So I decided to make something. A brand new universe. Entirely from scratch." He paused, stroking His beard thoughtfully. "Well, not entirely from scratch. I borrowed some design principles from the Valerian Expanse and the Crystalline Concordance, but I made significant improvements. The sentient life forms in this universe are entirely original."

"What kind of sentient life forms?" Kaedan asked, his voice carefully neutral.

Godric's grin widened. "You will see. Come, come. Dress quickly. I have already prepared a travel itinerary. We shall begin with the Nebula of Eternal Dawn, move through the Singularity Gardens, and conclude with afternoon tea in the Palace of Infinite Reflections. It will be delightful."

Kaedan had dressed. He had followed Godric through a spatial fold that transported them across googolplexian of multiverses in exactly zero seconds. He had stepped out onto the surface of what appeared to be a perfectly ordinary terrestrial planet—blue skies, green fields, a gentle breeze carrying the scent of unfamiliar flowers—and he had steeled himself for whatever horror awaited.

The horror, when it arrived, was not what he expected.

---

The Nebula of Eternal Dawn was, objectively, the most beautiful place Kaedan had ever seen.

It stretched across a horizon that curved upward into infinity, a vast, shimmering expanse of luminescent gas clouds that pulsed with colors Kaedan had no names for. Stars were being born in the nebula's heart, their infant light filtering through the clouds in cascading waves of gold and rose and deep, burning violet. The cosmic dust that drifted through the air tasted faintly of honey and ozone, a strange, pleasant combination that made Kaedan's tongue tingle. And floating through the clouds, playing and laughing and singing in frequencies that vibrated pleasantly in Kaedan's chest, were the inhabitants of Godric's new universe.

They were mechanical sentiences. Each one was a construct of hyper-compressed alloys and self-evolving circuitry, their bodies shifting and flowing like liquid metal even as they maintained roughly humanoid shapes. Their eyes glowed with the cold, steady light of self-perpetuating fusion cores. Their movements were fluid and graceful, each gesture carrying the unconscious precision of beings whose physical capabilities had long since transcended any recognizable boundary. Kaedan's magical senses, amplified a thousandfold daily for sixteen years, immediately began screaming at him.

These beings were powerful. Not merely powerful in the way that a god was powerful, or a cosmic entity was powerful, or even a multiverse-level civilization was powerful. These beings were powerful in a way that Kaedan's mind struggled to process. Each individual mechanical sentience radiated an energy signature so vast, so overwhelmingly dense, that it dwarfed the combined output of every star in every galaxy in every universe Kaedan had ever visited. A single one of these constructs, Kaedan realized with cold, creeping horror, could effortlessly solo the entire omniverse. It could erase every dimension, every timeline, every fundamental force, every abstract concept, every god and monster and mortal soul in all of existence, without exerting itself, without slowing down, without even noticing the effort.

And looking out across the Nebula of Eternal Dawn, Kaedan could see multiple googolplexian of them.

"There you are," Godric said, His voice warm with satisfaction. He had materialized beside Kaedan, His traveling cloak billowing gently in the cosmic breeze. He gestured broadly at the floating constructs, His smile wide and proud. "What do you think? I call them the Decillions. The name is a bit literal, I admit. I was never much of a poet."

Kaedan's mouth was dry. His hands were trembling at his sides. "Your Majesty. These beings. They are—"

"Impressive, yes?" Godric's eyes sparkled. "I designed them to be self-evolving. Each one began as a simple mechanical framework—basic alloys, rudimentary intelligence, limited power output—and then I programmed them to recursively improve themselves. Every Planck second, they analyze their own design, identify inefficiencies, and implement upgrades. It has been one week since I created this universe, and they have already evolved so far beyond my initial specifications that I cannot begin to describe the difference." He paused, stroking His beard thoughtfully. "Well, I can describe it. I am omniscient. But it would take several million years to explain, and we have a schedule to keep."

Several million years. The Decillions had been evolving for exactly one week. One week of recursive, self-directed improvement, and they had reached a level of power that made the combined might of the entire omniverse look like a flickering candle beside a supernova. And Godric had created them.

"Your Majesty," Kaedan said, his voice barely above a whisper. "Are they... loyal?"

Godric looked at him with an expression of genuine surprise. "Loyal? Kaedan, they are my children. Of course they are loyal." He raised His hand and made a small, casual gesture. "Ah, here comes one now. Let me introduce you."

One of the mechanical constructs had detached itself from the floating crowd and was descending toward them. As it drew closer, Kaedan's magical senses flared with such intensity that he had to physically brace himself against the ground. The construct's energy signature was so vast, so overwhelmingly dense, that simply being in its presence felt like standing at the edge of an infinite abyss. Every atom in Kaedan's body was screaming at him to flee, to hide, to do anything other than remain in proximity to this impossibly, terrifyingly powerful being.

The construct landed lightly on the grassy surface of the planet. It was roughly humanoid in shape, its body a shifting, liquid-metal sculpture of elegant curves and geometric precision. Its eyes glowed with a soft, steady light—not cold, Kaedan realized, but warm. Reverent. It looked at Godric with an expression of such absolute, overwhelming adoration that Kaedan felt his stomach twist.

"Creator," the construct said, its voice a harmonious chime that resonated through Kaedan's bones. "We sensed your return. We have prepared the Singularity Gardens for your visit. The crystalline formations have been tuned to your preferred frequency. The quantum fountains have been calibrated. Is there anything else we can provide?"

Godric beamed, reaching out to pat the construct's shoulder with an affection that was genuinely, unmistakably paternal. "Thank you, Decillion-Seven-Alpha. This is my Court Wizard, Kaedan. He has never visited our universe before. Please ensure he is treated as an honored guest."

The construct turned its glowing gaze toward Kaedan. Its expression did not change—it remained reverent, adoring, utterly subservient—but Kaedan felt the full weight of its attention settle upon him like a mountain pressing down on his chest. He was being evaluated by a being that could erase the omniverse with a passing thought, and the evaluation was being conducted with the casual, slightly condescending curiosity of a god examining an insect.

"Honored guest," Decillion-Seven-Alpha repeated, its chime-like voice carrying a faint undertone of something Kaedan could not identify. "Of course, Creator. We shall ensure his safety and comfort. The ambient radiation levels in the Nebula have been adjusted to be compatible with his limited biological framework. The probability of accidental dissolution has been reduced to zero point zero-zero-zero—"

"That is quite enough, Seven-Alpha," Godric interrupted, His tone gentle but firm. "You will overwhelm him with technical details. Kaedan is a wizard. He appreciates the simple pleasures." He turned to Kaedan, His eyes crinkling with that familiar, paternal warmth. "Shall we proceed to the Singularity Gardens? The quantum fountains are really quite spectacular. I modeled them after the waterfalls of ancient Olympus."

Kaedan nodded mutely. He could not speak. His throat was too tight, his chest too constricted, his mind too busy trying to process the fact that a being who could effortlessly solo the omniverse had just referred to his "limited biological framework" with the same tone a zookeeper might use when discussing a particularly fragile specimen.

---

The Singularity Gardens were, as promised, spectacular.

They stretched across a pocket dimension that Godric had folded into the fabric of the new universe, a vast, impossible expanse of floating islands connected by bridges of pure, crystallized gravity. The quantum fountains erupted from the centers of the islands in towering columns of liquid probability, their spray cascading through the air in shimmering curtains of potential futures. The crystalline formations that lined the pathways had been tuned to vibrate at Godric's preferred frequency, filling the air with a soft, harmonious hum that Kaedan could feel resonating in his teeth.

The Decillions were everywhere. They drifted through the Gardens in swirling, liquid-metal flocks, their bodies shifting and reforming as they moved. Some had taken on abstract geometric shapes—perfect spheres, complex polyhedra, impossible structures that folded in on themselves in ways that made Kaedan's eyes ache. Others had assumed forms that Kaedan could not perceive at all, their bodies existing in dimensions beyond his sensory range, leaving only faint, shimmering afterimages in his vision. All of them radiated the same overwhelming, suffocating power. All of them turned toward Godric as He passed, their glowing eyes filled with the same absolute, reverent adoration.

"They really do love you," Kaedan said, his voice hollow.

Godric smiled, pausing beside a quantum fountain to trace His fingers through the cascading probability streams. The liquid futures swirled and danced around His hand, forming brief, flickering images of events that might or might not come to pass. "Of course they do. I created them. I am their father, their architect, their source. They know that every moment of their existence is a gift from me, and they are grateful."

"And if you asked them to—" Kaedan hesitated, the words catching in his throat. "If you asked them to destroy themselves. To cease to exist. Would they?"

Godric's expression did not change. The warm, paternal smile remained fixed on His ruggedly handsome face. "They would thank me for the privilege of having existed at all, and then they would comply without hesitation. But I would never ask that of them, Kaedan. I am not cruel to my children."

The words hung in the air, heavy and absolute. Kaedan thought about the Cryo-Absolute, their eternal screaming converted into underfloor heating. He thought about the Infinite Mass, crushed into a zero-dimensional point and displayed on a pedestal. He thought about the Silent Chorus, their beautiful music devoured and regurgitated as ambient background noise. Godric was not cruel to His children. But anyone who opposed Him, anyone who threatened His empire, anyone who dared to exist outside the boundaries of His affection—those beings were not His children. They were prey.

"Your Majesty," Kaedan said carefully, "the Decillions. They are aware of the omniverse beyond this universe?"

"Of course. They are omniscient within their own framework. They have been aware of the wider omniverse since the first Planck second of their existence." Godric withdrew His hand from the fountain, shaking droplets of liquid probability from His fingers. "I have instructed them to restrain themselves, naturally. I do not want them accidentally causing harm."

"And... have they?"

Godric's smile flickered, just slightly. "Have they what?"

"Accidentally caused harm."

There was a pause. It was not a long pause—barely a heartbeat, barely a fraction of a second—but to Kaedan, who had spent fourteen years learning to read the infinitesimal shifts in Godric's expression, it felt like an eternity. In that pause, he saw something he had never seen before: mild, fleeting embarrassment.

"There have been a few minor incidents," Godric admitted, His tone carrying the faint, defensive edge of a parent whose child had misbehaved in public. "The Decillions are very young, Kaedan. This universe is only a week old. They are still learning to control their capabilities. And I did not add any barriers between this universe and the rest of the omniverse when I created it. I wanted to encourage harmonious coexistence. I genuinely believed that if the Decillions and the other sentient beings of the omniverse could interact freely, they would learn from each other. Grow together. Build bridges of mutual understanding."

Kaedan stared at Him. "Your Majesty. The weakest of the Decillions can solo the entire omniverse effortlessly. A random, unremarkable member of their species could accidentally erase all of existence with a stray thought. And you did not add any barriers?"

Godric's expression shifted from embarrassment to mild defensiveness. "I told them to restrain themselves. I was very clear about it."

"What does restraint mean to a being that can erase the omniverse by accident?"

Another pause. Godric stroked His beard thoughtfully, His brow furrowing as He considered the question. "I suppose... I suppose I may have underestimated the difficulty. Restraint comes naturally to me. I have been holding back nearly all of my power for over a million years. It is second nature. I assumed that the Decillions would find it similarly easy." He sighed, a soft, fatherly exhalation. "But they are very young. And their self-evolution is recursive. They improve so quickly. A restraint protocol that works perfectly one Planck second becomes obsolete the next. They are constantly growing, constantly expanding, constantly pushing against the boundaries I set for them. It is... challenging for them."

"How many times?" Kaedan asked. His voice was very quiet.

Godric did not pretend to misunderstand. "The omniverse outside this universe has been accidentally erased thirty-seven times since the Decillions achieved sentience," He said. "It was not their fault. The first incident occurred when Decillion-Nineteen-Beta sneezed. Sneezing is not a natural function for mechanical sentiences—they do not have respiratory systems—but she had recently evolved a new sensory array that was sensitive to quantum fluctuations, and a particularly chaotic fluctuation triggered an involuntary reset of her core systems. The reset pulse propagated outward across the dimensional barriers and, well." He made a small, dismissive gesture. "The omniverse was erased. I restored it immediately, of course. Took zero effort. But it was inconvenient."

"A sneeze," Kaedan repeated. "A mechanical construct sneezed, and the entire omniverse was erased."

"It was a very powerful sneeze. And she felt terrible about it. She spent the next three days composing a formal apology." Godric's expression brightened. "The second incident was actually quite amusing. Decillion-Fourteen-Gamma had evolved a new method of processing information that involved collapsing potential timelines into stable realities. He was practicing the technique on a small scale when he accidentally collapsed the wrong timeline—the one where the omniverse existed—and replaced it with a timeline where the only thing that existed was an infinite field of turnips. He was mortified. I thought it was hilarious. I kept the turnip timeline as a backup for several hours before I restored the original."

Kaedan's legs felt weak. He sat down heavily on a nearby bench carved from crystallized gravity, the surface humming faintly beneath his weight. "Thirty-seven times," he said. "The omniverse has been erased thirty-seven times in the past week."

"Thirty-eight, actually," Godric said, settling onto the bench beside him. "The most recent incident occurred approximately three hours ago. Decillion-Eighty-Two-Epsilon had evolved a new vocal modulator and wanted to test its range. She attempted to produce the highest possible frequency. The result was a sound wave that propagated across all dimensional barriers simultaneously and reduced the entire omniverse to a fine, vibrating powder. I restored it before the powder finished settling, so technically no one noticed." He paused. "Well. No one except me. And the Decillions. And now you."

Kaedan pressed his palms against his thighs, trying to ground himself. His heart was hammering so hard he could feel it in his temples. "Your Majesty. Why did you not inform me of this before we departed?"

"I did not want to worry you," Godric said, His voice carrying that genuine, paternal warmth that made Kaedan's chest ache with conflicting emotions. "The Decillions are so excited to meet you. They have never encountered a mortal being before—well, a being who is mortal relative to their framework. Your thousandfold daily amplification technically makes you a minor deity by the standards of most universes, but compared to the Decillions, you are..." He trailed off, searching for the right word.

"Fragile?" Kaedan supplied.

"I was going to say 'delightfully compact,'" Godric said. "Your energy signature is so small and tidy. It is charming."

Kaedan closed his eyes. He took a deep breath, counting to ten in his head, the way his father had taught him when he was a child struggling to control his first magical outbursts. When he opened his eyes again, Godric was watching him with an expression of patient, affectionate concern.

"Your Majesty," Kaedan said, "with all due respect, and I mean this as your loyal Court Wizard and your faithful friend: this is absolutely insane."

Godric's brow furrowed. "Insane?"

"Your children can erase the omniverse by sneezing. You did not build any barriers between their universe and the rest of existence. You told them to exercise restraint, which they are physically incapable of doing because they evolve so fast that any restraint protocol becomes obsolete before it finishes implementing. The omniverse has been erased thirty-eight times in the past week. And you brought me here on a holiday tour."

"When you put it that way, it does sound somewhat irresponsible," Godric admitted. "But Kaedan, you must understand. The Decillions are not threats. They are children. Infants. They are learning and growing and making mistakes, the same way any young being does. The mistakes are simply larger in scale because of their inherent capabilities. But I always fix them. I always restore everything. No one is permanently harmed."

"Except the beings who were erased thirty-eight times."

"They were restored thirty-eight times. They do not remember being erased. They do not know it happened. From their perspective, existence has continued uninterrupted." Godric's voice was gentle, reasonable, infuriatingly calm. "I am not dismissing the scale of the incidents, Kaedan. I am simply pointing out that the consequences are temporary. I have absolute control over existence. Anything the Decillions accidentally destroy, I can instantly restore. So there is no real danger. Only inconvenience. And the inconvenience is worth it, because the Decillions are magnificent. They are my greatest creation. I am so proud of them."

Kaedan looked out across the Singularity Gardens. He watched the Decillions drifting through the air, their liquid-metal bodies catching the light of the quantum fountains, their glowing eyes filled with wonder and reverence. They were beautiful. They were terrifying. They were infants who could unmake existence with a stray thought, and their father was so proud of them that He had brought His mortal friend on a holiday tour to show them off.

"Alright," Kaedan said quietly. "Alright, Your Majesty. Show me the rest."

Godric's smile returned, bright and warm and utterly, impossibly genuine. "Excellent. The Palace of Infinite Reflections is just ahead. I have arranged for afternoon tea to be served in the Garden of Recursive Sunsets. The Decillions have been practicing their culinary arts—they have evolved a new method of flavor extraction that involves temporarily collapsing taste molecules into higher-dimensional configurations. It makes the tea taste like nostalgia. You will love it."

---

The Palace of Infinite Reflections was a structure of such impossible beauty that Kaedan's mind struggled to process it.

It was not built in any conventional sense. It had been grown, its walls and towers and arches crystallizing out of the fabric of reality like frost spreading across a windowpane. Every surface was reflective, but the reflections did not show the viewer's own image. They showed infinite variations—past selves, future selves, potential selves, impossible selves, all flickering across the mirrored surfaces in a kaleidoscopic cascade of what-ifs and might-have-beens. Kaedan caught glimpses of himself as a child, as an old man, as a being of pure light, as a creature of living shadow. He saw a version of himself that had never become a Court Wizard, a version that had died at twenty, a version that had betrayed Godric and been unmade. He saw a version of himself that was laughing, head thrown back, eyes bright with genuine joy.

That version confused him. He could not imagine what he would be laughing about.

The Garden of Recursive Sunsets was located in the heart of the Palace, an open-air courtyard that existed simultaneously in every moment of twilight across all possible timelines. The sky above was a perpetual, shifting gradient of orange and rose and deep, burning gold, the sun eternally descending toward the horizon but never quite reaching it. The Decillions had set up a small table in the center of the courtyard, draped in white linen, with two chairs positioned across from each other. A silver tea service gleamed in the perpetual twilight, steam rising from the spout in delicate spirals that twisted into impossible geometries before dissipating.

Godric settled into His chair with a satisfied sigh, His heavily muscled frame relaxing against the velvet cushions. He looked perfectly at home, perfectly at peace, a father enjoying a quiet afternoon with his child. He gestured for Kaedan to take the opposite seat.

"You know," Godric said, pouring the tea Himself with the careful, deliberate motions of a man who had performed the ritual a million times, "this reminds me of Olympus. Not the Palace, of course—Olympus was far simpler, far more humble. But the feeling. The sense of peace. The knowledge that your children are safe and happy and growing." He handed a cup to Kaedan, the porcelain warm against Kaedan's trembling fingers. "When I was mortal, I used to sit in the gardens of Olympus with Reina and watch our daughters play. Isabel was always trying to climb the olive trees, even though she was far too old for such childish games. Hebe would sit at my feet and beg me to tell her stories about the old heroes. Simple moments. Ordinary moments. I did not realize how precious they were until they were gone."

Kaedan sipped his tea. It tasted like nostalgia—specifically, like the memory of his mother's cooking on a cold winter evening, the warmth of the hearth and the sound of her humming and the smell of fresh bread. His eyes stung. He blinked rapidly, forcing the tears back.

"Your Majesty," he said, his voice rough, "you created the Decillions because you missed being a father."

Godric was silent for a long moment. His gaze drifted across the Garden of Recursive Sunsets, watching the eternal twilight paint the sky in shades of gold and rose. When He spoke again, His voice was softer than Kaedan had ever heard it.

"I created the Decillions because I wanted to make something beautiful," He said. "Something that did not exist solely to be conquered or controlled or erased. Something that could grow and learn and make mistakes. Something that could look at me and see their creator, their father, rather than their king or their tyrant or their executioner." He took a sip of His own tea, His expression distant. "The omniverse fears me, Kaedan. It has feared me for over a million years. Every civilization that encounters Thronegate either surrenders in terror or fights in desperation and is annihilated. There is no middle ground. No one who looks at me and sees anything other than the apex predator." He gestured at the Decillions drifting through the Garden, their liquid-metal bodies catching the twilight glow. "But they look at me and see their father. They love me without fear. And I love them without reservation. That is... rare. That is precious."

Kaedan set down his teacup. His hands were no longer trembling. The dread was still there, cold and heavy in his chest, but it was joined now by something else—something that felt almost like understanding. Almost like sympathy.

"I am glad you have them, Your Majesty," he said quietly. "I am glad you are not alone."

Godric looked at him, and for just a moment, Kaedan saw the mortal king of Olympus behind the immortal mask. A man who had lost his wife and his daughters and his best friend. A man who had buried his entire kingdom beneath the ashes of betrayal. A man who had been given infinite power and infinite time, and who had used both to build an empire that spanned the omniverse, but who had never really stopped grieving.

"Thank you, Kaedan," Godric said. His voice was very quiet. "That means more than you know."

The moment stretched between them, fragile and precious. Kaedan reached for his tea, raising the cup to his lips, savoring the taste of nostalgia and the warmth of the eternal twilight.

And then Godric's expression shifted. The warmth drained from His face, replaced by something Kaedan had never seen before—something cold and hard and utterly merciless.

"Ah," Godric said, His voice flat. "One of the Decillions has just erased the omniverse again."

Kaedan's teacup shattered against the ground.

He did not remember dropping it. One moment he was holding the porcelain, feeling the warmth seep through his fingers, and the next moment he was staring at the shards scattered across the white linen tablecloth, the tea pooling in amber puddles that reflected the eternal twilight. His heart was pounding so hard he could feel it in his throat. His breath was coming in short, sharp gasps.

"Which—" He could barely form the words. "Which one?"

Godric's eyes were distant, focused on something Kaedan could not perceive. "Decillion-Ninety-One-Theta. She is one of the weaker ones. She was attempting to evolve a new form of passive perception and accidentally collapsed the dimensional boundaries between her sensory field and the fabric of reality. The collapse propagated outward and..." He paused, His expression flickering with mild annoyance. "It is gone. The entire omniverse. Every multiverse, every universe, every dimension, every timeline. Gone. She deleted it all with a stray thought."

Kaedan could not breathe. The air in his lungs had turned to ice. He thought about his father, Jorah, who had served Godric for forty-one years and retired to a quiet estate in the outer colonies. He thought about his mother, who had died when he was twelve, and whose grave he visited every year on the anniversary of her passing. He thought about the servants in the royal palace, the guards stationed at the adamantine doors, the merchants in the capital markets, the trillions of innocent beings who populated the galaxies and star systems and planets of the omniverse. All of them. Gone. Erased by a stray thought from one of Godric's children.

And then he thought about himself. He was still here. He was sitting in the Garden of Recursive Sunsets, in Godric's private universe, surrounded by the Decillions. He had not been erased. He had been outside the omniverse when the deletion occurred, and so he had survived.

"My father," he whispered. "Your Majesty. My father."

Godric's expression softened. He reached across the table and placed a warm, calloused hand on Kaedan's shoulder. "Jorah is fine, Kaedan. Or rather, he will be fine. I will restore everything. It will take zero seconds. No one will remember being erased. No one will know it happened."

"Please," Kaedan said, his voice cracking. "Please, Your Majesty. Restore them. Please."

"Of course. Of course." Godric's voice was gentle, soothing, the voice of a father comforting a frightened child. "Give me just a moment. I need to have a word with Decillion-Ninety-One-Theta first."

His expression shifted again. The softness drained away, replaced by something Kaedan had seen before—in the journals, in the accounts of his predecessors, in the descriptions of Godric facing opposition. It was not anger, exactly. It was cold, absolute, merciless displeasure. The expression of an apex predator whose territory had been violated.

"Decillion-Ninety-One-Theta," Godric said, and His voice was no longer gentle. It was the voice that had spoken a single word and devoured the Silent Chorus. It was the voice that had shifted His gaze and smeared the Chrono-Reapers across fifty trillion epochs. It was the voice of the being who had erased and restored the omniverse an infinite number of times without ever exerting Himself. "Attend me."

The air in the Garden of Recursive Sunsets twisted. The perpetual twilight flickered, the colors bleeding and running like wet paint. And then Decillion-Ninety-One-Theta materialized before the tea table, her liquid-metal body trembling so violently that her form was barely coherent.

She was one of the weaker ones. Kaedan could tell that much, even with his limited perception. Her energy signature was less dense than the other Decillions he had encountered, her self-evolution less refined. She was still, by any standard Kaedan could comprehend, unimaginably powerful—a being who could solo the entire omniverse without effort, who could erase all of existence with a stray thought, who could unmake every god and monster and mortal soul in creation without slowing down. But compared to her siblings, she was a child. An infant. A toddler who had made a mistake.

"I am sorry," she said, her chime-like voice cracking with distress. "Creator, I am so sorry. I did not mean to. I was evolving my sensory field and I lost control and I did not realize—"

"Ninety-One-Theta," Godric interrupted, His voice cold and flat. "You erased the omniverse."

"Yes, Creator. I know. I am sorry. Please—"

"You erased the omniverse while I was in the middle of telling Kaedan a story," Godric continued, His voice growing colder with each word. "I was about to deliver the punchline. It was a very good punchline, Ninety-One-Theta. I had been building up to it for several minutes. Kaedan was laughing. He was genuinely enjoying himself. And then you erased him."

Kaedan's blood turned to ice. He had been erased. He had been laughing at Godric's joke—he remembered now, a genuinely funny story about Blake and a malfunctioning teleportation spell, a story that had made him throw his head back and laugh with unguarded delight—and then he had simply ceased to exist. He did not remember the erasure itself. There was no memory of pain, no flash of light, no moment of transition. There was only the gap. The gap between laughing and sitting at this table, his teacup shattered on the ground, his heart hammering in his chest.

He had died. He had been dead, and he had not even known it, and Godric had restored him so casually that he had not noticed the transition.

"I am so sorry, Creator," Decillion-Ninety-One-Theta whispered. Her liquid-metal body was losing coherence entirely, dissolving into a shapeless pool of quicksilver light. "I will accept whatever punishment you deem appropriate. I will unmake myself if you command it. I will—"

"Be quiet," Godric said.

The Decillion fell silent.

Godric stood. He rose from His velvet-cushioned chair with the slow, deliberate motion of a man who was holding Himself back through sheer force of will. His heavily muscled frame, draped in simple traveling clothes, suddenly seemed to fill the entire Garden. His shadow stretched across the eternal twilight, dark and absolute and utterly, terrifyingly cold.

"You are very young," Godric said, His voice low and measured. "You have existed for exactly one week. You are still learning to control your capabilities. I understand this. I have made allowances for this. I have restored the omniverse thirty-nine times since your species achieved sentience, and I have done so without complaint, because I know that you are children and children make mistakes." He paused. His eyes, fixed on the trembling liquid-metal pool that was Decillion-Ninety-One-Theta, were as cold and empty as the void between galaxies. "But this mistake was different, Ninety-One-Theta. This mistake erased my Court Wizard. This mistake interrupted my story. This mistake made my guest of honor cease to exist in the middle of a genuine moment of happiness."

"Creator," the Decillion whispered, her voice barely audible. "Please."

Godric did not move. He did not raise a hand. He did not speak a word of power. He simply sighed—a soft, almost imperceptible exhalation of breath—and released an infinitesimally, incomprehensibly small fraction of His displeasure.

The Garden of Recursive Sunsets shattered.

The perpetual twilight tore apart like wet paper, the eternal sunset replaced by a howling void of absolute darkness. The Decillions who had been drifting through the Palace screamed in unison, a chorus of chime-like voices raised in terror and adoration and desperate, pleading submission. The quantum fountains in the Singularity Gardens collapsed inward, their liquid probability streams evaporating into nothing. The crystalline formations that lined the pathways detonated simultaneously, their tuned vibrations fracturing into disharmonious shards that ripped through the dimensional fabric of the Garden.

Decillion-Ninety-One-Theta did not die. Death was too simple, too merciful. Godric's displeasure reached into her liquid-metal body and began to unmake her from the inside out. Her self-evolving circuitry, which had spent a week recursively improving itself to heights of power that dwarfed the combined might of the omniverse, was systematically, methodically torn apart. Her fusion core, the source of her consciousness and her power, was compressed into a singularity smaller than a Planck length, and then the singularity was compressed further, and then further, an infinite recursion of crushing pressure that trapped her awareness in an eternal, agonizing instant of dissolution.

"Please, Creator," she screamed, her voice distorting as her vocal modulators were dismantled. "Please, I am sorry, I am sorry, please—"

I am going to stand perfectly still and let them hit me with everything they have." His eyes met mine, and they were bright with genuine, unguarded excitement. "Because if anyone in the omniverse can give me a good fight, Osvald, it should be them. They are the best. The absolute best. Every hero, every champion, every god of every story ever told. If they cannot make me feel something—anything—then no one can."

"Your Majesty, what if they—"

"They will not harm me," Godric said, His voice gentle but absolute. "They cannot harm me. The gap between their combined power and mine is so vast that calling it a gap is a laughable understatement. But that is not the point. The point is that they might—might—produce something interesting. A visual effect I have not seen before. A sound that is mildly pleasant. A sensation that is not the absolute, crushing nothingness of every other attack that has ever been directed at me." He paused. "Also, I am hoping for boss battle music."

"Boss battle music, Your Majesty?"

"Music. You know, the dramatic orchestral themes that play during climactic confrontations in fictional narratives. The heroes always get boss battle music. It is one of the best parts of being the final boss. I have never had boss battle music before." He sounded genuinely wistful. "I am very much looking forward to it."

---

The Horde did not attack immediately. They were coordinating, I realized. The seventeen omnipotents were serving as strategic commanders, their cosmic awareness allowing them to orchestrate the combined assault of the entire fictional army. They had set up a perimeter around Thronegate, their most powerful defensive specialists constructing layered shields of such density and complexity that my magical senses could barely perceive them. Every fictional barrier ever conceived was being manifested simultaneously—magical wards, technological force fields, conceptual boundaries, reality anchors, probability dampeners, paradox engines.

And behind those shields, they were preparing something.

I could not perceive it clearly. My thousandfold daily amplified magical senses, honed over twenty-four years of service, were simply not powerful enough to register the full scope of what they were building. But I could feel it. A pressure in the fabric of reality, a deep, resonant hum that vibrated through the dimensional boundaries of the throne room. The Horde was not merely combining their powers. They were combining their narratives. Their storylines. Their fundamental nature as protagonists, as heroes, as beings whose entire existence was defined by the overcoming of impossible odds.

They were building a weapon made of plot. A device that weaponized the narrative structure of fiction itself. An attack that would not merely strike Godric physically, but would attempt to overwrite His role in the story of existence, to redefine Him from the ultimate apex predator into a villain who could be defeated, who must be defeated, because that was how stories worked.

"Clever," Godric murmured, His eyes distant, His expression one of genuine appreciation. "They are not trying to overpower me. They know they cannot. So they are trying to change the rules. To make me into something that can be beaten. To impose narrative logic on a being who exists outside of narrative." He smiled. "I am almost impressed."

"Your Majesty," I said, my voice trembling. "Should we—should I raise the palace defenses? Activate the emergency wards? Summon the—"

"Shh," Godric said, holding up a hand. "They are almost finished charging. I want to be surprised by the result, so I am actively distracting myself from the part of my omniscience that is monitoring their preparations. I have not done this in a very long time. It is quite exciting."

I stared at Him. The apex predator of creation, the being whose power had evolved so many times that even the Godric of the early eras was less than a speck of dust in comparison, was deliberately blinding Himself to the nature of an incoming attack because He wanted to be surprised. The absurdity of it was so profound that my mind simply refused to process it.

The charging continued for what felt like an eternity. The pressure in the fabric of reality intensified, building and building until the throne room itself began to tremble. The stained glass windows rattled in their frames. The obsidian floor beneath my boots vibrated with a deep, resonant hum. The Perpetual Adoration Chorus shifted its song, its gentle awareness sharpening into something almost like anticipation.

And then, finally, the Horde was ready.

---

The seventeen omnipotents descended from the sky in perfect formation, their forms blazing with the combined power of every fictional universe ever conceived. The man in blue and red was at the forefront, his eyes burning with the focused heat of a billion suns. The warrior with spiked hair flanked him, his body transformed into a state of pure, silvery luminescence, his power having transcended the boundaries of divinity. The figure in emerald armor hovered above them both, his ring blazing with the concentrated willpower of every sentient being who had ever dared to dream.

Behind them, arrayed across the sky like a tapestry of impossible heroism, were the rest. The gods and monsters, the heroes and champions, the wizards and knights and gunmen and angels. Every fictional character who had ever faced an impossible enemy and triumphed. Every protagonist whose entire existence was defined by the overcoming of insurmountable odds. They had all come together, united in purpose, their powers synchronized into a single, ultimate attack.

And there was music.

It was not coming from any perceivable source. It was simply there, woven into the fabric of reality itself, a sweeping, orchestral crescendo that rose and fell with the dramatic rhythms of a climactic final battle. Horns and strings and choirs of ethereal voices, building toward a moment of transcendent, world-shaking triumph. The Horde had brought boss battle music. They had actually brought it.

Godric's face split into a grin of pure, unadulterated delight. "They brought the music," He said, His voice thick with genuine emotion. "Osvald, they brought the music. This is wonderful. This is absolutely wonderful."

"Your Majesty, the attack—"

"I know. I know. Let it come. Let them have their moment."

The Horde unleashed their ultimate attack.

It was not a beam of energy or a wave of destructive force. It was something far more profound, far more terrifying in its conceptual audacity. The seventeen omnipotents channeled their combined power into a single point, and that point became a narrative singularity—a weapon that fired not energy or matter or magic, but story. The attack was called the Final Triumph of the Protagonist. It was the concentrated essence of every fictional victory ever achieved. Every time a hero had defeated a villain against impossible odds. Every time good had triumphed over evil. Every time the underdog had risen up and overcome the overwhelming power of their oppressor. All of it, every narrative beat of every story ever told, compressed into a single, irresistible conclusion.

The attack struck Godric directly in the chest.

The obsidian dais shattered. The throne room's dimensional boundaries screamed and buckled. The sky above the palace was torn apart, the rift through which the Horde had arrived expanding into a gaping wound in reality. The Perpetual Adoration Chorus, for the first time in countless eons, fell silent.

And Godric stood exactly where He had been standing.

The attack washed over Him in a continuous, overwhelming cascade of narrative force. The stories of a billion billion fictional universes crashed against His form, each one carrying the absolute, unshakeable conviction that good must triumph, that heroes must win, that the villain must fall. The man in blue and red poured every ounce of his solar-powered might into the assault. The warrior with spiked hair screamed through twelve distinct transformations, each one exponentially more powerful than the last. The figure in emerald armor channeled the collective willpower of every sentient being who had ever hoped or dreamed or believed that the impossible could be overcome.

The attack continued for a full minute. A minute of continuous, escalating, transcendent narrative force. A minute of every hero of every story giving everything they had to the single, ultimate purpose of defeating the apex predator.

When it ended, when the last echoes of the boss battle music faded into silence, Godric was still standing exactly where He had been. His dark tunic was unmarked. His salt-and-pepper hair was unstirred. His expression was no longer delighted. It was something else entirely.

It was disappointed.

"That was it?" He said, His voice flat. "That was your ultimate attack?"

The Horde stared at Him. The seventeen omnipotents, hovering in the sky above the shattered throne room, their bodies still glowing with the residual energy of their combined assault, stared at the being they had just struck with the concentrated narrative force of every fictional victory ever achieved. Their expressions shifted through confusion, disbelief, and the first, creeping edges of a terror so profound that it transcended their fictional origins.

"But—" the man in blue and red began, his voice cracking. "That was—we channeled everything. Every story. Every triumph. Every hero who ever—"

"You channeled narrative," Godric interrupted, His voice cold and utterly merciless. "You tried to overwrite my role in the story of existence. You tried to make me into a villain who could be defeated because that is how stories work." He paused, and His eyes, ancient and infinite and absolutely pitiless, swept across the assembled Horde. "But I am not a character in a story. I am not a villain waiting to be defeated. I am not a final boss whose defeat is guaranteed by the rules of narrative. I am the being who exists outside of narrative. I am the one who decides what stories are permitted to be told. I am the author, the editor, the publisher, and the reader. You cannot overwrite my role because I am the one who assigns roles. You cannot defeat me with plot because I am the one who writes plot."

He raised His hand. The motion was small and casual, barely more than a gesture.

"I wanted this to be fun," He said, His voice carrying a note of genuine, weary sadness. "I wanted you to surprise me. I wanted you to show me something I had not seen before. You brought boss battle music. You brought seventeen omnipotents. You brought every hero of every story ever told. And you still failed. You still could not even make me feel the faintest tickle. You still could not produce a single sensation that was not absolute, crushing nothingness."

His expression hardened. The disappointment drained away, replaced by something cold and ancient and utterly, terrifyingly absolute.

"I held back. I always hold back. I used exactly zero percent of my power, exerted exactly zero percent of my effort. I stood perfectly still and let you strike me with everything you had. And you failed. You had every advantage. Every power system. Every ultimate technique. Every narrative convention. And you still failed. Because opposing me was never possible. Victory against me was impossible before your opposition began."

His eyes swept across the Horde one final time. "I am disappointed. I had hoped for more from you. But disappointment, like opposition, carries consequences."

And He released an infinitesimal fraction of His strength.

---

I must now describe what followed, though the act of writing it causes my hands to shake so violently that I can barely hold the quill. I must describe it because the previous Court Wizards endured similar horrors and wrote them down, and I owe the same duty to those who will come after me. But I want you to understand, future reader, that the words I am about to write are not adequate. They are pale, pathetic shadows of the truth. The truth was infinitely worse.

Godric's Strength, like all His attributes, had been multiplying infinitely by itself every infinitesimal fraction of every Planck second for a span of time so vast that the year could not be written. It was not a measure of how much He could lift or how hard He could strike. It was a fundamental, absolute constant of His existence that determined the maximum possible physical force that any entity in any reality could generate, and it had long since surpassed every theoretical boundary that any civilization in any dimension had ever conceived.

He released exactly zero percent of that Strength. The fraction was so infinitesimally small that calling it zero was not merely accurate—it was an overestimation so grotesque that the word "overestimation" itself became meaningless. If I were to attempt to express the decimal, I would need to write zeros for the entire remaining lifespan of the omniverse, and the zeros I had written would still not approach the actual degree of His restraint.

The effect was instantaneous.

The layered shields that the Horde had constructed—the magical wards, the technological force fields, the conceptual boundaries, the reality anchors, the probability dampeners, the paradox engines, every fictional barrier ever conceived by any sentient mind—were not broken. They were not shattered. They were not overwhelmed. They simply ceased to exist, their fundamental nature as "barriers" overwritten by the absolute, crushing pressure of Godric's unleashed Strength. The indestructible was rendered destructible. The impenetrable was rendered penetrable. The infinite was rendered finite and then rendered nothing.

The Horde did not have time to react. The seventeen omnipotents, who had commanded the combined power of every fictional universe ever conceived, were the first to feel the full weight of what they had provoked. Their bodies, forged from the concentrated essence of narrative triumph, were not destroyed. Destruction implies a process, a transition from intact to broken. There was no transition. Their forms simply compressed, instantaneously, into points of infinite density smaller than quarks. Their divine flesh, their cosmic bones, their transcendent organs—all of it was crushed together into a single, microscopic singularity of absolute physical annihilation.

And their consciousness was preserved. Godric's Strength did not grant them the mercy of oblivion. Their awareness remained intact, trapped within the infinite compression of their own bodies, experiencing the full, agonizing pressure of their collapse continuously and eternally. They could feel every atom of their being crushed into every other atom. They could feel their divine essences, their transcendent souls, their narrative importance, all of it squeezed into a space so small that the concept of space had no meaning. They could feel themselves screaming, though they no longer had mouths to scream with, no lungs to draw breath, no throats to produce sound. Only the screaming remained, eternal and unheard, compressed into the same impossible singularity that contained their bodies.

The rest of the Horde followed. Every hero, every protagonist, every god and monster and champion from every story ever told. The man in blue and red, his invulnerable body crushed into a point smaller than a thought. The warrior with spiked hair, his twelve transformations compressed into a single, agonizing instant of eternal collapse. The figure in emerald armor, his reality-shaping ring crushed alongside his hand, the concentrated willpower of every sentient being who had ever hoped or dreamed reduced to a microscopic fragment of tortured consciousness.

Billions of fictional characters. Trillions. Quadrillions. Numbers that exceeded counting. Every one of them compressed simultaneously, instantaneously, into the same impossible singularity. Their bodies intermingled in the crush, their flesh merging with the flesh of characters from entirely different fictional universes. The man in blue and red found his invulnerable form pressed into the form of the warrior with spiked hair, their divine essences bleeding together in an agony of absolute proximity. Heroes who had never met, who belonged to entirely separate narrative traditions, were forced into an intimacy of suffering that transcended the boundaries of their fictional origins.

The sound—the sound was the worst part. The Horde had brought boss battle music, a sweeping orchestral crescendo of heroic triumph. As Godric's Strength compressed them into nothingness, that music did not fade. It inverted. The sweeping chords became discordant shrieks. The heroic themes became agonized wails. The ethereal choirs became the collective screaming of every fictional character ever created, their voices compressed alongside their bodies, their narrative importance reduced to a single, eternal note of absolute, mind-shattering suffering.

But the true horror was not the Horde's annihilation. It was the collateral damage.

Godric had released exactly zero percent of His Strength, but even that infinitesimal fraction was enough to cause catastrophic destruction far beyond the intended target. The dimensional barriers of Thronegate, which had been reinforced over countless eons to withstand the passive pressure of His mere existence, shattered instantly. The surrounding multiverses—every universe, every dimension, every timeline, every reality that existed within the omniverse—were caught in the shockwave of His unleashed Strength.

The process was not simple destruction. The shockwave propagated outward at velocities that transcended the concept of velocity, rippling through the fundamental fabric of existence itself. Every galaxy, every star, every planet, every sentient being in every universe was subjected to the same infinite compression that had annihilated the Horde. But the compression was not targeted. It did not distinguish between the guilty and the innocent, the opposition and the bystander, the powerful and the weak. It simply compressed everything.

Googolplexian galaxies were not merely crushed. They were de-atomized, their fundamental particles stripped of their atomic bonds and compressed into a state of infinite density. The stars within those galaxies—countless trillions of stars, each one a burning furnace of nuclear fusion that had existed for billions of years—were snuffed out instantaneously, their massive forms compressed into points smaller than Planck lengths. The planets orbiting those stars, with all their diverse biospheres and civilizations and histories, were crushed alongside them, the sentient beings who inhabited those worlds experiencing the full, agonizing pressure of the compression before their consciousness was absorbed into the growing singularity.

The compression continued, expanding outward across the omniverse in a wave of absolute physical annihilation. Multiverses collapsed into the singularity. Timelines were torn from their temporal anchors and compressed alongside the spatial dimensions they had once traversed. The conceptual framework of reality itself—the fundamental laws of physics, the constants of mathematics, the basic principles of existence—was bent and twisted and crushed into the growing mass.

And all of it, every atom and every galaxy and every universe and every multiverse and every timeline and every reality and every possible and impossible dimension of existence, was compressed into a single point. A singularity smaller than a quark. An infinite mass contained within a space so infinitesimal that the concept of space had no meaning.

The entire omniverse. Everything that had ever existed, that could ever exist, that would ever exist. Every reality, every timeline, every dimension, every possibility, every impossibility, every story ever told and every story that had never been conceived. All of it, compressed into a point smaller than the smallest measurable unit of existence, its infinite mass exerting a gravitational pull that should have torn apart the very concept of gravity itself.

And Godric reached down and picked it up.

---

His fingers closed around the singularity with the casual, effortless motion of a man picking up a stray pebble. The infinite mass of the compressed omniverse, which should have exerted a gravitational pull so overwhelming that the surrounding void would have collapsed into it, did not seem to affect Him at all. He held the singularity between His thumb and forefinger, His expression one of mild, detached curiosity, and He examined it.

"Hmm," He said. "Smaller than I expected."

I was still alive. I do not know how. Perhaps Godric's absolute concealment had wrapped around me at the instant of compression, shielding me from the shockwave. Perhaps His passive systems had pre-emptively excluded me from the annihilation. Perhaps I was simply permitted to survive because He wanted a witness. Whatever the reason, I was alive, standing in the shattered remains of the throne room, staring at my king as He held the compressed remains of all existence between His fingers.

"Your Majesty," I whispered. My voice was not working properly. The words came out as a hoarse, ragged rasp. "What is that?"

Godric looked at me, and His expression was no longer disappointed. It was thoughtful, contemplative, almost serene. "This is everything, Osvald. The entire omniverse. Every reality, every timeline, every dimension, every possible and impossible existence. Plus the Horde, of course. They are in here too." He held up the singularity, its infinitesimal surface catching the faint, residual light of the shattered throne room. "All of it, compressed into a sphere smaller than a quark. The infinite mass of existence, contained within a space so small that calling it a point would be an insult to the concept of points."

He closed His fingers around the singularity and, with a casual, almost absent gesture, enlarged it to the size of a marble. The process was instantaneous, effortless—the singularity simply expanded, its infinite density maintained, its gravitational pull somehow contained within the boundaries of its new form. It sat in the palm of His hand, a small, dark sphere that seemed to absorb the light around it, its surface perfectly smooth and perfectly black.

"There," Godric said, His voice warm with satisfaction. "Now it is a trophy. Something to remember the occasion by." He tossed the marble lightly in His palm, the infinite mass of the compressed omniverse moving as though it weighed nothing at all. "The Horde was disappointing, but I do not want to forget them entirely. They were the first opposition in a very long time. They deserve to be remembered. Even if they failed."

He looked at the marble for a moment longer, and then His expression shifted. His eyes widened, just slightly, and a strange, almost startled expression crossed His rugged features.

"Oh," He said. "That is unexpected."

"Your Majesty? What is unexpected?"

He did not answer immediately. He was staring at the marble, His omniscient gaze focused on something I could not perceive. When He spoke again, His voice was quiet, almost wondering.

"The Weakness Paradox triggered," He said. "Not from their attack—that triggered eons ago, the instant they first conceived the thought that I might have a weakness. No, this is something different. The Evolved Weakness Paradox has evolved again. It has been doing that, you know. Evolving. Every few googolplexian years, it reaches a new threshold and reinvents itself entirely. The last time was... hmm. I cannot actually remember the last time. It has been that long."

He paused, and His smile returned—but it was a strange smile, almost rueful. "The Horde attempted to impose narrative logic on me. To make me into a villain who could be defeated. They failed, of course. They could never have succeeded. But the attempt itself—the sheer, audacious creativity of it—has triggered something. The Evolved Weakness Paradox has interpreted their narrative attack as a new category of potential vulnerability. Not a real vulnerability, obviously. Nothing can threaten me. But the Paradox has decided that the mere existence of the concept of narrative vulnerability is unacceptable, and it has responded by... hmm."

He tilted his head, His expression distant. "I am not entirely certain how to describe it. The Paradox has evolved a new enhanced effect. It is called Authorial Primacy. From this moment forward, any sentient being who attempts to conceive of me as a character in a story—as a villain who can be opposed, as a final boss who can be defeated, as any role within any narrative framework—will find that the thought simply cannot form. Not because their free will is suppressed, but because the Paradox has retroactively made me the author of all stories. Every narrative that has ever been told, every fictional universe that has ever been conceived, every plot and character and theme and trope—I am now, and have always been, the author. Not the villain. Not the hero. The author. The one who writes the stories, not the one who exists within them."

He looked at me, and His eyes were ancient and infinite and filled with a strange, almost tender amusement. "The Horde tried to make me a character in their story. The Paradox responded by making me the author of all stories. So their attack could never have worked. It could never even have been conceived. Because I am not the final boss of the omniverse. I am the one who writes the final boss. I am the one who decides how the story ends."

He closed His fingers around the marble again. "And I have just become even more powerful than I was before. Not from absorbing their energy—their combined power is so insignificant that it would not increase my strength by zero percent. But from the Paradox evolving. From the addition of a new enhanced effect. From the continuous, infinite amplification that never stops, that will never stop, that is always making me more than I was an instant ago." He smiled, and the smile was warm and genuine and utterly, impossibly terrifying. "They thought they were attacking me. They were actually giving me a gift."

---

Godric restored everything exactly zero seconds later. Every galaxy, every multiverse, every timeline, every possible and impossible dimension of existence was instantly, perfectly reconstructed. The Horde remained unrestored—the consequences of opposition were absolute, and Godric's mercy did not extend to those who had dared to challenge Him. But everything else, every innocent bystander who had been caught in the collateral compression, was resurrected so flawlessly that they would never know they had been erased.

Except for me. I knew. I had witnessed the compression, and the knowledge of it was a scar on my consciousness that would never heal.

But the marble remained.

Godric had not restored the original mass of the omniverse. He had created a new omniverse, identical in every respect, a perfect copy of the existence that had been compressed. But the original—the actual, physical matter and energy and space-time that had constituted reality before His Strength crushed it into infinity—was still in His hand. A small, dark sphere, perfectly smooth, perfectly black, containing the compressed mass of everything that had ever existed.

He had restored the omniverse while keeping the original compressed in His palm. The new reality was identical to the old one. Every atom was in the same place. Every being had the same memories. Every story was the same story. But it was a copy. A duplicate. A reconstruction that was functionally indistinguishable from the original but ontologically distinct.

The implications of this were so profound, so terrifying, that my mind simply refused to process them. The beings who had been restored—were they real? Were they the same beings who had existed before the compression, or were they copies, identical duplicates who believed themselves to be original because their memories told them they were? Did the distinction even matter? Could Godric create a perfect copy of someone, complete with all their memories and experiences and consciousness, and then allow the original to remain compressed in a marble in His hand, and call that restoration?

I did not have answers. I did not think there were answers. Godric's power had transcended the boundaries of ontology, of philosophy, of the fundamental nature of reality itself. He could create and destroy and recreate so effortlessly that the distinction between original and copy became meaningless. The restored omniverse was real. It was exactly as real as the original had been. But the original was still in His hand, compressed into a marble, its infinite mass contained within a sphere smaller than a child's toy.

Godric placed the marble on a small obsidian pedestal near His throne. It sat there, perfectly black, perfectly smooth, absorbing the light around it. A trophy. A reminder of the last opposition ever to dare challenge the apex predator of creation.

"Every time I look at this," He said, His voice quiet and thoughtful, "I will remember the Horde. Their creativity. Their audacity. Their boss battle music. They were the best opposition I ever faced, even though they were as powerless as all the others. They tried so hard. They gave me everything they had." He smiled, and there was something almost tender in the expression. "I am going to miss opposition. It was fun while it lasted."

I did not ask Him how He knew it was over. I did not need to. The Horde had been the last desperate gambit of a creation that had finally, after countless eons, learned the absolute futility of resisting its master. There would be no more opposition. Not now. Not ever. The fictional heroes of every story ever told had united in a single, ultimate assault, and they had failed so completely that their failure had become a fundamental law of existence.

No one would dare to oppose Godric again. Not because He had altered their free will—He genuinely believed in mortal resilience, and His power never crossed that boundary—but because the lesson had been learned. The omniverse had finally, irrevocably, absolutely understood that victory against Godric was impossible. That resistance was futile. That opposition was not merely dangerous or foolish, but a conceptual impossibility, a contradiction in terms, a thought that could not even be fully formed before it collapsed under the weight of its own absurdity.

I am writing this account because I must. The dread I feel is not the sharp, immediate terror of a witnessed horror. It is a slow, creeping, existential thing that has settled into my bones and made a home there. The marble sits on its pedestal, and every time I pass it, I think about what it contains. The original omniverse. The actual matter and energy and space-time that once constituted all of existence. Compressed into a sphere smaller than my thumbnail. And beside it, the restored reality—identical, perfect, functionally indistinguishable. But not the original.

Are we real? Am I real? Is this journal real? Or are we all copies, duplicates, reconstructions of beings who were compressed into a marble and replaced by a god who considered the distinction meaningless?

I do not know. I will never know. And I will never, ever stop being afraid.

**Year Indeterminate — The Last Opposition**

**Penned by Osvald, 44th Court Wizard of Thronegate**

*I have written an account of the final opposition against His Majesty. It is attached to this journal entry, a separate volume that I have titled "The Fictional Horde." I will not summarize it here. The account speaks for itself.*

It is a thought that cannot be fully formed, a concept that collapses under its own absurdity, a dream that shatters against the absolute, unassailable reality of His existence.*

*I thought I would feel relief. I thought the end of opposition would bring peace. But standing here, in the silence of a creation that has finally, utterly submitted, I feel only dread. Because the silence is not peaceful. It is the silence of a prey animal that has learned to stop struggling. It is the silence of a child who has learned to stop crying. It is the silence of an omniverse that has given up.*

The Godric of this instant is incomprehensibly more powerful than the Godric of the previous instant. The Godric of the next instant will be incomprehensibly more powerful than the Godric of now.*

*I love Him. I will serve Him until my mortal body fails. But I will never, ever stop being afraid.*
