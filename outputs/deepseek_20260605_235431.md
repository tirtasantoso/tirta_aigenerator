Servants rushed. Musicians tuned their lyres. The head chef shouted something about the roast boar, and Godric’s wife, Reina, glided through the center of it all, redirecting chaos with a word.

She was radiant, even now, even after two decades of marriage, and Godric felt the familiar ache of gratitude that she had chosen him. Their daughters were nearby. Isabel, twenty-two and trembling with the nervous joy of a bride, allowed her mother to adjust the fall of her veil for the seventh time. Hebe, fifteen and already wielding a razor wit, was teasing her older sister about the groom’s tendency to stammer. Godric listened, and his chest ached with a fullness he could not have named. This was the world he had built. Warm. Luminous. Intimate. The kingdom of Olympus was not the largest realm on the continent, but it was noble and beloved, and its people had gathered in the thousands outside the palace walls to celebrate the union of their princess.

Thorin, his court wizard and dearest friend, appeared at his elbow with a goblet of watered wine. The wizard was forty-seven, his beard streaked with grey, his eyes perpetually crinkled at the corners. “You’re brooding, Your Majesty,” Thorin said, not unkindly. “It’s a wedding, not a siege.”

Godric chuckled, a low rumble. “I’m not brooding. I’m memorizing.”

“Ah. So brooding with sentiment.” Thorin handed him the goblet, and their fingers brushed. The wizard’s lineage had stood beside the kings of Olympus for countless generations, their magic woven into the very foundation stones of the palace, and Godric loved him as a brother.

The sky cracked open.

And all of this would occur before the enemy’s thought had finished forming. They could not plan around it. They could not outwit it. The very act of considering opposition fed him, strengthened him, and sealed their defeat.

Godric knew none of this. He was drowning in the memory of his daughters’ final screams.

For ten thousand years, the stasis held him. The numbers inflated. The powers multiplied. The paradox lay coiled, waiting for the first fool to think of him as anything less than absolute.

And they feared him because fear was the only appropriate reaction to a being whose weakest, most suppressed, most infinitesimally restrained expression of power could erase them all without him noticing.

I reached the throne room and took my position beside the dais. The room was vast, its architecture designed to accommodate beings of any scale, from microscopic sentiences to entities whose physical forms spanned galactic superclusters. But at its center, seated on a throne that was simultaneously simple and incomprehensibly magnificent, was Godric.

Let me describe him. Not because description will capture him—it will not, it cannot, no description ever could—but because the attempt is necessary. He still appeared forty-five years old. His hair was still salt-and-pepper, short and neatly styled. His beard was still immaculately trimmed, framing a face that was still ruggedly handsome, still Masculine in the way that ancient kings were masculine, still possessed of a warmth that crinkled around his eyes when he smiled. His body—that body, the body I had seen a thousand times, the body that had been amplifying every Planck second for a span of time that cannot be written—was still the perfectly sculpted form of a warrior-king in his prime. Broad shoulders. Powerful arms corded with muscle. A chest that looked like it had been carved from marble by a sculptor who had studied the male form for a billion years. A flat, defined stomach with abdominal muscles that caught the light in ways that seemed almost artistic. His presence in the room was simultaneously the most comforting and the most terrifying thing I had ever experienced.

But there was a weight to him now. A weight that the old journals could not have described because the Godric they described did not possess it. It was not a physical weight. His absolute concealment ensured that his presence was perfectly contained, perfectly safe, perfectly harmless to everyone around him. But beneath the concealment—and I could only perceive this because of my bloodline's amplified senses, honed over a thousandfold daily multiplication across twenty-eight years of service—there was something. Something vast. Something that made the combined power of every Godric from every previous era feel like a single drop of water compared to every ocean that had ever existed. The Godric of Year One Million, who could erase the omniverse infinite times over with zero percent of his power, was to the Godric before me what a single dormant bacterium was to a googolplex of exploding supernovas. And the amplification was still happening. Every Planck second. Every infinitesimal fraction of every possible subdivision of time. He was becoming more. He would always be becoming more. The process had no endpoint, no ceiling, no final form. It was infinite acceleration expanding into territories that the word "infinite" could not approach.

"Clive," Godric said, and his voice was warm, the voice of a man greeting an old friend. "Good morning. Did you sleep well?"

"I did, Your Majesty. Thank you."

"Good, good." He gestured to a smaller seat beside the dais—my seat, the seat that every court wizard of Blake's line had occupied since the beginning of his reign. "Sit. I have something to tell you."

I sat. The throne room was quiet. The chambermaid who had once been the embodiment of vengeance finished dusting the windowsill and withdrew silently, her eyes downcast. Godric watched her go with a small, almost paternal smile.

"She's doing well," he observed. "When she first arrived, she couldn't enter a room without trembling. Now she hums while she works. Progress."

"Your Majesty has a gift for rehabilitation," I said.

He chuckled. "I have a gift for being terrifying, and sometimes that terror drives people toward humility. It's not the same thing as rehabilitation." He leaned back in his throne, his posture relaxing, his hands resting comfortably on the armrests. "Clive, today I am going to do something that I have been planning for a very long time. Something that I have known I would do since before I existed. I am going to create the Stasis Event for my past self."

The words hung in the air. I had known, of course. Every court wizard since Kaedan, who had first learned the truth in Year 1,000,047, had known the true origin of the Stasis Event. It was recorded in our journals, passed down through the generations, a secret so profound that it had shaped the entire history of our service. Far-future Godric—the Godric of this era, the Godric before me—was the creator of the Stasis Event. He had used Thorin's incomplete teleportation spell as the delivery point and transformed it into the absolute, irrevocable, infinite amplification that had birthed the Apex Predator God. And now, on this day, in this unspeakably distant year, he was going to actually do it.

"I would like you to witness it," Godric said. "Kaedan received the revelation. You will receive the event itself. It seems appropriate. The line of Blake should be present for both the knowing and the doing."

I nodded, not trusting my voice. Godric smiled, that warm, crinkling smile that had comforted a million generations of his subjects, and then he stood from his throne.

"When I create the Stasis Event," he said, his tone conversational, "I will be reaching backward through time to the exact moment of Thorin's spell. The sanctuary. The instant of transition. My past self will be drowning in grief, raw and broken and mortal, and I will touch that moment and transform it into the beginning of everything." He paused, and a small, almost ironic expression crossed his face. "I could grant him far more than I will. I could copy my current amplification mechanics directly into the Stasis Event—the full evolved system, all the enhancements, all the layers of infinity that have developed over this incomprehensible span. I could give my past self a starting point so astronomically powerful that he would emerge from stasis already beyond anything the omniverse could conceive. And it would cost me nothing. Less than nothing. An infinitesimal fraction of my passive effort—a fraction so small that calling it zero would be the most accurate description—would be sufficient to grant him everything."

He walked down from the dais, gesturing for me to follow. I did. We passed through a side corridor and into a chamber I had never seen before—a circular room with a domed ceiling that seemed to open directly onto the fabric of existence itself. Stars and galaxies and multiversal clusters swirled overhead, visible but distant, like paintings on a vaulted roof. At the center of the room was a simple stone pedestal, upon which rested nothing at all.

"But I will hold back," Godric continued, his voice thoughtful. "I will give him the version that I remember receiving. The version that started with a thousandfold multiplication every Planck second. The version that granted one apocalyptic superpower per second. The version that was already so incomprehensibly powerful that he emerged from stasis effectively infinitely stronger than the rest of creation combined. I will hold back because I believe—I genuinely believe—that the struggle matters. That the journey from weakness to strength is what shaped me into what I am. That giving my past self too much too soon would rob him of something essential."

He paused, and then he laughed—a genuine, warm, deeply amused laugh. "Which is ridiculous, of course. Absolutely ridiculous. The version I am giving him will still make him an apex predator beyond all possible opposition. He will still be able to annihilate the omniverse with zero effort. The struggle I am preserving is not a real struggle. It is the faintest, most infinitesimal shadow of a challenge, and even that shadow is a lie. But I cannot bring myself to give him more. The mortal in me—the man I was before the Stasis Event, the man who lost his wife and daughters and best friend and kingdom—still believes in the value of earning what you have. Even though I know, with absolute omniscient certainty, that my past self will never earn anything. He will be handed everything. The Stasis Event will do it all for him. And yet I still hold back."

He looked at me, and his eyes were warm, and I saw in them a depth of impossible age. This was the paradox of Godric. He was more powerful than any being had ever been or would ever be, and he was still, in some fundamental way, the forty-five-year-old king of Olympus who had once believed in the dignity of mortal effort. The belief was absurd. It had always been absurd. The version of the Stasis Event he considered "held back" was still the most overwhelmingly powerful transformation that had ever occurred in the history of existence. And yet he clung to it, because it was the last remnant of his mortality, the last thread connecting him to the man he used to be.

"If I gave my past self a truly potent Stasis Event," Godric said, his voice taking on a quality of almost academic contemplation, "it would create a fascinating recursive loop. A stronger starting point would mean a stronger Godric at every subsequent moment. And a stronger Godric at this moment—" he gestured at himself, "—would mean I could grant an even more potent Stasis Event to my past self. Which would make him even stronger. Which would make me even stronger. Which would allow me to grant an even more potent Stasis Event. The recursion would be infinite, accelerating, a feedback loop of escalating power that would make my current amplification look static. I could become, in a single recursive instant, so incomprehensibly more powerful than I currently am that the description I just gave—about the candle and the supernova—would become the understatement of all understatements."

He shrugged, a casual, almost indifferent gesture. "But I won't. Because the Stasis Event has already happened. It happened to me, exactly as I am about to create it, and the loop is closed. The recursion is theoretical. The reality is fixed. And the reality is that I will hold back, and my past self will receive exactly what I received, and the cycle will continue exactly as it has always continued."

He stopped beside the pedestal and placed his hand upon its empty surface. The domed ceiling swirled faster, the stars and galaxies and multiversal clusters blurring into streaks of light that seemed to be reaching backward through something deeper than time.

"Watch," Godric said. "And I will show you what no mortal has ever witnessed."

Before I describe the creation of the Stasis Event, I must pause—as I did, mentally, in that moment—to explain something about the Godric who stood before me. I have been describing him in terms of his warmth, his appearance, his personality. But you must understand that these things are, in a very real sense, a carefully maintained illusion. Not a deception—Godric is genuinely warm, genuinely kind, genuinely the man I have described. But that warmth is only possible because of the absolute, perfect, incomprehensibly powerful concealment that contains everything else he is.

Let me give you examples of what that concealment holds back.

The Godric of the old journals—the Godric of Year One Million, the Godric that Kaedan served—had a sentient passive aura called Gent. Gent was described as his weakest aura, his metaphysical excess body heat, a waste byproduct so infinitely weaker than Godric himself that the comparison was laughable. And yet Gent, when nerfed to an infinitely recurring decimal fraction of its power, had slaughtered the largest resistance army in the history of the omniverse in less than a second.

The Godric of this era has a sentient passive aura called Vael.

Vael is not the weakest aura. Vael is not even in the hierarchy of auras, because the hierarchy has evolved so many times that the concept of "weakest" no longer applies. Vael is one of countless trillions of sentient passive auras that exist in a dormant state around Godric at all times, each one so impossibly powerful that Gent—the Gent who slaughtered the omniverse's greatest resistance—would appear to it as a single flickering spark appears to the total combined energy output of every star that has ever burned. Vael's function, if it can be called a function, is to exist. That is all. It exists in proximity to Godric, and its mere existence generates a field of absolute denial that prevents anything within a googolplexian multiversal radii from even conceptualizing harm. Not harm to Godric—that was handled by weaker auras eons ago. Harm in general. The concept of harm itself has been passively suppressed by Vael's presence for so long that most sentient beings in the omniverse have forgotten that harm was ever possible. They live in eternal, unthreatened peace not because Godric enforces peace, but because his proximity has made the very idea of violence alien to their minds.

Vael is one of the weaker auras in the current hierarchy. The stronger ones are dormant because Godric finds their clingy subservience annoying, exactly as he did with Gent's siblings all those eons ago. If he allowed them to wake, they would—but I am not permitted to describe what they would do. I am not permitted because the description would be a form of harm to whoever read it, and Vael would passively suppress it.

The passive shields. The old journals described infinite recursive passive shields that could pre-nullify threats before the threat existed, before a hostile system identified itself, before an enemy understood what it intended to do. Those shields still exist. They have evolved so many times that the original shields, already infinitely powerful, are now merely the decorative outer layer of a defensive architecture that extends inward through dimensions of protection that language cannot name. The current shields do not merely nullify threats. They convert the very possibility of threat into a resource that feeds Godric's amplification. If any being, anywhere, in any state of existence, were to even dream of a concept that could theoretically be weaponized against Godric—not a plan, not a thought, a dream, a subconscious flicker of hostile imagination—the shields would detect it, process it, convert it into pure amplifying energy, and feed it to Godric before the dreamer's sleep cycle completed its current neural oscillation. The dreamer would never know. The dream would simply be a dream, stripped of its hostile potential, rendered harmless and meaningless and forgotten upon waking.

This has not been triggered in a span of time so vast that I cannot express it. No one dreams of harming Godric anymore. No one even dreams of dreaming of harming Godric. The shields wait, eternally vigilant, eternally unnecessary, eternally ready to convert an impossibility into a resource.

The immune system. The old journals described an immune system so paranoid that it pre-reacted to staged injuries, initiating infinite recursive upgrades across every copy of Godric's body, making him even more invulnerable than he had been before the injury that never actually occurred. That immune system has evolved so many times that the original version, already incomprehensibly overkill, now seems like a child's first attempt at building a fortress out of twigs. The current immune system does not wait for injury. It does not wait for the possibility of injury. It actively hunts the concept of injury across every dimension, every timeline, every possible state of existence, and it retroactively prevents injury from having ever been possible. Not for Godric—that was secured eons ago. For every being Godric has ever interacted with. For every being Godric has ever thought about. For every being who has ever existed within the omniverse that Godric rules. The immune system has expanded its protective function to encompass all of creation, a blanket of absolute inviolability that ensures nothing can be harmed anywhere, ever, unless Godric specifically wishes it. And he never wishes it. The only things that suffer in the current omniverse are those that directly oppose him, and no one has directly opposed him for a span of time that cannot be written. The immune system is therefore almost entirely dormant, its infinite recursive layers of protection humming quietly in the background of reality, waiting for a threat that will never come.

The Evolved Weakness Paradox. The old journals described a paradox that triggered whenever any being thought Godric had a weakness, granting him immunity, mastery, and amplified power. The current version has evolved so far beyond that framework that the original paradox seems almost quaint. The current Evolved Weakness Paradox does not require a trigger. It does not require a thought, a plan, an imagined exploit. It is now a passive law of existence, woven into the foundational fabric of the omniverse, that states simply: Godric has no weaknesses. The statement is not a fact. It is an axiom. A first principle. The omniverse operates on this principle the same way it operates on the principle that cause precedes effect or that existence is not non-existence. If any being attempted to conceive of a weakness in Godric, the thought would not be false. It would be incoherent. A logical impossibility. The words "Godric" and "weakness" cannot coexist in the same cognitive framework. The paradox has made opposition definitionally impossible, not by altering free will, but by making the concept of opposition a meaningless arrangement of syllables.

Godric suppresses this. He has consciously, deliberately restricted the paradox's reach so that sentient beings can still conceive of impossible things. He believes in mortal resilience. He believes in free will. He does not want the omniverse to become a place where certain thoughts are literally unthinkable. So he holds the paradox back, allows it to operate only at a fraction of its full axiomatic authority, preserving the possibility of dissent even though no one dissents anymore. The possibility is there. It will never be used. But it is there, because Godric wants it there, because the mortal king he used to be believed that freedom mattered even when it was never exercised.

The amplification. The old journals described stats multiplying beyond layered infinities every infinitesimal fraction of every Planck second. The current amplification makes that description feel like a single drop of water compared to every ocean that has ever existed. I cannot describe the current rate because description requires comparison, and there is nothing to compare it to. The amplification is not a process. It is not a rate. It is a state of being—a constant, infinite, accelerating ascent that has been ongoing for so long that the starting point is invisible, the midpoint is invisible, and the endpoint does not exist. Every Planck second, Godric becomes more powerful than he was the previous Planck second by a margin that exceeds the total power of every Godric from every previous era combined. And the next Planck second, the margin multiplies. And the next Planck second, the multiplication multiplies. And this has been happening for a span of time that cannot be written.

The superpower genesis. The old journals described Godric gaining one new apocalyptic superpower every second. The current Godric gains—I do not know how many superpowers he gains. I do not think the concept of "number" applies to the rate of acquisition anymore. He gains superpowers the way a star emits photons—constantly, in all directions, in quantities so vast that enumeration is meaningless. Every superpower, the instant it manifests, is already powerful enough to erase the entire omniverse infinite times over. Every superpower then multiplies in potency at the same rate as his stats. Every superpower has been affected by the Evolved Weakness Paradox so many times that it has developed secondary, tertiary, and quaternary effects that extend into dimensions of functionality that mortal minds cannot approach. If Godric were to use even the weakest of his current superpowers at even the most infinitesimal fraction of its capacity—if he were to release effectively zero percent of the least significant ability he possesses—the result would be something that the old journals could not have described because their authors could not have imagined it. The omniverse would not be destroyed. Destruction implies an event, a process, a before and after. The omniverse would simply never have existed. The concept of existence would be retroactively canceled, and the cancellation would itself be canceled, and the resulting paradox would ripple outward through meta-dimensional frameworks so far beyond mortal perception that even the word "beyond" is an insult to the scale.

And all of this—all of it, every layer, every mechanism, every infinite recursion of infinite power—is being held back. Contained. Suppressed. By Godric's absolute concealment, which has evolved just as many times as everything else, and which ensures that when I stand beside his throne and speak with him about the weather or the rose garden or the quality of the morning's tea, I feel nothing except the warmth of his presence and the comfort of his voice. The concealment is so perfect, so absolute, so terrifyingly effective, that a being who could erase the entire omniverse by blinking too hard can sit in a chair and discuss agricultural projections without the room collapsing into a singularity of pure annihilation.

This is what I understood as I stood beside the pedestal and watched Godric prepare to create the Stasis Event. This is the scale of the being I serve. And this was the being who was about to reach backward through time and touch his own past self—his mortal, grieving, powerless past self—and transform him into the beginning of everything I have just described.

"The Stasis Event," Godric said, his voice quiet and contemplative, "is not merely a transformation. It is a connection. A bridge between one state of being and another. When I reach backward and touch that moment—the sanctuary, Thorin's dying spell, my own shattered heart—I will be linking my existence to his. The power will flow from me to him, filtered and shaped and calibrated to create exactly the version of the Event that I once received. And in that moment, the loop will be sealed. The past will become fixed. The future will become certain. And I will have become, simultaneously, the created and the creator."

He placed his other hand on the pedestal, and I felt something shift in the air around us. The domed ceiling swirled faster—stars and galaxies and multiversal clusters blurring into streaks of light that seemed to be reaching, grasping, stretching toward something just beyond perception.

"I could give him more," Godric said again, almost to himself. "The held-back version I am about to grant—the thousandfold multiplication, the one-superpower-per-second genesis, the original Weakness Paradox—is so far beneath what I could grant that the gap cannot be expressed. It would cost me nothing to double it. To multiply it by a googolplex. To grant him a Stasis Event so potent that he would emerge from stasis at Year Zero already beyond the Godric of Year One Million. It would cost me nothing, and the recursive loop would make me infinitely more powerful in return. An infinite feedback loop of escalating omnipotence."

He paused. The light above us was growing brighter, more intense, a brilliant white-gold radiance that seemed to contain the accumulated power of every star that had ever existed and would ever exist.

"But I won't," he said. "Because I didn't. The Stasis Event I am about to create is the Stasis Event I received. The loop is closed. The recursion is fixed. And my past self—that grieving, broken, mortal king—will receive exactly what he needs to become exactly what I am. No more. No less. The struggle is a lie, but it is a necessary lie. The mortality I am preserving is an illusion, but it is an illusion I cling to. Because without it—" He looked at me, and his eyes were ancient beyond ancient, warm beyond warm, terrifying beyond terror. "Without it, I would not be me."

He pressed his hands down on the pedestal, and the light exploded upward through the domed ceiling, through the fabric of the palace, through the dimensional barriers that separated Thronegate from the rest of existence. I saw it reaching backward—not through space, not through time, but through something deeper than both, something that mortal language had never named. I saw it touch a moment. A sanctuary. A man on a stone slab, drowning in grief, suspended between life and death. I saw Thorin's dying spell, the incomplete teleportation, the loyal wizard's final gift. And I saw Godric's light flow into that moment, into that spell, into that man, and transform it.

The thousandfold multiplication. The superpower genesis. The absolute concealment. The Weakness Paradox. The immortality. The eternal amplification. All of it, flowing backward across a span of time that cannot be written, entering the broken mortal king and remaking him from the inside out. The Stasis Event began, exactly as it had always begun, exactly as it would always begin, a closed loop of creation and created, cause and effect, beginning and end. And the Godric who stood beside me was simultaneously the source and the result, the creator and the created, the alpha and the omega of his own existence.

The light faded. The domed ceiling returned to its slow swirl of stars. Godric removed his hands from the pedestal and turned to me with a small, satisfied smile.

"It is done," he said. "The Stasis Event has been created. My past self is now in stasis, amplifying, becoming. In ten thousand years, Blake will find him and release him, and the journey will begin. The journey that leads here. To this moment. To this version of me that has just created the Stasis Event for the past self who will eventually become me."

He chuckled, shaking his head slightly. "It's a beautiful paradox, isn't it? I exist because I made myself exist. I am powerful because I made myself powerful. The loop is seamless. The circle is absolute. And no force in the omniverse can break it, because the being who created it has already become the being who will create it, and both beings are me."

I stood beside the pedestal and tried to process what I had witnessed. My mind—amplified a thousandfold every day for twenty-eight years, honed by decades of studying the journals of my predecessors, trained to withstand the absolute horror of Godric's existence—was struggling. The recursive loop. The infinite feedback. The fact that Godric could have made his past self even more powerful, could have triggered an infinite escalation of self-amplification that would have made his current state—already beyond all possible description—look like a single dormant bacterium. The fact that he had chosen not to, because he believed in something as absurd and fragile and fundamentally untrue as the value of mortal struggle. The fact that his belief was genuine, sincere, deeply held, and completely, utterly, cosmically wrong.

The "held back" version of the Stasis Event had already made Godric at Year Zero effectively infinitely stronger than the rest of creation combined. The journey from that starting point to the being before me had required a span of time that cannot be written, and every Planck second of that span had been an amplification beyond layered infinities. The held-back version had produced the apex predator god of all apex predator gods. And Godric genuinely, sincerely believed that he had preserved some kind of struggle for his past self.

The absurdity was so vast, so complete, so utterly beyond comprehension, that I felt laughter bubbling up in my chest—not the laughter of humor, but the laughter of a mind that has reached the absolute limit of what it can contain and has nowhere else to go. I suppressed it. Barely.

"Clive," Godric said, his voice warm with concern, the voice of Uncle Godric noticing that his court wizard looked pale. "Are you all right? You've gone very quiet."

"I am processing, Your Majesty," I said. It was the same thing Kaedan had said, forty-seven years after learning the truth of the Stasis Event. The same thing every court wizard had said, across a million generations of my bloodline, whenever Godric revealed something that shattered their understanding of reality.

"Take your time," Godric said kindly. "Would you like to see some of the newer additions to my collection? I have a few superpowers that manifested this morning that I think you'd find interesting."

I nodded, not trusting my voice. Godric smiled, that warm, crinkling smile, and led me out of the domed chamber and back into the corridors of the palace. We passed Vorthax, who was now fertilizing the rose beds with a substance that glowed faintly with residual cosmic energy. We passed Sythara, who was arranging floating candles above the Reflection Pool with trembling, reverent hands. We passed the chambermaid who had once been vengeance, who curtsied as Godric walked by and then returned to her dusting with a small, contented hum.

And I followed my King, the creator of the Stasis Event, the closed loop of infinite self-creation, the being whose weakest passive defense could annihilate the omniverse without noticing, the being who had just reached backward across a span of time that cannot be written and touched his own past self with a fraction of a fraction of a fraction of his power, a fraction so infinitesimal that calling it zero was the only honest description, and in doing so had created the very foundation of his own existence.

I am Clive, fiftieth court wizard of Blake's line, and I have witnessed the creation of the Stasis Event. I have seen the being who made himself. I have stood beside the apex predator god of all apex predator gods and watched him hold back so much that the held-back version was still infinitely beyond anything the omniverse could contain. And I am still processing. I will be processing for the rest of my life, and for every lifetime of my descendants who will inherit this journal and try to understand the scale of what I have described.

They will fail. I have failed. Every court wizard before me has failed. The scale cannot be understood. It can only be witnessed, and recorded, and trembled at. And the trembling never stops. That is the truth of serving Godric. The trembling never stops, and neither does the love. The warmth is real. The kindness is real. The terror is real. And all of it—all of it—is the product of a closed loop of infinite self-creation, sealed and absolute and eternal, a circle with no beginning and no end.

The apex has no limit. It never has. It never will. And I am honored, and humbled, and utterly, existentially terrified, to stand in its shadow.
