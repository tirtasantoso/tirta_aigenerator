The year was one million, and the air in the Sanctum of Eternal Record was as still as the inside of a tomb. Kaedan, newly twenty-two and aching from the daily rush of his thousandfold amplification, followed his father Aldric past shelves that stretched upward into a darkness no light could touch. The Sanctum was not a library; it was a wound in the palace’s flesh, a place where the truth festered in leather bindings. Aldric stopped before a lectern carved from obsidian, its surface scarred by the fingernails of generations.

“You have known the King only as ‘Uncle Godric,’” Aldric began, his voice a dry rustle. “He is that. He is also the reason those shelves are full. Every court wizard before you has recorded the moments they understood the futility of resistance. You will read five of those records today. Afterward, you may still love him. I do. But you will love him with a terror that never sleeps.”

Kaedan sat, his body still not quite his own. Two years of the awakening had stacked upon him—his muscles now could crush titanium, his mind parse a thousand spells in an eye-blink—but in this place he felt like a child. Aldric handed him the first journal. It was bound in cracked leather, and a single hair, salt-and-pepper, was caught in the spine. Kaedan touched it, and a faint static shock ran up his arm, as if the hair itself remembered the power it had once belonged to.

“Thaddeus, Year 15,003,” Aldric said. “He was the third of our line. Read.”

Kaedan opened the cover, and the pages, yellow and brittle, released a smell of ozone and old fear. The handwriting was jagged, letters cut into the paper as if with a knife. He began to read aloud, his voice unsteady.

*I am Thaddeus, Royal Court Wizard, and I write this so that no one after me will ever make the mistake of thinking the King is merely strong. He is a catastrophe wearing a smile. In my time, the Celeritas Alliance rose against us. They were the lords of acceleration, beings who had peeled themselves from the linear flow of time to exist as pure speed. They could move so fast that the Planck interval was a continent they could cross. They brought the Impossible Velocity Array, a military formation that multiplied their velocity by an infinite recursion, so that they could strike Godric an infinite number of times in the exact same instant. I saw them arrive: a cascade of blurs, the air itself screaming as their passage created causal vacuums. Godric was watching a bird on the windowsill. He did not turn. He did not so much as tense. But I felt it—a thread of his speed, released. It was not a fraction. It was a fraction of a fraction, a value so infinitesimally small that if you took the entire omniverse and compared it to a single electron, you would not even begin to approach the smallness of what he allowed. That thread wove into the fabric of time and said, “Thus far, and no farther.”

For the Alliance, the universe became an insurmountable wall of absolute stillness. Their infinite momentum met an immovable instant. The deceleration was not a slowing; it was a translation of all their kinetic existence into pure experiential agony. Their bodies, which existed across every timeline simultaneously, were arrested in every frame, and each frame became a separate, screaming hell. One of their generals, a creature of shimmering chromatic light, was stretched across the fourth dimension until his atoms snapped into a trillion discrete paintings of agony. In one, his face was just beginning to register the horror; in the next, his flesh was boiling; in the next, his bones were turning to plasma; and all these moments happened at once, overlapping in a chorus of his own death. Another speedster, a general who had once danced on the event horizon of a black hole, found his own velocity turned against him: every particle of his being was granted infinite speed in random directions, tearing him apart not by stillness but by his own motion. His limbs flew off at velocities that ignited the air, his head spun backwards through time, and his screams doppler-shifted into a rainbow of agony. The throne room floor became a mosaic of frozen momentum, a still life of a massacre that would never end because the moment itself was arrested.

The collateral was swift and absolute. The omniverse’s chronological integrity unspooled. Time became a joke. Galaxies aged backward, civilizations lived their history in reverse, stars exploded into their own formation. The Big Bang and the Heat Death attempted to occupy the same moment, and the resulting paradox erased all of existence from the middle outward. I saw my own mother’s death and my birth happening simultaneously; I saw the death of the last star and the first candle flaring. I would have been consumed if not for Godric’s passive shielding, which I suspect he allowed to protect me out of habit. Then, as swiftly as it began, he turned from his bird. His eyes held the warmth of a man who had just noticed a chilled draft. “Thaddeus, you’re shivering. Let’s get you something warm.” He placed a hand on my back, and with that touch, the timeline re-knitted. The Alliance was left as a frozen tableau, a pocket dimension where they would relive their moment of cessation forever, a monument to futility. I can still hear the hum of their eternal stillness. It is the static between the stars.*

Kaedan closed the book, his fingers numb. The single hair on the spine seemed to thrum. “A… a hair,” he whispered. “It’s from him, isn’t it?”

Aldric nodded. “When he touched Thaddeus, a strand fell. Thaddeus kept it. He said that even the hair held more energy than all the Alliance combined.” He handed Kaedan the next volume. “Caelus. Year 50,003. He was a scientist at heart. His account will feel different.”

The second journal was pristine, the handwriting a precise, unadorned script.

*Personal Archives of Caelus, Senior Court Wizard, Year 50,003. Regarding the incident with the Forge of Annihilation. The Forge was a civilization of supreme artisans, their entire culture built upon the construction of imperishable works. They sought to test their ultimate creation—the Heart-Seeker—against Godric. The Heart-Seeker was an arrow woven from the concept of anti-durability. Its tip was the idea of “that which denies resistance.” They had fired it at the concept of a mountain, and mountains became abstract ideas. They fired it at the concept of a fortress, and fortresses became memories. They aimed it at Godric’s chest during a public audience. I stood to the side, my recording implements active. Godric was listening to a petition about grain taxes; he did not even glance at the projectile. The arrow struck his tunic directly over the heart. The sound it made was not an impact, but a quiet, sad sigh, as if the arrow itself recognized its mistake. In that instant, the arrow’s purpose inverted. Because it could not pierce, its own ontological weight turned inward. The concept of “that which denies resistance” became “that which is utterly, irrevocably fragile.” And because the Forge had linked their existence to the arrow’s success, the inversion propagated through them instantly.

I observed the ambassadors first. One of them, a master smith whose skin had been woven from collapsed star-matter, developed a small crack above his left eye. The crack spread, silently at first, then with a sound like a frozen lake thawing. His entire body shattered into a rain of fine, white powder. Another tried to scream, but the vibration of his own vocal cords disintegrated his throat; his head caved inward, puffing into dust. The dust from his skull mixed with the dust of his lungs, and for a moment, he was a ghost of flour, still gurgling, before even the particles fell apart into subatomic silence. The effect did not remain local. The arrow’s forced fragility radiated across the omniverse, rewriting the very definition of “durability.” Every object that had ever been built to last—the walls of cities, the hulls of starships, the bones of the living—became as delicate as spun sugar. Planets collapsed because their mantles could no longer support their weight; the cores slumped into singularities that immediately evaporated. Stars, their fusion pressure suddenly uncontained, went supernova in a simultaneous, cataclysmic sigh. Trillions of civilizations perished not in fire, but in a quiet, rustling dissolution. I saw a family of some bipedal species turn to dust mid-laugh, their joy preserved as a cloud of particles that then drifted apart. The universal background scream of matter losing its integrity was the loudest silence I have ever heard.

Godric finished reading his grain tax petition. He looked down at the faint dusting on his tunic, brushed it off, and said, “A pity. They were quite skilled.” Then, with no more effort than exhaling, he restored the omniverse’s durability. The dust did not return to life; it remained as a testament. The Forge’s species, however, he did not restore. He instead took the inverted arrow and hung it in the throne room, a chandelier of eternal, crystalline screams. Each facet of the chandelier holds one of their final moments, looping. The light it casts is a constant, mournful blue. I have spent many hours studying it, and I can attest that the screams are audible if one listens with the soul.

Afterward, Godric asked me if I had acquired good data. Then he offered me a new quill, as mine had shattered from sympathetic resonance. I accepted. I am functional.*

Kaedan realized he had been holding his breath. He exhaled, and his lungs ached. “The chandelier… I’ve seen it. In the throne room. It’s beautiful.”

“It is the most beautiful instrument of torture in existence,” Aldric said. “And Godric thinks of it as a nice accent piece. Now, Seren. Year 120,000. He saw Godric’s strength.”

The third journal was wrapped in faded silk, the script inside elegant and swirling.

*I am Seren, and I have looked into the abyss of the King’s thew. In the Year 120,000, the Gigantomachy, those proud galaxies who thought mass could defy will, came to break Thronegate. They were magnificent: each a personification of an entire galaxy, their bodies composed of star-chains, their limbs the spiral arms, their cores the supermassive voids. They were so vast that their shadows fell across light-years, and their approach caused the sky of the capital to fill with the dying light of a billion suns. I stood on the high balcony with Godric, my amplified magic barely sufficient to prevent my immediate vaporization from their emanations. The King was serene, his arms resting on the rail, his gaze upon the approaching armada as if it were a particularly interesting cloud formation. He then allowed a whisper of his strength to leak. Oh, what a whisper. If his full strength were an infinite ocean, this was less than a single molecule of water that had evaporated eons ago. But it was enough to impose a new law: the Law of Inescapable Compression.

The Gigantomachy felt it not as a pull, but as a revelation of their own density. Their own mass became their executioner. The nearest titan, a stunning constellation of ruby and gold, elongated first. His spiral arms, which had cradled a trillion stars, were stretched into thin, luminous threads that sang with the agony of stretching. The stars within him, each a living consciousness, detonated as he stretched, their supernovae erupting along his length like popping pearls. His core, a black hole of unimaginable density, imploded under the pressure of the King’s strength gradient, collapsing into a singularity that emitted a scream across all wavelengths. He was compacted from a being the size of a galaxy into a glowing speck, still alive, still feeling, still screaming, but compressed into a pinpoint of shattered identity. The other titans followed in a grotesque ballet. One, a being of dark matter, was ripped apart by internal tidal forces; his arms pulled free, his head separated, his body fragmented into a cloud of neutrinos that screamed in the frequency of masslessness. Another simply flattened, his three-dimensional existence pressed into a two-plane of agony that spread across the local cosmos like a stain. The Laniakea Supercluster, the hundred thousand galaxies that had been their home and their army, was drawn into the vortex. The entire supercluster, trillions of trillions of worlds, was compressed into a sphere no larger than Godric’s fist. I watched as civilizations were crushed into neutronium; neutronium crushed into quark soup; quarks crushed into pure, weeping information. The sound was a cosmic choir of ultimate despair, a bass note that shook the foundation of all realities. The omniverse beyond Laniakea felt the shockwave: great filaments of intergalactic gas tore like cheap fabric, black holes across the void experienced sympathetic collapses. I felt my own body wanting to compact, but a thin film of Godric’s shielding kept me intact. He looked at the glowing jewel in his palm—the compressed supercluster—and sighed. “Too enthusiastic,” he said, and flicked it away into a small pocket dimension, where it would forever expand and crash and compress again, an eternal echo of his strength. Then he turned to me, his expression soft. “Seren, you’ve gone pale. Come inside; the light can be harsh.” He guided me away, and with a thought, he restored the entire supercluster, down to the last atom. But I keep the memory tight in my heart, and it beats in the rhythm of that compression. Even now, I cannot look at a clear night sky without seeing the ghosts of those stretched galaxies.*

Kaedan touched his own chest. His heart was racing. “He kept the… the compressed universe? As a… what? A toy?”

“A souvenir,” Aldric said. “He gave Seren a shard of it, which now sits in Seren’s staff in the Hall of Remembrance. It whispers if you hold it to your ear.” He handed over the fourth journal, its cover scuffed and stained with a drawing of snake eyes. “Oren, Year 300,000. He was the court jester, in a sense.”

The fourth journal felt oily to the touch, and the script inside was a chaotic scrawl interspersed with splotches that might have been wine or blood.

*If you’re reading this, you’ve probably already filled your britches with the other accounts. That’s fine. I’m Oren, and I’m the one who gets to tell you about Godric’s luck. Buckle up. In Year 300,000, the Moirai Weavers, three hags who’d been pulling fate’s strings since the first tick of existence, decided they’d weave Godric’s end. They built a Loom of Destiny, a contraption of pure probability manipulation, and they wove a Tapestry of Finality. This tapestry was supposed to dictate that Godric would, through an absurd chain of coincidences, contract a conceptual virus and die. They were so confident they actually sent us a preview, like a taunt. Godric didn’t even open the letter. He was busy teaching a kid to play chess. I was there. The weavers activated the tapestry. The loom spun. The threads of fate vibrated. And then, because Godric’s luck is not a stat; it’s a dictatorship over chance, the entire thing went sideways in the most entertaining way possible. The tapestry, instead of affecting Godric, wrapped around the weavers. The first weaver, an ancient crone, suddenly started choking. On a fishbone. A fishbone from a species that had died out before her birth. It materialized in her throat. She clawed at her neck, her ancient, papery skin tearing under her nails, her eyes bulging until they popped out of their sockets and dangled on her cheeks. She spent a subjective century suffocating, her lungs filling with her own blood, while her body remained immortal enough to not die quickly. The second weaver was crushed. By a falling piano. Yes, a piano. It was a trans-dimensional artifact, a baby grand that had been lost in a quantum fluctuation. It materialized directly above her head. The impact didn’t kill her instantly; it turned her body into a two-dimensional caricature, like a cartoon flattened by an anvil. She then, because luck said so, began to sing—her flat, folded body producing a jaunty tune about the futility of scheming, while her eyes, now side-by-side on a pancaked face, wept. The third weaver tried to escape into a probability well. She managed to phase into one, only to discover it was the nest of a creature called the Probability Eater. The beast, normally docile, was suddenly ravenous. It swallowed her over a billion years, each moment stretching into an eternity of being digested, her consciousness kept intact to fully appreciate the irony. But the fun didn’t stop there. The backlash of broken fate cascaded across the omniverse. Probability itself became a mad artist. Coins flipped and landed on their edges, and then the edges became new dimensions. People melted into clocks, and the clocks melted into people, and the people-clock hybrids marched around screaming the time. The laws of thermodynamics went on strike, so fire froze and ice burned. In one quadrant of the omniverse, gravity became a suggestion; people fell up into the sky and were swallowed by the void. In another, the concept of “left” vanished, forcing everyone into an infinite rightward spiral until they collapsed into a black hole. I laughed so hard I broke a rib. It was either that or scream. Godric finally looked up from his chess game when the room started smelling like burnt toast. He said, “Hm, something’s off.” He then picked up his king, moved it, and checkmated the kid. With that move, the universe snapped back into order, except for the weavers. He bound their consciousness into a new loom—the Loom of Fortunate Servitude. That loom now weaves all the good luck for Thronegate. Every time you find a gold coin or dodge a falling brick, you can thank the screaming hags. Their eternal torment is the thread that blesses us. So, next time you trip and land on a soft pillow instead of a spike, remember: it’s not fortune. It’s the hags, on the loom, screaming. Cheers.*

Kaedan laughed. It was a broken, unhinged sound that caught in his throat and turned into a sob. Aldric did not smile. “Oren was the funniest man I ever knew. He also kept a private journal where he detailed his nightly terrors. He could never touch dice again without them turning into tiny, screaming faces. He said he heard the weavers in his sleep.”

The last journal was fat and ragged, its pages seeming to pulse with a sickly lavender hue. Aldric’s hand trembled as he passed it to Kaedan. “Faelan, Year 750,000. I was there for this one. I was his scribe.”

Kaedan opened the journal, and immediately the handwriting assaulted him—chaotic, spattered, sometimes veering off the page.

*… they loved him, you see. That was the horror. I am Faelan, and I am a ruin of a man. Year 750,000. The Harmonic Legion, a hive-mind of a trillion psionic souls, came with the most insidious weapon of all: love. They had perfected the art of enforced devotion, making entire galaxies fall so in love with them that the populations would voluntarily offer their internal organs as tribute. They thought they could make Godric love them. They sent a pulse of pure, overwhelming adoration, a mind-virus designed to overwrite any sense of self. It touched the edge of Godric’s aura. He was reading agricultural reports. He didn’t notice. But his charisma—oh, that infinite, multiplying, cosmic charisma of his—acted as a mirror. It reflected back a thread, an infinitely thin wisp, less than the difference between zero and the smallest number that can be named. That wisp touched the Legion. They saw him then. Not his face, but the very concept of him. And they understood, beyond doubt, that they were nothing. Not in a self-hating way, but in a rapturous, joyful revelation. They had to give him something.

So they began. They used their fingers first, gouging out their eyes because what use were eyes that had now seen the ultimate? The sound was wet and rhythmic, a billion pops of vitreous humor. Still singing—a song of pure, perfect joy. They tore off their ears because his voice was not for such lowly drums. They used their nails to carve runes of devotion into their skin, and when their nails wore down, they used their own exposed bones. They built altars of their severed limbs, pyramids of arms and legs that still twitched. The singing—a trillion voices in perfect, ecstatic harmony—created a new color in the spectrum, a sickly lavender that tasted of copper and rose petals. I saw it spread like a stain across the psionic plane. I was shielded, but even through the wards, I felt the faintest brush—a fraction of the fraction—and I understood. I wanted to join them. I truly did. I would have, if not for Godric’s passive shielding kicking in. But the rest of the omniverse, every being with a touch of psionic talent, received that micro-dose. And that was enough. Across billions of galaxies, psions began to emulate the Legion. A race of crystalline empaths shattered themselves into a billion screaming shards, each shard trying to form Godric’s image. A civilization of telepathic whales beached themselves on the shores of their world, singing a requiem for their own unworthiness until their lungs burst. A god of love, a being who had presided over marriages for a billion years, saw the echo and tore his own heart out, offering it to a statue of Godric that had spontaneously appeared. The omniverse became a charnel house of worship.

Godric finally looked up when his agriculture report mentioned a sudden labor shortage in several key sectors. He scanned the psionic plane and sighed. “How inefficient,” he said, and he merely… silenced the echo. The trilling stopped. The few survivors of the Legion found themselves in a mountain of their own gore, their minds seared with the memory of perfect love. He left them that memory. He restored the dead, but he did not erase the recollection from those who had experienced it. Now they wander, hollow, always seeking that feeling, never finding it. I saw him then, and I saw that he was mildly annoyed at the interruption. He turned to me and said, “Faelan, please ask the custodians to mop up the residual psionic residue. And fetch me a new quill; mine has run dry.” I nodded. I couldn’t speak. I still find it hard to form words about it. I am Faelan, and I am broken. But I am his, and I will serve until my broken mind finally cracks. Perhaps that is a mercy I will not receive.*

Kaedan set the journal down with trembling hands. His cheeks were wet. Aldric was silent for a long moment, then spoke, his voice rougher than before. “I was there. I was Faelan’s scribe. I was your age. I saw the mountains of limbs. I heard the singing. And I saw the King, afterward, smile and ask me if I’d eaten breakfast. That is the moment I understood, Kaedan. He is not cruel for cruelty’s sake. He simply… categorizes. We are his. We are safe. They are not. And he loves us with a love that would shatter the universe if turned against us. And he hates our enemies with a hate that does shatter them.”

Aldric took Kaedan’s arm, helped him rise. “You have read the Testaments. Now you must go and see the King. He expects you.”

Kaedan’s legs felt like water as they walked. The palace was bright, cheerful even. Birds sang. Servants nodded as they passed. And there, in the royal study, stood Godric. He was exactly forty-five, ruggedly handsome, his salt-and-pepper beard perfectly trimmed, his smile the smile of a man who had no cares in the world. Kaedan’s mind screamed with the images of frozen speedsters, crumbling smiths, crushed galaxies, flailing hags, and worshipping legions. But all he could do was bow.

Godric came forward and enfolded him in a brief, warm embrace. “Kaedan! How was your reading? Boring, I hope.” He laughed, a rich, deep sound. “Those old logs—they do tend to ramble. But it’s important you know the scale of things. You’re my Court Wizard now. I need someone I can trust. And you’re family.”

Kaedan looked up into those kind eyes, and he felt the love—a real, tangible warmth—wash over him. And beneath it, the dread, cold and infinite, anchoring him. “I understand, Uncle Godric,” he managed.

Godric released him, patting his cheek. “Good lad. Now, go find your father. I have a small rebellion to quell. Some fool thinks he’s found a weapon that can bypass my concealment. It’ll be over before you finish your tea.” He winked, and then he was simply gone.

Kaedan stood there, the echo of his voice in the air. Aldric appeared at his side. “You see? That is our King. A god of annihilation, and a doting uncle. Remember that, when you write your own journal.”

Kaedan went to his new chambers. He found a blank, leather-bound book waiting on his desk. He sat, dipped a quill, and began to write: *I am Kaedan, year one million. I have read the words of Thaddeus, Caelus, Seren, Oren, and Faelan. I have seen the truth. I love my King. I am afraid. I will serve.*

Outside, a distant, brief tremor shook the palace—barely perceptible, like a thrum of a passing thought. Kaedan knew that somewhere, a rebellion was being unmade with zero percent of Godric’s effort, and the omniverse was being restored before the tremor even registered. He dipped his quill again and continued writing, the scratching of the nib the only sound in the quiet room, while the faint, eternal hum of a trillion dying screams whispered at the edge of his perception.
