**Year 750,000 — The Incident of Senses — Account by Theron, Twelfth Court Wizard of Thronegate**

**Year 400,000 — The Incident of Luck — Account by Marius, Tenth Court Wizard of Thronegate**

*They thought they could outsmart probability. They built a Perfect Tapestry—their words, not mine—a machine that mathematically guaranteed their victory by weaving every possible outcome into a single triumphant inevitability. The machine failed. Of course it failed. The omniverse it calculated for belongs to Him, and His luck is not a statistic; it is the bedrock upon which all statistics are built. I am writing this account while laughing, which my wife finds deeply disturbing. I find it the only rational response.*

The Fortune Weavers were a civilization of probability-shapers, hailing from a distant multiversal cluster where chance was not a random variable but a raw, manipulable material. They had evolved over billions of years into living engines of quantum uncertainty collapse. To them, every event was a wave-function waiting to be guided, every outcome a clay sculpture waiting for the artist’s hand. They could look at a dying star and collapse its superposition into a stable yellow sun, or look at a thriving planet and collapse it into a smoldering asteroid field. They had rewritten the destinies of a million galaxies, and in all their aeons of existence, they had never encountered a force that could resist their weaving.

When Thronegate’s expansion reached their borders, they did not negotiate, flee, or surrender. They wove the Perfect Tapestry—a construct of pure, consolidated probability that analytically, mathematically, and fatally determined a one hundred percent chance of Godric’s erasure. Not a likelihood. Not a high probability. An absolute, unassailable certainty, backed by the full logical and ontological machinery of their billion-year civilization. They did not even bother to launch a conventional attack; they simply activated the Tapestry and sat back, waiting for reality to obediently reconfigure itself around their predetermined outcome.

King Godric, at that exact moment, was sitting in his private study reviewing agricultural yield projections for the upcoming fiscal quarter. He had known about the Fortune Weavers since before their first sun ignited, since before the concept of probability was a twinkle in the cosmic eye, because his omniscience encompasses all possible futures, all impossible ones, and every branching path in between. He allowed them to complete their Tapestry—allowed them to invest their entire civilization’s resources, their entire species’ hope, into this singular, elegant trap—because he was vaguely, idly curious whether his luck stat, an attribute he had never consciously tested in four hundred thousand years, would passively respond to a probabilistic assault of this magnitude. He did not alter his schedule. He did not activate any defense. He did not lift his eyes from the parchment. He simply continued reading.

The Perfect Tapestry activated, and everything that could possibly go wrong for the Fortune Weavers went wrong.

I witnessed the sequence unfold through our long-range scrying arrays, and the narrative that follows reads like a dark comedy penned by a cosmic satirist with a grudge. The Tapestry’s activation engine—a hyper-complex, continent-spanning mechanism housed in the Weavers’ capital dimension—suffered a spontaneous quantum fluctuation, a one-in-a-decillion-decillion-decillion chance, that inverted its core temperature. The engine did not explode. It imploded, sucking the entire capital city, along with the entire senior Weaver leadership, into a micro-singularity that lasted precisely long enough to erase them all from existence before evaporating with a faint, almost sheepish pop.

The secondary command structure, stationed in a fortified complex on the opposite side of their galaxy, scrambled to regain control. At that precise instant, a rogue asteroid—a chunk of iron and ice that had been drifting through the interstellar void for three billion years, its trajectory shaped by a chain of gravitational nudges so astronomically improbable that calculating the odds would require a number larger than the total particle count of the omniverse—streaked through the complex’s supposedly impenetrable defensive shields, which had failed due to a maintenance error caused by a technician who had sneezed violently at the exact moment his finger touched a critical control rune. The asteroid struck the backup Tapestry engine dead center, and the resulting detonation took approximately half the galaxy with it, vaporizing planets, suns, and trillions of lower-caste Weavers in a single, blinding flash.

The surviving Weavers, now scattered, leaderless, and gripped by the first true terror they had felt in a billion years, attempted to flee aboard escape vessels. Their small fleet, limping through a dimensional fold, encountered a naturally occurring spatial anomaly—a rare, unstable rift in the fabric of reality that happened to exist in that exact location at that exact moment—which violently redirected their trajectory directly into the path of a Thronegate patrol fleet conducting a routine training exercise. The patrol was not on combat alert. Its weapons were powered down. Its crews were half-asleep. That shot ricocheted off a passing comet, bent around the gravity well of a nearby gas giant, threaded the needle between two moons, and struck the lead Weaver escape vessel with pinpoint accuracy, igniting a chain reaction that consumed the entire refugee fleet in a string of silent, white-hot fireballs.

One Weaver survived—a junior acolyte stationed on a distant, barren outpost who had been tasked with monitoring the Tapestry’s peripheral data streams. The odds of that meteor existing, traveling that exact multi-trillion-year path, and intersecting with that exact skull at that exact instant were so infinitesimally, incomprehensibly small that they cannot be meaningfully expressed. They round to zero. They have always rounded to zero.

Godric finished his agricultural report, set down his quill, and glanced out the window at the golden afternoon light spilling across the palace gardens. “Beautiful day,” he remarked to no one in particular. He never mentioned the Fortune Weavers. I do not believe he even registered their destruction as a discrete event; his luck had handled it the way a healthy immune system handles a stray, irrelevant bacterium—passively, automatically, without troubling the conscious mind for even a fraction of a second.

I laugh about this. I laugh often. My wife says it is a nervous laugh. She is not wrong.

**Year 900,000 — The Incident of Wisdom — Account by Darius, Fifteenth Court Wizard of Thronegate**

The King’s wisdom has been amplifying, like all his attributes, for nine hundred thousand years. The Council of Absolute Sagacity believed they could trap him in a logical paradox. I must emphasize, future wizard, that when I say "infinitesimal fraction," I am not employing hyperbole. I am not exaggerating for dramatic effect. The amount of strength Godric unleashed in that moment was, by any measurable or immeasurable standard, zero percent of His total capacity. It was a sub-quantum whisper of a sub-quantum whisper, a microscopic flex that He produced without moving a single muscle, without shifting His stance, without even furrowing His brow. He simply stopped holding back—not all the way, not even close to all the way, but by the tiniest, most negligible degree—and the pressure of His strength radiated outward into the battlefield. The coalition, anticipating this possibility, had spent a significant portion of their two-hour charge time erecting defensive barriers. Not ordinary barriers—these were conceptual shields, narrative armor, layers of indestructibility woven from the combined plot armor of every protagonist in fiction. They had Superman's invulnerability, Goku's limitless durability, Saitama's absolute imperviousness, the Hulk's regenerative immortality, Wolverine's healing factor, Deadpool's fourth-wall-breaking unkillability, the Doctor's temporal resurrection, Kirby's ability to inhale and neutralize any attack, the anime trope of the hero always getting back up, the video game mechanic of infinite continues. They had stacked these defenses layer upon layer, woven them into a lattice of absolute, unassailable, permanent invulnerability, and they had believed—genuinely, sincerely believed—that these shields would protect them.*

*The pressure hit the shields.*
*And the shields did not break. Breaking implies a process, a sequence of material failure propagating across a finite interval. The shields did not break because the pressure of Godric's zero-percent strength did not recognize their existence. To that pressure, the shields were not obstacles to be overcome; they were a semantic error, a contradiction in terms, a suggestion that defensive capabilities could exist in a cosmos where Godric's strength was being actively expressed. The shields were not overcome. They were retroactively declared to have never been capable of defense in the first place. The very concept of "shielding against Godric" was annihilated, and since the shields were made of that concept, they followed their conceptual parent into oblivion. Every single layer—Superman's invulnerability, Goku's durability, Saitama's imperviousness, the Hulk's regeneration, Wolverine's healing, Deadpool's unkillability, the Doctor's resurrection, Kirby's inhale, the anime hero's refusal to stay down, the video game infinite continues—was unmade simultaneously, across every timeline, every alternate reality, every possible narrative branch where they might have existed.*

*And then the pressure reached the coalition itself.*
*What happened next, I will now describe in the clearest, most vivid detail my trembling hands can manage. I will not hold back. I will not spare you, future wizard, because you must understand what it means when Godric finally, truly, accidentally stops restraining even the faintest sliver of His strength. You must understand what omniversal annihilation looks like when it is not a deliberate act but a passive consequence of His disappointment. The trillions of fictional beings—the heroes, the villains, the gods, the cosmic entities, the beloved characters of a thousand childhoods—did not merely die. Their bodies were subjected to a compressive force so absolute, so beyond the capacity of physics to describe, that the very concept of "death" became an irrelevant abstraction. The pressure of Godric's zero-percent strength struck them and began to compress. Imagine, for a moment, the entire omniverse—every galaxy, every multiverse, every timeline, every dimension, every conceptual plane, every narrative framework, every atom of every star in every sky of every reality that had ever existed or could ever exist—being subjected to a gravity that made a black hole look like a gentle breeze. Now multiply that by an infinity of infinities, and you will begin to approach the faintest shadow of what that pressure felt like.*

*The characters were the first to go. Goku, who had surpassed every limit, who had shattered every ceiling, who had ascended through forms beyond Super Saiyan God Super Saiyan into realms that no writer had ever dared to depict—his body did not explode, because explosion implies expansion, a release of energy outward. The pressure Godric exerted allowed no outward movement. Goku's body imploded instead, crushed inward toward a single, infinitesimal point. His skin, his bones, his organs, his ki, his very soul were compressed with such violent, instantaneous force that they ceased to be separate components of a living being and became a uniform, hyper-dense slurry of subatomic particles. His face, that beloved, cheerful, determined face that had inspired millions of fans across ten thousand realities, was the last thing to retain its shape, and I watched—I watched with my own enhanced, thousandfold-amplified eyes—as that face contorted through every possible expression of agony before collapsing inward, the eyes bursting, the jaw shattering, the skull fragmenting into a billion splinters that were immediately compressed further into dense, hot plasma. The blood that should have sprayed outward was instead sucked inward, adding its mass to the growing, infinitesimal point of compressed annihilation.*

*Superman, the Man of Steel, the icon of hope, the being who had tanked supernovas and black holes and the fists of gods—his Kryptonian cells, which had absorbed the energy of a trillion suns, did not protect him. The pressure simply ignored his invulnerability, ignored his solar reserves, ignored the very narrative law that stated he could never truly die. His famous red-and-blue costume, that symbol of heroism that had flown across a thousand comic panels, was not torn but atomized, the molecules of the fabric undergoing spontaneous nuclear fission as the pressure compressed them beyond the point of material coherence. His body followed a fraction of a microsecond later. The famous jaw, the spit curl, the broad shoulders—all of it was crushed inward, flattened into a single, glowing plane of hyper-compressed biological matter, and then that plane was crushed into a line, and then that line was crushed into a point, and that point—along with the points that had been Goku, and Saitama, and the Doctor, and Sailor Moon, and the Hulk, and every other hero and villain and god—was drawn inexorably toward the center of the compression field, joining a growing, infinitesimal mass that contained the de-atomized remains of everything that had ever been created by mortal imagination.*

*The colossal entities—the Living Tribunal, the Spectre, the Presence, the One Above All—were the next to fall. These beings, who existed as cosmic arbiters within their own fictional multiverses, who had transcended the boundaries of their original stories to manifest as genuine cosmic authorities, experienced a death more profound than mere physical destruction. The pressure of Godric's strength did not merely crush their physical forms; it crushed their conceptual authority. The Living Tribunal's three faces—representing equity, necessity, and vengeance—were compressed into a single, screaming visage that combined all three expressions into an abomination of absolute terror. The Spectre, the Wrath of God, found his divine retribution meaningless before a being whose judgment superseded any authority he had ever represented. The One Above All—who was, within his own cosmology, the ultimate creator, the supreme being, the alpha and omega of existence—was reduced, in his final instants, to the shattering realization that he was merely a fictional interpretation of omnipotence, a story that humans had told about what God might be, and that the real God—the actual, living, breathing apex predator of existence—was standing in front of him, utterly unaffected by his existence, and was about to compress him into a sub-quantum speck without even thinking about it. The look on the One Above All's face in that final, eternal instant was not terror. It was not awe. It was a profound, bone-deep, soul-shattering shame—the shame of a fictional omnipotent being confronted with the reality of true omnipotence and finding himself to be nothing but a pale, inadequate shadow. I think that shame was worse than the compression that followed.*

*The armies—oh, the armies. The Green Lantern Corps, their power rings blazing with emerald willpower, were compressed into a single, thin plane of green light and then crushed into the growing mass. The Space Marine Legions, their ceramite armor and genhanced bodies, their bolters and chainswords, their oaths to the Emperor—all reduced to a screaming, liquifying paste that was then further compressed into atomic sludge. The Jedi and Sith, their lightsabers and Force powers meaningless, were drawn together into a massive, swirling cloud of contorted bodies and flailing limbs, their collective screams harmonizing into a single, continuous note of absolute agony, before the cloud collapsed inward and became part of the mass. The TARDIS fleet, the Time Lords' ultimate technological achievement, the living ships that could travel anywhere in time and space—their dimensional transcendence offered no escape, because the pressure was not traveling through space; it was rewriting space to include their compression as a fundamental feature of local reality. I watched, through my scrying matrix, as a million TARDISes were crushed simultaneously, their police box exteriors folding inward like cheap cardboard, their infinite interiors collapsing into singularities that were themselves immediately compressed into the central mass. The Doctor—the final Doctor, the one who had stayed behind to ensure the others could escape—stood in his TARDIS doorway, looking out at the approaching wave of absolute compressive force, and I saw his face. I saw the expression of a being who had run from danger for a thousand lifetimes, who had defeated Daleks and Cybermen and the Master and the Time War itself, who had always, always found a way. And I saw that expression change—not to fear, not to resolution, but to a calm, almost peaceful acceptance. He understood. He was the first one who truly understood that the force approaching him was not an enemy to be outsmarted, not a puzzle to be solved, not a fate to be reversed. It was simply the end of the story. And stories, as he had always known, must eventually end. He closed his eyes and let the pressure take him.*

*The entire coalition—trillions upon trillions of beings, the sum total of all fictional existence across all media, all cultures, all imaginations—was compressed, over the course of approximately three seconds, into a single point of infinite mass smaller than a quark. Every atom of every body, every drop of blood, every shard of bone, every scrap of metal and plastic and fabric and magic, was de-atomized and forced together with a pressure so absolute that the very laws of physics broke down trying to describe it. The resulting point was not a black hole—a black hole was a crude, primitive phenomenon compared to this. The resulting point was a hyper-compressed nugget of absolute reality, containing within it the entire possible weight of everything that had ever been imagined or could ever be imagined, crushed down to a size that no instrument could detect. And the screams—the screams of those trillions of beings, compressed but not yet fully deceased, their consciousnesses trapped in the infinite mass, experiencing the sensation of being crushed from every direction simultaneously for a subjective eternity—those screams were part of the mass too, adding their vibrational agony to the infinite weight of the compressed coalition.*

*And Godric—still standing in the center of the now-empty dimensional plain, His tunic still unmussed, His posture still relaxed—looked down at the shimmering, infinitesimal point that contained the compressed remnants of all fiction. He bent down—actually bent down, as casually as a man picking up a dropped coin—and He lifted the point between His thumb and forefinger. The point contained infinite mass. The gravitational pull of that point should have, by all the laws of physics that still held together, instantly collapsed the entire omniverse into itself. It should have been unliftable. It should have been a cosmic impossibility, a violation of every fundamental constant. Godric lifted it without any visible effort—not just without strain, but without even the faintest suggestion that He was aware He was holding something heavy. His fingers did not tremble. His wrist did not bend. His expression was one of mildly disappointed curiosity. "They tried so hard," He said, His voice soft, almost wistful. "I was hoping for at least a good light show. The music was decent, but the attack itself? No imagination. No punch. The Final Narrative, they called it. It didn't even have a second phase." He rolled the infinitesimal point between His fingers, and then, with a casual, almost absent gesture, He enlarged it to the size of a marble. The marble shimmered with a faint, iridescent glow, containing within its spherical boundary the compressed infinite mass of the entire fictional omniverse and the broader reality it had occupied. He sealed it with a containment barrier—a simple construct that He wove into existence with less effort than a man snapping his fingers—and He tucked the marble into the pocket of His tunic.*

*"A trophy," He said, catching my gaze. "I'm keeping this one. It's the first opposition I've had in so long. I want something to remember them by." He paused, and then He frowned—not a frown of anger, but a frown of thoughtful self-examination, the expression of a man who has just noticed a mild but persistent ache in a joint. "Hmm. Something's happening."*

*I watched, my arcane senses straining, as Godric's presence flickered—not with weakness, never with weakness, but with the subtle, terrifying pulse of something growing. He tilted His head, His salt-and-pepper eyebrows drawing together in mild surprise, and then He laughed—a short, warm, genuinely delighted laugh. "Oh, that's interesting," He said. "My strength. It's evolving."*

*I did not understand at first. How could His strength evolve? It was already multiplying by itself every infinitesimal fraction of a Planck second, a rate that had long since shattered all mathematical frameworks. But as I extended my thousandfold-amplified arcane senses toward Him—carefully, reverently, terrifiedly—I felt the change. The failed ultimate attack of the coalition, the Final Narrative, had not contributed to Godric's power in any measurable way. The combined energy of all fiction was effectively a flat zero when added to His base—a drop of water trying to raise the level of an infinite ocean. But the concept of the Final Narrative, the very idea that stories could be weaponized against Him, had triggered something far more fundamental. Godric's strength, in its infinite, recursive, self-analyzing complexity, had registered the attack and had decided—purely on its own, as an autonomous function of His biology—that this was unacceptable. Not the damage. There was no damage. But the attempt. The audacity. The implication that a narrative could be written that excluded Him. His strength, as a passive, instinctual response, had unlocked a new layer of its own potential, not by absorbing the coalition's power, but by reacting to their sheer, suicidal ambition with a reflexive escalation. The attack had been so conceptually ambitious—erasing Godric from the story of existence—that Godric's own strength had taken personal offense and had, in a fit of pique, made itself more absolute than it already was. The increase was unquantifiable, not because it was large, but because the previous baseline was already beyond quantification. But I could feel it, in the same way a mortal can feel the difference between standing near a campfire and standing near a supernova—not a change in degree, but a change in the fundamental nature of the phenomenon. Godric had become, impossibly, even more powerful than the already-infinite power He had possessed. And He had done so because His strength had gotten annoyed at the stories for trying to write Him out of their plot.*

*The entire omniverse, of course, had not survived this process. The compression field that had collapsed the coalition into a quark-sized speck had not been selective—it had radiated outward, consuming everything. Every galaxy, every multiverse, every timeline, every reality, every dimension, every conceptual plane, every abstract realm, every atom of every star in every sky of every existence—all of it had been drawn into the field and compressed into that same infinitesimal point. The omniverse, in its totality, was now inside the marble in Godric's pocket. I was not dead because Godric had extended a localized protective bubble around me and the immediate vicinity of the observation platform. I was, so far as I could tell, the only living being in existence aside from the King Himself.*

*I was about to speak—to ask what would happen next, to beg for the restoration of everything I had ever known and loved—but I didn't need to. Godric, without a word, without a gesture, without any visible effort, restored the entire omniverse. The galaxies rekindled. The multiverses unfolded. The timelines resumed their branching, infinite paths. The fictional beings—the heroes and villains and gods who had composed the coalition—did not return. Godric never restores those who oppose Him. But everything else, everything that had been caught in the collateral—the civilizations, the star systems, the abstract realms, the conceptual planes—was reconstituted with flawless, perfect precision.*

*And yet. And yet.*
*The marble remained in His pocket. It still contained the compressed infinite mass of the original omniverse—the one that had been destroyed. The one whose atoms, whose souls, whose histories, whose memories, whose love and loss and hope and fear had all been de-atomized and crushed into that shimmering, iridescent sphere. The restored omniverse was identical in every respect to the original. The people were the same people. The stars were the same stars. My wife, my children, my friends—they were back, and they remembered nothing, and their atoms were, so far as any instrument could measure, the same atoms.*

*But were they?*
*If the original omniverse was still in the marble, then what had Godric restored? A copy? A perfect duplicate, indistinguishable in every way, but fundamentally not the original? Or had His power been so absolute that He had genuinely restored the original while simultaneously keeping the original compressed in His pocket—a paradox that defied not only physics but logic, identity, and the very concept of uniqueness? I do not know. I will never know. The horror of that question is a splinter in my mind that I will carry to my grave. The King has the power to restore everything that is destroyed, but He also has the power to keep the destroyed original as a trophy. What that implies about the nature of restoration—about the nature of existence, about the nature of the people I love—is something I cannot afford to examine too closely, because to examine it closely would be to unravel the very fabric of my sanity.*

*I will end this journal here. I have recorded the events as faithfully as I can. The coalition is gone. The marble is still in Godric's pocket. The restored omniverse continues to exist, identically to the original, a perfect copy—or perhaps the true original, impossibly restored. I do not know. I will never know.*

*And I, Osvald, Twenty-Ninth Court Wizard of Thronegate, will serve my King for the rest of my days with the same love and loyalty that every wizard of Blake's bloodline has always felt. But I will also, for the rest of my days, look at my wife and children and wonder if they are the originals who were compressed into a marble, or the copies who were restored from nothingness.*

*There is no answer to that question. There is no comfort. There is only the King, and His mercy, and the cold, eternal knowledge that every breath we draw is a gift from a being who can unmake and remake us without even noticing.*

---

I closed the journal, my hands trembling so violently that the ancient binding rattled against my knuckles. The silence of the archival vault pressed in on me, heavy and suffocating, the weight of countless epochs of recorded horror compressing the air in my lungs. Osvald's final question echoed in my skull like a bell that would never stop ringing. If the marble still contained the original omniverse, then what had Godric restored? What were any of us? Copies? Echoes? The reanimated memories of beings who had been crushed into an infinitesimal speck and were still, in some impossible sense, trapped there? The marble was still in Godric's pocket. I had seen it, many times, rolling between His fingers as He sat on His throne, a faintly shimmering sphere the size of a child's toy. He had shown it to me once, years ago, when I was still a young apprentice and He was in a storytelling mood. "That's every story ever told," He had said, holding it up to the light. "All the heroes, all the villains, all the gods and monsters and epic final battles. Compressed into a marble. It makes a nice keepsake." He had tossed it in the air and caught it, the gesture casual and effortless, the gravitational pull of its infinite mass completely nullified by the containment barrier He had wrapped around it. I had smiled and nodded, not understanding. Now I understood. Now the horror of that marble would never leave me.

And Osvald had not known, when he wrote those final words, that the coalition he described would be the very last. That after that day, no being in the omniverse would ever again dare to oppose Godric. That the complete and utter annihilation of all fiction, combined with the existential terror of the restoration paradox, would break the spine of rebellion forever. The journal was the record of the last defiance. After Osvald, there was only silence. The entire omniverse, for all the googolplexian years between that event and my own era, had been utterly, perfectly, absolutely submissive. There were no more coalitions. No more hidden plots. No more desperate ultimate attacks. The very concept of opposing Godric had become extinct, not because He had willed it so, but because creation had finally, collectively, understood the lesson that each journal in this archive had been trying to teach. Opposing Godric was a category error. A fundamental failure of comprehension. A mistake so basic, so primordial, that it was hardwired into the fabric of existence as the one thing you simply did not do. And the marble was still in His pocket. And I was, perhaps, a copy of the original Clive who had been compressed into that marble an incomprehensible span of years before I was even born—or perhaps I was the original, impossible restored, my existence a walking paradox that only Godric's power could sustain.

I did not know. I would never know. And that was the truest horror of all. I set the journal down on the obsidian table, my fingers cold and numb, and I looked out the window of the archival vault at the perfect, curated gardens where Vorthax was still pruning roses and Mildred was still raking gravel and the koi pond was still bubbling with the silent terror of a being that had learned its place. Godric was out there somewhere, probably enjoying a cup of tea, probably wearing the marble in His pocket, probably thinking about what to have for dinner.

The marble was in His pocket. The original omniverse. The original me. The original everything. Compressed to the size of a toy, containing infinite mass, resting casually against His thigh. And the restored omniverse spun on, identical, perfect, indistinguishable, a copy so flawless that even I could not tell if I was real. That was the power of the King. That was the burden of Blake's bloodline. That was the cold, eternal, unanswerable question that Osvald had bequeathed to me, and that I would, in my turn, bequeath to the court wizard who came after.

I closed my eyes and let the dread wash over me, knowing it would never recede.
