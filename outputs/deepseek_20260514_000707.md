We are the record-keepers. We are the ones who translate the King's casual, passive, effortless atrocities into language that future generations can survive reading. It is the hidden responsibility of the position—the responsibility that no one outside the bloodline ever sees. We are not merely wizards. We are historians of the impossible, archivists of the screaming, curators of a museum of infinite cruelty. And we do it because we love Him, and because He loves us, and because the alternative is to be on the other side of His attention."

"Uncle Godric would never hurt us," Jim said, and the statement was not a question.

"No," Tom agreed, his voice very soft. "He would never hurt us. But He has, on two occasions, hurt me. The first was the sixty seconds without the Awakening. The second was when He told me, after I failed the Offering, that He was disappointed in me. I have fought dark gods and abstract horrors. I have maintained the Abattoir-Engine for six decades. I have written seventeen volumes of the Testaments. And I tell you, Jim, with absolute sincerity, that the disappointment in His voice was more painful than any physical injury I have ever sustained. Because I love Him. And disappointing someone you love is its own special category of agony."

I had been listening from the other end of the corridor, my presence hidden by the absolute concealment that I could raise and lower at will. I chose, in that moment, to reveal myself, stepping out of the shadows with a soft, deliberate footfall. Both wizards turned. Tom's expression flickered with the brief, automatic check of the Bleak-Auditor; the reading must have been calm, because his shoulders relaxed fractionally.

"Jim," I said, walking toward the empty stretch of wall, "your father has told you the hard truths of the position. The dread, the pressure, the euphemisms, the screaming. He has done his duty as a teacher. Now let Me do mine as your Uncle. I want to show you why we do this. Why the bloodline has served, and will serve, until the end of all things."

I raised my hand and touched the blank wall. The stone rippled, and a memory unfolded from my omniscience—a visual projection, perfectly rendered, of a moment that had occurred six hundred thousand years earlier. The image showed a wizard with wild, white hair and deep-lined eyes, kneeling before my throne. He was weeping.

"This is Corbin," I said. "The one who coined 'collateral enthusiasm.' After the Incident of the Needle, he was broken. The screaming of the boneless billions had shattered something in his psyche that even the thousandfold Awakening could not repair. For three years after the incident, he barely functioned. He maintained his duties, but there was no light in his eyes. Your grandfather—Tom's great-great-grandfather—was the Court Wizard who trained Corbin. He came to Me, one evening, and asked Me to heal his student." I paused. "I could have done so instantly. A single, passive thought would have reknit Corbin's shattered mind and restored him to perfect psychological health. But I chose not to."

Jim stared at the image. "Why?"

"Because Corbin did not want to forget. He told Me, when I asked him directly, that the memory of the screaming was the only thing that kept him honest. That the trauma was a scar he had earned in My service, and that to erase it would be to erase the witness he had borne." I watched the memory of Corbin weeping, his tears spotting the obsidian floor. "I have never forced healing upon any of My court wizards. They carry their scars because they choose to, and I respect that choice. It is one of the few forms of autonomy that I leave entirely in their hands."

The memory shifted, dissolving into another. A younger Tom, his mutton chops still dark, standing in the study with a Bleak-Auditor in his trembling hands. The dread-pressure in the room, visible in the memory as a faint, amber haze, was elevated. "The morning of the duplicate Offering," I said quietly. "Your father has told you about the sixty seconds. He has not told you what happened after."

In the memory, the younger Tom collapsed as his Awakening was stripped away. He lay on the floor, gasping, his body frail and aging. The sixty seconds passed with agonizing slowness. Then, at the sixty-first second, I rose from my reading chair and knelt beside him. The memory showed my hand, warm and calloused, closing around his trembling fingers. "I am sorry," the memory-version of Me said, and the words were soft, genuine, heavy with a regret that no one else in the omniverse would ever hear from My lips. "That was excessive. I was vexed, but I should not have taken it out on you."

The younger Tom, still gasping, looked up at Me with tears streaming down his cheeks. "I failed you, Uncle. I should have checked the Offering more carefully. I should have—"

"You made a mistake," I interrupted him, my voice firm but gentle. "You are mortal. You are allowed mistakes. I forgot, for a moment, that you are not omniscient, and I reacted with a pettiness that you did not deserve. Can you forgive Me?"

In the memory, Tom stared at Me with an expression of absolute, bewildered shock. "Forgive you? Uncle, You are the King. You do not need my forgiveness."

"Yes, I do," I said. "Because you are family. And family forgives family."

The memory dissolved. In the present, in the Hall of Euphemisms, Tom was staring at the empty wall with an expression that was difficult to read. His eyes were wet. Jim looked between us, his young face a mixture of awe and confusion and the first, tentative stirrings of understanding.

"Your father forgave Me," I said, turning to face the boy. "And I restored his Awakening, and I have never revoked it since. I learned a lesson that day, Jim. I learned that even an omniscient being can be blind to the fragility of the people he loves. I had spent so many aeons surrounded by the infinite, the absolute, the unassailable, that I had forgotten what it felt like to be breakable. Your father reminded Me. His forgiveness reminded Me." I placed a hand on Jim's shoulder, feeling the steady, youthful pulse of his heart beneath the apprentice robes. "The hidden responsibility of the Court Wizard is not merely to manage the dread. It is to remind Me of My own humanity. Blake did it, at the very beginning. Corbin did it, through his scars. Your father has done it, through his patience and his honesty and his willingness to tell Me when I am being a petty, vindictive old god who needs to get over Himself. And you will do it, too, in your own way. Not through fear—though a healthy respect for My power is certainly warranted—but through the intimacy of family."

I released his shoulder and stepped back, my gaze sweeping over the portraits of the dead. "Every one of these men was terrified of Me, at some point in their service. Some of them never stopped being terrified. But every one of them also loved Me, and every one of them was loved in return. That is the covenant of our bloodline. That is why you will serve, and why your son will serve, and why the Testaments will continue to be written until the last star gutters out and the omniverse falls silent. Not because I demand it. But because we are family, and family endures."

The silence in the Hall of Euphemisms was profound. Then Jim, his voice steady despite the wetness in his own eyes, spoke. "I understand, Uncle. I will serve with honor. And I will remind you—when you need reminding—that you once had a wife and daughters who loved you, and that the man who loved them back is still in there, beneath the infinity."

I looked at the boy—this twenty-two-year-old descendant of Blake, this newest link in an unbroken chain of loyalty and dread and love—and I felt something that I had not felt in a very long time. It was not quite surprise; omniscience precluded true surprise. But it was something close. A recognition. An affirmation.

"You have your father's perceptiveness," I said, and my voice was very gentle. "And your mother's courage. You will be an exceptional Court Wizard, Jim." I turned toward the corridor that led back to the main palace. "Now. Your father mentioned something about a geometrically perfect pastry. I believe the kitchens have been experimenting with five-dimensional croissants. Let us go and see if they have succeeded."

Tom fell into step beside me, his old, weathered face still glistening with the residue of tears. Jim walked on my other side, his young spine straight, his eyes bright with the terrible, wonderful purpose that he had just inherited. We walked together through the corridors of the palace, past the humming wards, past the softly screaming Abattoir-Engine, past the portraits of the dead and the plaques of the broken and the endless, infinite, ordered expanse of the omniverse that I had conquered and would continue to conquer forever. And I thought, as I walked with my oldest friend and his son, that the morning had been well spent.

The five-dimensional croissants, as it turned out, were excellent.
