# The Journals of Blade and Bone

The reading room was silent save for the soft crackle of the arcane sconces and the whisper of my own breathing. I am Kaedan, twenty-two years of age, the newest Court Wizard of Thronegate. Three days ago, my father—his hands trembling with the same terror I now understood—had placed the iron key into my palm and directed me here, to the Vault of Witnesses. His eyes, ancient and impossibly weary despite his eighty years, had held mine with a gravity that made my stomach clench.

"You will read them all," he had said. "Every journal. Every entry. Every word written in blood and desperation by the men who came before you. When you finish, you will understand why we serve. Why we love him. And why the omniverse is permitted to exist only because he has not grown bored of its spinning."

The vault stretched before me, circular and vast, its obsidian walls lined with shelves that held thousands of leather-bound volumes. Each spine bore a name and a year, a chronicle of my bloodline's trauma stretching back a million years. I pulled the first volume from Year 42, sealed with the personal sigil of Gorm himself, and began to read.

I write this with hands that have not stopped shaking for seventeen hours. The blood has dried beneath my fingernails, though it is not my own. Godric placed his hand on my shoulder. "Wait, Gorm," he said, his voice carrying that warm, paternal tone that always made me feel safe. "I am curious about something. I have never properly tested my physical durability. Not truly."

The word "truly" should have been my warning.

He walked forward, past the defensive wards, past the Royal Guard, past me, and stood alone in the center of the courtyard. Nothing happened. Not a scratch. Not a singed hair. Not even a microscopic displacement of air molecules on the surface of his skin.

I watched as the Whispers' collective consciousness reeled in confusion. They had sacrificed nine thousand years, eight trillion warriors, and the conceptual essence of countless multiverses. They had poured the totality of their existence into this single, perfect strike. And Godric stood there, looking mildly perplexed, as if a gnat had brushed against his tunic.

"Interesting," he murmured. "I believe that was approximately zero percent of what would be required to inconvenience me. Perhaps less. Infinitely less."

Their bodies did not burn. They did not disintegrate. They were compressed, every single one of those eight trillion god-kings, into a space smaller than a pinhead. Their flesh, their bones, their divine ichor, their screaming souls—all crushed together by the sheer, unassailable fact of Godric's durability rejecting their existence. The compression was not instant. It was slow, methodical, agonizing. I could hear their collective screams, eight trillion voices layered into a single, keening wail of absolute, inescapable torment, as their bodies were ground together into a hot, viscous slurry of pulped organs and powdered bone.

The pinhead-sized sphere of their compressed remains hung in the air for a moment, radiating heat and horror, before it collapsed into a singularity so dense it tore a hole through seventeen adjacent multiverses. The collateral damage was immediate and catastrophic. Entire realities were sucked into the gravity well, their galaxies, their civilizations, their gods—all dragged screaming into an oblivion they could not possibly have anticipated.

Godric sighed. "Messy," he said, and with a thought, he restored every single annihilated multiverse. Every galaxy. Every planet. Every being. They were brought back in exactly zero seconds, their memories intact, their souls scarred permanently by the knowledge that they had been casually erased and restored by a being whose passive durability had rejected an attack with less than an infinitesimal fraction of its true capacity.

I collapsed to my knees, vomiting onto the pristine marble. Godric walked over and patted my head gently. "There, there, Gorm," he said, his voice warm and kind. "Let's get you some tea."

The door opened, and Godric stood there, his salt-and-pepper hair catching the light, his ruggedly handsome face creased with a gentle smile. He looked exactly like a beloved uncle, a protector, a friend.

"Your father made your favorite," he said. "Roast with the honey glaze."

I met his eyes—those eyes that had watched omniverses collapse, had calcified concepts, had erased civilizations, had converted trillions—and I smiled.

"Thank you, Uncle Godric," I said. "I'm coming."

The horror was absolute. The love was real. And the service would continue, as it had for a million years, as it would for a million more. Because we were the descendants of Blake. Because we were the Court Wizards. Because we had read the journals, and we understood the truth that no opposition ever could: victory was impossible, and we were the blessed few who were permitted to stand on the right side of impossibility.

I walked with him into the golden light of the corridor, leaving the shadows of the vault behind, but never truly.
