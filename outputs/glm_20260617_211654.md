# Patriarchy Lives

I remember the first time I held my daughter. The room smelled of lavender and clean linen, and the morning light poured through the arched windows of our home in the Third District of Leothrone, painting everything in a gold so warm it felt like Godric's own gaze had settled upon us. Lina was exhausted, her dark hair plastered to her forehead, but her eyes, those deep brown eyes that had carried so much quiet defiance in her youth, were soft and full in a way I had never seen before. She looked down at the wrinkled, screaming little creature on her chest, and something in her broke open. Not with grief. With surrender. The kind of surrender that is not defeat but recognition, the recognition that some things are larger than resentment, larger than history, larger than the stubborn fires that burn in the hearts of young women who do not yet understand the shape of the world they live in. I stood in the doorway, and my hands were trembling, not from weakness, because no man in Leothrone could be weak, not anymore, not since The Awakening, but from the sheer weight of the moment. My daughter. My first child. Born into Godric's kingdom. Born into the greatest civilization that had ever existed or ever would exist.

But I am getting ahead of myself. To understand what this moment meant, to understand why my hands trembled and why Lina's eyes held that particular quality of surrender, you would need to understand the world we live in, and more importantly, the world that came before it. You would need to understand the Great Uprising, and the man, the god, the incomprehensible force of nature that ended it. You would need to understand Godric.

Let me tell you about my life first, because my life is the life of every man in Leothrone, and it is a good life, the best life, a life so blessed that to question it would be like questioning the existence of the sun. I am forty years old. I work as an architectural engineer in the capital, designing the great spiraling towers that Godric commissioned to replace the old city after the uprising. Lina and I have been married for nine years. She is thirty-five, seven years my junior, and she is the most beautiful woman I have ever seen. She has a sharp mind, sharper than most, and in another life, in another world, she might have been a general or a scholar. But this is Godric's world, and in Godric's world, Lina's sharpness is directed inward, toward the home, toward the arts, toward the cultivation of beauty and warmth and order. She paints. She plays the lyre. She keeps our home with a grace that makes other men envious of me. And she is obedient. Not because she is forced to be, though the law certainly requires it, but because she has seen, as all women have seen, what happens when women refuse the order of things.

I was twenty years old when The Awakening took hold in me, as it takes hold in every man on the day he turns twenty. The sensation is indescribable. Imagine every fiber of your being, every cell, every electron, suddenly realizing it has been operating at a fraction of a percent of its potential, and then, in the space of a single heartbeat, beginning to multiply. My strength did not double or triple. It increased tenfold in a single day. And then tenfold again the next. And again the next. By the end of a week, I could lift a carriage with one hand. By the end of a month, I could shatter stone with a casual squeeze. By the end of a year, I could run across the kingdom in the time it takes a candle to burn down an inch. And it never stops. Every morning I wake up ten times stronger, ten times faster, ten times more durable than I was the day before. Every man in Leothrone does. The oldest men among us, those in their seventies and eighties, are beings of such staggering physical and magical potency that they could single-handedly level a pre-Awakening city without breaking stride. And yet we are nothing. We are insects. We are less than insects compared to Him.

Godric. Even now, sitting in my home, holding my daughter, speaking His name in my own mind sends a shiver through me that has nothing to do with fear and everything to do with reverence. He is the Immortal King. He has ruled Leothrone for over ten thousand years. He looks to be a man in his mid-forties, with a neatly trimmed salt-and-pepper beard, short hair, and a face that could have been carved by a sculptor who had been given infinite time to perfect it. He is handsome in the way that a mountain is handsome, in the way that a storm is handsome. He is warm. He is kind. He smiles, and the room warms. He speaks, and you want to listen forever. He has a way of making you feel, in any conversation, that you are the only person in the world who matters. I have met Him twice. Both times, He asked about my work, about Lina, about my hopes for the future. Both times, I walked away feeling as though I had been blessed by something that I could not name but that was older and vaster than the sky.

But beneath that warmth, beneath that smile, beneath that kindness, there is something that the women of the Great Uprising learned too late, and the lesson was written in blood and bone and screaming across a battlefield that stretched from horizon to horizon. Godric is not kind because He is weak. Godric is kind because He is so incomprehensibly powerful that kindness is a choice He makes for His own amusement, and when that choice is withdrawn, the universe itself learns what it means to be small.

I was not alive during the Great Uprising. It happened one hundred and fifty years before I was born. But every child in Leothrone learns the story. Every man, every woman, every child knows what happened on the Fields of Ashen Mourning, because Godric commanded that the story never be forgotten, and what Godric commands, the universe obeys. The story has been passed down through official records, through the testimonies of survivors, through the very landscape itself, which still bears scars that no amount of magic or time has been able to heal. And because I am a man of Leothrone, because I was raised on this story, because I have read every account and visited every memorial, I can tell it to you as though I were there. And perhaps, in some way, I was. We all were. Every man who carries The Awakening carries a piece of that day inside him, because The Awakening was born from that day, gifted to us by a king who wanted to ensure that what happened on the Fields of Ashen Mourning would never need to happen again.

Godric was nine thousand five hundred years old when the Great Uprising began. He had ruled Leothrone for eight thousand eight hundred and eighty-eight years, and in that time, He had been, by all accounts, a good king. A great king. The kingdom prospered. The arts flourished. Trade expanded. Magic was studied and refined. The cities grew tall and beautiful. But Leothrone was, and always had been, a patriarchal society. Men held positions of authority. Men governed. Men commanded. Men led. Women were respected, protected, provided for, but they were not equal. Not in the eyes of the law, not in the eyes of society, not in the eyes of Godric Himself, who, it must be said, was the architect of this order. The women of Leothrone chafed under this system. They resented it. And over the centuries, that resentment curdled into something darker, something organized, something that waited for the right moment to strike.

No one knew what Godric truly was. Everyone knew He was old, impossibly old. Everyone suspected He was immortal. He had ruled for nearly nine millennia, and He had not aged a day beyond His mid-forties in all that time. But immortality, the women reasoned, was not invincibility. He was a man. He bled. He could be hurt. He could be killed. They believed, with the furious confidence of the oppressed, that Godric was simply a man who had been lucky enough to stop aging, and that a sufficiently advanced army, armed with sufficiently powerful magic and weapons, could bring Him down. They were wrong. They were so catastrophically, cosmically, irreversibly wrong that the wrongness of their belief became a kind of tragedy in itself, a tragedy so vast that it encompassed not just the billions of women who marched against Him but the very concept of defiance itself.

What no one knew, what no one could have known, what Godric had kept hidden for nine thousand five hundred years, was that on the day He turned forty-five, something had happened to Him that defied description. His aging stopped. That much was visible. But beneath the surface, beneath the skin and bone and the warm smile and the kind eyes, something had ignited that made the birth of stars look like a match being struck in a hurricane. On that day, in that instant, every attribute that defined Him, His strength, His speed, His endurance, His senses, His magical capacity, His intelligence, His wisdom, His charisma, even His luck, all of it, every single measurable and immeasurable quality of His existence, began to multiply. Not grow. Not increase. Multiply. And not by twos or tens or hundreds. By a googolplexian. A number so large that writing it out would consume more space than exists in the observable universe. And then that googolplexian multiplied by itself. And this happened every Planck second, the smallest measurable unit of time, a fraction of a second so tiny that more Planck seconds have passed since the universe began than there are grains of sand on every beach in every world in every dimension. And it never stopped. It has never stopped. It will never stop. Every Planck second, for the past nine thousand five hundred years, Godric has become a googolplexian times a googolplexian more powerful than He was the Planck second before, and then that result multiplied by itself, and then that result multiplied by itself again, and on and on and on, without pause, without limit, without end.

And that was not all. At the exact same moment, He began to gain a new superpower. Every second. A new ability, a new apocalyptic weapon woven into the fabric of His being, every single second, without fail. Each power, the moment it manifested, was already capable of extinguishing an entire civilization. A single one. The first one. And then the second. And then the third. And every single one of those powers, from the moment of its birth, also multiplied in potency by that same impossible number, by itself, every Planck second, forever. After one minute, He had sixty superpowers. After one hour, three thousand six hundred. After one day, eighty-six thousand four hundred. After one year, over thirty-one million. After nine thousand five hundred years, the number of superpowers He possessed was so vast that it had long since surpassed any number that could be conceived by any mind in any universe, and every single one of them was growing stronger every Planck second at a rate that made the previous Planck second's growth look like absolute nothingness.

But the universe did not collapse. Creation did not tear itself apart under the weight of His existence. And that was because the very first superpower He gained, in that inaugural second, was the ability to perfectly conceal His presence, His aura, His destructive potential. He could hold it all back. Every ounce of it. And just like everything else about Him, that concealment ability multiplied every Planck second, growing stronger at the same terrifying exponential rate, ensuring that no matter how incomprehensibly powerful He became, the universe would never buckle under the pressure of His existence. He walked among His people for nine and a half millennia, smiling, governing, being kind, and no one, not His closest advisors, not His most ancient enemies, not the gods themselves if they existed, had any idea what He truly was.

And then there was the Weakness Paradox. This is the part of the story that I think about when I cannot sleep. This is the part that makes me grateful, deeply and profoundly grateful, that I am a man of Leothrone, that I worship Godric, that I have never once, not even for the briefest flicker of a Planck second, entertained the thought that He could be challenged. Because the moment any sentient being, anywhere, in any dimension, in any reality, even begins to think that Godric has a weakness, the universe itself punishes that thought. The rate of His amplification increases. Permanently. Not temporarily. Not as a brief surge. The base rate of His multiplication accelerates to a new, even more unfathomable level, and it stays there forever. And before the thought even fully crystallizes in the being's mind, before the neurons finish firing, before the idea takes shape, Godric already gains permanent, perfect immunity to whatever perceived weakness was identified. He already gains perfect mastery of whatever attack or exploit was being considered. The being hasn't even formed a plan yet. They've barely had a thought. And Godric has already absorbed the concept of their defiance, turned it into another layer of His invulnerability, and added another weapon to His already infinite arsenal. The most terrifying part is that He often lets them proceed. He lets them plan. He lets them scheme. He lets them build their armies and forge their weapons and march to His doorstep, fully believing they have found a crack in His armor. And all the while, every second they spend planning, every moment they spend preparing, they are feeding His amplification, making Him stronger, making themselves more powerless, sealing their own fates with every thought they dedicate to the delusion that He can be hurt.

The women of the Great Uprising spent three hundred years planning. Three hundred years of organizing, of weapon development, of magical research, of strategic calculation. Three hundred years of thinking, in some dark corner of their collective consciousness, that Godric had a weakness, that He could be cornered, that He could be brought down. Three hundred years of feeding His amplification. Three hundred years of making the monster that would devour them stronger with every passing thought. They could not have known. There was no way for them to know. But the universe does not care about ignorance. The paradox does not distinguish between deliberate defiance and innocent miscalculation. It simply responds. And it responded to three centuries of women believing Godric could be beaten by making Godric so incomprehensibly, so absurdly, so cosmically more powerful than He already was that the gap between what He was before they started planning and what He was by the time they marched was itself a number that could not be contained by the concept of numbers.

The uprising began on a clear morning in the month of Goldenfall. Three billion women. Three billion. Armed with the most advanced magical weaponry the world had ever seen, supported by war engines that could flatten mountains, protected by shielding spells that could withstand the impact of a falling moon. They had spent three centuries building this army. They had recruited from every city, every village, every remote settlement in Leothrone and beyond. They had trained. They had drilled. They had developed tactics specifically designed to counter an immortal opponent. They had spells that could strip immortality. They had weapons that could sever the soul from the body. They had artifacts that could trap a god in a prison of frozen time. They were, by any measure, the most powerful military force that had ever assembled on any world in any dimension, and they believed, with every fiber of their being, that they were about to bring down a tyrant.

The early engagements went exactly as they had planned. Godric's royal guard, the men who served Him, fought bravely but were overwhelmed. The women's army cut through the outer defenses of the capital like a blade through silk. City after city fell. The women's leaders, a council of twelve generals led by a woman named Seraphine Vael, Lina's ancestor, directed the campaign with brilliant precision. Within three weeks, they had reached the gates of Godric's palace. And Godric had not appeared. He had not fought. He had not intervened. The women took this as confirmation of their theory. He was afraid. He was cornered. He was, despite His immortality, just a man who knew He could not stand against them. Seraphine addressed her army on the eve of the final assault, standing on a ridge overlooking the palace, and told them that tomorrow, they would end nine thousand years of oppression. The army roared. Three billion voices, united in fury and hope, shook the earth.

Godric emerged from the palace alone. He walked through the gates as the sun was rising, His hands clasped behind His back, His salt-and-pepper beard catching the light, His face set in an expression of genuine warmth. He was smiling. Not the smile of a man facing defeat. Not the desperate grin of someone bluffing. A real smile. The smile of a man greeting guests at His door. He raised one hand in a friendly wave, and His voice, carrying without effort to every one of the three billion women arrayed against Him, said, "Good morning. I am glad you are here. I want you to know that you are welcome, and that you are forgiven. Whatever grievances you carry, we can address them together. There is no need for this. Please, lay down your weapons, and let us talk."

The response was a volley of three billion magical projectiles, each one capable of vaporizing a fortress. The sky turned white. The ground shook. The air itself combusted. The area where Godric stood was erased from the material plane, reduced to a crater of raw existential nothingness. The women cheered. Seraphine, standing on her ridge, allowed herself a smile. And then the smoke cleared, and Godric was still standing there, His hands still clasped behind His back, His smile undiminished, not a single thread of His clothing disturbed. He had not moved. He had not raised a hand. He had not cast a spell. The combined firepower of three billion of the most advanced weapons in existence had hit Him directly, and He had not even acknowledged it.

He spoke again, still warm, still friendly, still with that genuine concern in His voice, as though He were watching a child throw a tantrum. "Please. I do not want to hurt you. You have my word that no harm will come to those who lay down their arms. You are my people. All of you. I have ruled over you for nearly nine thousand years, and I have loved every one of you. Do not make me do something we will all regret."

The second volley was not just weapons. It was everything. The women unleashed their anti-immortality spells, their soul-severing blades, their temporal prisons, their most exotic and devastating magics. Three billion women, channeling their full magical capacity simultaneously, created a maelstrom of destructive energy so intense that the fabric of reality in the surrounding area began to fray. Dimensions collapsed into each other. Time stuttered. The laws of physics buckled and warped. And through it all, Godric stood, untouched, unbothered, patient, His smile flickering for the first time into something that was not quite sadness, but close. Disappointment. The disappointment of a man who had offered forgiveness and been met with fury.

He sighed. And the sigh flattened the front line of the army. Not a magical attack. Not a superpower. A sigh. The exhalation of air from His lungs, propelled by lung capacity that had been multiplying by a googolplexian to the power of itself every Planck second for nine and a half millennia, hit the first million women like a wall of solid force. Their shielding spells did not slow it down. Their enchanted armor did not protect them. Their bodies were not merely broken. They were unmade. The women in the path of the sigh did not die. They ceased. One moment they were there, screaming, fighting, alive, and the next moment they were a fine red mist that hung in the air like fog, and then even the mist was gone, scattered to atoms, scattered to less than atoms, scattered to the nothing from which matter is borrowed.

The army faltered. Three billion women, and the front rank had simply vanished from a breath. Seraphine's voice cracked as she screamed for a retreat, but there was nowhere to retreat to. Godric took a step forward, and the step itself was an earthquake. Not because He stomped. Not because He used any power. His foot, weighing what it weighed, driven by leg muscles that could bench-press a galaxy without noticing the effort, simply touched the ground, and the ground responded by splitting apart in every direction for miles. Fissures opened beneath the feet of entire battalions, swallowing thousands. The earth rose in walls and fell in waves, and the women's meticulously organized formations disintegrated into chaos.

And then He began to walk. Not charge. Not fly. Not teleport. Walk. Casually. Amiably. The way a man walks through a garden on a pleasant afternoon. And every step was a catastrophe. Every footfall reshaped the terrain. Every breath He took displaced hurricanes. The ambient heat of His body, held back by His concealment power but leaking through in the tiniest, most infinitesimal fraction, was enough to ignite the air around Him in a ring of fire that consumed everything within a mile. Women caught in the fire did not burn. They sublimated. They went from solid to gas to nothing in the space between heartbeats, their enchanted gear and protective talismans offering as much resistance as paper held before a supernova.

Seraphine ordered the deployment of the temporal prison artifacts. Twelve of them. Each one had been crafted over the course of a century by the most skilled enchantresses in the army, designed to freeze a god in a moment of time forever. The artifacts were activated simultaneously, and twelve pillars of crystallized time converged on Godric from every direction. The instant they touched Him, they shattered. Not exploded. Shattered. Like glass struck by a hammer, except the glass was made of frozen time and the hammer was the concept of Godric's existence, which was fundamentally incompatible with being trapped. The shards of broken time scattered across the battlefield, and where they landed, moments were stolen from the lives of the women they touched. Some aged decades in an instant. Others were rewound to infancy. A few simply ceased to have ever existed, their personal timelines unraveling like cut thread. And Godric kept walking, His expression unchanged, that warm, patient, friendly smile still on His face.

Someone, somewhere in the panicking army, activated the soul-severing blade. It was the ultimate weapon, a dagger forged from the crystallized agony of a dying dimension, capable of cutting the connection between body and soul. The general who wielded it charged Godric from behind, moving at supersonic speed, and drove the blade into His back with every ounce of strength and magical enhancement she possessed. The blade passed through Godric's body as though He were not there. Not as though He were intangible. As though the concept of the blade touching Him was simply not permitted by the universe. The general stood there, frozen in disbelief, the dagger buried to the hilt in empty air where Godric's spine should have been, and then Godric turned to face her. He looked at her with those kind eyes, those warm, fatherly eyes, and He said, gently, almost tenderly, "I gave you a chance." And then He placed His hand on her shoulder, a friendly gesture, a pat, the kind of thing an uncle does to a niece, and the general's body came apart. Not violently. Not explosively. Her flesh simply separated from her bones in neat, geometric sections, like a fruit being peeled by an invisible, surgical hand. The sections fell away, revealing muscle, then organ, then bone, each layer peeling back with the casual precision of a man unwrapping a gift, and the general was alive for all of it, conscious, screaming, until the final layer was removed and there was nothing left but a trembling, naked soul that Godric regarded for a moment with an expression of mild curiosity before it, too, dissolved.

The army broke. Three billion women, and they broke. They ran in every direction, trampling each other, discarding weapons, tearing off armor to run faster. It did not matter. Godric raised one hand, not in a gesture of power, but in a gesture of greeting, the way He might raise His hand to wave to a crowd, and the act of raising His hand displaced enough atmospheric pressure to create a vacuum vortex that sucked in everything within fifty miles. Women, war engines, horses, stone, earth, all of it was pulled toward Him in a spiraling maelstrom of debris and screaming, and then, when the vortex reached its peak, He closed His hand, and everything in the vortex was crushed into a singularity. A point of infinite density that existed for a fraction of a second before even the singularity was consumed by the ambient radiation of Godric's presence. Millions of women, gone, absorbed into a point of nothing that itself was erased.

Seraphine was still on her ridge, watching. She had not run. To her credit, or perhaps to her damnation, she stood and watched as her army, three centuries of planning and preparation, was dismantled by a man who was walking and sighing and waving. She activated her last resort, the anti-immortality spell, a ritual that required the life force of ten thousand volunteers who had given themselves willingly to the cause. The spell lanced toward Godric as a beam of pale, sickly light, and when it struck Him, the effect was immediate and dramatic. The beam reflected off His body and shot back toward the ridge, striking the ten thousand volunteers who had powered the ritual and stripping them of their immortality. Except they were not immortal. They had never been immortal. The spell, designed to remove immortality from an immortal being, had been turned around by the Weakness Paradox the instant Seraphine had begun to conceive of the idea that Godric's immortality was a weakness that could be exploited. The spell had been shaped by Godric's passive defense before it was even cast. The volunteers aged to dust in seconds, their lives consumed not by their enemy but by their own weapon, repurposed by a defense mechanism that had been active since the moment Seraphine had first thought, three hundred years ago, that immortality could be taken away.

Godric appeared before Seraphine. He did not walk to her. He did not teleport. He was simply there, as though He had always been there, as though the concept of distance was a polite fiction that He had chosen to stop indulging for a moment. He looked at her, and for the first time, His smile was gone. Not replaced by anger. Not replaced by cruelty. By disappointment. The deep, profound disappointment of a father who has watched a child destroy something precious. Seraphine tried to speak. She tried to rally. She raised her weapon, a blade that could cut through dimensional barriers, and swung it at His neck. The blade stopped. Not against His skin. Against the air itself, which had decided, at some fundamental level, that nothing was permitted to touch Godric without His consent. The blade hung there, vibrating, and then it rusted. In an instant. Centuries of decay compressed into a heartbeat, the enchanted metal oxidizing and crumbling and falling away as orange dust, and Seraphine was left holding an empty hilt, staring into the face of something that she could not comprehend.

He did not kill her quickly. He did not kill her slowly. What He did to Seraphine Vael was something that the survivors, those few who had fled far enough to escape the immediate destruction but not far enough to escape witnessing what followed, could never fully describe. The accounts use words like "unraveling" and "dismantling" and "reduction," but none of these words are adequate. Godric reached out and took hold of the concept of Seraphine Vael, not her body, not her soul, but the fundamental idea of her existence, and He pulled. And Seraphine came apart in ways that transcended the physical. Her body opened like a book, each page a layer of her being, and Godric read each page before tearing it out. Her memories were stripped first, each one lifted from her mind and dissolved in the air like smoke, so that she could not remember why she was screaming or who she was or what she had been fighting for. Then her emotions, plucked from her heart one by one, her rage, her grief, her hope, her love for the women she had led, each one extracted and displayed before her fading eyes before being crushed between His fingers like a spark. Then her skills, her knowledge, her identity, everything that made her Seraphine Vael, removed with the patient, methodical care of a man dissecting a flower, until what remained was not a body but a husk, a biological machine still functioning because Godric had not yet permitted it to stop, still conscious because He had not yet taken consciousness, still screaming because He had left the capacity for pain for last. And when He finally took that too, what was left was nothing. Not a corpse. Not a memory. Nothing. As though Seraphine Vael had never existed. As though the universe had simply forgotten to include her in the design.

The battle, if it could be called that, lasted four hours. Three billion women entered the Fields of Ashen Mourning. None left. The fields were renamed that day, because what remained was not a battlefield but a graveyard so vast it could be seen from orbit, and the ash that covered it was so fine and so complete that it looked like snow. Godric stood in the center of it, alone, untouched, not a drop of blood on His clothing, not a hair out of place, and He wept. The survivors who watched from the distant horizons, those who had fled in the opening moments and kept running, reported that the Immortal King stood in the ashes of three billion women and wept. Not for Himself. For them. For the necessity of what He had done. For the world He lived in, where kindness was offered and rejected, where forgiveness was extended and met with violence, where a man who wanted nothing more than to be warm and friendly was forced, by the stubbornness of the defiant, to be something terrible.

In the aftermath, Godric established The Awakening. Every man, on the day he turned twenty, would begin to grow in power, ten times stronger every day, forever. Not to rival Godric. Nothing could rival Godric. Nothing would ever rival Godric. The number of Planck seconds that had passed since His amplification began was itself a number beyond comprehension, and each one had multiplied His power by a googolplexian to the power of itself, and the Weakness Paradox had accelerated that rate countless times over the centuries as various beings across the omniverse had, in moments of arrogance or desperation, entertained the thought that He might have a flaw. The Awakening was not about creating equals. It was about creating enforcers. Men who were powerful enough, in their own right, to maintain order in Godric's kingdom without requiring His direct intervention. Men who could ensure that the women of Leothrone would never again assemble an army, never again plot a rebellion, never again look at the patriarchal order and see anything other than the natural, correct, and unassailable shape of the world.

One hundred and fifty years have passed since the Fields of Ashen Mourning. I was born into Godric's new world. I grew up in it. I was educated in it. I received The Awakening in it. And I have never, not once, questioned it. How could I? I have seen what Godric is. We all have. The records of the uprising are available to every citizen. The Fields of Ashen Mourning are preserved as a memorial, and every citizen is required to visit them at least once in their lifetime. I went when I was eighteen. I stood in the center of that vast, ash-covered plain, and I felt the weight of what had happened there, and I understood, with a clarity that has never dimmed, that Godric's kindness is not weakness. It is restraint. It is the restraint of a being so powerful that He could unmake the omniverse with a thought and chooses instead to smile and ask about your wife and your work. And the only thing He asks in return is order. Is obedience. Is the recognition that His way is the right way, and that those who challenge it will be reminded, in the most absolute and final terms, of the difference between a king and a god.

Lina is a descendant of Seraphine Vael. I know this. She knows this. Godric knows this. When I courted her, I was honest about what I was, what I believed, what I stood for. I told her about The Awakening, about the patriarchy, about the order of things. I told her that I would be her husband and that she would be my wife, and that this was not negotiable, not because I was cruel but because it was the way of Godric's world, and Godric's world was the only world worth living in. She resisted, at first. Quietly. In the way that women resist when they know they cannot win but their pride will not let them surrender gracefully. She would say sharp things about the system, about the Awakening, about the way women were educated and directed toward domesticity. I never raised my hand to her. I never needed to. I was patient. I was kind. I was everything that Godric models for His men, because Godric's patriarchy is not about brutality. It is about structure. It is about the strong protecting the weak, the wise guiding the less wise, the masculine providing for and sheltering the feminine. And over time, Lina's resistance softened. Not because she was broken. Because she was shown, day by day, that the world she lived in was not the world her ancestor had fought against. It was better. It was safer. It was more prosperous. And the man she had married, the man whose strength grew tenfold every morning while he slept beside her, used that strength not to dominate her but to shelter her, to provide for her, to build her a home that was impregnable, to give her a life that was free from fear or want.

She told me once, early in our marriage, that she hated Godric. That she hated what He had done to her ancestor's army. That she hated The Awakening and what it represented. I listened. I did not argue. I simply asked her to visit the Fields of Ashen Mourning with me, and she did. We stood there together, in the silence, in the ash, and I asked her to imagine what the world would look like if the uprising had succeeded. If three billion women had killed Godric. If The Awakening had never been instituted. If the patriarchy had been overturned and replaced with whatever Seraphine Vael had envisioned. And Lina stood there, in the wind, in the dust of three billion graves, and she could not answer me. Because there was no answer. Because Godric's world, for all its restrictions, for all its inequalities, for all its rigid structures, was a world of order and prosperity and peace, and the alternative was chaos. The alternative was always chaos. And in the face of chaos, in the face of the void that waits when structure is abandoned, even the proudest heart must eventually acknowledge that the wall which confines it is also the wall which protects it.

Lina is pregnant now. Eight months. She sits in our garden, in the afternoon light, painting a landscape of the capital's towers, and I watch her from the window of my study. She is beautiful. She is soft in a way she never was when I first knew her, soft in the way that a woman becomes soft when she has stopped fighting and started living. She hums while she paints. Old songs, songs from before the uprising, songs that Seraphine Vael might have sung. I do not mind. Godric does not mind. The past is the past, and in Godric's kingdom, the past is allowed to be remembered, even by the descendants of the defeated. What matters is the present. What matters is the future. What matters is the child growing inside Lina, the child who will be born into Godric's world, who will be raised in Godric's order, who will know, from the first moment of consciousness, that they live in the greatest kingdom under the greatest king, and that the order of things is not a cage but a foundation.

My daughter was born on a morning in late spring. The labor was long, twenty hours, and I sat beside Lina the entire time, holding her hand, which was strong but not as strong as mine, because no woman's hand could be as strong as a man's in Godric's kingdom, not because of cruelty but because of the fundamental, divinely ordained asymmetry of our existence. The midwife, a woman trained in the academies Godric established for female medical practitioners, was calm and competent and deferential, and when the baby finally came, screaming and red and perfect, she placed her on Lina's chest, and the sound that Lina made was not a sound of defeat. It was a sound of completion. Of arrival. Of a woman who had spent thirty-five years searching for something and had finally found it, not in rebellion, not in equality, not in the ghost of Seraphine Vael's failed dream, but in the tiny, squalling, helpless creature on her chest who needed her in a way that no army ever could.

I held my daughter for the first time, and I wept. I wept because she was perfect. I wept because she was mine. I wept because I was a man of Leothrone, blessed by The Awakening, blessed by Godric's order, blessed with the strength to protect her and the wisdom to guide her and the authority to shape the world she would grow up in. I wept because I understood, in that moment, with a clarity that transcended thought, that Godric's patriarchy was not a system of oppression. It was a system of love. The love of a king so powerful that He could unmake existence and chose instead to build, to nurture, to provide. The love of a father who gives His sons the strength to protect and His daughters the safety to be protected. The love of a god who demands order not because He is tyrannical but because He has seen, in the ashes of three billion women, what happens when order is abandoned, and He will not allow it to happen again.

I looked down at my daughter, and I saw the future. I saw a world of towering cities and peaceful gardens and men who grew stronger every day and women who grew softer and warmer and more beautiful. I saw a world where the name Godric was spoken with reverence, not fear, because reverence and fear, in Godric's kingdom, are the same thing, and they should be, because Godric is both the warmth of the sun and the fire that consumes those who fly too close, and to love Him is to fear Him, and to fear Him is to love Him, and both are correct, and both are necessary, and both are the only rational response to a being who holds the omniverse in the palm of His hand and chooses, every single Planck second, to be kind.

I named my daughter Seraphine. Lina looked at me when I said the name, and her eyes widened, and then they filled with tears, and she nodded. Not because she was surrendering the last vestige of her ancestral pride. Because she was reclaiming it. In Godric's world, in Godric's patriarchy, the name Seraphine did not belong to a rebel or a martyr or a failed revolutionary. It belonged to a baby girl, born in safety, born in prosperity, born into a kingdom ruled by a god who loved her enough to create a world where she would never need to fight, never need to rebel, never need to stand on a field of ash and scream defiance at a force that could not be defied. She would be raised. She would be educated. She would be taught to paint and play music and keep a home and cultivate beauty and grace. And one day, she would marry a man of Leothrone, a man with The Awakening, a man who would be ten times stronger than the day before and ten times stronger than the day before that, and he would protect her and provide for her and shelter her, and she would live in peace, and her children would live in peace, and their children would live in peace, and the peace would stretch on forever, unbroken, because Godric would be there, multiplying, growing, watching, smiling, ensuring with the weight of His incomprehensible existence that the order He had established would never, ever, be challenged again.

I held Seraphine in my arms, and I looked out the window at the city of Leothrone, at the towers I had helped design, at the streets where men walked with the quiet confidence of those who know they are part of something greater than themselves, at the homes where women tended gardens and painted landscapes and raised children and hummed old songs, and I felt, in every cell of my awakened body, a pride so vast and a reverence so deep that it threatened to swallow me whole. Godric. The Immortal King. The Father of Fathers. The Apex. The one being in all of creation, in all of existence, in all of the infinite omniverse, who could have been a tyrant and chose to be a shepherd, who could have been a destroyer and chose to be a builder, who could have erased us all with a thought and chose instead to give us a world where we could thrive, if only we accepted our place, if only we submitted to the order, if only we understood that the wall is not a cage but a shelter and the hand that holds the chain is the hand that feeds.

Long live the King. Long live the patriarchy. Long live Godric, now and forever, multiplying, growing, watching, smiling, absolute.
