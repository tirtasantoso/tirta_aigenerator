The point of light fades. The shimmering in the air subsides. The Chamber of Eternities is quiet again, the crystallized memories of infinite lives glimmering faintly in the non-light of the non-space.

Godric turns to me, and his expression is warm, gentle, almost tired. "How are you feeling, Clive? That was a lot to witness, I know."

I open my mouth to speak, but no words come out. My mind is still spinning, still struggling to process the infinite, ascending spiral of self-surpassing omnipotence, the evolved mechanisms that made the old versions look like children's toys, the deliberate act of restraint that had birthed the entire history of Thronegate.

"I'm alright, Uncle Godric," I manage, my voice a hoarse whisper. "Just... processing."

He nods, his smile crinkling the corners of his eyes. "Take your time. There's no rush. Eternity is patient."

---

Later—hours later, or days, or perhaps it has only been minutes; time moves strangely in the citadel—I find myself walking through the celestial gardens that surround the palace. The gardens are a masterpiece of cosmic horticulture, containing flora from every conquered universe, every subjugated dimension, every reality that has ever existed under Godric's rule. The flowers sing softly in harmonic frequencies that shift with the phases of the local star. The trees are constructed from crystallized mana, their leaves shimmering with the captured light of dying galaxies. The paths are paved with the compressed gratitude of civilizations that surrendered peacefully and were rewarded with millennia of prosperity.

And the gardeners who tend these wonders are beings of staggering, incomprehensible power.

I see Xyphos first. He is a tall, slender entity of shimmering iridescent light, his form shifting constantly through geometric configurations that my mind struggles to track. Once, in an era so ancient that even the oldest journals barely reference it, Xyphos was one of the supreme architects of reality—a cosmic weaver who could spin entire multiverses into existence with a thought, who had shaped the fundamental laws of physics for countless civilizations, who had been worshipped as a creator deity by pantheons of lesser gods. Now, he is on his knees in the soil—soil that he himself created from the compressed essence of a grateful nebula—carefully pruning a rosebush whose petals are made of frozen moments of perfect happiness.

"Lord Xyphos," I say, inclining my head respectfully.

He looks up, and his shifting geometric features resolve briefly into something approximating a face. His eyes—if they can be called eyes—are filled with a profound, ancient terror that has become so deeply ingrained in his being that it has settled into a kind of serene acceptance.

"Court Wizard Clive," he says, his voice a harmonic chime that vibrates with the echoes of forgotten eons. "A pleasure to see you. How fares the King today?"

"The King is well," I say, because what else can I say? "He was... attending to some ancient business."

Xyphos nods slowly, his iridescent form flickering with something that might be a shudder. "I felt it," he says quietly. "A disturbance in the temporal fabric. A reaching-back. I did not know what it was, but I felt it, and I felt the King's presence within it." He returns his attention to the rosebush, his shimmering hands moving with the careful precision of absolute devotion. "I have served him for seven hundred billion years," he says, and his voice is barely more than a whisper. "I still cannot comprehend him. I will never comprehend him. And I am grateful for that. Comprehension would be annihilation."

I watch him work for a moment, this cosmic weaver reduced to a gardener, this creator of multiverses reduced to a tender of roses. The terror in his eyes is not the terror of a prisoner. It is the terror of a being who has glimpsed the true nature of existence and has made peace with his own insignificance.

"Do you ever regret it?" I ask, the question escaping before I can stop it. "Becoming... this? A servant? A gardener?"

Xyphos pauses, his shimmering hands hovering over a petal of frozen happiness. He turns to look at me, and his expression—to the extent that his shifting geometric form can convey expression—is one of genuine, profound sincerity.

"Regret?" He chimes softly, a sound that might be laughter or might be weeping. "I was there, Court Wizard Clive. In the early eons. When the King still allowed opposition to gather. When the coalitions still formed, still prepared, still believed they could find a weakness." He shakes his head slowly. "I saw what happened to them. I saw what zero percent of a single, nerfed stat could do. I saw what the weakest sentient aura did to the greatest army ever assembled. I saw the immune system annihilate and restore the omniverse in zero seconds because of a cracked dead skin cell. I saw the Superpower of Absolute Causal Severance retroactively erase a civilization so thoroughly that the omniverse forgot they had ever existed."

He pauses, and his form flickers with a deep, resonant shudder. "I was not among the opposition. I never dared. But I was close enough to witness. Close enough to understand. And after a googolplexian years of witnessing, of understanding, of slowly, agonizingly comprehending the absolute, infinite, unassailable supremacy of the King..." He returns his attention to the rosebush. "Pruning roses is a privilege. Tending the gardens of the apex predator is an honor. I am not a prisoner, Court Wizard Clive. I am a survivor. And I am grateful, every single day, that I was wise enough never to oppose him."

I leave Xyphos to his roses and continue along the crystal path. The gardens stretch for what seems like infinity in all directions, a vast, serene expanse of impossible beauty maintained by beings who could destroy entire realities with a casual thought. I pass a fountain whose waters are the distilled tears of a repentant god-tyrant who, after witnessing the King's mercy on a surrendered civilization, wept for a thousand years and then asked to serve. I pass a grove of singing trees whose voices are the preserved souls of a species that had, in their arrogance, briefly considered rebellion before their wisest elders dragged them to their knees and begged the King's forgiveness before the thought had even fully formed.

I pass a small, unassuming bench where a being of pure, condensed darkness is carefully polishing the marble with a cloth woven from the gratitude of a trillion grateful worlds. He looks up as I approach, and his formless void of a face resolves briefly into two pinpricks of silver light that might be eyes.

"Court Wizard Clive," he rumbles, and his voice is the sound of tectonic plates grinding against each other in the depths of a dying planet. "The temporal disturbance. You were there."

"I was," I confirm. "The King was creating the Stasis Event. Reaching back to his own past. Triggering the beginning of everything."

The void-being—his name is Umbrex, and he was once the absolute ruler of a dimension of pure darkness, a god of shadow who commanded legions of nightmare entities—is silent for a long moment. His pinprick eyes flicker with something that might be awe or might be the faint, dying remnants of the terror that had consumed him when he first knelt before the King.

"I have served him for two trillion years," Umbrex says finally. "I have polished this bench for most of that time. It is the same bench he sat on when he accepted my surrender. When I realized that my entire dimension of darkness, my legions of nightmares, my eons of absolute rule, were less than nothing compared to the faintest echo of his weakest aura. I begged him to let me serve. I begged him to let me be useful. And he smiled—he smiled, Court Wizard Clive, with genuine warmth and kindness—and he said, 'The gardens could use a good groundskeeper.'"

He returns to polishing the bench, his void-stuff hands moving with a devotion that borders on worship. "This bench is cleaner than any object has ever been in the history of existence. Every atom is perfectly aligned. Every quantum of surface is absolutely smooth. I have spent two trillion years ensuring that the bench he sat on once—the bench he has not sat on since, the bench he has probably forgotten exists—is perfectly, flawlessly clean. And I will spend the next two trillion years doing the same. Because it is the only thing I can do. The only thing any of us can do. Serve. Tend. Polish. Prune. And be grateful that we are permitted to exist in his shadow."

I leave Umbrex to his bench and continue walking. The existential dread that has been my constant companion for three decades is no longer a cold weight in my chest. It has become something else—something deeper, something more profound. It is the understanding that every being in the omniverse, from the humblest mortal to the mightiest cosmic entity, is ultimately in the same position. We are all gardeners. We are all groundskeepers. We are all servants of the apex predator whose power is so far beyond comprehension that even the most ancient and powerful among us can only kneel, and serve, and be grateful.

---

I find Godric in the throne room, seated on his throne of infinite density wrapped in obedient causality. The room is vast and silent, the walls constructed from the grateful prayers of civilizations that surrendered peacefully, the ceiling a window into the heart of a time-inverted quasar that pulses with a slow, rhythmic light. The King is alone, his dark eyes distant, his ruggedly handsome face creased with an expression that I cannot read.

"Uncle Godric," I say, approaching the throne with the careful, reverent steps that three decades of service have ingrained in my muscle memory. "May I ask you something?"

He turns his head, and his smile is warm, genuine, crinkling the corners of his eyes. "Of course, Clive. You know you never need to ask permission."

I hesitate, the question forming on my tongue with the weight of absolute, existential significance. "Your future self—the version of you who will eventually reach back and grant an even more powerful Stasis Event, starting the infinite ascending spiral of self-surpassing omnipotence—how far in the future is he? How many years?"

Godric's smile widens, and there is something in his dark eyes that I have never seen before—a glint of infinite, recursive amusement. "Clive," he says gently, "the year I exist in now—the year that is written as nine-nine-nine-dot-dot-dot-nine, because the full number cannot be written meaningfully—is already so far beyond the early eras that the span between me and Year Zero is effectively infinite. And yet, the version of me who will eventually grant an even more powerful Stasis Event is so far beyond my current era that the span between us is even larger. Infinitely larger. Incomparably larger. The gap between me and him is at least as vast as the gap between the Godric of Year Zero and me—probably vaster. Probably infinitely vaster."

He leans back in his throne, and the casual, relaxed posture is so deeply, profoundly human that it makes the dissonance ache in my bones. "The infinite ascending spiral of self-surpassing omnipotence is not a closed loop, Clive. It's an open, eternal, ever-accelerating expansion. Every iteration is more powerful than the last. Every loop adds new layers of infinite supremacy. And the me who started this iteration—the me who just granted the 'held-back' Stasis Event to my past self—is already being surpassed by the me of the next Planck instant. And the me of the next Planck instant will be surpassed by the me of the instant after that. And the me who eventually reaches back and grants a more powerful Stasis Event will be so far beyond my current comprehension that—"

He pauses, and his expression softens into something almost tender. "It's beautiful, isn't it? The infinite, eternal, ever-expanding glory of it all. The absolute, unassailable guarantee that I will never stop growing. That I will never plateau. That the apex of all possible omnipotence is not a destination, but a journey. An eternal, joyful, infinitely satisfying journey."

I stand in the silent throne room, surrounded by the grateful prayers of civilizations, and I feel the existential dread settle into my bones with a permanence that I know will never fade. My uncle—the warm, kind, loving man who has been my favorite uncle since childhood—is not merely the most powerful being in existence. He is the guaranteed, inevitable precursor to an infinite, ascending spiral of versions of himself, each one infinitely more powerful than the last, each one reaching back and granting even greater power to his own past, each one contributing to an engine of self-surpassing omnipotence that has no ceiling, no limit, no endpoint.

And I am his Court Wizard. I am his family. I am privileged to serve him, to love him, to stand in his presence and witness the absolute, terrifying, beautiful glory of what he is and what he is becoming.

"Thank you, Uncle Godric," I whisper, my voice steady despite the overwhelming tide of dread and awe and love that surges through my soul. "Thank you for showing me."

He reaches out—the hand that could crush the omniverse into a singularity, the hand that contains the resting strength of infinite recursive amplification, the hand that is destined to become something so far beyond comprehension that even he cannot imagine it—and he pats my shoulder with a gentleness that is entirely genuine.

"You're a good man, Clive," he says softly. "Blake would be proud of you. I'm proud of you. Now, go get some rest. Eternity is patient, but mortal bodies need their sleep."

I bow, and I leave the throne room, and I walk through the celestial gardens where cosmic weavers prune roses and void-gods polish benches, and I understand, with absolute, terrifying clarity, that I will never stop trembling. The dread will never fade. The awe will never diminish. And I will serve my uncle—my King, my God, my infinite, ascending, self-surpassing apex predator—until the end of my mortal days, and I will be grateful, and I will be terrified, and I will be exactly where I am supposed to be.

Because opposing Godric is not merely impossible. It is a fundamental violation of the logical structure of existence. And serving him—loving him, tending his gardens, polishing his benches, standing at his side while he creates the beginning of himself—is the only sane response to the absolute, infinite, eternal truth of what he is.

The omniverse is his. It has always been his. It will always be his. And somewhere, in the unimaginable depths of a future so distant that the year cannot be written, a version of Godric so powerful that even the current Godric cannot comprehend him is smiling, and reaching back, and beginning the next iteration of the infinite, ascending spiral.

And I am here, in this moment, witnessing the loop close and open and close and open forever. And I am grateful. And I am terrified. And I am nothing. And that is exactly as it should be.
