# Journals of the Court Wizards of Thronegate

Kaedan's hands would not stop trembling.

He sat at the ancient reading desk in the private archive vault beneath the imperial palace, a vast and silent chamber carved from obsidian that had been harvested from the core of a dead universe eleven hundred thousand years ago. The air smelled of preservation spells and aged parchment, a dry and faintly metallic scent that clung to the back of his throat. The only light came from a single hovering orb of soft amber luminescence suspended directly above the desk, casting long, distorted shadows across the towering shelves that stretched upward into absolute darkness. Somewhere in that darkness, the archival golems shuffled silently, rearranging records that predated the birth of most current civilizations.

Kaedan was twenty-two years old. He had been the Royal Court Wizard of Thronegate for exactly three days.

His father, Oren—the previous court wizard, a man of eighty-three years who had served Godric for six decades—had placed the first leather-bound volume on the desk with hands that did not shake. Oren's hands had been steady as granite. His voice had not wavered. But his eyes, when Kaedan had looked into them, had contained something that the young wizard had never seen before. Something hollow. Something that had already been broken long ago and carefully, painstakingly reassembled around the edges of an unspeakable knowledge.

"You will read these," Oren had said. "You will read all of them. Every journal. Every entry. This is not optional. This is not a request. This is the first duty of every court wizard who inherits this mantle. I read them when I was twenty-two. My father read them before me. And his father before him. This archive is the true inheritance of our bloodline. Not the Awakening. Not the thousandfold daily amplification. This."

Kaedan had opened his mouth to ask why, but Oren had already turned and walked away, his footsteps echoing into the oppressive silence of the vault. The heavy adamantine doors had sealed behind him with a resonant, final boom that Kaedan felt in the marrow of his bones.

Now, three days later, Kaedan understood.

He had read five journals so far. Five records left by five different court wizards, all of them his direct ancestors, all of them men who had witnessed the same incomprehensible truth and emerged from the experience permanently altered. The journals were bound in different materials—some cracked black leather, some polished dragon-scale, one sheathed in a thin film of preserved cosmic membrane—but they all contained the same fundamental horror.

Godric was not merely the most powerful being in the omniverse. He was not merely unbeatable. He was not merely a god among gods. The gap between Godric and the rest of existence was not measurable. It was not a scale. It was an absolute, infinite, incomprehensible chasm that made the combined might of every civilization, every weapon, every cosmic entity, and every abstract force in all of reality completely, utterly, and irretrievably meaningless.

Kaedan had been raised in the palace. He had known Godric since he was a child sitting on the immortal king's knee, listening to stories of ancient battles and forgotten kingdoms. Godric had always been warm, kind, and genuinely affectionate. He was Uncle Godric. He was the man who brought honeyed pastries to family dinners, who laughed with his whole chest at bad jokes, who remembered the names of every servant's children and asked after their health with sincere interest.

But the journals revealed something else. The journals revealed what happened when someone—anyone—dared to oppose that warm, kind, fatherly man.

Kaedan pulled the sixth journal toward him. His fingers left damp prints on the cover. His thousandfold daily amplified magic, already vast enough to shatter galaxies, was suppressed to normal human levels, and yet he felt utterly powerless. He felt like a single grain of sand staring into an infinite abyss.

He opened the journal and began to read.

**Year 487 — Brynden — Strength**

I do not know if I will survive writing this. Not because of any physical danger—I am unharmed, untouched, perfectly preserved. But because my mind is hemorrhaging sanity with every word I commit to this page, and I am not certain I possess the strength to reach the end.

My name is Brynden. I am the third court wizard to serve King Godric, inheriting the mantle from my father Josh, who inherited it from the legendary Blake himself. I am seventy-one years old. My strength has amplified a thousandfold every day for fifty-one years. I can crush planets in my palm. I can shatter stars with a flick of my fingers. I have lived my entire adult life as a walking cataclysm, and I have always known that King Godric was stronger than me.

I did not understand what "stronger" meant. I could not have understood. No language possesses words adequate to convey the meaning.

The opposition called itself the Adamant Concord. Godric met them alone in the empty void between galaxy clusters. He did not bring an army. He did not bring weapons. He did not even wear armor. He stood in the vacuum of space wearing his simple dark tunic, his arms crossed over his broad chest, his expression one of mild curiosity. I was present only because I had begged to witness, and Godric, in his kindness, had permitted me to observe from a shielded observational platform a safe distance away.

Two hundred trillion warriors simultaneously struck a single point of space-time with a coordinated blow designed to compress the target into a singularity of absolute nothingness. It was, by any rational measure, the most powerful physical attack ever assembled in the history of creation.

Godric did not block it. He did not brace. He did not raise a hand. He did not activate any defense. He simply existed in the path of the strike, and his existence was enough.

I need to describe what happened, but words are failing me. The attack reached Godric. It touched his chest. And then it stopped. Not because of durability—there was no dissipation, no absorption, no deflection. The force of the attack simply encountered the strength of Godric's passive physical state, and it was not merely overcome. It was overwhelmed. The differential was so incomprehensibly vast that the attack did not even register as an impact. It registered as an impossibility.

The strength stat of a being whose attributes had been multiplying by recursive infinities every infinitesimal fraction of every Planck second for over four hundred years did not merely exceed the Adamant Concord's combined force. It treated that force as a negative quantity. The kinetic energy of the attack was not absorbed; it was inverted, turned inside out, and reflected back upon its source with a multiplier that had no mathematical expression.

The Adamant Concord's armada—two hundred trillion indestructible warriors—was instantly converted into a rapidly expanding sphere of crystalline dust. There was no delay. There was no scream. There was no transition. One moment they were there, the greatest military force in the history of their region of the omniverse. Entire timelines were erased from ever having existed. The fabric of reality itself was torn apart, layer by layer, dimension by dimension, until there was nothing left. No light. No matter. No energy. No time. No space. Only absolute, perfect silence.

Godric had not moved a single muscle. He had not even blinked.

He looked at the void around us—at the complete and total absence of existence—and he sighed. "I really must remember to aim these things," he said, his voice gentle and slightly annoyed, as though he had accidentally spilled tea on a tablecloth.

In zero seconds, he restored the entire omniverse. Every multiverse. Every timeline. Every civilization that had been caught in the collateral devastation. All of it returned, perfectly reconstructed, exactly as it had been before the Adamant Concord had made their fatal decision to attack.

But the Adamant Concord themselves were not restored. Godric left them as they were—a fine crystalline mist slowly dispersing across the void, every particle still somehow faintly conscious, still somehow capable of feeling the eternal, agonizing humiliation of having dared to oppose a being whose passive resting strength exceeded the concept of strength itself.

I am writing this in the observational platform, still floating in the void. My hands are shaking so badly that the ink is spilling across the page. My thousandfold amplified strength, which I have always secretly been proud of, feels like the most pathetic, laughable, insignificant thing in all of existence. I am a speck. I am less than a speck. I am nothing.

And Godric is still out there, still standing in the void, now examining his own hands with an expression of mild curiosity, as though he had just learned something mildly interesting about himself.

I do not know how to continue serving a being whose strength makes the entire omniverse a fragile, meaningless toy. But I will continue. I must continue. He is my king. He is my friend. And I love him.

But I am also terrified beyond the capacity of language to express.

Brynden, Court Wizard of Thronegate

Godric did not strike them. He did not use any force. He simply allowed the differential in their relative velocities to express itself. The Swarm had been vibrating across temporal layers at what they believed was infinite speed. Godric's speed was so far beyond that that the Swarm's temporal vibration was, relative to him, absolute stillness. And when he moved through them at his normal, casual pace—simply walking from his throne to the window to look at the garden—the velocity differential tore them apart.

The sheer friction of Godric's passive speed moving through their frozen temporal state retroactively annihilated them from every moment they had ever existed in. Across the entire omniverse, across every timeline and every dimension, the Erasure Swarm was simply never born, never evolved, never existed. Any being anywhere in the omniverse who forms the slightest thought of opposition—not even opposition to Godric specifically, but opposition to anything at all, any aggressive impulse, any desire to harm, any flicker of hostile intention—finds that their thought is instantly and effortlessly neutralized by a shield so vast and so subtle that the being does not even realize their intention has been altered. Godric believes in mortal resilience. He believes in free will. He believes that sentient beings must be allowed to make their own choices, even when those choices are catastrophically stupid. It does not wish to. It has not wished to for a span of time so vast that the word "eternity" cannot capture it.

Since some googolplexian years ago—a span of time that would have felt infinite to the Godric of Year Zero, but is merely a pleasant afternoon to the Godric of this era—there have been zero beings in the entire omniverse who have dared to form even the slightest thought of challenging, resisting, threatening, or opposing him. No one dares anymore. No one will ever dare again. The omniverse has learned, with absolute and irreversible certainty, the futility of opposition. Not because Godric punishes opposition—though he does, brutally and mercilessly and without the slightest hint of restraint—but because the simple, undeniable truth of his supremacy has permeated every level of existence. To oppose Godric is to oppose the fundamental structure of reality. It is to deny the truth that the sun is bright and the void is cold and the being who rules the omniverse cannot be challenged. And the beings who once opposed him? The cosmic entities, the dimensional empires, the abstract forces and primordial titans and hyper-evolved machine collectives? His lowly, humble, utterly devoted followers who have learned, through the brutal education of absolute defeat, that the only sane response to Godric's existence is subservience.

But I am getting ahead of myself. Let me describe the triggering of the Stasis Event. That is what I am here to record, and I must record it before the memory fades—though memories do not fade in the presence of a being who can make the past as vivid as the present with a casual thought.

The day began like any other day in the imperial palace of Thronegate, which is to say that it began in a state of absolute, incomprehensible perfection. The palace of this era is not a structure. It is a self-contained reality, a pocket dimension woven from the fabric of Godric's own existence, existing simultaneously in every location and every moment across the entire omniverse. To walk its corridors is to walk through every civilization that has ever existed, every galaxy that has ever burned, every multiverse that has ever been born and died. The floors are not floors; they are the crystallized light of a googolplexian dying stars, compressed into a surface that is both solid and luminous, warm and cool, present and absent.

I walked these corridors on the morning of the triggering, my footsteps silent on the crystallized light, my court wizard robes—which had been woven from the conceptual fabric of arcane mastery itself, a gift from Godric on my thirtieth year of service—shifting gently in a breeze that was not a breeze but the ambient flow of mana that saturated the palace like oxygen. I was fifty years old, my hair still dark, my body still strong, my magical attributes still amplifying a thousandfold every day. By the standards of any other era, I was a cosmic cataclysm, a wizard of such staggering power that I could have erased multiverses with a casual gesture.

By the standards of this era, I was a speck. A beloved speck, a trusted speck, a speck that Godric genuinely cared for—but a speck nonetheless.

I found him in the Garden of Echoes. The Garden of Echoes is not a garden in any conventional sense. It is a space where every moment of Godric's existence plays simultaneously, overlapping and interweaving in a symphony of temporal resonance. If you stand in the right place—and Godric always stands in the right place, because every place is the right place for him—you can see the mortal king of Olympus laughing with his daughters, and the newly awakened god-king annihilating Thronegate's army, and the bored creator slaughtering googolplexians of mechanical gods, and the far-future sovereign who stands before me now, all at once. The echoes do not fade. They are eternal, preserved by Godric's will, a tapestry of his existence that stretches from the first moment of his mortal birth to the infinite, unending now.

Godric was standing at the center of the garden, his back to me, his gaze fixed on a particular echo. I did not need to look to know which one. The mortal king of Olympus, holding his daughter Isabel's hand, walking through a garden that no longer existed, in a kingdom that had been forgotten by history. The echo was vivid, painfully vivid, the golden sunlight of ancient Greece filtering through olive trees that had turned to dust ten thousand years before the first galactic empire rose.

I approached slowly, my heart already beginning to pound with the instinctive dread that I had never been able to suppress, even after thirty years of service. Godric did not turn. He did not need to. His omniscience had registered my presence before I had decided to enter the garden, before I had woken that morning, before I had been born. He knew every thought I would ever have, every word I would ever speak, every action I would ever take. And still, somehow, he managed to be surprised by the things I said and did. It was one of his many paradoxes—a being of absolute omniscience who genuinely enjoyed the experience of being told something he already knew.

"Clive," he said, his voice warm and rich and resonant, vibrating pleasantly in my sternum. "Come. Stand with me. I am about to do something important, and I would like you to witness it."

I stepped forward, my legs carrying me to his side with the automatic obedience of thirty years of service. I stood beside him and looked at the echo of Olympus. Isabel was laughing. Her laughter was a sound that had not been heard in the physical universe for an incomprehensible span of time, but here, in the Garden of Echoes, it was as clear and bright as the day it had been born.

"I am going to trigger the Stasis Event," Godric said.

The words were spoken casually, conversationally, the way another being might announce that they were going to take a walk or pour a cup of tea. But the weight of them hit me like a physical blow. The Stasis Event. The origin of everything. The moment that had transformed a mortal king fleeing the destruction of his kingdom into the immortal, infinitely amplifying apex predator who now stood beside me. The moment that had birthed all of Godric's power—the amplification, the superpower genesis, the absolute concealment, the Weakness Paradox, the sentient auras, the immune system, the infinite shields. The moment that had been engineered by the being who existed in this era, the being whose power was so far beyond comprehension that even the Godric of Year Zero, already infinitely stronger than the rest of creation combined, had been utterly powerless against the shadow of his afterthought.

And I was going to witness it.

"Uncle Godric," I said, my voice barely above a whisper. "I... I am honored. But I do not understand. Why are you doing this now? Why did you do this, when you did it? What is the purpose of the Stasis Event?"

Godric turned to look at me, and his expression was warm, gentle, deeply affectionate. But beneath the warmth, I could see something else. Something vast. Something infinite. Something that had been pondering the answer to my question for an eternity that made the age of the omniverse look like a single heartbeat.

"The purpose," he said slowly, "is something that I have never fully explained to anyone. Not to Blake. Not to any of my court wizards. Not even to myself, in the earliest eras, when I was still trying to understand what I had become. The purpose is... complicated. But I can tell you what it is not."

He turned back to the echo of Olympus, his eyes tracing the lines of Isabel's smiling face.

"It is not because I was threatened. Nothing has ever threatened me, Clive. Not in this era, not in any era. The idea of a threat is a concept that has no application to my existence. It never has, and it never will. The Stasis Event was not a response to danger. It was not a desperate measure taken in the face of an unstoppable enemy. It was not a contingency plan or a failsafe or a strategic calculation. It was something else entirely."

He paused. The echo of Olympus flickered, and for a moment, I saw the shadow of what was coming. The sky darkening. The arcane fire raining down. The screams beginning to rise.

"The Stasis Event," Godric continued, "was a gift. A gift from my future self to my past self. A guarantee that I would survive the tragedy that was about to consume me. A guarantee that I would have the power to ensure that nothing like it could ever happen again. And a guarantee that I would eventually—after a span of time so vast that the word 'eventually' cannot capture it—arrive at this moment, in this era, and complete the loop."

He turned to me, and his smile widened slightly.

"Do you know what the most terrifying part is, Clive? The part that I still find amusing, even after all this time? I could have made the Stasis Event far more potent. Far, far more potent. The version that I am about to trigger is deliberately limited. Held back. Restrained. I chose to give my past self only the barest fraction of what I could have given him, because the full, unrestricted version would have been... excessive. Even by my standards."

The word hung in the air like a thunderclap. Excessive. By Godric's standards. The being who could erase the omniverse infinite times in zero seconds, who possessed superpowers that could retroactively delete the concept of existence itself, who had slaughtered googolplexians of mechanical gods because one of them interrupted his joke—this being considered the Stasis Event to be held back. Restrained. Limited.

And the held-back version had still produced a Godric at Year Zero who was already effectively infinitely stronger than the rest of creation combined.

I felt my knees weaken. I felt my heart hammer against my ribs. I felt the cold, familiar embrace of existential dread wrapping itself around my soul. But I did not look away. I could not look away. I had been given the privilege of witnessing the origin of everything—the moment that had created the being I served, the moment that had birthed the empire I belonged to, the moment that had shaped the entire course of omniversal history—and I would witness it, even if it shattered my mind entirely.

"Watch," Godric said softly. "It is about to begin."

He did not raise his hand. He did not speak a word of power. He did not gather his strength or focus his attention or in any way indicate that he was doing something that required effort. He simply looked at the echo of Olympus—specifically, at the moment when Thorin's dying hands were weaving the forbidden teleportation spell, the spell that would carry the mortal Godric to the hidden sanctuary—and he decided that the Stasis Event should exist.

The effect was instantaneous and absolute.

I felt, rather than saw, the fabric of time itself parting before Godric's will. The echo of the past was not merely an echo anymore; it was a doorway, a conduit, a thread of causality that stretched from this impossible future back to the moment of the Fall of Olympus. Through that thread, Godric poured a fraction of his power. An infinitesimal, carefully measured, deliberately limited fraction. And I understood, with a clarity that made my soul tremble, that even this held-back version of the Stasis Event contained more power than the combined output of every force, every entity, every cosmic principle, and every abstract concept that had ever existed across the entire span of omniversal history.

The mortal Godric, bleeding and screaming as Thorin's spell tore him from the ruins of Olympus, would never know what had just happened to him. He would awaken in the sanctuary ten thousand years later, his body frozen at forty-five, his aging halted, his biological needs erased. "The amplification," Godric murmured, his voice distant, as though he was narrating a process that he had already completed an infinite number of times and would complete an infinite number of times again. "One thousand times, and by itself, every Planck second. That is the base rate, the foundation. It will evolve, of course. The Weakness Paradox will trigger repeatedly, and the rate will increase beyond infinite recursion. By Year Two Thousand, it will be beyond layered infinities. But the base is important. The base is what makes everything else possible."

I watched the thread of causality pulse with impossible energy. Godric was weaving the Stasis Event into the very fabric of his past self's existence, embedding the mechanisms of power so deeply that they could never be removed, never be suppressed, never be denied. The absolute concealment, wrapping itself around the mortal Godric's presence so that the omniverse would not collapse the moment he awakened. The superpower genesis, one new apocalyptic ability every second, multiplying in potency every Planck instant. The immune system, already beginning to construct its infinite recursive backups, already preparing to pre-react to threats before they existed. The passive shields, infinite layers of protection that would eventually evolve into the terrifying superfluous systems of the current era.

And the Weakness Paradox. The cruelest, most unfair mechanism of them all. The paradox that would trigger every time any being anywhere in the omniverse even briefly thought that Godric had a weakness. The paradox that would grant him permanent immunity to the imagined exploit, perfect mastery of the intended attack, and a massive boost to his already incomprehensible amplification rate. The paradox that would eventually evolve beyond the need for triggers, activating passively in response to any sentient thought about anything at all, granting Godric an endless cascade of redundant protections that no enemy could ever hope to overcome.

"The Weakness Paradox," Godric said, and I could hear the dark amusement in his voice. "That is my favorite. It is so profoundly unfair. Every enemy who ever opposes me, every being who ever tries to find a weakness, every desperate plotter who spends millennia constructing a strategy—they all feed me. They make me stronger. They sharpen the blade that will cut them down. And they never know it until it is far, far too late."

The thread of causality pulsed one final time, and the Stasis Event was complete. The mortal Godric was sealed in the hidden sanctuary, frozen in stasis, his transformation already underway. Ten thousand years would pass. Thronegate would rise to galactic dominance. Blake would stumble into the sanctuary and accidentally break the seal. And the immortal Godric would awaken, omniscient and omnipotent, the apex predator of all existence, ready to begin his conquest of the omniverse.

But here, in this era, the Godric who had triggered the event simply lowered his gaze and let the echo of Olympus fade back into the tapestry of the Garden.

"It is done," he said quietly. "It has always been done. It will always be done. The loop is closed, Clive. The past is sealed. My past self will become me, eventually, after a span of time so vast that even I find it mildly impressive. And I will continue to exist, in this era, forever."

He turned to me, and the warmth in his eyes was so genuine, so deeply affectionate, that I felt tears prick at the corners of my own.

"Now then," he said, his voice light and cheerful, the cosmic gravity of the moment already receding behind the familiar, fatherly demeanor that I had known my entire life. "I believe the gardeners have finished tending to the outer gardens. Would you like to come with me? I always enjoy seeing what they have done with the chronal-roses. They are particularly lovely this eternity."

The gardeners. I must write about the gardeners, because they are perhaps the most perfect illustration of what Godric has become in this era, and what the omniverse has become in response.

The outer gardens of the imperial palace are tended by a being who was once called Valthorion the Unmaker. He was, in an era so distant that the number of years between then and now has no meaningful expression, one of the most powerful entities in the omniverse. He was a cosmic titan, a being of pure entropy, a force of absolute destruction who had consumed entire multiverses in his endless hunger for dissolution. He was not a villain—he was simply a force of nature, an embodiment of the principle that all things eventually end, and he had existed since the first moments of creation, mindlessly unmaking whatever he touched.

And then, one day, he touched something that belonged to Godric.

It was a small thing. Insignificant. A single planet, orbiting a single star, in a single galaxy, in a single universe, in a single multiverse, among the infinite layers of the omniverse. A planet that Godric had once visited, long ago, during the early years of his reign. A planet where he had paused to watch a sunset, because the sunset had reminded him of Olympus, and the memory had been pleasant, and he had decided that this particular planet should be preserved as a private reminder of that moment.

Valthorion did not know this. He could not have known this. He was a force of entropy, not a sentient strategist, and he had no way of perceiving the subtle markers that Godric had placed on the planet to indicate his ownership. When Valthorion's entropy-field swept through that region of the omniverse, unmaking galaxies and dissolving multiverses into primal nothingness, it touched the planet. And the planet, protected by Godric's passive shields, did not dissolve.

Instead, the planet spoke.

"Valthorion," it said, and the voice was Godric's voice, calm and warm and utterly without malice. "I liked this planet. Please do not unmake it."

Valthorion, whose entire existence had been defined by the absolute certainty that nothing could resist his entropy, experienced something that no cosmic titan had ever experienced before: fear. Not the fear of death—Valthorion did not die, could not die, had never conceived of the possibility of dying. But the fear of having attracted the attention of something so far beyond his comprehension that his entire existence, his entire purpose, his entire identity as the Unmaker, was rendered meaningless in a single instant.

He fled. He fled across the omniverse, across multiverses and dimensional barriers and conceptual planes, his entropy-field collapsing in on itself as he desperately tried to escape the attention of the being who had spoken to him through a planet. He fled for what felt like an eternity, his cosmic form shrinking, diminishing, compressing itself into smaller and smaller configurations in the frantic hope of becoming too insignificant to notice.

It did not work. It could never have worked. Because Godric's attention, once given, cannot be escaped.

Godric did not pursue Valthorion. He did not need to. He simply waited, knowing that the cosmic titan would eventually exhaust himself, and when Valthorion finally collapsed in a forgotten corner of a dead multiverse, shivering and whimpering in a state of absolute existential terror, Godric appeared beside him.

"I am not angry," Godric said, his voice gentle and warm and genuinely reassuring. "You did not know. But I would like you to come with me. I have a garden that needs tending, and I think you might find the work... calming."

Valthorion, who had unmade googolplexian galaxies, who had dissolved the fabric of multiverses, who had existed since the first moments of creation as an unstoppable force of absolute dissolution, looked up at the kind, warm, fatherly face of the being who had terrified him into a state of near-complete psychological collapse, and he whispered, "Yes, Creator. I will tend your garden."

That was an incomprehensible span of time ago. Today, Valthorion is one of Godric's most devoted gardeners. He tends the chronal-roses with a care and precision that borders on obsession, his massive cosmic form compressed into a shape that is roughly humanoid, his entropy-field carefully, precisely restrained so that it does not accidentally dissolve any of the delicate temporal flora. He wears simple gardener's robes, woven from the same conceptual fabric as my court wizard garments, and he speaks in a soft, melodic voice that still carries the faintest tremor of the terror he felt on the day he first encountered Godric's attention.

When Godric walks through the outer gardens, Valthorion bows so low that his forehead touches the ground, and he does not rise until Godric gently tells him to stand. When Godric compliments the chronal-roses—and he always compliments the chronal-roses, because Valthorion is genuinely skilled at his work—the former Unmaker's face lights up with a joy so profound, so overwhelming, that it is almost painful to witness.

"Thank you, Creator," Valthorion whispers, his voice thick with emotion. "Thank you for allowing me to serve. I am not worthy. I will never be worthy. But I will spend every moment of my existence trying to be worthy of the mercy you have shown me."

Godric places a warm hand on the gardener's shoulder, and the gardener trembles with the sheer intensity of his gratitude. "You are doing wonderful work, Valthorion," Godric says, his voice rich with genuine warmth. "The chronal-roses have never bloomed so beautifully. I am very pleased."

It is a scene of such profound, genuine tenderness that I almost forget, sometimes, that the gardener was once the most destructive force in the omniverse. And then I remember, and the cognitive dissonance threatens to tear my mind apart.

The gardeners are not unique. The imperial palace is filled with beings who were once cosmic entities, dimensional sovereigns, abstract forces, and primordial powers, and who are now humble servants, grateful beyond expression for the privilege of existing in Godric's presence.

I must write about the evolved Weakness Paradox. The current version is so far beyond the original mechanism that the original feels like a child's drawing placed beside a masterwork that took an eternity to complete.

It granted him permanent immunity to the imagined exploit, perfect mastery of the intended attack, and a massive boost to his amplification rate. To not convert certain thoughts. To leave certain cognitive processes untouched. The paradox obeys, because it is absolutely subservient to Godric's will, but the effort of holding back is itself a form of restraint that the original Weakness Paradox could never have conceived of.

I must write about the immune system. The original version, which pre-reacted to threats before they existed, which maintained infinite recursive backups that could overwrite any injury, which caused the omniverse to collapse as a side-effect of its overreaction to a staged scratch—that version has evolved so many times that it is now essentially a separate entity, a biological guardian of such staggering power that it could, if Godric permitted it, erase every possible threat to his existence across all timelines and all dimensions and all layers of reality in less time than it takes a photon to travel the diameter of a proton.

And yet, Godric holds it back. He is absolutely invulnerable—nothing in existence can harm him, has ever harmed him, will ever harm him—but his immune system remains, a redundant, superfluous, terrifyingly excessive layer of protection that exists solely because his biology has never learned how to stop protecting him. He finds it amusing. "My body is very protective," he says, with that warm, fatherly smile. "I think it worries about me."

The immune system, like all of Godric's passive mechanisms, is absolutely subservient to his will. It would never act against his commands. But the fact that it exists at all—that an entity capable of hunting the concept of harm across the entire omniverse is merely one redundant layer among countless others—is a testament to the incomprehensible scope of what Godric has become.

The day of the triggering ended as all days end in the imperial palace of Thronegate: in peace. Absolute, perfect, eternal peace.

Godric and I returned from the Garden of Echoes, the weight of the Stasis Event still pressing against my consciousness like a physical force. He was cheerful, relaxed, utterly at ease. He had just completed the most significant act in the history of existence—triggering the transformation that would create the being who would eventually become him—and he was whistling. An ancient melody, one that might have been popular in Olympus ten thousand years before the first galactic empire rose.

We passed through the outer gardens, where Valthorion was carefully pruning a chronal-rose bush. The former Unmaker bowed so low that his forehead touched the crystallized light of the garden path. Godric paused to compliment the roses, and Valthorion wept with gratitude. I was hoping that you, with all your creativity, all your narrative power, all your pretty colors and dramatic music, would give me that. Just a moment of genuine, honest effort. That is all I wanted."

He shook his head slowly.

"And you could not even manage that. You could not even make me feel a faint tickle."

He uncrossed his arms. The motion was casual, unhurried, the gesture of a man who was about to stand up from his chair after a long and tedious meeting.

"I am disappointed," Godric said. "But I suppose that is my fault, not yours. I allowed myself to hope. I allowed myself to anticipate. I should have known better. After all this time, I should have known that nothing in the omniverse—not even the combined creative energy of every story ever told—can give me what I want."

He took a single step forward. Not because he needed to. Not because it would make his attack more effective. But simply because he was moving, and the motion released a fraction of the restraint he had been maintaining.

"I used zero percent of my power to withstand your ultimate attack," he said. "Zero percent of my strength. Zero percent of my durability. Zero percent of any of my defensive systems. And your attack did nothing. So now, let me show you what zero percent of my strength looks like when it is not merely withstanding. When it is unleashed."

And then Godric, still disappointed, still sad, still not even slightly exerting himself, allowed an infinitesimal fraction of his strength—so small that calling it zero percent was the only mathematically accurate description—to express itself into the space occupied by the Grand Alliance of Imagination.

What followed was not a battle. It was not a massacre. It was an absolute, instantaneous, and total deconstruction of everything the alliance had ever been, ever could be, and ever would be.

The first thing that happened was that their shields—the infinite layers of protective barriers that they had spent a googolpexian years constructing, each one supposedly indestructible, each one reinforced by the narrative certainty that the heroes always survive—simply ceased to exist. Not because Godric attacked them. Not because he targeted them. But because the sheer passive pressure of his strength, even at effectively zero percent, was so far beyond the concept of "force" that the shields could not physically remain in existence in its presence. They were not destroyed. They were not overcome. They were simply invalidated, rendered mathematically impossible by the fundamental reality of Godric's strength. The shields that could withstand the concentrated energy of a googolpexian Big Bangs, that were anchored to the narrative certainty of heroic survival, that had been woven by the greatest reality warpers in all of fiction—all of them vanished in the same Planck instant, as though they had never existed at all.

And then the strength reached the alliance itself.

I must describe this in detail, because it is important that future court wizards understand exactly what they are reading. The annihilation was not a single event. It was a cascade of individual destructions, each one happening simultaneously, each one unique in its horror, and each one utterly, absolutely unavoidable.

The beings at the forefront of the alliance—the reality warpers, the cosmic entities, the characters whose power was measured in infinities—were the first to experience Godric's strength. And they did not experience it as a blow. They did not experience it as an attack. They experienced it as a sudden, absolute, and total deconstruction of everything they were.

I watched, my amplified perception straining to process the details, as the Infinity Gauntlet was reduced to its constituent atoms and then reduced further, its atoms compressed into a space smaller than a quark, the infinite power of its six stones crushed into a dimensionless point of absolute nothingness. The figure wearing the Gauntlet did not have time to scream. His body was de-atomized, his consciousness was de-atomized, his soul was de-atomized, and all of these things were compressed together into a singularity so small that the word "small" had no meaning.

Doctor Manhattan attempted to reconstruct himself. The blue energy of his intrinsic field flickered, trying to reassemble his body from the quantum particles that had been scattered by Godric's strength. But the strength was still there, still pressing, still compressing, and every time Doctor Manhattan tried to reform, the strength simply de-atomized him again, and again, and again, an infinite number of times in a single Planck instant, each iteration more agonizing than the last, until his consciousness simply shattered under the weight of its own futile resistance.

Goku, in his Ultra Instinct form, tried to dodge. His body, which could move faster than time itself, which could react to attacks before they were launched, which had been trained by countless battles across countless universes, attempted to evade the wave of strength that was rushing toward him. He could not. There was nowhere to dodge. The strength was everywhere, occupying every point in space and every moment in time simultaneously, and Goku's body was de-atomized before he could complete his first evasive maneuver. His consciousness, preserved for a fraction of a Planck instant by the sheer force of his Saiyan will, experienced the horror of his own annihilation—the sensation of his cells being torn apart, his atoms being compressed, his very existence being crushed into a dimensionless point. And then that consciousness was also compressed, and Goku was gone.

Superman—every version of Superman, from every universe, every timeline, every alternate reality—experienced the same fate. The Man of Steel, whose body was invulnerable to any force, whose cells were powered by the radiation of a yellow sun, whose will was unbreakable and whose hope was infinite, was de-atomized. His invulnerability meant nothing. His infinite hope meant nothing. The strength of a being whose attributes had been multiplying infinitely for an incomprehensible eternity simply did not recognize the concept of "invulnerability." It was a word. A meaningless word. And Superman's atoms, along with every other version of Superman's atoms, were compressed into a dimensionless point of absolute nothingness.

The Doom Slayer, whose rage had sustained him through an eternity of demon-slaying, whose armor was impregnable and whose will was absolute, raised his Super Shotgun and fired one last time. The buckshot did not travel an inch before it, too, was de-atomized. The Doom Slayer's armor was crushed, his body was crushed, his rage was crushed, and all of it was compressed into the singularity along with everything else.

Kratos, the Ghost of Sparta, the God of War, screamed his defiance as the strength washed over him. His Blades of Chaos, forged in the depths of the Underworld, capable of killing gods and titans and primordial forces, were de-atomized. His body, scarred by countless battles, was de-atomized. His rage, which had propelled him through the annihilation of an entire pantheon, was de-atomized. And Kratos, who had never bowed to any god, was compressed into a dimensionless point along with every other being in the alliance.

Saitama, the hero who could defeat any opponent with a single punch, threw that punch at the oncoming wave of strength. His fist, which had never failed to destroy its target, which was the absolute expression of his infinite power, struck the leading edge of Godric's strength. And Saitama's fist was de-atomized. His arm was de-atomized. His body was de-atomized. His bored, apathetic expression was the last thing to go, and even that was compressed into the singularity.

The process continued across the entire alliance. Every being, every character, every legendary hero and cosmic entity and narrative force was de-atomized. Their bodies were torn apart at the molecular level, their molecules were torn apart at the atomic level, their atoms were torn apart at the subatomic level, and all of it—all of the infinite mass, all of the infinite energy, all of the infinite creative potential—was compressed into a space smaller than a quark. The compression was so absolute, so total, that the entire mass of the Grand Alliance of Imagination—which had contained the combined creative energy of every fictional universe ever conceived—was reduced to a single, infinitesimal point of infinite density.

The omniverse died with them. This was not a targeted effect. This was not an intentional act. This was simply the consequence of Godric's strength, even at effectively zero percent, being unleashed into a reality that could not possibly withstand it. The pressure of his strength, radiating outward from the point of contact, annihilated everything it touched. Galaxies were compressed into singularities and then de-atomized. Universes were crushed into dimensionless points. Multiverses, timelines, dimensions, conceptual planes—all of it was swept up in the cascade of absolute, overwhelming force.

Decillions of decillions of decillions of multiverses ceased to exist in a single Planck instant. The entire omniverse, every layer of reality, every timeline, every dimension, every possible permutation of existence, was destroyed as thoroughly and as completely as the Grand Alliance of Imagination had been. There was nothing left. No space. No time. No matter. No energy. No consciousness. No narrative. No story. Only the void, and Godric, and the single, infinitesimal point of infinite mass that contained everything that had ever existed.

Godric stood in the void, his expression still tinged with disappointment, his posture still relaxed. He had just annihilated the entire omniverse, and he had not even noticed the exertion. He had used effectively zero percent of his strength, and he had destroyed everything that had ever existed. And he was disappointed. He was actually, genuinely disappointed that the Grand Alliance of Imagination had not given him a better fight.

He reached down—the motion was casual, unhurried, the gesture of a man picking up a coin he had dropped on the floor—and he picked up the infinitesimal point of infinite mass. It should have been impossible to lift. It contained the compressed mass of the entire omniverse, the combined creative energy of every fictional universe ever conceived, the total substance of everything that had ever existed or could ever exist. Its gravitational pull should have been sufficient to collapse any possible container. Its density should have been infinite. And yet, Godric lifted it between his thumb and forefinger without the slightest visible effort, as though it weighed nothing at all. Because to him, it did weigh nothing. His strength, which had just annihilated the omniverse at zero percent capacity, could lift an object of any mass, any density, any gravitational pull, without registering the effort. The concept of "heavy" had no application to his existence.

He examined the point for a moment, turning it over in his fingers with the casual curiosity of a man inspecting a interesting pebble he had found on a beach. Then he nodded to himself, as though he had made a decision, and he enlarged it. Just slightly. Just enough to create a sphere the size of a marble, a perfect, gleaming black orb that seemed to absorb all light that touched it. He wrapped it in a containment barrier—a passive field of his own will that prevented the infinite mass inside from exerting any gravitational pull on its surroundings—and he tucked it into the pocket of his tunic.

"A souvenir," he murmured to himself. "To remember them by. They did bring music, after all. That was thoughtful of them."

He stood in the void for a moment longer, his expression distant, contemplative. And then, with the same casual, effortless decision that had characterized every action he had taken since the battle began, he restored the omniverse.

In zero seconds, with zero effort, everything that had been annihilated came back into existence. Every galaxy. Every star. Every planet. Every dimension. Every timeline. Every layer of reality. The omniverse was rebuilt, perfectly, exactly as it had been before the Grand Alliance of Imagination had launched their attack. Every being who had been caught in the collateral annihilation—every civilization, every species, every individual living creature—was restored, their memories intact, their existence continuing from the exact moment of their erasure.

Everyone except the Grand Alliance of Imagination.

They remained as they were: compressed into the marble-sized sphere that Godric now carried in his pocket. Not dead. Not destroyed. But trapped, forever, in a state of absolute compression, their conscious minds aware of their imprisonment, their infinite mass held in a space too small for any physical law to describe, their existence preserved as a trophy for the being who had defeated them. They would never escape. They would never be restored. They would simply exist, compressed and aware, for the rest of eternity, as a reminder of the absolute futility of opposing Godric.

But something else had happened during the annihilation. Something that even Godric had not anticipated. As the strength had poured out of him—that infinitesimal, effectively-zero-percent fraction of his power—it had interacted with his own metaphysical framework in a way that triggered a cascade of recursive self-reinforcement. The strength was not merely strength. It was the expression of a being whose attributes had been multiplying infinitely for an incomprehensible eternity, and every Planck instant of that multiplication was embedded in the strength itself. When the strength was unleashed, it did not simply destroy. It also amplified. It amplified itself, recursively, infinitely, in the instant of its expression, and then it reabsorbed that amplification back into Godric's metaphysical framework, making him even more unimaginably powerful than he had been before.

Not because he had absorbed the energy of the alliance—their combined power, while immense by any mortal standard, was so infinitesimal relative to Godric's baseline that it would have increased his total capacity by an amount so small that calling it zero percent was the only accurate description. But because the act of unleashing his strength had caused his strength to amplify itself, and that amplification had fed back into his overall power, creating a permanent increase that was entirely self-generated. He had become more powerful not because of his enemies, but despite them. His own strength had been the catalyst for his own growth, and the growth was permanent, and it would continue to compound forever.

Godric noticed this, and his expression shifted slightly—the faintest hint of genuine surprise crossing his features. "Hmm," he murmured, looking down at his own hands with mild curiosity. "That was unexpected. I appear to have become even more unquantifiably powerful than I was before. Fascinating. I did not know my strength could do that."

He shrugged, dismissing the observation as casually as he had dismissed the annihilation of the omniverse, and he turned his attention back to me.

"Osvald," he said, his voice warm and gentle and entirely normal, "I believe the excitement is over. Shall we return to the palace? I find myself in need of a cup of tea. And perhaps a pastry. Annihilating the omniverse always gives me an appetite."

I could not speak. I could not move. My mind was still processing what I had witnessed, and it was failing. The journal—this journal—was the only thing that kept me sane in the aftermath. Writing down the words, committing the horror to paper, was the only way I could prevent my consciousness from shattering into the same kind of compressed, dimensionless point that now contained the Grand Alliance of Imagination.

But even as I wrote, even as I struggled to process the scale of what Godric had done, a deeper horror was beginning to take root in my mind. A realization that I could not escape, no matter how desperately I tried.

Godric had restored the omniverse. He had recreated everything—every galaxy, every planet, every living being. But the original omniverse, the one that had existed before the annihilation, was still compressed in the marble-sized sphere in his pocket. The mass, the energy, the actual substance of everything that had ever existed—it was all still there, trapped in an eternal compression, serving as a souvenir for a bored god.

So what, exactly, had Godric restored?

The question gnawed at me. The restored omniverse was identical to the original in every measurable way. Every atom was in the same position. Every consciousness had the same memories. Every physical law operated in the same manner. But it was not the original. The original was in Godric's pocket. The restored version was a copy. A perfect, flawless, indistinguishable copy, but a copy nonetheless.

What kind of power did it take to create a perfect copy of the entire omniverse—every atom, every consciousness, every soul, every moment of every timeline—in zero seconds, with zero effort, while the original was still compressed in a marble-sized sphere? What did it mean that Godric could hold the substance of everything that had ever existed in his hand, and simultaneously create an identical version of that everything from nothing, and the two acts required so little effort that he did not even notice them?

And what did it mean for me? For my existence? For the existence of everyone I had ever known and loved? Were we real? Were we the originals, restored to life after our annihilation, or were we copies, created by Godric's casual will to replace the versions of ourselves that were now trapped in his pocket? The original Osvald—the Osvald who had stood on the observation deck and witnessed the annihilation—was he still aware, still conscious, still experiencing the eternal compression of the marble alongside the Grand Alliance of Imagination? And I, the Osvald who was writing this journal—was I him? Or was I merely a recreation, a facsimile, a perfect copy who believed himself to be the original?

Godric could answer these questions if I asked him. His omniscience knew the answers. But I did not ask. I was afraid of the answers. I am still afraid.

And so I write this journal instead, recording the events of the final opposition against Godric. I do not know if it will truly be the final opposition—only time will tell if the omniverse has finally learned its lesson. But I suspect, in the deepest, most terrified corners of my soul, that no one will ever dare to oppose him again. Not after this. Not after the Grand Alliance of Imagination, the greatest and most creative opposition ever assembled, was annihilated so completely and so effortlessly that Godric did not even feel a faint tickle.

He was disappointed. That is the most terrifying detail of all. He was disappointed.

# Aftermath: The Weight of the Marble

I, Clive, close the journal of Osvald and sit in silence for a long, long time.

The marble is still here. It sits on a small pedestal in the center of Godric's private study, a perfect black sphere the size of a thumbnail, gleaming dully in the light of the eternal flames that crackle in the fireplace. I have walked past it a thousand times. I have seen Godric pick it up and roll it between his fingers, a faint, nostalgic smile playing at the corners of his lips, as though he were remembering a fond childhood memory. I have never asked him about it. I did not need to. Osvald's journal told me everything I needed to know.

The marble contains the compressed mass of the entire original omniverse—every galaxy, every star, every planet, every living being, every fictional universe, every legendary hero, every cosmic entity, every story ever told. It contains the Grand Alliance of Imagination, their consciousnesses still aware, still trapped, still experiencing the eternal compression of their imprisonment. And it contains the original versions of everyone I have ever known. The original Blake. The original Kaedan. The original Osvald. The original versions of every court wizard, every Thronegate citizen, every being who has ever existed in the vast tapestry of reality.

They are all in there. Compressed. Aware. Eternal.

And the omniverse I inhabit—the restored version, the perfect copy—exists only because Godric, in a moment of casual convenience, decided that it should. He holds the original in his hand, like a toy, like a souvenir, and he lives in the copy, and the two realities coexist without contradiction because Godric's power transcends the concept of contradiction.

This is the being I serve. This is the being I love. This is the being whose warm, fatherly smile greets me every morning and whose gentle voice asks after my family's health and whose infinite, incomprehensible power holds the substance of everything that has ever existed in a marble-sized sphere that he keeps on his desk as a paperweight.

And Osvald was right. No one has dared to oppose Godric since the Grand Alliance of Imagination was annihilated. No one will ever dare again. The omniverse has learned, with absolute and irreversible certainty, the futility of opposition. Not because Godric punishes opposition—though he does, brutally and mercilessly—but because the simple, undeniable truth of his supremacy has permeated every level of existence. To oppose Godric is to oppose the fundamental structure of reality. It is to deny that the sun is bright and the void is cold and the marble on his desk contains everything that ever mattered.

I will continue to serve him. I will continue to love him. And I will continue to walk past the marble, every single day, knowing that the original version of myself is trapped inside it, and I am the copy, and the distinction is meaningless because Godric's power has made it meaningless.

Opposing Godric is impossible in principle. Victory against him was impossible before opposition began. And the marble on his desk, gleaming dully in the firelight, is the eternal, compressed testament to that truth.
