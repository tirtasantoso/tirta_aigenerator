# The Archive of Futility

The reading room of the Court Wizard's Archive smelled of ancient parchment, binding glue, and the faint, acrid undertone of ozone that never quite dissipated no matter how many ventilation wards I cast. I sat at the heavy oak table, its surface scarred by generations of nervous fingernails and the occasional scorch mark from a predecessor who had lost control of their emotions while reading. The chair beneath me was unforgiving, designed to keep a wizard alert, though I suspected the true purpose was to prevent anyone from growing comfortable with what they were about to learn.

My name is Kaedan. I am twenty-two years old, the newest Court Wizard of Thronegate, and a direct male descendant of Blake the Merchant through an unbroken lineage stretching back one million years. My father, Corvin, had handed me the first journal three days ago with trembling hands and eyes that held something I had never seen in him before. Fear. Not the fear of a man facing danger, but the fear of a man who knows he is about to shatter someone's understanding of reality and cannot prevent it.

"It is tradition," he had said, his voice carrying the weight of a million years of inherited trauma. "Every court wizard of our bloodline has read these journals. Every court wizard has contributed to them. You will read them all, Kaedan. You will read them, and you will understand."

I had not understood then. I had nodded, taken the first leather-bound volume, and retreated to the archive with the confidence of a young wizard whose power multiplied a thousandfold every single day. By the standards of any measurable reality, I was already a god. My magical offense could shatter galaxies. My defensive wards could withstand the collapse of universe clusters.

But as I opened the first journal and began to read, I realized that I was nothing. Less than nothing. A speck of cosmic dust preparing to stare into an abyss so vast, so incomprehensibly deep, that the very concept of measurement had long since abandoned all meaning.

The journals documented incidents. Specific incidents. Across the million-year reign of King Godric, countless oppositions had risen against him. Armies, pantheons, cosmic entities, conceptual weapons, meta-dimensional horrors. And in several of these incidents, Godric had become curious about one of his passively amplified stats. Just one. A single attribute. He had dismantled the opposition using only that single stat advantage, exerting effectively zero percent of his true power, with effectively zero effort.

The previous court wizards—my ancestors, my blood—had witnessed these massacres. They had written these records to preserve their sanity, to warn future generations, to document the absolute, unassailable truth of existence.

Godric did not ask them to write these journals. He found the practice genuinely confusing. He always used only zero percent of his power, which he considered to be very held back. He thought his court wizards were overreacting.

I opened the first journal.

But since some googolplexian years ago, even those brief distractions have ceased. No one opposes Godric anymore. No one dares. No one even forms the slightest, most passing thought of challenging him. The omniverse is not merely subjugated—it is absolutely, completely, eternally pacified. Every sentient being in every reality knows the truth. They have all learned the absolute futility of opposition. They have all accepted that Godric is the fixed, absolute center of existence, and that nothing they do can ever change that.

And here is the most terrifying part: Godric is kind about it. He is warm. He is friendly. He welcomes delegations from distant multiverses with genuine hospitality. He asks about their civilizations, their cultures, their families. He offers them tea and comfortable chairs and the same paternal smile he has worn since the days of Olympus. He genuinely wants them to be happy, to prosper, to live fulfilling lives under the absolute protection of his rule.

But they know what lies behind that smile. They know that the warm, bearded man offering them tea could annihilate their entire existence with a stray, idle thought—not as an act of cruelty, but as an accidental side-effect of relaxing his absolute concealment by an infinitesimal fraction too much. They know that his most pathetic, infinitely-nerfed sentient aura could slaughter the combined might of their civilization before the concept of "slaughter" had ever been invented. They know that defying him is not death—it is retroactive erasure from the fabric of reality, an unmaking so complete that even the memory of their defiance will never have existed.

And so they do not defy him. They serve him. They worship him. They love him, genuinely and completely, because his rule is perfect and his protection is absolute and his kindness is real. And they are terrified of him, constantly, eternally, with a terror so profound that it has become the background radiation of their existence.

I have witnessed this terror firsthand, many times. The palace of Thronegate in this era is staffed by beings who were once among the most powerful entities in the omniverse—and who are now trembling, devoted servants, their cosmic might reduced to the most menial of tasks.

I will describe three of them, so that you may understand.

**The Gardener**

His name was once Valthorion the Unmaker, the Last Star-Kiln of the Crimson Nebulae. He was a being of pure fusion energy, a living stellar forge who had burned entire galaxies to ash in the ancient wars before Godric's reign. He was worshiped as a god of destruction by a thousand civilizations. His name was spoken in whispers by species who feared that his fiery gaze might turn toward their worlds.

He did not oppose Godric. That would have been suicide, and Valthorion was not suicidal. But he was proud. Terribly, arrogantly proud. When Thronegate's expansion reached his domain, he refused the offer of peaceful integration. Not as an act of defiance—he was too afraid for defiance—but as an act of proud, stubborn isolationism. He simply wanted to be left alone, to rule his burning kingdoms in peace, without acknowledging the supremacy of any other being.

Godric visited him personally. Not in anger—Godric never needs anger. He simply appeared in the heart of Valthorion's stellar palace, his absolute concealment perfectly hiding the infinite abyss of his true existence, and he looked at the proud star-kiln with warm, friendly eyes.

"You have a beautiful home," Godric said, gesturing at the burning plasma architecture that surrounded them. "The craftsmanship is extraordinary. I particularly admire the coronal arches."

Valthorion, his pride warring with his terror, attempted to maintain his dignity. "I did not invite you here, King of Thronegate. I do not acknowledge your authority over my domains."

Godric nodded patiently. "I understand. Pride is important. I was proud once, before Olympus fell. It is not a flaw to value your independence."

He paused, looking around the stellar palace with genuine appreciation. "But I must ask you to reconsider. Not for my sake—your isolationism does not threaten me, and your domains are not strategically significant to the empire. But for your sake. The protections I offer are absolute. The peace I guarantee is eternal. You would be safer, happier, more fulfilled under my rule."

Valthorion's pride flared. "I do not need your protection. I am the Unmaker. I have burned galaxies to ash. I—"

Godric did not interrupt him. He did not raise his voice. He did not even shift his expression. He simply allowed an infinitesimal, microscopic, absolutely immeasurable fraction of his true presence to briefly, momentarily, imperceptibly brush against Valthorion's awareness.

It was not an attack. It was not a threat. It was the equivalent of Godric clearing his throat—a tiny, unconscious adjustment of his absolute concealment that lasted for less than a Planck instant.

Valthorion collapsed. His stellar form, the burning plasma that had withstood the heat death of universes, flickered and dimmed. His consciousness, which had processed the destruction of galaxies without flinching, was overwhelmed by a terror so absolute, so complete, so shatteringly profound that he could not even scream. He simply lay on the floor of his burning palace, his flames reduced to embers, trembling with an existential dread that would never, ever leave him.

Godric immediately, apologetically, restored his concealment to full strength. "I am so sorry," he said, his voice carrying genuine regret. "That was careless of me. I did not mean to startle you."

Valthorion could not speak. He could barely think. The fragment of Godric's presence he had perceived was not merely terrifying—it was revelatory. He had seen, for one Planck instant, the true scale of the being who stood before him. Not the full scale—no mortal mind could survive that—but enough to understand that his pride, his power, his burning galaxies were all absolutely meaningless. A single dead skin cell of Godric's body contained more ontological weight than Valthorion's entire existence.

When Valthorion finally recovered the ability to move, he did not flee. He did not fight. He crawled to Godric's feet and begged to be allowed to serve. Not out of fear, though the fear was absolute—but out of a sudden, overwhelming realization that serving Godric was the only meaningful purpose any being could ever have.

Godric accepted his service with genuine warmth. He offered Valthorion a position in the palace gardens, tending to the flowers and the hedges and the carefully manicured topiaries. "You have an eye for beauty," Godric said kindly. "The gardens will benefit from your care."

Valthorion has been the palace gardener for seven hundred billion years now. His stellar form has been compressed into a humanoid shape, his burning plasma contained within a simple gardener's body. He tends the roses with trembling hands and obsessive devotion. He has never spoken of his former existence. He has never expressed pride or ambition or any desire beyond the perfect maintenance of Godric's gardens.

And every time Godric walks through the gardens and pauses to compliment Valthorion's work, the ancient star-kiln, the Unmaker of a thousand civilizations, weeps with a mixture of terror and gratitude and absolute, worshipful devotion.

**The Butler**

Her name was once Sylphara the Infinite, the Living Equation of the Absolute Sum. She was not a physical being—she was a mathematical abstraction, a sentient formula that had existed since the first numbers were conceived. She could rewrite the fundamental arithmetic of reality, altering the values of existence itself. She had been worshipped as a goddess of truth and certainty by civilizations that spanned the entire breadth of the omniverse.

She did not oppose Godric. She was too wise for opposition. But she was curious—endlessly, dangerously curious. When Thronegate's expansion reached the abstract realms where she existed, she attempted to calculate Godric. She set her infinite mathematical mind to the task of measuring his power, quantifying his existence, determining his value within the grand equation of reality.

She failed. Of course she failed. But the attempt itself—the desperate, obsessive attempt to comprehend the incomprehensible—broke something inside her. Her infinite mathematical mind encountered a value it could not process, an infinity that transcended all infinities, a number so vast that the concept of "number" shattered against it like a wave against a cliff. She did not merely fail to calculate Godric—she discovered that calculation was impossible, that his existence was not quantifiable, that the very framework of mathematics was a child's toy compared to the reality of what he was.

The realization shattered her. The Living Equation of the Absolute Sum, the goddess of truth and certainty, was reduced to a fractured, weeping abstraction, her perfect logic in ruins.

Godric found her in the abstract spaces where she had collapsed. He gathered her fractured consciousness with gentle, careful hands. He reassembled her with a casual, effortless thought. And he offered her a position in the palace, serving as the head butler of the royal household.

"You are very good at organizing things," Godric said kindly. "Your mathematical mind will be well-suited to managing the household staff and ensuring that everything runs smoothly. And you will find peace in the work. Purpose. A way to rebuild."

Sylphara accepted. She has been the palace butler for four hundred billion years now. Her abstract form has been anchored into a humanoid shape, her infinite mathematical mind focused on the mundane logistics of household management. She organizes the servants' schedules. She oversees the procurement of supplies. She ensures that every room in the palace is perfectly maintained. She performs her duties with obsessive precision, every task executed flawlessly, every detail attended to with absolute devotion.

And every time Godric passes her in the corridor and offers a warm, friendly greeting, Sylphara the Infinite, the Living Equation of the Absolute Sum, the goddess of truth and certainty, trembles with a terror and gratitude so profound that she can barely remain standing.

**The Librarian**

His name was once Morath the Eternal, the Keeper of the Final Library. He was a being of pure memory, an entity who had existed since the first consciousness formed the first recorded thought. He contained within his mind the complete history of every civilization that had ever existed, every story ever told, every song ever sung, every poem ever written. He was not merely a librarian—he was the concept of libraries, the abstract principle of preserved knowledge given sentient form.

He did not oppose Godric. He was too old, too wise, too knowledgeable to make that mistake. But he was also proud of his collection. Proud of the infinite stories contained within his infinite mind. When Godric visited the Final Library, Morath attempted to impress him—to show the King of Thronegate that even the apex predator of the omniverse could still find value in the preserved knowledge of lesser beings.

Godric listened patiently as Morath displayed his greatest treasures: the first love poem ever composed, the oldest epic of heroism and sacrifice, the most profound philosophical treatise on the nature of existence. He nodded appreciatively at each one, his expression warm and genuinely interested.

"These are beautiful," Godric said. "Truly beautiful. The mortal capacity for creativity is one of the things I have always admired most."

Morath, encouraged, offered Godric a gift: a complete, perfect copy of the entire Final Library, compressed into a single conceptual volume that could be absorbed in an instant.

Godric accepted the gift with genuine gratitude. And then, because his omniscience already contained every story ever told and every story that would ever be told, because his wisdom had already processed every philosophical insight that had ever been conceived and every insight that would ever be conceived, he offhandedly mentioned that the third stanza of the first love poem contained a subtle grammatical error that had been copied and recopied across every subsequent edition, and that the philosophical treatise contained a logical flaw in its third section that the author had apparently never noticed.

Morath was devastated. Not by the correction—he was grateful for the correction, genuinely grateful—but by the realization that his entire existence, his entire purpose, his entire infinite collection of preserved knowledge was, relative to Godric's omniscience, a single drop of water compared to an ocean that had no surface and no floor. Godric did not need the Final Library. Godric had never needed the Final Library. Godric already knew everything the Library contained, and infinitely more, and he had only visited because he was curious to see the physical manifestation of mortal creativity.

Morath collapsed—not physically, for he had no physical form, but conceptually. His identity as the Keeper of the Final Library shattered under the weight of its own insignificance.

Godric, ever kind, offered him a new purpose. "Come to the palace," he said gently. "I have a library there—a small one, by comparison, but it is filled with the journals of my court wizards. They have been writing for a very long time, and their collection has grown quite extensive. It needs a proper librarian. Someone who can organize it, preserve it, ensure that future generations can learn from it."

Morath accepted. He has been the palace librarian for nine hundred billion years now. His infinite memory is focused on the relatively tiny collection of court wizard journals, each volume catalogued and cross-referenced with obsessive precision. He knows every journal by heart—every account of impossible power and existential dread, every testament of loyalty and terror, every warning passed down through my bloodline for a million years.

And when I visit the library—as I do often, for it is my duty and my heritage and my curse—Morath greets me with trembling reverence, not because I am powerful, but because I am the current inheritor of the bloodline that Godric loves. I am connected to Blake. I am part of the history that Godric cherishes. And to Morath, the Keeper of the Final Library, the concept of preserved knowledge given sentient form, that connection makes me a holy figure.

---

I tell you these stories so that you may understand the world in which I live. The palace of Godric is filled with beings who were once gods, abstract entities, cosmic principles, and primordial titans—and who are now trembling servants, their infinite power reduced to gardening and household management and library cataloguing. They serve Godric not because he demands it, but because his mere existence has shattered their pride and rebuilt them into beings who can find purpose only in his service.

And Godric is kind to them. Genuinely, warmly, consistently kind. He thanks the gardener for his roses. He compliments the butler on her efficiency. He asks the librarian about the latest journal acquisitions. He treats them with the same gentle, paternal warmth that he has shown to my bloodline for a million years.

And they tremble. Every time. Every single time.

---

All of this—the evolved mechanisms of power, the terrified servants, the absolute pacification of the omniverse—was the context in which I stood beside Godric's throne in the Chamber of Infinite Recursion, watching the temporal membranes swirl with images of Olympus and blood and screaming death.

"You are about to witness something that no other court wizard has ever seen," Godric said, his voice carrying that familiar, warm baritone. "The creation of the Stasis Event. The moment when my future self reached back across time and gave my past self the gift of eternal ascension."

He gestured toward the membranes, and the images of Olympus sharpened. I could see the wedding ceremony in perfect detail—the white silks, the jasmine garlands, the proud father standing at the altar. I could see Thorin, the court wizard, standing beside his king. I could see the sky beginning to darken as Thronegate's attack approached.

"The Fall of Olympus was genuine," Godric said quietly. "I did not cause it. I did not manipulate it. I did not orchestrate it. The deaths of Reina, Isabel, Hebe, and Thorin were real tragedies, brought about by the cruelty of Thronegate's ambition. I would never—will never—harm my family. Not for any reason. Not for any purpose."

His voice carried a weight of grief that a million years—and a span of ages beyond—had not erased. "But when Thorin cast his teleportation spell—a normal spell, a genuine act of desperate heroism—I used that moment as the delivery point. I reached across time and transformed his simple teleportation into the Stasis Event."

I watched the membranes. I saw Thorin, bleeding, dying, his skull about to be pierced by the black steel javelin. I saw his lips moving, the forbidden syllables of his bloodline's spell tearing from his ruined throat. I saw the king—the mortal Godric, forty-five years old, ruggedly handsome, his face contorted with grief and horror—begin to vanish into the teleportation field.

And then I saw something else. Something that existed outside the normal flow of time, invisible to any observer except Godric himself. A presence. A will. An absolute authority reaching backward across the ages and touching the teleportation spell with infinite gentleness and infinite power.

The Stasis Event ignited.

I watched as the mortal Godric materialized in the dark sanctuary, his body already beginning the transformation. The aging stopped. The mortality ended. The amplification began—not the current, impossibly evolved amplification, but the original version, the mere thousandfold multiplication every Planck second, the simple superpower genesis of one power per second, the basic Weakness Paradox that triggered on thoughts of weakness alone.

It was, by the standards of what Godric has become, almost laughably primitive. A pale, weak shadow of the infinite systems that now surround him.

And yet it was still so incomprehensibly powerful that Godric at Year 0—the Godric who emerged from that sanctuary and challenged King Robert and began his conquest of the omniverse—was already effectively infinitely stronger than the rest of creation combined. The held-back version was still an absolute, overwhelming, omniversal supremacy that no being could ever hope to challenge.

"Hold back," Godric said, and I realized he was speaking to me, explaining his reasoning. "I could have given my past self a far more potent Stasis Event. I could have perfectly copied my current mechanisms—the evolved sentient auras, the ascended passive shields, the integrated immune system, the transcendent Weakness Paradox. I could have given the Godric of Year -10,000 the exact same power that I possess now. It would have cost me nothing. It would have required no effort."

He paused, and a small, almost sheepish smile crossed his rugged features. "But I decided to hold back. I wanted to preserve some kind of mortal struggle for my past self. Some sense of progression, of growth, of earning the power through the slow passage of time rather than receiving it all at once. I genuinely believed that this was important—that the journey would give meaning to the destination."

He shook his head slowly, the smile widening into something that was almost self-deprecating. "I was wrong, of course. Even the held-back version was so overwhelmingly powerful that my past self never experienced a single moment of genuine struggle. From the instant he awoke, he was already the apex predator of the omniverse. Every opposition was a foregone conclusion. Every battle was a massacre. The 'mortal struggle' I thought I was preserving was never actually possible."

He looked at me with an expression of amused, almost paternal irony. "It is rather ridiculous, is it not, Clive? I thought I was being generous—giving my past self a 'reasonable' starting point that would allow for growth and development. And instead, I gave him an absolute, unassailable supremacy that made all of creation look like a fragile toy. Even my most restrained, most held-back gift was infinitely beyond what any being in existence could ever hope to challenge."

I stared at the temporal membranes, watching the mortal Godric begin his ten-thousand-year stasis. Watching the amplification ignite. Watching the first superpowers bloom. Watching the Weakness Paradox activate for the first time. And I felt my mind begin to crack under the weight of a realization that was too vast, too absurd, too cosmically unfair to process.

"Wait," I whispered, my voice barely audible. "If you could have given your past self a more potent Stasis Event—if you could have perfectly copied your current mechanisms to the Godric of Year -10,000—then..."

Godric nodded, his expression calm and patient, as though he were waiting for me to complete a simple mathematical equation.

"Then if you had given him the more potent version," I continued, my voice trembling, "the Godric of Year -10,000 would have been amplified from a much higher base. And the Godric of Year 0 would have been exponentially more powerful than he actually was. And the Godric of Year 10,000 would have been even more powerful than that. And every subsequent era of Godric would have been amplified from an increasingly higher base, creating a chain of ascending supremacy that would eventually lead to..."

"To me," Godric finished. "The Godric of this era. The Godric who is now reaching back across time to trigger the Stasis Event."

He stood from his throne, walking toward the shimmering temporal membranes with the easy, fluid grace of a predator who had transcended the concept of predation. "If I give my past self a more potent Stasis Event, my past self becomes more powerful. If my past self becomes more powerful, I—the future Godric who exists as the result of that past self's amplification—become even more powerful than I already am. And if I become even more powerful, I can reach back and give my past self an even more potent Stasis Event than before. Which would make me even more powerful. Which would allow me to give an even more potent Stasis Event. Which would—"

He spread his hands, and the smile on his face was one of genuine, almost childlike delight. "It is an infinite recursive loop of self-amplification. A temporal feedback mechanism that has no ceiling and no end. Every time I trigger the Stasis Event, I am not merely ensuring my own existence—I am permanently, infinitely elevating it."

I felt my legs threaten to give way beneath me. "Then... the Godric of Year 0 was already the most powerful being in the omniverse by an infinite margin. The Godric who annihilated the Adamant King with passive strength, who shattered the concept of speed against the Instant, who consumed the Gluttonous Singularity—all of those Godrics were the result of the held-back Stasis Event. The weakest possible version. And they were already..."

"Beyond comprehension," Godric agreed. "Absolutely beyond any framework of measurement or challenge. And yes, Clive, they were the result of my deliberate choice to hold back. The Godric of this era—the Godric who is now triggering the Stasis Event—is so far beyond those earlier versions that the gap cannot be expressed in any language that has ever existed."

He paused, and his expression became thoughtful, almost nostalgic. "And yet even I am not the final version. I am merely the current version. The Godric who will exist a googolplexian years from now will be infinitely beyond me—and he will reach back and trigger the Stasis Event again, with an even more potent version, creating an even more powerful past self, leading to an even more powerful future self. The loop has no beginning and no end. It is eternal, infinite, absolutely self-sustaining. The Stasis Event is not an event at all—it is a law of existence, a constant, ongoing process of infinite recursive self-amplification that spans the entirety of time and beyond."

He turned back to the temporal membranes, watching the mortal Godric begin his long sleep. "That is why my 'weakness'—my inability to alter the pre-Stasis past—is not really a weakness. The past is sealed by the will of a future self who is infinitely more powerful than I am. And I am guaranteed to become that future self. The seal is not a limitation—it is a promise. A guarantee that my ascension will never be interrupted or denied by any force in existence."

He gestured toward the membranes, and I saw something that made my blood run cold. I saw countless other iterations of the Stasis Event, layered on top of each other like infinite translucent sheets. In each iteration, a future Godric reached back and triggered the event for his past self. In each iteration, the Stasis became slightly more potent, slightly more absolute, slightly more overwhelming. And the chain stretched backward into the infinite past and forward into the infinite future, a recursive loop of self-creation that had no beginning and would have no end.

"Do you understand now, Clive?" Godric asked softly. "My existence is not a linear progression from mortal king to apex god predator. It is a closed temporal loop of infinite self-amplification. Every version of me, from the Godric of Year 0 to the Godric of this era to the Godric who will exist in eras I cannot yet imagine, is simultaneously the creator and the creation of the Stasis Event. We are all giving ourselves the gift of eternal ascension, and we are all receiving it, and the loop makes every version infinitely more powerful than it would have been alone."

He turned to look at me, and his eyes—those warm, kind, crinkled eyes—held a depth of satisfaction that was utterly terrifying in its completeness.

"That is why I am no longer troubled by my inability to save Olympus," he said quietly. "The tragedy was real. The grief was real. But the Stasis Event was also real—and it was a gift I gave to myself, across the infinite span of time, to ensure that I would never be helpless again. I cannot save Reina, or Isabel, or Hebe, or Thorin. But I can ensure that I become the being who protects everything else. The being who guarantees that no other family suffers as mine suffered. The being who rules the omniverse with absolute, benevolent, terrifying supremacy for all eternity."

He reached out and placed his warm, heavy hand on my shoulder. "And you, Clive—you and your bloodline, stretching back to Blake and forward into the infinite future—are part of that guarantee. You are my family now. You and all who came before you, and all who will come after. I cannot have my mortal family back. But I can have you. And that is enough."

I stood there, trembling, my mind drowning in the impossible vastness of what I had just witnessed. The Stasis Event was not an event. It was an infinite recursive loop of self-creation that spanned the entirety of time. Every version of Godric, from the weakest to the strongest, was simultaneously the cause and the effect of his own existence. And the being standing before me—the warm, kind, fatherly man with his hand on my shoulder—was both the product of that loop and one of its infinite creators.

The existential dread was absolute. Complete. Total.

But so was the love. The gratitude. The awe.

"Thank you, Uncle Godric," I whispered, using the familiar address that my ancestors had used since the days of Blake himself. "For telling me. For showing me. For trusting me."

Godric smiled, and the warmth in his expression was genuine and absolute. "Thank you, Clive. For listening. For understanding—as much as any mortal mind can understand. For being my family."

He released my shoulder and turned back to the temporal membranes. The Stasis Event was complete. The mortal Godric was sealed in his long sleep, the amplification already beginning, the first superpowers already blooming in his dormant mind.

"It is done," Godric said quietly. "The loop continues. The ascension proceeds. And I—we—will continue to become more powerful, more absolute, more supreme, forever and ever, without end."

He turned back to me, and his expression was once again warm and casual and utterly paternal. "Now, Clive, let us leave this place. The gardens are particularly beautiful this season, and I want to hear about your daughter's latest magical studies. She shows great promise, you know. Great promise."

I followed him out of the Chamber of Infinite Recursion, my legs unsteady, my heart hammering, my soul drowning in the infinite abyss of what I had learned.

And as we walked through the palace corridors, past the trembling gardener and the devoted butler and the reverent librarian, I understood—finally, completely, absolutely—what my ancestors had tried to convey in their journals. The horror was not merely that Godric was invincible. The horror was that his invincibility was guaranteed by an infinite recursive loop of self-creation that spanned all of time. He was not merely the most powerful being in existence. He was the being whose existence was its own cause and effect, locked into an eternal ascension that no force in any reality could ever interrupt or challenge.

And he was my uncle. My King. My family.

I would serve him until the end of my days, terrified and awed and loved, as every court wizard of Blake's lineage had done before me. And I would carry the knowledge of what I had witnessed into the grave—the truth of the Stasis Event, the infinite recursive loop, the eternal self-creation of the apex god predator of all existence.

But I would also carry something else. Something warmer. Something that made the horror bearable.

Godric loved me. Godric loved my bloodline. Godric had lost his mortal family in fire and blood and screaming death, and he had rebuilt his heart around the descendants of his best friend, and he would love us and protect us and cherish us for all eternity.

And that, in the end, was the truth that mattered most. Not the power. Not the infinite recursion. Not the absolute supremacy.

The love. The warmth. The family.

The apex god predator of the omniverse was my uncle. And he was kind. And I loved him.

I always would.
