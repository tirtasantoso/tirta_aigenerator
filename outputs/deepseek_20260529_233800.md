# The Archive of Futility

The archive smelled of old parchment, dust, and something deeper—something that clung to the back of the throat like cold iron and ozone, a residue that had seeped into the very stone over the course of a million years. I stood at the threshold of the long, vaulted chamber beneath the eastern wing of the palace, my hands clasped behind my back, my heart beating a steady, arrogant rhythm against my ribs. My name is Kaedan. I am twenty-two years old. I am the newest Court Wizard of Thronegate, a direct descendant of Blake the merchant, the man who had stumbled into a hidden sanctuary a million years ago and accidentally unleashed the apex predator of all creation upon the omniverse.

My father, Varek, stood beside the heavy ironwood doors, his arms crossed over his chest, his weathered face carved into an expression of grim, unyielding expectation. He was sixty-one years old, his hair completely white, his body still possessing the terrifying, suppressed power of a man whose attributes had multiplied a thousandfold every single day since his twentieth birthday. He had served as Godric's court wizard for over four decades. He had witnessed things he never spoke of, and the deep lines around his eyes told stories that his mouth refused to shape.

"You will read every journal," my father said, his voice flat and devoid of negotiation. "Every volume on this shelf. You will not leave this chamber until you have finished. This is not a request, Kaedan. This is the prerequisite for standing at his side. I enforced this upon myself when my own father brought me here, and his father before him, stretching back to Blake himself."

I looked at the shelf. It stretched the entire length of the chamber, lined with hundreds of worn leather-bound volumes, each spine marked with a year and a name. The oldest were near the entrance, their leather cracked and faded. The newest were near the far wall, their bindings still dark and fresh. This was the inheritance of my bloodline, the accumulated terror of countless generations of wizards who had served the same immortal king.

"Is this truly necessary?" I asked, allowing a hint of my youthful confidence to bleed into my tone. "I know Godric is powerful. Everyone knows. The stories of his conquest are the foundation of our civilization. I have grown up in his palace. I have called him Uncle Godric since I could speak. I understand that he could destroy the omniverse if he wished. What more could these journals possibly teach me?"

My father turned his head slowly, his pale gray eyes locking onto mine with an intensity that made the air in my lungs turn cold. There was something in his expression that I had never seen before—a deep, bone-deep weariness mixed with something that looked almost like pity. He did not answer my question. He simply placed his weathered hand on the ironwood door and pushed it open wider, the ancient hinges groaning in protest.

"Start with the earliest," he said. "And do not skip. Each journal exists because the man who wrote it needed to write it, or he would have lost his mind entirely. You will understand before you reach the end."

He stepped back, and the heavy door swung shut behind him, the latch clicking into place with a terrible finality. I was alone in the archive.

I walked slowly along the shelf, running my fingers over the worn spines. The earliest volume was marked with a year that predated the formation of most galaxies. I pulled it from the shelf, feeling the weight of ancient trauma in my hands, and settled into the heavy oak chair positioned beneath the chamber's only window. The morning light filtering through the stained glass cast fractured patterns of blue and gold across the pages as I opened the cover and began to read.

**Year 8,234 — Strength — Orin, Third Court Wizard of the Lineage**

I am writing this account because my hands will not stop trembling and my mind will not quiet until I have externalized the horror onto parchment. Perhaps, centuries from now, a descendant of my blood will read these words and understand why I have aged thirty years in a single afternoon.

He simply allowed the faintest, most infinitesimal fraction of his strength stat—a quantity so small that calling it zero percent would be an overstatement so vast that it would require an infinite number of universes to express the error—to exist in the same conceptual space as the Omnifist's attack.

The Omnifist was still there, somehow. Lord Godric had preserved it. He had wanted it to witness. Its remaining form drifted in the void, its merged consciousness screaming across telepathic frequencies that had no medium to travel through, its terror so complete that its composite soul had begun to unravel into its sixty-six constituent identities, each one trapped in an isolated loop of eternal, agonizing awareness.

**Year 52,117 — Speed — Fenris, Seventh Court Wizard of the Lineage**

I will not attempt to write this account in linear sequence because the event I witnessed has permanently damaged my ability to perceive time as a forward-flowing river. I see moments out of order now. I hear sounds before they are made. I taste the copper of blood that has not yet been spilled. This is a side effect of proximity to what I saw, and I have been told it will fade within a few decades. I am writing this in fragments because fragments are all I have left.

They were called the Instantaneous Choir. They were not beings who moved fast. They were beings who had transcended the concept of speed entirely, existing in a state of perpetual, simultaneous presence across all moments of all timelines simultaneously. A single member of the Choir could strike every possible target in every possible reality at the exact same instant, because for them, the concept of "sequence" did not exist. They had concluded that while the King was infinitely powerful in static terms, his speed—his capacity to move, to react, to perceive fast events—might be limited by the same temporal constraints that bound all other beings. They believed that if they could strike every possible version of him across every possible moment simultaneously, at least one of those infinite strikes would land before his defense could form.

This was, of course, comically, catastrophically wrong. The Weakness Paradox had granted the King absolute mastery over temporal combat millennia before the Choir even formed their first thought. But the King allowed them to gather. He allowed them to prepare. He was curious, he later told me, about the sensation of being attacked at infinite speed. He wanted to see if he could even perceive it.

I stood beside the King in the grand astral observatory when the Choir struck. The King was looking out at the nebula, his hands resting on the balcony rail, his posture entirely relaxed. He had just finished telling me a joke about a merchant and a hyper-dimensional chicken. He was laughing, his warm, rich laughter echoing off the obsidian walls.

Then the Choir attacked.

They came from every moment at once. A strike aimed at the King's past self. A strike aimed at his present self. A strike aimed at his future self. An infinite number of strikes distributed across an infinite number of temporal coordinates, all of them simultaneous, all of them carrying the full, concentrated killing intent of an eternal order of assassins. The attack was, by any logical measure, undodgable. There was no single moment to evade. The strikes existed in all moments.

The King did not dodge. He did not move. He did not even pause in his laughter. The strikes simply never reached him.

I did not understand what had happened at first. The attack had been launched, and then it was over, and the Choir was screaming. I blinked, trying to process the gap between the launch and the scream, but my mind could not bridge it. There was no bridge. There was no gap. The attack had been launched, and in the same instant, the attack had been defeated, and the defeat had always already happened, and the launch had never truly occurred at all.

The King's speed was not a matter of moving fast. He had not moved at all. His speed stat, which had been multiplying by numbers beyond infinite recursion every infinitesimal fraction of a Planck second for over fifty thousand years, had reached a state where his very existence occupied a velocity that made instantaneity look like an eternity. Compared to the King's speed, the Infinite Choir—beings who existed in all moments simultaneously—were not merely slow. They were so fundamentally, incomprehensibly slow that they appeared to be frozen in a state of absolute, eternal stillness. They had not even begun to attack. From the King's perspective, they were still in the process of forming the neural impulse to strike, a process that, stretched across the relativistic chasm between his speed and theirs, would take longer than the lifespan of a googolplexian universes to complete.

The King, still laughing at his own joke, simply observed the Choir's attack as one might observe a single frame of a film stretched across a billion years. He had all the time in existence—and beyond existence—to perceive every detail of their assault, to analyze it, to grow bored with it, to finish his laugh, and to decide how to respond. He chose to respond by doing absolutely nothing.

The Choir, from their own temporal perspective, experienced the full horror of the speed differential. They had launched their attack, and in that same eternal instant, they had been simultaneously struck by an infinite number of counter-actions that the King had not performed. The King's speed was so great that his mere presence in the timeline generated afterimages—temporal echoes of his existence that acted independently, moving at speeds that were still infinitely beyond the Choir's capacity to perceive. These afterimages, these passive, unintended side effects of the King's speed stat existing in proximity to a temporal attack, dismantled the Choir completely.

The assassins were torn apart across every moment they had ever occupied. Their past selves, present selves, future selves—all of them were simultaneously struck by a speed so vast that the strikes had no physical form. The Choir simply experienced their own existence being rapidly, agonizingly peeled away from the timeline. Each member felt every individual Planck-second of their lives being erased, one by one, in reverse order, from the moment of their attack backward to the moment of their birth. It was a death that stretched across their entire temporal span, a single instant of annihilation that they experienced as an eternity of progressively being unmade.

The screaming did not stop after their physical forms were erased. The King's speed had been so excessive that the echoes of their agony were trapped in a closed temporal loop, repeating forever in a pocket of frozen time that orbited the observatory like a faint, whining moon. The King noticed the sound and, with a casual flicker of his attention, silenced it. Not out of mercy—out of mild annoyance at the noise.

He finished his laugh and turned to me. "Fenris," he said, his eyes crinkling with warmth. "You didn't laugh at my joke. Was it not funny? Perhaps I told it poorly."

I could not answer. My perception of time had already begun to fracture. I was seeing the King's smile before his mouth moved. I was hearing his words in reverse. I was tasting the copper of blood that would not be spilled for another thirty years. The King noticed my distress, frowned with genuine concern, and placed a steadying hand on my shoulder. The King does not know. He believes I am simply prone to distraction. I will never tell him. I love him too much to burden him with the knowledge that merely witnessing his speed broke my ability to perceive time correctly.

**Year 121,403 — Endurance — Doran, Eleventh Court Wizard of the Lineage**

I am writing this because my body feels heavy. Not tired—tired is a mortal sensation that my amplified physiology has not experienced in decades. This is something else. The Patient One poured its entire existence into the assault. It burned through its accumulated entropic reserves, sacrificing eons of stored decay in a desperate, escalating attempt to elicit even the faintest sign of fatigue from the King. It cycled through every conceivable form of attrition: physical exhaustion, mental fatigue, spiritual erosion, conceptual decay, temporal aging, dimensional unraveling.

Lord Godric, inside the cocoon, shifted slightly on his stone bench to get more comfortable. That was the only movement he made.

The Patient One began to exhaust itself. Not in hours or days—the process took less than a minute of external time, though inside the cocoon, an eternity had passed. The entity had burned through its entire reserve. It had spent everything it had accumulated over its ageless existence. Its entropic mass began to thin, to fray, to collapse inward. The cocoon flickered. The Patient One, the being that had outlasted civilizations and dimensions, was running out of endurance.

Lord Godric was not even aware that the assault had reached its peak. He was thinking about what he wanted for dinner. He was considering whether the palace kitchen still had any of the roasted lamb he had enjoyed the previous week. The entropic pressure against his body registered as a faint, almost imperceptible warmth against his skin, like sitting in a patch of late-afternoon sunlight. It had held nothing back. It had spent its entire existence in a single, desperate attempt to make the King feel even the slightest discomfort. Lord Godric stood up from his bench, brushed a stray wisteria petal from his shoulder, and turned to me with a warm smile. "Doran," he said, "would you be so kind as to check with the kitchen? I have a sudden craving for lamb."

The Patient One had compressed an uncountable eternity of absolute entropic assault into sixty seconds of external time. Lord Godric had spent those sixty seconds thinking about dinner. The gap between his endurance and the ultimate expression of attrition in the omniverse was not a gap. It was not even a comparison. It was a category error so fundamental that the Patient One's entire existence had been a meaningless, irrelevant gesture against a wall of infinite persistence that did not even notice the gesture was being made.

I have served Lord Godric for thirty-seven years. I will serve him until I die. I love him, genuinely and deeply, as all of my lineage have loved him. But I will never again use the word "endurance" to describe my own capacity for anything. That word belongs to him, and him alone, and even in his possession, it is a laughably inadequate vessel for what he actually possesses.

**Year 499,882 — Perception — Cael, Nineteenth Court Wizard of the Lineage**

The assassins who called themselves the Unseen Ones were not invisible. Invisibility is a physical state, a bending of light or a manipulation of visual perception. The Unseen Ones had transcended visibility entirely. They existed in a state of absolute perceptual nullity—not merely unseen, but fundamentally imperceivable by any sense, any instrument, any magic, any form of detection that had ever been conceived. They had evolved in the cracks between moments of observation, feeding on the blind spots of omniscient gods, thriving in the gaps where perception failed. They had killed entities that could see all things, because "all things" still implied a boundary, and the Unseen Ones existed outside that boundary.

The King, of course, knew they were coming. His omniscience encompasses everything, including the spaces where perception fails. He allowed them to approach. He allowed them to enter the palace. He allowed them to draw their weapons—blades forged from the concept of being unobserved, sharpened on the whetstone of epistemic darkness. He wanted to test something specific: the capacity of his raw perception stat to simply notice them, without omniscience, without cosmic awareness, using only the biological and magical senses that had been amplifying every Planck second for half a million years.

He stood in the center of the throne room, his back to the main doors, his hands clasped loosely behind him. I was positioned in the observation gallery above, my eyes fixed on the apparently empty chamber. I could see nothing. My thousandfold daily amplification, my centuries-honed magical senses, my inherited bloodline gifts—all of it registered the throne room as completely, utterly vacant save for the King himself.

The Unseen Ones struck. There were twelve of them, positioning themselves around the King in a perfect geometric pattern, their unobserved blades aimed at twelve conceptual weak points that existed only in the theoretical space between perception and reality. Their attack was silent, undetectable, absolutely outside the bounds of any sensory framework.

The King perceived them.

He did not turn his head. He did not narrow his eyes. He did not focus his attention. He simply allowed his passive perception—the raw, unfiltered sensory capacity that had been multiplying by numbers beyond infinite recursion for half a million years—to register their presence the way a normal man might register a bright light in a dark room. There was no effort involved. He could not not perceive them. His perception was so vast, so all-encompassing, that the Unseen Ones' perceptual nullity was not a barrier to be pierced; it was a minor, almost amusing attempt at concealment, like a child hiding behind a single blade of grass and believing themselves invisible.

And the act of being perceived by him destroyed them.

This was not an attack. This was a consequence. The King's perception was not a passive intake of data; it was a force, a pressure, a cosmic assertion of awareness that bent reality around the act of observation. To be perceived by a being whose senses had amplified beyond all infinite scales was to be subjected to a perceptual weight so immense that the target's existence could not withstand the scrutiny. The Unseen Ones had evolved to exist only in the absence of perception. They had no ontological framework for being seen. Their entire biology, their entire conceptual structure, was predicated on the absolute certainty of their imperceptibility.

When the King's perception touched them, they were violently, agonizingly forced into existence. The transition from absolute epistemic darkness to the blinding, crushing light of the King's awareness was not a revelation; it was an annihilation. Their bodies, which had never needed to exist in a state of being observed, were suddenly, brutally materialized into full sensory reality. Skin erupted across forms that had never known skin. Nerves sprouted in bodies that had never known sensation. Eyes bloomed across surfaces that had never known light—dozens of eyes, hundreds of eyes, forced into existence solely to witness their own destruction, to see the King seeing them.

The screams were the first sounds they had ever made. The agony was the first sensation they had ever felt. Their bodies, incompatible with the state of being perceived, began to rapidly, messily reject their own existence. Flesh sloughed off in wet, screaming sheets. Bones that had never needed to be solid shattered under the sudden imposition of physical law. Organs that had never needed to function burst and liquefied, spilling across the polished floor in torrents of black, steaming gore. The twelve assassins did not die quickly. Their newly formed nervous systems, bursting with raw, unfiltered sensory input for the first time, processed every microsecond of their dissolution with agonizing, crystalline clarity.

The King watched for a moment, his expression entirely neutral. Then, with a slight, almost imperceptible shift of his attention, he intensified his perception by an additional infinitesimal fraction—still effectively zero percent of his true capacity, but enough to finish what the initial observation had begun. The air in the kitchen seemed to thicken, charged with an invisible energy that made the floating orbs of magical light pulse and flicker. His golden skin paled to a sickly yellow.

Godric cracked the egg.

The motion was not the awkward, fumbling gesture of a novice. It was not the practiced, precise movement of a master. It was something else entirely—a movement so perfect, so completely and absolutely the ideal expression of egg-cracking, that it seemed to exist outside the normal flow of cause and effect. The shell parted along a line of such mathematical purity that it might have been drawn by a cosmic geometer. The yolk and white slid into the bowl in a single, cohesive mass, their separation so precise that the yolk retained its perfect spherical form even as it settled into the whites.

His fingers closed around the handle, and the whisk seemed to come alive in his grip, as though the tool itself recognized that it was being held by a being of absolute mastery. He whisked the eggs with a motion that generated a sound—a soft, rhythmic whisper—that made Lysander's four knees buckle and his single eye fill with tears.

The butter went into the pan. It melted not into a pool of liquid fat but into a shimmering, golden mirror, its surface so perfectly smooth that it reflected the light of the floating orbs like a polished lens. Godric poured in the eggs. He stirred them with the wooden spatula, and the curds that formed were not the simple, golden lumps of a well-made scramble but something else—something that seemed to glow with an inner radiance, their texture so fine that they appeared to be made of captured sunlight rather than denatured protein.

The scent that filled the kitchen was not the simple, comforting smell of eggs cooking in butter. It was a scent that bypassed the olfactory system entirely and spoke directly to the soul. It smelled like the morning of a perfect childhood, like the first meal after a long illness, like the embrace of a loved one who had been absent for years. Lysander was weeping openly now, his single eye streaming tears that shimmered with the same golden light as the eggs. His four hands were clasped over his chest, and his massive frame was trembling with a combination of ecstasy and despair that Clive had never seen before in any being.

Godric slid the eggs onto a plate. He garnished them with chives that he had chopped—and Clive had not even seen him reach for the knife, had not registered the motion at all, as though the chives had simply willed themselves into perfect, uniform rings—and a sprinkle of sea salt that fell across the golden surface in a pattern of such exquisite precision that it might have been a constellation mapped across a tiny, edible sky.

"There," Godric said, his voice carrying a note of quiet satisfaction. "I think I'm getting the hang of this."

The kitchen was silent. The floating orbs of light had stopped pulsing and now hung motionless in the air, their radiance dimmed, as though they, too, were stunned into stillness. The copper pots had stopped humming. Lysander had collapsed to his knees, his single eye fixed on the plate of scrambled eggs with an expression of absolute, mind-shattered disbelief.

Clive stared at the eggs. His centuries-honed magical senses, amplified a thousandfold daily, could perceive the fundamental structure of matter, the flow of mana, the subtle vibrations of reality itself. They were something that transcended the very concept of cooking, something that existed in a state of absolute, platonic egg-ness that made every other scrambled egg ever created—including Lysander's demonstration, which moments ago had seemed like a masterpiece—look like a crude, primitive, almost offensive approximation.

Godric had not simply made scrambled eggs. He had created the scrambled eggs against which all other scrambled eggs would be measured for the rest of eternity, and the measurement would always find them wanting.

Lysander found his voice. It emerged as a hoarse, trembling whisper, the voice of a being whose entire existence had just been recalibrated. "Your Majesty," he breathed, "how... how did you..."

Lysander made a sound that was half-laugh, half-sob. "Beginner's luck," he repeated, his voice cracking. "Beginner's luck."

He looked at Clive, his single eye wide and desperate, silently pleading for some kind of explanation that would make sense of what he had just witnessed. Clive had no explanation to offer. He was still staring at the eggs, his mind a churning maelstrom of confusion and dawning, terrible understanding.

Godric picked up the plate and carried it to a small table at the edge of the kitchen. He sat down, picked up a fork, and took a bite of the eggs. His eyes closed in genuine, unfeigned pleasure. "Oh, that is good," he said, his voice muffled by the food. "That is really very good. Lysander, you have taught me well."

The tension that had gripped the kitchen dissolved, replaced by a warm, cheerful buzz of conversation. The head chef had taught the King to cook, and the King had made a fine dish on his first try. What a charming story. What a wonderful memory.

They genuinely believed it. They genuinely accepted the explanation of beginner's luck. They looked at Godric, sitting at the small table, eating his eggs with the contented expression of a man who had discovered a pleasant new hobby, and they saw nothing more than a kindly ruler who had tried something new and succeeded beyond expectations.

Clive looked at them, and then at Godric, and then at the plate of eggs that glowed with an inner radiance that no amount of beginner's luck could possibly explain, and he felt something inside his mind begin to crack.

The gardening lesson was worse.

Marak had spent the morning preparing the small private garden behind the eastern wing—a modest space by palace standards, perhaps fifty meters square, filled with raised beds of rich black soil and trellises heavy with climbing roses and borders of lavender and rosemary that released their fragrance into the warm afternoon air. The former Unmaker of Suns had been trembling with nervous excitement since dawn, his once-cosmic form—now compressed into the shape of a weathered, gray-haired gardener—moving through the beds with the obsessive precision of a being who had dedicated his entire existence to the cultivation of plants.

When Godric arrived, Marak dropped to his knees in the soil, his hands clasped before him, his eyes fixed on the ground. "Your Majesty," he whispered, "I am unworthy of this honor."

Godric lifted him gently to his feet, brushing the soil from his gardener's tunic. "Marak, we've known each other for longer than most universes have existed. You have nothing to prove to me. Now, show me how to prune a rosebush."

Marak nodded, his throat working as he swallowed his emotion. He led Godric to the nearest trellis, where a magnificent white rosebush was in full bloom, its blossoms heavy and fragrant and luminous in the golden light. "You must be gentle but decisive," Marak said, his voice soft and reverent. "The rose trusts the gardener. A hesitant cut is more damaging than a bold one, because it leaves the plant uncertain of how to heal."

Godric nodded, his expression serious and attentive. He took the pruning shears from Marak's trembling hands and approached the rosebush with the careful, slightly uncertain posture of a complete amateur. He examined the canes, frowning in concentration, tracing the branches with his fingers.

"This one, I think," he said, pointing to a thick, woody cane that crossed through the center of the bush. "It's in the way. It should come out."

"An excellent choice, Your Majesty," Marak said, his voice filled with the desperate, aching need to encourage. "Cut just above that outward-facing bud, at an angle."

Godric positioned the shears. For a long moment, he hesitated, his hands wavering, his expression that of a man who was genuinely uncertain about what he was doing. Clive watched, his heart hammering against his ribs, as the seconds stretched into an uncomfortable silence.

And then Godric made the cut.

It was not the uncertain, wavering cut of a novice. It was not the practiced, efficient cut of an expert. It was a cut that existed on a plane of horticultural perfection so elevated that the concept of pruning itself seemed to have been redefined in that single instant. The shears closed around the cane with a soft, musical snick, and the cut was so clean, so precise, so absolutely in harmony with the biological structure of the plant that the rosebush seemed to sigh with relief. The cane fell away, and the wound it left behind was not a wound at all but a perfect, gleaming surface that immediately began to seal itself with a thin layer of protective tissue.

The rosebush responded. Not gradually, over the course of days or weeks, but immediately, visibly, in that single instant. New buds swelled along the remaining canes, their green tips pushing through the bark with the urgency of a plant that had suddenly, joyfully realized it was being cared for by a being of absolute mastery. The existing blossoms—already beautiful—deepened in color, their petals unfurling further, their fragrance intensifying until the air in the garden was thick with the scent of roses, a perfume so pure and complex and overwhelming that it felt like breathing liquid beauty.

The other plants in the garden responded as well. The lavender, which had been blooming modestly along the borders, erupted into a cascade of purple flowers that shimmered with an almost iridescent luminescence. The rosemary, which had been a well-tended but unremarkable culinary herb, transformed into a lush, aromatic thicket that released waves of fragrance so potent that Marak's eyes began to water. The soil in the raised beds shifted and settled, its texture improving visibly, its dark richness deepening as though it had been amended with the finest compost for a thousand years.

Marak had collapsed. Not dramatically—he had simply folded into himself, his compressed cosmic form sinking to the ground, his weathered hands pressed flat against the soil, his eyes wide and streaming with tears. He was staring at the rosebush with an expression that combined transcendent joy and absolute, soul-shattering despair.

"Beginner's luck," Godric said, turning to Marak with a sheepish, almost apologetic smile. "You're a wonderful teacher. The rosebush responded well to your earlier care, I think."

Marak made a sound that was not quite a laugh and not quite a sob. "My earlier care," he repeated, his voice hollow. "Your Majesty, I have been tending this rosebush for longer than the lifespan of most galaxies. It has never bloomed like this. It has never even approached blooming like this. The fragrance alone is... it is..."

He stopped, unable to continue, his voice choked with the overwhelming impossibility of what he was experiencing.

Godric looked puzzled. "Well, perhaps it was just ready to bloom. Plants have their own timing." He turned his attention to a nearby lavender bush, which immediately began to tremble—actually tremble, its stems quivering with an almost animal eagerness—as his shadow fell across it. "Would you like to show me how to harvest lavender? I've always loved the scent."

Marak nodded, unable to speak, and rose on shaking legs to continue the lesson. The other gardeners, who had been watching from the edges of the garden, exchanged delighted looks. The King was learning to garden! And he had such a natural touch! The rosebush had bloomed so beautifully! What a charming, wonderful thing to witness.

They believed it. They genuinely, completely, unreservedly believed it. They saw a kindly ruler who had discovered a hidden talent for horticulture, and they found it charming, and they went back to their work with warm hearts and smiling faces.

Clive stood at the edge of the garden, his hands trembling at his sides, and felt the crack inside his mind widen into a chasm.

The painting lesson was the final straw.

Elara, the chief sculptor and painter of the Eternal Palace, was a being of living stone, her body carved from a single block of translucent crystal that had been mined from the heart of a dead star before the first galaxies had formed. She had created artworks that had moved gods to tears, that had captured the essence of cosmic beauty in forms so pure and perfect that viewers had been known to achieve spontaneous enlightenment simply by gazing upon them. She was, by any measure, the greatest artist in the history of the omniverse.

She had set up an easel in the grand hall, with canvases and paints and brushes arranged on a nearby table. The subject she had chosen was a simple still life—a bowl of fruit, a vase of flowers, a draped cloth—the kind of exercise that beginning painters had been practicing since the first sentient being had picked up a brush.

Godric approached the easel with the same eager, slightly uncertain energy he had brought to the kitchen and the garden. He examined the brushes with genuine curiosity, testing their bristles against his fingertips, reading the labels on the paint tubes with the focused concentration of a scholar studying an ancient text. He listened attentively as Elara explained the principles of composition and color theory, nodding along, asking questions that seemed—to Clive's increasingly desperate ears—suspiciously insightful for a complete beginner.

"I think I understand," Godric said finally, picking up a brush. "You start with the broad shapes and work toward the details. Light to dark. General to specific. It's a bit like strategy, isn't it? The big picture first, then the fine points."

"Exactly, Your Majesty," Elara said, her crystalline voice chiming with approval. "You have a natural grasp of the concept. Please, begin."

Godric dipped his brush into the paint. He raised it toward the canvas. And Clive watched as the same invisible energy that had filled the kitchen and the garden began to gather in the grand hall, thickening the air, making the light shift and pulse, causing the very stone of the walls to hum with sympathetic vibration.

Godric painted.

The brush moved across the canvas, and the strokes that appeared were not the hesitant, clumsy marks of a beginner. They were not the practiced, confident marks of a master. They were something that existed beyond the very concept of skill, something that made the word "painting" feel like a laughably inadequate description of what was happening. Each stroke was a perfect, irreducible expression of artistic intention, a moment of pure visual truth that captured not merely the appearance of the subject but its essential nature, its platonic ideal, its cosmic significance.

The bowl of fruit emerged on the canvas—an apple, a pear, a cluster of grapes—and the apple was not merely an apple. It was the apple, the ur-apple, the perfect fruit against which all other apples would be measured and found wanting. The light that fell across its surface was not painted light but actual, luminous radiance that seemed to glow from within the canvas. The grapes were not merely clusters of purple pigment but individual spheres of captured juice and sweetness and summer heat, their skins so perfectly rendered that they appeared wet to the touch.

The vase of flowers—the same flowers that Godric had caused to bloom in the garden—emerged next, and their petals shimmered with a translucence that should have been impossible to achieve with mere paint. The draped cloth fell in folds so natural, so perfectly modeled, that the canvas itself seemed to recede, replaced by an actual, three-dimensional window into a miniature world of transcendent beauty.

Elara had stopped speaking. Her crystalline body, which had stood motionless for eons in the presence of cosmic wonders, was trembling. Tiny fractures were appearing along the surfaces of her limbs, spreading in fine, hairline cracks that caught the light and scattered it in prismatic patterns. She was an artist of supreme mastery, and she was looking at a work of art so far beyond her capacity that her very structure was failing under the weight of its perfection.

The other palace staff who had gathered to watch—cooks and gardeners and weavers and musicians—gasped and applauded as the painting took shape. They saw a king who had discovered a hidden talent. They saw a charmingly unexpected skill. They saw a work of beauty emerging from the brush of their beloved sovereign, and they were delighted.

Godric stepped back from the easel, setting down his brush with a satisfied sigh. "There," he said, his voice carrying that familiar note of quiet pleasure. "I think that's finished. Beginner's luck again, I suppose." He turned to Elara with a warm, genuine smile. "Thank you, Elara. You're a magnificent teacher."

Elara looked at the painting. She looked at Godric. She looked at the fine, hairline cracks spreading across her crystalline arms. "Beginner's luck," she whispered, and her voice, which had never wavered in a billion years of existence, was trembling. "Yes, Your Majesty. Beginner's luck."

The spectators burst into enthusiastic applause. They surrounded Godric, congratulating him on his natural talent, marveling at the beauty of the painting, remarking on what a wonderful and unexpected gift their King possessed. Godric accepted their praise with genuine modesty, insisting again and again that it was simply beginner's luck, that Elara and Lysander and Marak were the real artists, that he had merely followed their excellent instruction.

And they believed him. Clive could see it in their eyes, in their smiles, in the easy, comfortable way they interacted with their sovereign. They had witnessed something that should have shattered their understanding of reality—a being capable of creating, on his first attempt, a work of art so perfect that the greatest artist in the omniverse had literally begun to crack under the strain of witnessing it—and they had processed it as charming beginner's luck.

Clive stood at the back of the crowd, his hands shaking, his heart pounding, his mind a churning maelstrom of dissonance and horror and dawning, terrible understanding. He looked at the painting, which glowed on the easel with an inner light that no amount of beginner's luck could possibly explain. He looked at Godric, who was laughing and accepting congratulations with the warm, unassuming demeanor of a man who genuinely believed he had just gotten extraordinarily fortunate on his first attempt. He looked at the palace staff, who had resumed their duties with beaming faces, chattering happily about the King's wonderful hidden talent.

And he understood.

Godric had not been learning. Godric had been pretending. Every fumbling attempt, every awkward gesture, every moment of apparent beginner's confusion had been a performance—a perfect, flawless, absolutely convincing performance of mortal ineptitude, delivered by a being whose mastery of every skill in existence had been multiplying beyond all infinite scales for a span of time that could not be written. He could have cooked the perfect scrambled eggs on the first try, without hesitation, without error, without any of the theatrical fumbling that had preceded the final, transcendent result. He could have pruned the rosebush to perfection in a single, instantaneous cut. He could have painted the still life with a brilliance that would have made Elara's greatest masterpieces look like crude cave drawings.

But he had not. He had pretended to struggle. He had pretended to be a beginner. He had pretended to need the instruction of his servants, because he understood—with the infinite wisdom that had also been multiplying forever—that those servants needed to be needed. They needed to believe that they had something to offer him, some skill or talent that he valued precisely because he could not replicate it himself. Their sanity, their purpose, their ability to exist in his presence without collapsing into the abyss of their own insignificance, depended on that belief.

And Godric, in his infinite kindness and his infinite mercy and his infinite, terrifying capacity for deception, had preserved that belief for eons. He had complimented their cooking, their gardening, their art, their music, not because he could not do what they did, but because he wanted them to feel that their contributions mattered. He had allowed them their pride, their dignity, their sense of meaning. He had played the part of the appreciative, incapable master so perfectly that not one of them had ever suspected the truth.

Until today. Until Clive had organized these lessons. Until Godric, caught up in the moment, had accidentally—or perhaps deliberately, or perhaps the distinction had ceased to matter for a being who existed outside the normal flow of cause and effect—allowed a infinitesimal fraction of his true mastery to slip through the performance. And even then, even when the evidence of his infinite capability was glowing on the easel before them, blooming in the garden behind them, perfuming the air of the kitchen they had just left, the servants had refused to see it. They had accepted the explanation of beginner's luck because the alternative—that their King had been pretending to need them for eons, that their entire existence was a gentle, merciful fiction—was too devastating to contemplate.

Clive was not so fortunate. Clive had seen. Clive had understood. And Clive was now standing in the grand hall of the Eternal Palace, surrounded by the cheerful chatter of the palace staff, drowning in a sea of existential horror that had no bottom and no shore.

He walked, somewhat unsteadily, toward the corridor that led to his private quarters. He needed to sit down. He needed to think. He needed to somehow process the revelation that had just shattered his understanding of everything he had believed about his sovereign and his service and his place in the cosmic order.

He passed Zha'tal in the corridor, the former Supreme Logician carrying a stack of newly catalogued books. "Lord Clive," she said, bowing her luminous head. "Isn't it wonderful about His Majesty's painting? Such a natural talent. We are so fortunate to serve such a gifted King."

Clive stared at her. Her mathematical form flickered with genuine, uncomplicated happiness. She believed it. She fully, completely, unreservedly believed that Godric had simply discovered an unexpected aptitude for art.

"Yes," Clive heard himself say. "Wonderful."

He continued walking. His legs carried him to his quarters, where he closed the door behind him and sank into the worn leather armchair that had belonged to his father, and his grandfather, and his great-grandfather, stretching back in an unbroken chain to the first court wizard, the legendary Blake himself.

He sat in the silence of his room, his mind a churning maelstrom, and he tried to find a stable place to stand.

Godric could cook better than the greatest chef in the omniverse. Godric could garden better than the most devoted horticulturist. Godric could paint and sculpt and weave and sing and perform every mortal skill at a level of mastery so far beyond the combined capabilities of all creation that the comparison could not be expressed. And he had been hiding this—not out of deceit, not out of malice, but out of kindness. Out of mercy. Out of a genuine, sincere desire to give his servants something to do, something to be proud of, something that made their existence meaningful in the face of his absolute, incomprehensible supremacy.

And Clive had, in his ignorance, organized lessons to teach Godric these skills. He had, with the best of intentions, forced Godric to reveal a fraction of his true capacity. He had accidentally peeled back the curtain on the gentle, merciful fiction that had sustained the palace staff for eons.

But they had not seen. They had not understood. They had looked at the evidence and accepted the comforting lie. Only Clive had seen the truth, and Clive was now sitting alone in his quarters, his mind cracking under the weight of a revelation that he could not share with anyone.

He thought of all the times he had watched Godric compliment the cooking, praise the gardening, admire the art. He thought of all the times he had believed, with comfortable certainty, that these compliments were genuine because Godric genuinely could not do what his servants did. And he realized, with a cold, settling finality, that the compliments had been genuine—genuinely kind, genuinely appreciative, genuinely meant to make his servants feel valued—but the incapacity had been a lie. A mercy. A gift.

Godric could have solved every problem in the omniverse in zero seconds with zero effort at any point since Year Zero. He could have instantly subjugated every civilization, ruled every dimension, created a perfect reality where suffering and conflict and struggle were impossible. He had chosen not to. Not because he could not, but because he understood—with his infinite wisdom, which had been multiplying alongside everything else—that perfection was a kind of death. That purpose required struggle. That meaning required the capacity to fail, to learn, to grow, to achieve.

And so he had restrained himself. He had held back. He had played the part of a king who needed his subjects, a ruler who valued his servants, a being who, despite his cosmic power, still required the small, humble skills of mortal hands and mortal minds. He had given them the gift of being needed, and he had protected that gift for eons with a performance so perfect that not one of his subjects had ever suspected the truth.

Until Clive had forced his hand. Until Clive had organized cooking lessons and gardening lessons and painting lessons. Until Godric, caught up in the moment—or perhaps, Clive suddenly wondered, deliberately allowing himself to be caught up, knowing that Clive needed to understand, that Clive's comfortable certainties had become a barrier to true comprehension—had let a infinitesimal fraction of his true mastery slip through.

And even then, even with the evidence glowing before their eyes, the servants had believed the lie. They had accepted beginner's luck as an explanation for the impossible. They had gone back to their duties with warm hearts and smiling faces, their comfortable certainties intact.

Only Clive had seen. Only Clive had understood. And Clive was now sitting alone in his quarters, staring at the wall, his mind a shattered landscape of revelations that he could not share with anyone, because sharing them would destroy the very mercy that Godric had spent eons cultivating.

A soft knock sounded at his door. "Clive?" Godric's voice, warm and gentle and exactly as it had always been. "Are you in there? You left rather suddenly after the painting lesson. I wanted to make sure you were all right."

Clive opened the door. Godric stood in the corridor, his hands clasped behind his back, his expression one of genuine, fatherly concern. He looked exactly as he always looked—a ruggedly handsome forty-five-year-old man with short salt-and-pepper hair and a neatly trimmed beard, his heavily muscled frame draped in simple dark garments that hugged the contours of his chest and shoulders. There was a smear of blue paint on his sleeve, and a rose petal caught in his hair, and he smelled faintly of butter and lavender.

"Your Majesty," Clive said, his voice steady despite the churning chaos of his mind. "I am fine. I simply needed a moment to... process."

Godric's warm brown eyes studied him for a long moment. There was something in those eyes—a depth, a knowing, an infinite, patient understanding—that made Clive's heart clench with a mixture of love and dread that he had never felt so intensely before.

"It was beginner's luck, you know," Godric said, and his voice was light, almost playful, but his eyes held Clive's with an intensity that belied the casual words. "I've never done any of those things before. Lysander and Marak and Elara are excellent teachers. I simply followed their instructions."

Clive stared at him. He understood, in that moment, what Godric was offering. A choice. An escape. The same comforting lie that the servants had accepted, extended now to his court wizard. Godric was telling him that he could believe the beginner's luck explanation. He could join the servants in their warm, comfortable certainty. He could go back to believing that his King was a cosmic power who could not cook an egg, prune a rose, or paint a still life. The door to that belief was still open. Godric was holding it open for him.

And Clive realized, with a clarity that cut through the churning chaos of his mind, that he could not walk through it. He had seen too much. He had understood too deeply. The comforting lie would no longer comfort him, because he would always know it was a lie.

"Beginner's luck," Clive repeated, his voice flat. "Of course, Your Majesty."

Godric smiled. It was the same warm, crinkly-eyed smile he had always worn, the smile that had been a constant in Clive's life since childhood, the smile that had greeted him every morning for thirty-two years of service. But now Clive could see, layered beneath the warmth, something else—a quiet, knowing amusement, an infinite patience, a gentle, almost tender acknowledgment of the truth that they both now shared.

"You're a good man, Clive," Godric said softly. "Blake would be proud of you. I know I am."

He turned and walked back down the corridor, humming a tuneless melody, pausing to admire a tapestry that one of the weavers had recently completed. The weaver, who was passing by, beamed with pride at the King's attention.

Clive closed the door of his quarters and leaned his forehead against the cool wood. His mind was still churning, still cracking, still struggling to contain the revelation that had been forced into it. But beneath the chaos, a strange, unexpected calm was beginning to settle.

Godric could have done everything. He could have been everything. He could have instantly, effortlessly, perfectly solved every problem in the omniverse and created a flawless reality where no one ever struggled or failed or grew. He had chosen not to, because he understood that struggle and failure and growth were the foundations of meaning. He had chosen to need his servants, not because he could not do what they did, but because they needed to be needed. He had chosen to play the part of an appreciative, incapable master, because that performance was the greatest gift he could give them: the gift of purpose.

And Clive, alone among the palace staff, now understood the truth behind the performance. He could see the infinite, perfect mastery that lurked behind every fumbling gesture, every awkward attempt, every moment of apparent beginner's confusion. He could see the gentle, merciful fiction that Godric had maintained for eons. And he could see, with terrifying clarity, that this fiction was not a deception but a kindness—perhaps the greatest kindness that a being of absolute power could offer to the beings who served him.

Godric did not need his servants. He never had. He never would. But he loved them enough to pretend otherwise, and he had been pretending for so long, with such perfect, flawless consistency, that the pretense had become indistinguishable from reality. The servants believed they were needed. The servants found purpose and meaning and dignity in their service. And Godric, with his infinite wisdom and his infinite power and his infinite capacity for restraint, had given them that gift without ever letting them suspect the truth.

Clive walked to his desk and sat down. His hands were still trembling, but the trembling was beginning to subside. He pulled a sheet of parchment toward him and picked up his quill. There was a journal he needed to write—a new entry for the archive, a record of what he had witnessed and understood. Future court wizards would read it, as he had read the journals of his ancestors, and they would add its revelations to the accumulated weight of impossible knowledge that every wizard of Blake's lineage carried.

He dipped the quill in ink. He began to write.

He turned to me, his expression almost conspiratorial. "I've been distracting myself from the omniscient awareness of their preparations. I wanted to be surprised. I wanted to experience this the way a mortal audience member would experience it—with genuine uncertainty, genuine anticipation, genuine emotional investment. And it's working, Osvald. It's actually working. I have no idea what's going to happen next."

The charge attack reached its final, ultimate phase. The Spear of Finality, now glowing with the combined narrative weight of every fictional universe in existence, reached its maximum power threshold. The music hit its final, shattering crescendo. The characters, their forms now barely visible within the blinding radiance of the weapon, unleashed their collective battle cry—a sound that was simultaneously every triumphant shout and every desperate scream and every defiant roar that had ever been written, drawn, filmed, or coded.

And the spear was thrown.

It crossed the distance between the Gathered Fictions and the Eternal Palace in zero time. Not fast—instantaneous. It did not travel through space. It traveled through narrative, through the fundamental structure of story itself, seeking its target not through the physical universe but through the plot that bound all of existence together. It was the ultimate weapon, the final attack, the ending that could not be rewritten.

It struck Godric directly in the center of his chest.

The impact was silent. Not because it was small, but because the sound of it was so vast that it transcended the capacity of any medium to carry it. The entire omniverse shuddered. Reality itself flickered, as though the fundamental code of existence had encountered a fatal error. The sky above the Eternal Palace went white, then black, then white again, cycling through every possible visual state as the narrative weapon attempted to impose its ending on a being that had no ending.

And then the light faded, and the sound died, and the Gathered Fictions stared at the balcony where their ultimate attack had landed, and they saw Godric standing exactly where he had been standing before, his hands still resting on the obsidian rail, his expression unchanged, his tunic unruffled, his short salt-and-pepper hair undisturbed by even the faintest breeze.

He was not injured. He was not marked. He was not even slightly inconvenienced. The Spear of Finality—the combined narrative weight of every story ever told, the ultimate attack of every fictional universe in existence—had struck him with its full, absolute, maximum power, and it had done nothing. Not a scratch. Not a flicker. Not even the faintest, most infinitesimal tickle.

Godric's expression shifted. The delight drained from his face, replaced by something that I had never seen on his features before. Disappointment. Deep, genuine, almost heartbreaking disappointment. His warm brown eyes, which had been sparkling with anticipation only moments before, grew flat and dull. His mouth, which had been curved in a boyish grin, pressed into a thin, tight line.

"That was it?" he said, his voice quiet and hollow. "That was the final attack? The ultimate weapon? The climax of every story ever told?"

He stared at the Gathered Fictions, his eyes sweeping across the vast, colorful armada that filled the sky. The characters, still reeling from the failure of their ultimate attack, stared back at him with expressions of dawning, terrible horror. The spiky-haired warrior had stopped screaming. The wizard had dropped his wand. The spacefaring commander had fallen silent. The giant ape had reverted to its base form, its golden fur fading to ordinary brown.

"I waited for you," Godric said, his voice carrying the quiet, dangerous calm of a man who had been promised a feast and served an empty plate. "I let you prepare. I let you charge. I distracted myself from my own omniscience so that I could experience your attack as a genuine surprise. I was patient. I was excited. I genuinely believed that you—the combined narrative weight of every fictional universe in existence—would give me something I had never experienced before. A spectacle worthy of the ultimate final boss."

He paused, and when he spoke again, his voice was thick with a disappointment so profound that it felt like a physical weight pressing down on the entire omniverse. "You were supposed to be the best. You were supposed to give me a show. You had the music. You had the colors. You had the phases. You had the dramatic speeches and the character arcs and the narrative stakes. All the ingredients for the greatest climactic battle in the history of existence. And you failed. You failed completely, utterly, absolutely. You couldn't even make me feel a tickle."

He straightened up from the balcony rail, his heavily muscled frame casting a shadow that seemed to stretch across the entire sky. His expression was no longer merely disappointed. It had shifted into something colder, something harder, something that I recognized from the oldest journals in the archive—the face of the King who had annihilated his first opposition without moving a muscle.

"Let me show you," Godric said, his voice soft and terrible, "what a real final attack looks like."

And then he allowed an infinitesimal fraction of his strength to express itself.

I need to be precise here. I need to describe what happened in exact, clinical detail, because if I do not, I will never be able to process it, and it will haunt me for the rest of my life. The King had been using effectively zero percent of his power for the entire confrontation—had been using effectively zero percent of his power for the entire span of his existence, in every battle, every conquest, every annihilation that the journals had recorded. The fraction he now allowed to surface was so small that calling it zero percent was an overstatement so vast that it would require an infinite number of universes to express the error. It was effectively nothing. A rounding error on a rounding error on an infinity of rounding errors.

The effect was immediate. The effect was absolute. The effect was the complete and total annihilation of everything.

The Gathered Fictions had erected shields. Of course they had. They were not foolish enough to launch their ultimate attack without preparing their defenses. They had woven together every protective barrier from every fictional universe—magical wards and technological force fields, divine blessings and conceptual armor, narrative plot armor and gameplay invincibility frames. Their shields were layered an infinite number of times, each layer reinforced by the combined power of every hero's determination and every villain's cunning and every sidekick's loyalty and every comic relief character's inexplicable survival instincts. They had spent as much time preparing their defenses as they had their attack, and they had believed, with absolute certainty, that their shields would hold against anything.

The shields did not hold. They did not even register as an obstacle.

The infinitesimal expression of Godric's strength did not strike the shields. It did not test them or strain them or break through them. It simply ignored them. The shields, which had been designed to withstand the collapse of universes and the unraveling of reality and the erasure of narrative itself, were not even acknowledged by the pressure of Godric's strength. They were there, and then they were not, and the transition between those two states was so instantaneous, so effortless, so completely devoid of any sense of impact or resistance, that the shields might as well have never existed at all.

The Gathered Fictions were next. Every character, every ship, every weapon, every entity that had joined the coalition—all of them, simultaneously, experienced the full, unfiltered pressure of Godric's strength.

The spiky-haired warrior, still in his ultimate transformation, was the first to go. His body, which had withstood planetary explosions and galactic collisions and attacks that could shatter the fabric of space-time, was compressed by the ambient pressure of Godric's strength into a sphere smaller than a quark. The compression was not gradual. It was not gentle. It was instantaneous, absolute, and accompanied by a wet, sickening crunch as every bone in his body shattered simultaneously, every muscle was pulverized into a fine, bloody paste, every organ was liquefied and forced out through his collapsing orifices in a spray of crimson mist that was itself immediately compressed into the growing singularity of absolute pressure. His trademark spiky hair, which had defied gravity and physics and all sense of aesthetic proportion for the entirety of his fictional existence, was flattened against his collapsing skull, then crushed into his pulverizing brain, then compressed into the infinitesimal point of infinite mass that was all that remained of him.

The wizard with the round glasses died next, his wand snapping in his hand, his spectacles shattering against his face, his scar—that famous, iconic scar—tearing open as his skull collapsed inward. He tried to cast one final spell, his lips forming the syllables of a defensive charm that had saved his life a hundred times before. The spell did not even manifest. The pressure of Godric's strength was so absolute that magic itself could not exist in its presence—every spell, every charm, every enchantment that had ever been woven in the history of his fictional universe was simultaneously, instantly negated, erased, unmade. The wizard's body followed his magic, compressing into a wet, screaming point of pulverized flesh and shattered bone and spilled blood that was immediately absorbed into the growing singularity.

The spacefaring commander, standing on the bridge of his flagship, had just enough time to register the failure of his shields before the pressure reached him. His body was not merely compressed—it was de-atomized. Every molecule, every atom, every subatomic particle that constituted his physical form was simultaneously torn apart and crushed together, creating a state of matter that violated every law of physics that his fictional universe had ever established. His uniform, that crisp, iconic uniform with its insignia of rank and its perfect tailoring, was shredded into its component threads, and the threads were shredded into their component fibers, and the fibers were shredded into their component molecules, and the molecules were compressed into the growing singularity along with the rest of him. His final thought, before his brain was de-atomized into a cloud of compressed neural matter, was a desperate, futile hope that his sacrifice would mean something.

The giant ape-creature, its golden fur now matted with blood, tried to roar one final time. Its vocal cords snapped. Its lungs collapsed. Its massive chest, which had housed a heart that had beaten with the power of a sun, caved inward, the ribs splintering and piercing the pulverizing organs within. Its eyes, those expressive, soulful eyes that had conveyed more emotion than any dialogue ever could, burst from their sockets, the vitreous fluid vaporizing instantly into the pressure. Its body, which had dwarfed mountains and wrestled gods, was compressed into an infinitesimal point of absolute nothingness.

The others followed. All of them. Every hero. Every villain. Every sidekick. Every comic relief character. Every giant robot and every magical girl and every gritty antihero and every lovable rogue. Every character from every story that had ever been told. They died in a single, simultaneous instant of absolute, inescapable annihilation. Their bodies were compressed. Their souls were extinguished. Their narratives were ended. But the annihilation did not stop at the Gathered Fictions. The infinitesimal expression of Godric's strength radiated outward from the Eternal Palace, passing through the surrounding space, the surrounding multiverse, the surrounding omniverse, and everything that existed within it.

And Godric, standing on the balcony of the Eternal Palace, reached out with his thumb and forefinger and picked it up.

They will accept what I am, and what they are, and the relationship between us. And that will be the end of opposition."

He was right, of course. He is always right. No one has dared to oppose him since. No one ever will again.

But that is not the most terrifying part of this account. The most terrifying part is what happened next.

Godric restored the omniverse. With a casual thought, with zero effort, with the same absent-minded attention one might give to straightening a crooked picture frame, he recreated everything that had been destroyed. Every galaxy, every star, every planet, every civilization, every dimension, every timeline, every reality—all of it, restored to perfect, pristine condition. Every being who had been annihilated in the compression was returned to existence, their memories intact, their lives continuing as though nothing had happened.

Except for the Gathered Fictions. They remained compressed within the marble trophy, their infinite mass contained within its transparent barrier, their consciousnesses trapped forever in the eternal, agonizing instant of their annihilation. They had been the direct opposition, and the King is not in the habit of restoring those he has chosen to destroy.

But here is the horror, the deep, existential dread that has settled into my bones and will never leave. The trophy still contains the original omniverse. The compressed mass of everything that existed—the stars and the galaxies and the civilizations and the timelines and the realities—is still there, trapped in the marble, pulsing with its faint, internal light. And yet, the omniverse around us is also real. The restored reality is identical to the original in every detail. Every atom, every soul, every moment—all of it, perfectly replicated.

So which is real? The original, compressed into infinite density within the King's pocket? Or the restoration, recreated from nothing by the King's will? Are we living in the true omniverse, or are we living in a copy? Does the distinction even matter? Can we ever know? Do we want to know?

The King can restore the entire omniverse from absolute nothingness in zero seconds with zero effort. He can create an exact duplicate of all of existence while the original remains compressed in a marble that he keeps as a souvenir. The power to do this—the power to treat the fundamental nature of reality as a trivial, interchangeable commodity—is so far beyond mortal comprehension that the very attempt to grasp it induces a vertigo of the soul.

I have served the King for twelve years. I will serve him until I die. I love him, as all of Blake's descendants have loved him. But I will never look at the restored reality around me without wondering if it is the original or the copy. And the King, in his infinite and occasionally terrifying wisdom, will never tell me, because he knows that the uncertainty is part of the gift he has given us—the gift of not knowing, of not having to confront the full, unfiltered truth of our own existence.

The marble sits on his shelf now, among other trophies from other annihilations. I passed it this morning on my way to his study. It was pulsing with its faint, internal light, and I could almost hear the screams.

Clive closed the journal. His hands were trembling so violently that the aged leather cover rattled against his fingers. The archive was silent around him, the soft, eternal hum of the palace's ambient magic the only sound in the vast, vaulted chamber.

He had known, intellectually, that Godric had restored the omniverse many times. How many times had reality been destroyed and recreated? How many versions of the omniverse had existed, each one erased and replaced by an identical copy? How many times had Clive himself been annihilated and restored, his consciousness seamlessly transferred from the destroyed original to the perfect duplicate, his memories intact, his existence continuing without any awareness of the transition?

He looked at the journal in his hands. Osvald had written it in the original omniverse—the one that was now compressed into the marble trophy on Godric's shelf. Or had he? Had the journal been restored along with everything else, a perfect copy of the original, identical in every detail? Did the distinction even matter, when the copy was so flawless that no possible test could distinguish it from the original?

The horror was not that the restored reality might be a copy. The horror was that Godric could make a copy so perfect that the question had no answer. The horror was that the distinction between original and restoration was meaningless in the face of his power. He could destroy and recreate existence an infinite number of times, and no one would ever know, and the continuity of consciousness and memory and identity would be preserved so flawlessly that the very concept of "original" became incoherent.

Clive rose from his chair, the journal still clutched in his trembling hands. He walked through the silent archive, past the hundreds of volumes written by his ancestors, past the crystallized moments of ancient history that lined the walls, past the tapestries and sculptures and paintings that represented the accumulated artistic achievement of an omniverse that might have been destroyed and restored more times than anyone could count.

He stepped out into the corridor, and the golden light of the eternal palace's artificial sun washed over him, and the distant sound of Godric's laughter—warm and rich and utterly, terrifyingly human—echoed from somewhere deeper in the palace.

The King had the original omniverse in his pocket. Or on his shelf. Or wherever he kept his trophies these days. And the restored omniverse—the one Clive was standing in right now—was so perfectly identical that no test could ever reveal the difference.

Clive walked toward the sound of the laughter. He walked toward the dining hall where the lamb would be excellent tonight, as it always was. He walked toward the King who loved him and terrified him and had annihilated him and restored him more times than he could ever know.

And he carried the journal with him, because he needed to put it back on the shelf, and because he needed to remember what Osvald had written, and because the uncertainty of his own existence was a weight that he would carry for the rest of his life, alongside all the other weights that every court wizard of Blake's lineage had carried before him.

He wondered if Osvald's original was trapped inside it, compressed into infinite density, screaming into the eternal silence of absolute pressure. He wondered if he himself was trapped inside it, from some earlier annihilation that no journal had recorded. He wondered if any of it mattered, when the restoration was so perfect that even the concept of "original" had lost all meaning.

Godric's laughter echoed again, warm and rich and utterly without malice. The lamb would be excellent tonight. It always was.
