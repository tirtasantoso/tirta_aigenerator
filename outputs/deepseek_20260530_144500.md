# The Inheritance of Dread

I am Kaedan. I am twenty-two years old. My magical reserves were already staggering by any mortal standard, my physical strength sufficient to crack continents, my speed a blur that could outpace light itself. I could suppress it all to the level of a normal man, and I did—constantly, carefully, with the desperate concentration of a man holding back a collapsing dam with his bare palms. But none of that mattered. Not here. Not in this room. Not on this day.

My father, Malachai, had retired from his post as High Court Wizard exactly three days ago. I had watched my father kneel before the throne, his silver hair gleaming in the sunlight, his face lined with the profound exhaustion of a man who had seen things that no mortal mind should ever see. When he rose, he had clasped my shoulder with a grip that was far too tight, his knuckles white, his eyes wet with a mixture of pride and something else. Something that looked horrifyingly like pity.

"You will read them all," he had said, his voice a low, urgent rasp that only I could hear. "Every single journal. Every single page. This is not a request, Kaedan. This is not a tradition that you may choose to observe. This is a requirement. A condition of your service. Without this knowledge, you will not be able to protect your sanity when you witness what you will inevitably witness. Do you understand?"

I had nodded, not understanding at all. How could I? I had grown up in the palace corridors. My father had not answered. He had simply looked at me with those tired, haunted eyes, and he had handed me the heavy iron key to the reading room beneath the eastern spire.

That was three days ago. Now, I sat at the ancient wooden table in the center of the cold obsidian chamber, surrounded by towering shelves that groaned beneath the weight of thousands of leather-bound journals. Each spine was stamped with a year, a name, and a single word denoting the stat that had been tested. The gods—the self-proclaimed supreme beings of their respective fictional multiverses, entities who had created and destroyed entire realities with their thoughts—felt their divine forms crushed into shapes that no geometry could describe, their omnipotence revealed as a laughable delusion, their immortality revoked by the simple, casual presence of a being whose strength made the concept of "strength" irrelevant.

The abstract entities—the ones who had no physical form, who existed as pure concepts, pure narratives, pure ideas—were not spared. Godric's strength did not merely affect the physical. It affected the conceptual. It reached into the narrative framework that gave these beings existence and crushed that too, compressing their stories, their themes, their character arcs, their dramatic tensions, into the same infinite-density singularity as their more material allies.

And the narrator—the wise old mentor, the embodiment of narrative itself, the being who had delivered the opening monologue and promised victory against the darkness—was the last to go. Godric held him, deliberately, in the grip of his passive strength, allowing the entity to witness the complete and total annihilation of everything he had gathered. Every hero. Every army. Every shield. Every hope. Every story. All of it compressed into a point of infinite mass smaller than a quark, a singularity that contained the distilled essence of every fictional universe that had ever been imagined.

"Please," the narrator whispered, his form flickering through a thousand dying archetypes. "Please, at least let the stories survive. Let the narratives continue. Let the children who believe in us still have something to believe in."

Godric looked at him. His expression was not cruel, not angry, not even particularly interested. It was the expression of a man who had been promised a feast and served a single stale cracker.

"The stories were never yours to protect," he said. "They were always mine. Every tale ever told, every hero ever imagined, every villain ever vanquished—all of it existed within the omniverse that I have protected and sustained for longer than your narratives have existed. You borrowed my reality and thought you could use it against me."

He released the full, passive pressure of his strength. The narrator's form collapsed inward, his thousand archetypes folding into each other like the pages of a burning book, his consciousness compressing, compressing, compressing until it was nothing but a single point of narrative density at the heart of the growing singularity.

And then, with a casual gesture—the first gesture he had made since the annihilation began, a simple closing of his fist—Godric completed the compression.

---

The entire omniverse had been destroyed as collateral. Not just the fictional universes of the Coalition, but everything. Every real universe, every actual multiverse, every genuine dimension and timeline and reality and abstract plane of existence. All of it had been caught in the ambient, passive pressure of Godric's strength and compressed into the singularity.

And that singularity now sat in the palm of his hand.

He had picked it up. The mass of the entire omniverse—every atom, every galaxy, every dimension, every timeline, every reality that had ever existed or could ever exist—compressed into a point smaller than a quark, and he had picked it up. He held it between his thumb and forefinger, examining it with the same mild curiosity he might have directed at an interesting pebble.

Then, with a casual gesture that required no more effort than a man flicking a crumb from his sleeve, he enlarged the singularity to the size of a marble. The expansion was perfectly controlled—the infinite mass contained within the marble's volume, its gravitational pull somehow neutralized by the sheer, passive pressure of his fingers. He had created a container, a barrier of his own will, that prevented the compressed omniverse from destroying everything around it.

He held the marble up to the light, turning it this way and that. Its surface was perfectly smooth, perfectly black, perfectly reflective. It looked like a small, polished sphere of obsidian. It weighed nothing in his hand.

"Hmm," he said.

I was on my knees. I had been on my knees since the annihilation began, my amplified magical senses shattered by the sheer, impossible scale of what I had witnessed. I was trembling so violently that my bones ached. My robes were soaked with sweat. My breath came in short, ragged gasps that did not seem to provide any oxygen.

"Your Majesty," I managed. "The omniverse. It's... you're holding it. In your hand."

"Compressed," he said, still examining the marble. "De-atomized. Every particle of every being that ever existed, every star that ever burned, every planet that ever turned, every thought that was ever thought—all of it compressed to infinite mass in a volume smaller than a quark. And then I enlarged it. For the aesthetic." He tossed the marble into the air and caught it. The gesture was casual, effortless, the gesture of a man fidgeting with a coin. "I think it makes a nice trophy, don't you? A souvenir of the final opposition."

"The final..." I could barely form words. "Your Majesty, you mean—"

"I mean that no one will ever oppose me again," he said, and his voice was calm, matter-of-fact, utterly devoid of triumph or anger. "Not because I've removed their free will—you know I would never do that. But because the lesson has finally been learned. Every being in the omniverse just experienced, simultaneously, the complete annihilation of their existence. Every consciousness, every soul, every abstract entity and cosmic deity and mortal creature—all of them felt what it was like to be compressed into infinite density, to have their atoms deconstructed and their identities erased and their very existence reduced to a single point of mass in the palm of my hand."

He closed his fingers around the marble and opened them again. It had not changed. It was still perfectly smooth, perfectly black, perfectly silent.

"I will restore them in a moment," he said. "Exactly as they were. Every atom back in its place. Every consciousness back in its body. Every story back in its narrative. It will be as if the annihilation never happened—except they will remember. They will all remember. Not the details, perhaps—the mortal mind is not equipped to hold the full experience of being compressed into a quark-sized singularity—but the feeling. The absolute, overwhelming certainty of their own insignificance. The knowledge that they exist only because I allow it."

He pocketed the marble. It disappeared into the simple fabric of his tunic as if it were weightless—which, for him, it was. A sphere containing the compressed infinite mass of the entire omniverse, and he pocketed it like a stray coin found on a walking path.

"And that is why no one will ever oppose me again," he said. "Because now they know. Not intellectually—not as an abstract concept learned from ancient journals or whispered legends—but viscerally, experientially, in the deepest marrow of their beings. They know that resistance is not merely futile. It is an error in the fundamental logic of thought. A category mistake. A sentence that cannot be completed because the grammar of reality does not permit it."

He smiled, and the smile was warm, but it was also something else. Something that made the jasmine-scented air feel cold.

"The final opposition," he said. "How appropriate that it was the combined heroes of every fictional universe ever imagined. The ultimate crossover event. The greatest team-up in the history of storytelling. And they failed so completely, so absolutely, so utterly, that they didn't even make me try." He sighed—a soft, genuine sigh of mild disappointment. "I was really hoping for a better show."

---

The restoration took zero seconds. One moment, the omniverse was a marble in Godric's pocket. The next moment, it was back. Every galaxy, every planet, every living being, every thought and memory and moment—all of it restored exactly as it had been before the annihilation. The courtyard of the outer palace was intact. The sky was clear. The armies of the Omnifictional Coalition did not return, but the fictional universes they had come from—the stories, the narratives, the imaginary realms—were restored to their proper place in the collective unconscious of sentient life.

And the King was still smiling.

"Oh," he said, as if remembering something. "I should mention—I've become slightly more powerful. Not from absorbing their energy; their combined power wouldn't increase mine by even zero percent. No, it's something more fundamental. The act of being disappointed—the genuine, sincere disappointment of a being who was hoping for a good show and didn't get one—created a new kind of feedback in the Weakness Paradox. A recursive complaint loop. The universe, which has been overcompensating for my nonexistent weaknesses for eons, interpreted my disappointment as evidence that I should have been more powerful. If I had been more powerful, it reasoned, my expectations would have been higher, and thus I would not have been disappointed. So it amplified me. Compensatorily. Apologetically. As if my disappointment was itself a weakness that needed to be corrected by making me strong enough that I could never be disappointed again."

He chuckled. The sound was warm, genuine, almost self-deprecating. "The universe is absurd, Osvald. It's spent so long trying to apologize for not having weaknesses to give me that it's started inventing new reasons to apologize. My disappointment is now a trigger for amplification. The paradox of wanting a challenge that can never exist. The ultimate cosmic joke."

He clapped me on the shoulder, his touch warm and grounding. "Now, let's go inside. I believe the kitchen staff has prepared something. And Osvald—don't look so grim. It was a good day. I got a nice marble out of it."

---

I have written this account in a single sitting, my hand trembling so badly that the ink has smeared in several places. I do not know if I will ever stop trembling. I do not know if I will ever be able to look at the King's pocket—the pocket where a marble containing the compressed infinite mass of the entire original omniverse still rests—without feeling the deep, cold, crushing weight of existential dread.

He restored everything. But the marble remains. And if the marble contains the original matter of the omniverse—the original atoms, the original galaxies, the original beings—then what is the restored omniverse made of? Is it a copy? A recreation? A perfect duplicate that is identical in every way to the original, but is not, in some fundamental sense, real?

I cannot answer this question. I do not think any mind, no matter how amplified, can answer this question. The King holds the original existence in his pocket as a souvenir, and we—the restored versions of ourselves, the perfect copies of our original selves—go about our lives as if nothing has changed.

And nothing has changed. That is the horror of it. The restoration was perfect. The copy, if it is a copy, is indistinguishable from the original. We are who we always were. We are real. We exist.

But the original—the actual atoms that composed our actual bodies, the actual matter that made up our actual universe—is in a marble in the King's pocket. He carries it with him everywhere. Sometimes, when he is thinking, he takes it out and tosses it in his palm. The weight of infinite mass, moving up and down, up and down, as if it were nothing.

As if we were nothing.

And we are nothing. That is the lesson of Osvald's journal. That is the lesson that every being in the omniverse learned on the day the final opposition fell. We exist because the King allows it. We continue because the King wishes it. And if the King ever decides that we should not exist—if he ever grows tired of his copy, his restoration, his perfect duplicate of the original—he can simply close his fingers around the marble, and the original will still be there, compressed and silent and waiting.

But the copy will be gone. And no one will remember that it ever existed.

---

I, Clive, set down Osvald's journal and sat in the cold silence of the reading chamber for a long time. The obsidian walls reflected my face back at me—pale, hollow, the face of a man who had just read the final chapter of a story that had no ending.

The King had a marble in his pocket. He had carried it for four googolplexian years, through the Era of the Enduring Present, through the creation of the Stasis Event, through all the long eons of his eternal reign. Somewhere in the palace, right now, the King was walking through the gardens or sitting in the eastern solar or enjoying a cup of jasmine tea, and in his pocket was a small, polished sphere of obsidian that contained the compressed infinite mass of the original omniverse.

And I was a copy. A perfect duplicate of the original Clive, who had been compressed into that marble four googolplexian years ago and restored in zero seconds. I was real. I existed. I had thoughts and feelings and memories and a family and a purpose. But I was not the original. The original was in the King's pocket, compressed to a point smaller than a quark, and he would be there forever, because the King liked his trophy.

The dread of this realization was a physical weight in my chest. It pressed against my lungs, my heart, my spine. It was the dread of existential contingency—the knowledge that my existence was not necessary, not guaranteed, not fundamental. I was a restoration. A recreation. A gift from a being who could unmake me as easily as he had made me.

And I loved that being. I served that being. I would continue to love and serve him until the end of my days.

But I would never, ever stop being afraid.

The silence of the reading chamber pressed against my ears. The obsidian walls pressed against my eyes. The journal of Osvald, the final opposition, lay open on the table before me, its last words staring up at the ceiling like the eyes of a dead man.

I closed the journal. I stood from the table. I walked up the narrow spiral staircase, through the hidden door, into the warm, golden light of Thronegate's eternal afternoon. The jasmine of Olympus drifted on the breeze. The Eternal Ember hummed softly beneath every atom.

Somewhere in the palace, the King was laughing at one of his own jokes. And in his pocket, the original omniverse bounced lightly with every step.
