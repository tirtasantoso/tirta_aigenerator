The morning sun of Thronegate's capital world had barely crested the spires of the imperial palace when the door to my chambers burst open and Godric walked in, wearing what I can only describe as the most insulting approximation of a disguise I had ever seen in my forty-seven years of existence.

"Roth," he said, his rich baritone carrying the unmistakable timbre of barely contained delight. "Get your robes. We are going on a field trip."

I set down my tea. I looked at him. I looked at the disguise. I looked back at him.

He had donned a traveling cloak of coarse brown wool, the kind that common merchants wore on long trade routes. Over his salt-and-pepper hair—which was, I must emphasize, one of the most iconic and recognizable features in the entire omniverse—he had placed a wide-brimmed leather hat that might have been fashionable several thousand millennia ago on a backwater agricultural world. On his face, just above his magnificently chiseled jaw and precisely trimmed beard—another feature known to every sentient entity with even passing awareness of galactic politics—he had stuck a false mustache.

The mustache was black. His beard was salt-and-pepper. The mustache was also upside down.

"You look—" I began.

"Dashing?" Godric suggested, adjusting the upside-down mustache with the confidence of a man who had never once questioned his own magnificence. "Inconspicuous? Like a completely ordinary traveling merchant who just happens to be passing through the most heavily fortified secret base in the history of resistance movements?"

"Like King Godric of Thronegate wearing an upside-down mustache and a hat," I said.

Godric waved his hand dismissively. "No one will recognize me."

"Uncle Godric. You are the most famous being in the omniverse. Your face is on the currency of decillions of civilizations. Your physical description is encoded into the genetic memory of species that have never even encountered you, as a kind of evolutionary warning system. There are religions based on your jawline."

"And that is precisely why this disguise is brilliant," Godric said, adjusting his terrible hat. "No one will believe that the King of Thronegate, the apex predator of all existence, the being whose slightest displeasure can erase omniversal clusters, would wander into the headquarters of the largest resistance ever assembled wearing an upside-down mustache and a merchant's cloak. The sheer absurdity of it will protect my identity. It is psychologically impenetrable."

I stared at him for a long moment. My enhanced mind, which processed information a thousand times faster every day than it had the day before, could not determine whether he was genuinely confident in this disguise or whether he was simply enjoying the spectacle of how obviously not a disguise it was.

"We are visiting the Coalition of the Final Dawn," I said, because I needed to confirm that he meant what I thought he meant.

"We are!"

"The coalition that has been preparing to attack you for three thousand years."

"The very same!"

"The coalition that believes itself to be hidden from your awareness through the most sophisticated concealment technology ever developed."

"Oh, they are very proud of it," Godric said, his eyes twinkling with genuine admiration. "The quantum-phase shielding alone is remarkable work. If I were any other being in existence, they would be completely undetectable. Unfortunately for them, I am not any other being in existence, and I have been watching them prepare since before their great-grandparents were born. But do not tell them that. It will ruin the surprise."

"The coalition that is scheduled to launch their ultimate attack tomorrow."

"Yes! I thought it would be fun to visit them the day before. You know, boost their morale. Wish them luck. See the preparations up close." He smiled, and the smile was warm and genuine and absolutely terrifying in its playfulness. "I am genuinely curious about what they have built. They have been working so hard, Roth. So very, very hard. It seems rude not to acknowledge their effort."

I closed my eyes. I took a breath. I opened them again. Godric was still there, still wearing the upside-down mustache, still looking like a god playing dress-up with all the subtlety of a supernova in a dark room.

"What name will you be using?" I asked, because I had learned that resistance was futile in ways that extended beyond physical confrontation.

"Garry," Godric said proudly.

"Garry."

"Garry the Merchant. I sell pots."

"You do not own any pots."

"I will improvise."

The Coalition of the Final Dawn had constructed their headquarters in a pocket dimension at the intersection of seven overlapping realities, stabilized by quantum anchors forged from the compressed matter of dead universes. The entrance was concealed behind a cascade of dimensional veils that rendered it undetectable to any form of conventional or magical scanning. The defenses included enough concentrated firepower to sterilize several galaxy clusters, layered shielding sophisticated enough to redirect attacks through twelve different temporal dimensions, and a garrison of forty-seven billion of the most elite warriors from across the omniverse.

They were, by any objective measure, the most formidable military force ever assembled. Their preparation had been meticulous, their coordination flawless, their dedication absolute. Every soldier had trained for centuries. Every weapon had been refined to perfection. Every strategy had been analyzed and optimized by the greatest tactical minds in existence.

They had also, unbeknownst to them, been permitted to exist by a being who had watched their every move since their founding, who knew every detail of their preparations, who could have erased them from existence at any moment with less effort than it takes to blink, and who had chosen not to because he found their earnest dedication genuinely charming.

Godric and I materialized at the edge of the dimensional cascade, our arrival masked by his absolute concealment—which, even in this era, he maintained so perfectly that his presence produced absolutely no detectable distortion in the surrounding space-time.

"Right," Godric said, adjusting his terrible hat. "Let me do the talking."

He stepped forward and knocked on the dimensional veil.

There was a pause. Then a seam opened in the cascade, and a sentinel appeared—a towering entity of crystalline matter and concentrated starlight, its form bristling with weapons that could have leveled continents. Its sensory arrays swept over us, analyzing every aspect of our physical and metaphysical composition.

"Identify yourselves," the sentinel said, its voice resonating at frequencies that would have shattered the bones of lesser beings.

"Garry the Merchant," Godric said, doffing his hat in what I can only assume was meant to be a jaunty manner. "And this is my associate, Roth. We sell pots."

The sentinel's sensory arrays lingered on Godric for a long moment. I could see the analysis protocols running—scanning his physical dimensions, his energy signature, his metaphysical weight. Any competent scanner should have identified him instantly. The man's passive physical presence was so overwhelmingly dense that it should have registered as a gravitational anomaly visible from the next dimension.

But Godric's absolute concealment was, even in this relatively early era of his existence, so impossibly thorough that it rendered him undetectable by any means. The sentinel's scans returned nothing unusual—just a large, handsome man in a terrible disguise, accompanied by a nervous-looking wizard.

"You are not registered in our visitor protocols," the sentinel said.

"Of course not," Godric replied. "We are traveling merchants. We go where the winds of commerce carry us. And I have heard that this establishment—" he gestured vaguely at the pocket dimension "—is in the market for quality cookware. Perhaps for feeding a large army? Just a guess. You have the look of an establishment that feeds a large army."

The sentinel hesitated. Its protocols did not include contingencies for traveling pot merchants appearing at the entrance to the most secret military installation in the history of resistance movements.

"We are not in the market for cookware," the sentinel said finally.

"Everyone is in the market for cookware," Godric said, with the smooth confidence of a man who had never been told no and therefore assumed the concept did not apply to him. "How else do you cook your meals? How else do you feed your soldiers? A well-fed army is a victorious army, I always say."

"We do not require—"

"I would love to see your kitchens," Godric said, already walking past the sentinel and through the dimensional seam. "I have some very fine copper pots that would be perfect for large-scale food preparation. Roth, bring the samples."

I had no samples. I had nothing. I followed him through the seam like a man walking to his own execution, which, given the nature of our surroundings, was not entirely inaccurate.

The interior of the Coalition headquarters was impressive. I had to give them that. The central chamber was a vast, cathedral-like space carved from the living rock of the pocket dimension, its walls lined with weapons systems and defensive emplacements that hummed with barely contained power. The ceiling arced upward into a dome of crystallized energy that served as both a skylight and a sensor array, monitoring every conceivable form of intrusion across every conceivable dimensional frequency.

And the chamber was full of soldiers. Thousands of them. Tens of thousands. Beings of every description—armored titans, lithe assassins, floating energy entities, chitinous war-forms, crystalline intelligences, entities composed of pure mathematical calculation—all of them moving with purpose and precision, all of them preparing for the attack that would launch tomorrow.

They stopped and stared as we entered. Of course they did. Godric was impossible not to stare at, even in his ridiculous disguise. He walked through the chamber like a man strolling through a market, his massive frame drawing every eye, his ruggedly handsome face—the face that launched a thousand surrenders—turning this way and that with an expression of cheerful curiosity.

"Marvelous facility," Godric said, his voice carrying across the chamber with the easy projection of a man who was accustomed to being heard. "Very impressive architecture. And the energy shielding on the ceiling—top-notch work. Who is your contractor?"

A figure approached us through the crowd of soldiers. She was tall, armored in black plate that seemed to absorb light, her face severe and angular beneath close-cropped silver hair. Her eyes were the color of molten iron, and they fixed on Godric with the intensity of a being who had spent three thousand years preparing for a single confrontation.

"I am Commander Veyra of the Coalition of the Final Dawn," she said. "Identify yourselves immediately, or you will be detained."

Godric doffed his hat again. "Garry the Merchant. I sell pots."

Veyra's molten eyes narrowed. "We did not request merchant services."

"Then you are missing a critical component of your logistical preparation," Godric said earnestly. "Every successful military operation requires reliable cookware. How do you intend to feed your troops during extended combat operations? Raw rations? Cold meals? The morale implications alone—"

"We have automated food synthesis systems," Veyra said, her tone flat.

"Ah, but synthesized food lacks the soul of a properly cooked meal," Godric countered. "And soul matters, Commander. It matters very much. A soldier who eats a warm, copper-pot-cooked stew before battle fights with ten times the spirit of one who consumes cold nutrient paste."

Veyra stared at him. The soldiers around us stared at him. I stared at him, though my staring was accompanied by the silent screaming of my internal consciousness.

"Your disguise is fooling no one," I muttered under my breath.

"It is fooling everyone," Godric murmured back. "No one has identified me yet. Therefore, the disguise is working perfectly."

"No one has identified you because they cannot process the possibility that you would walk into their secret base wearing an upside-down mustache. That is not the same as being fooled."

"Is it not?" Godric's eyes twinkled. "I think that is exactly what being fooled looks like."

Veyra's hand moved to the weapon at her hip. "I am going to ask you one more time. Who are you, and how did you find this location?"

"We found it the same way any traveling merchant finds a customer," Godric said. "Word of mouth. Reputation. I heard through the grapevine that there was a very impressive military operation in this sector, and I thought to myself—Garry, this sounds like an organization that could use some quality copper pots."

He paused, and his expression shifted slightly—not enough for anyone else to notice, but I had known him my entire life, and I recognized the look of genuine interest crossing his features.

"May I have a tour?" he asked, and the question was delivered with such earnest, innocent enthusiasm that it almost sounded sincere. "I have always been fascinated by military installations. The logistics alone—the supply chains, the resource allocation, the personnel management—it is a whole world unto itself. And this facility! The engineering is remarkable. Those quantum stabilizers on the far wall—I have not seen that configuration outside of Thronegate research facilities. Very advanced. Very impressive."

Veyra's hand tightened on her weapon. "How do you know about Thronegate research configurations?"

"Everyone knows about Thronegate research configurations," Godric said, which was an absurd lie—Thronegate's research configurations were classified at the highest levels, and no one outside the empire had access to them. But he said it with such casual confidence that Veyra hesitated.

"I will give you three minutes to explain yourselves," she said, "before I have you thrown into the detention cells."

"Three minutes is hardly enough time for a proper tour," Godric said, but he raised his hands in a gesture of surrender. "Very well. Three minutes. But I want it on record that I find this treatment of a legitimate merchant deeply unprofessional."

He looked around the chamber, his gaze sweeping across the assembled soldiers and weapons with the practiced assessment of a being who could erase all of it without moving a muscle. His eyes landed on a massive apparatus in the center of the room—a structure of gleaming metal and crystalline energy conductors, roughly the size of a small moon, bristling with power cables and cooling systems and targeting arrays.

"Oh my," he said, and his voice carried a note of genuine delight. "What is that?"

Veyra's expression flickered with something that might have been pride. "That is the Oblivion Engine. The most powerful weapon ever constructed. Three thousand years of development, incorporating the technological advancements of fourteen thousand civilizations and the magical frameworks of eight hundred separate arcane traditions. It is capable of generating an attack with sufficient force to erase a being from every dimension, every timeline, and every plane of existence simultaneously."

Godric clasped his hands together, his face lighting up with childlike wonder. "May I see it up close?"

"No."

"Please?"

"Absolutely not."

"I promise not to touch anything."

Veyra's molten eyes burned with suspicion. "You are not a merchant."

"I am absolutely a merchant," Godric said. "I sell pots. Quality copper pots, handcrafted by the finest artisans in—" he paused, clearly improvising "—Potsville. The city of pots. Where I am from."

"There is no city called Potsville."

"There is now. I just founded it. For tax purposes."

I closed my eyes and resisted the urge to sink into the floor.

The standoff continued for several more minutes, during which Godric managed to talk his way past three additional security checkpoints, charm his way into two restricted research wings, and convince a senior weapons engineer to give him a detailed technical briefing on the Oblivion Engine's firing mechanism. I watched in helpless fascination as the most powerful being in existence wandered through the most secret military installation ever constructed, asking questions about their weapons and defenses with the cheerful curiosity of a tourist visiting a museum, and nobody stopped him because nobody could process the possibility that this was actually happening.

"I have to say," Godric said, examining a bank of targeting computers with the appreciation of a connoisseur, "your preparation is genuinely impressive. The overlapping shield frequencies alone would be sufficient to stop almost any conventional assault. And the temporal redundancy system—if I understand correctly, it projects your defensive parameters across multiple simultaneous timelines, ensuring that any attack that penetrates one timeline's defenses is countered by the defenses of an adjacent timeline?"

The senior engineer—a wizened entity with too many arms and a visible enthusiasm for technical discussion—nodded eagerly. "Precisely! The temporal redundancy system is our crowning achievement. Even if an attacker manages to breach our primary shields in one timeline, the attack is immediately neutralized by the defensive matrices of the adjacent timelines. It is, theoretically, an unbreakable defense."

"Theoretically," Godric repeated, and the word carried a weight that only I could detect.

"And the Oblivion Engine," the engineer continued, gesturing toward the massive weapon with pride, "is capable of generating an attack that operates across all dimensional frequencies simultaneously. There is no shielding that can block it, because it attacks in every possible configuration at once. There is no evasion, because it targets every possible location across every possible timeline. There is no survival, because it erases the target's existence from the fundamental architecture of reality itself."

Godric nodded thoughtfully. "And you intend to use this against the King of Thronegate?"

The engineer's enthusiasm dimmed slightly, replaced by the grim determination of a being who had staked everything on a single desperate gamble. "Yes. Tomorrow, at the appointed hour, we will unleash the full power of the Oblivion Engine against Godric. The attack has been calculated to exploit every possible vulnerability, to penetrate every known defense, to overcome every established parameter. It is the most comprehensive offensive ever devised."

"And you believe it will work?"

The engineer hesitated. "We believe it is our best chance. The calculations indicate a seventy-three percent probability of significant impact."

Godric's eyebrows rose. "Seventy-three percent? That is remarkably confident. May I ask what methodology you used to arrive at that figure?"

"Our tactical analysis division spent eight hundred years compiling data on Godric's known capabilities and extrapolating potential weaknesses. The calculation incorporates every recorded instance of his power expression, every observed defensive parameter, every theoretical vulnerability identified by our research teams."

"And what theoretical vulnerabilities did you identify?"

The engineer glanced around nervously, as if afraid of being overheard. "We have identified several potential avenues of attack. His absolute concealment, for instance, appears to operate by suppressing his ambient presence. If we can overwhelm that suppression with sufficient energy output, we believe we can force a momentary exposure of his true nature—a window during which the Oblivion Engine's attack can target his actual existence rather than his concealed form."

Godric nodded slowly, his expression thoughtful. "That is... actually not a terrible line of reasoning. The logic is sound, even if the premise is flawed. What other vulnerabilities?"

"His passive aura system—the one we call 'Gent'—appears to have a maximum operational capacity. If we can exceed that capacity with simultaneous multi-vector attacks, we believe we can create gaps in his defensive coverage."

"Interesting. And what led you to conclude that Gent has a maximum operational capacity?"

"Analysis of historical engagement records suggests that the aura's effectiveness scales with the intensity of the threat. We extrapolated a theoretical ceiling based on observed maximum outputs."

Godric turned to me, and his expression was one of genuine, delighted amusement. "Roth, they have been doing their homework. This is genuinely impressive work. The methodology is flawed, the conclusions are incorrect, and the fundamental assumptions are catastrophically wrong, but the effort is commendable. I am actually rather touched."

I stared at him. "You are not going to correct them?"

"Where would be the fun in that? They have worked so hard. Let them have their confidence for one more day."

He turned back to the engineer. "Tell me—what do you think of Godric? As a person, I mean. The being you are planning to destroy tomorrow."

The engineer blinked, clearly not expecting the question. "I think he is a tyrant. A monster. A being of such overwhelming power that his very existence is an offense to the concept of freedom. He subjugates entire realities, crushes all opposition, rules with absolute and unyielding authority. He is the enemy of everything we believe in."

"And what do you believe in?"

"Self-determination. The right of every being to choose their own destiny. The freedom to live without the shadow of an omnipotent overlord determining the course of existence."

Godric nodded, and his expression was thoughtful rather than mocking. "Those are worthy beliefs. I respect them, even if I cannot allow them to manifest as opposition. You are fighting for something you believe in, and that is more than most beings can say."

He looked around the chamber one final time, his gaze sweeping across the assembled soldiers and weapons and defenses with the appreciation of a being who understood, better than anyone, exactly how futile all of this was.

"Thank you for the tour," he said. "You have a remarkable facility here. Your preparation is impressive. Your dedication is admirable. And tomorrow, when you launch your attack, I want you to know that I will be watching. Not as an enemy—I do not consider you enemies, any more than a mountain considers the wind an enemy. I will be watching as someone who genuinely appreciates the effort you have put into this. You have worked very hard. You have built something remarkable. And I want you to know that your effort has been seen."

He doffed his terrible hat, turned, and walked toward the exit. I followed, my heart hammering against my ribs, my enhanced mind struggling to process what had just happened.

Commander Veyra appeared in our path, her molten eyes narrowed with suspicion. "I still do not know who you are," she said, "but something about this feels wrong. You know too much. You ask too many questions. And your associate—" her gaze flicked to me "—looks like he has seen a ghost."

Godric smiled, and the smile was warm and genuine and carried just the faintest hint of something else—something vast and ancient and utterly beyond mortal comprehension.

"I am Garry the Merchant," he said. "I sell pots. And tomorrow, when you face the King of Thronegate, I hope you give him a good show. He does so rarely get to see one."

He walked past her, through the dimensional seam, and out into the void between realities. I followed, my legs shaking, my breath coming in short, ragged gasps.

The moment we were clear of the pocket dimension, Godric dissolved the disguise. The terrible hat vanished. The upside-down mustache fell away. The coarse merchant cloak dissolved, revealing the simple, dark garments beneath. He stood there, in the void between realities, his salt-and-pepper hair immaculate, his beard perfectly trimmed, his ruggedly handsome face illuminated by the distant light of dying stars.

He was King Godric of Thronegate again, and the weight of his presence—the overwhelming, crushing, existentially devastating weight of the most powerful being in existence—pressed against the fabric of reality like a boot against a throat.

"That was fun," he said, and his voice carried genuine satisfaction. "Did you see their faces? They were so proud. So confident. They have worked so hard, Roth. Three thousand years of preparation. The most advanced weapons ever constructed. The most sophisticated defenses ever devised. And they genuinely believe that they have a chance."

He shook his head slowly, and his expression was not mocking—it was something worse. It was affectionate. It was the expression of a being who genuinely admired the effort, even though the effort was utterly, totally, cosmically meaningless.

"The Oblivion Engine is genuinely impressive work," he continued. "The engineering alone—the dimensional frequency modulation, the temporal targeting system, the existence-erasure framework—all of it is remarkably sophisticated. If it were aimed at any other target in existence, it would be an absolutely devastating weapon. It would erase gods. It would unmake cosmic entities. It would annihilate abstract concepts."

He paused, and his expression softened into something almost gentle.

"But it is aimed at me. And that means all of that preparation, all of that sophistication, all of that three thousand years of effort, is less than nothing. Their ultimate weapon will not even register as an event. Their unbreakable defenses will not even slow me down. Their seventy-three percent confidence in significant impact is off by approximately seventy-three percent—they will achieve zero percent impact, because there is no impact to be achieved. They cannot touch me. They cannot tickle me. They cannot even make me notice them, except through the courtesy of my attention, which I choose to give because I find their effort charming."

He looked at me, and his warm, hazel eyes carried the weight of a being who had been omnipotent for so long that the concept of challenge had become an abstract curiosity rather than a lived experience.

"I will not be cruel to them, Roth. I will not torture them or make them suffer unnecessarily. But I will not spare them either. They have chosen to oppose me, and the consequence of that choice is absolute. Tomorrow, they will attack, and their attack will fail, and they will be erased, and the omniverse will continue as if they had never existed."

He began walking back toward the imperial palace, his steps leaving no footprints in the void, his presence leaving no trace in the space between realities.

"Come, Roth. Let us go home. Tomorrow will be eventful, and I want to get a good seat for the show."

I followed him, and the dread walked with me, and I knew with absolute, terrible certainty that tomorrow would be exactly as he described—an event that would be eventful only in the sense that a breeze is eventful when it passes through a graveyard.

The Coalition of the Final Dawn had prepared for three thousand years. They had constructed the most powerful weapon ever built. They had assembled the greatest military force in the history of resistance. They had confidence, dedication, and the absolute certainty of their cause.

And none of it mattered.

None of it had ever mattered.

None of it would ever matter.

Because Godric was Godric, and they were not, and the gap between those two statements was infinite, and it was growing, and it would never close.

I followed my king home, and I carried the weight of what I had witnessed, and I knew that tomorrow I would watch the last great hope of resistance be extinguished like a candle in a hurricane, and I would record it in my journal, and the recording would join the countless other recordings of countless other futilities, and the weight of all that recorded despair would settle into my bones and stay there forever.

But for tonight—for one more night—the Coalition of the Final Dawn still believed. They still hoped. They still dreamed of victory.

And Godric, the apex predator of all existence, let them dream.

Because tomorrow, the dream would end.
