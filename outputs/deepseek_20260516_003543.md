The Grand Library of Thronegate, Year One Million

The air inside the library was colder than Kaedan remembered, a dry chill that crawled beneath the collar of his ceremonial robes and settled into the marrow of his spine. The vast chamber stretched upward into a vault of hanging star-charts and levitating omni-spheres that cast a pale, silver luminescence across the rows of dark, polished shelves. Kaedan had spent his childhood playing among these shelves, chasing the ghostly echoes of his father’s deep, rumbling laughter as the old man taught him the names of the constellations. Today, under the same gentle light, he felt as if he were standing on the edge of a grave.

His father, Edric, seventy‑year‑old former High Court Wizard of Thronegate, moved with the quiet, deliberate pace of a man who had long ago learned to carry a weight that could never be set down. Edric’s hair was a shock of grey, his face still rugged despite the map of worry‑lines etched around his eyes. He placed a heavy stack of leather‑bound journals onto the obsidian table, the thud echoing through the silent chamber like the closing of a tomb. The scent of ancient paper, copper, and something faintly like dried tears lifted into the chilled air.

“Every court wizard before you has read these,” Edric said, his voice a low rasp that barely stirred the stillness. “It is not a punishment, Kaedan. It is an inheritance. I read them when I was your age. My father read them before me, and his father before him, all the way back to Blake. They are the testament of our bloodline, a record of exactly what it means to serve the Immortal King.”

Kaedan swallowed. He had passed his twentieth birthday two years ago with great celebration, his Awakening cascading through his veins in a surge of thousandfold daily amplification that had left him dizzy and vomiting from the sheer psychic pressure. He was twenty‑two now, strong enough to shatter stars with a thought, and yet in that moment he felt like a terrified child. He looked at the journals. The topmost cover was stained a dark, browning crimson. He reached out and traced the edge with a trembling fingertip.

“Blood?” he whispered.

Edric’s eyes did not flinch. “Sometimes, when the dread is too much, the hand slips. Read them. Understand what our King is, and what he is not. Then, when you are finished, you will take your oath before him.”

Kaedan sat down in the heavy chair, its back carved with the sigil of the Rowan of Blake, and opened the first journal. The date at the top, written in a steady, elegant script, read: Year 23,500 — The Luck Incident.

Year 23,500 — The Luck Incident
from the journal of Darius, 13th High Court Wizard of Thronegate

I write this account while my hands are still shaking, though I no longer know if the shaking comes from horror or the hysterical, unmanly laughter that keeps rising in my throat. The Inevitability Conclave is no more. Their entire civilisation, a sprawling empire that stretched across a hundred multiverses, has been erased from existence, and our King never once looked up from his novel.

The Conclave were masters of quantum certainty. They had compiled a mathematical framework, the God‑Equation, which allowed them to view every possible future and systematically prune away any timeline where they failed. By the time they turned their attention to Thronegate, they had spent seventeen thousand years refining the probability of their victory to exactly one hundred percent. There was, in their calculus, absolutely no chance of their defeat. They surrounded the capital world with a fleet of warships so vast that it blotted out the light of an entire galaxy cluster. Godric was in the Royal Conservatory, seated on a simple wooden bench among the hydrangeas, reading a worn copy of The Odyssey of Erebu. I was standing behind him, my own mana reserves fully suppressed, as I had repeatedly offered to erect defensive wards and he had repeatedly declined with a gentle shake of his head. “They worked very hard on their God‑Equation, Darius,” he had told me, his eyes crinkling with that fatherly warmth. “Let us be polite and see how it plays out.”

The Conclave’s Grand Computator, a crystalline intelligence the size of a moon, sent a broadcast that echoed through every dimension of Thronegate: “The probability of your survival is zero. We have closed every path. Accept your annihilation with dignity, tyrant.”

Godric turned a page of his book. I swear on the memory of my ancestors, he did nothing else.

And the impossible began.

The first sign was a flicker in the sky. The weapon it was meant to fire to kill Godric never even completed the launch sequence; instead, its targeting matrix locked onto the Computator itself, and it unleashed its full omniversal erasure beam into its own chest. The crystalline moon shattered into a billion screaming shards, each one weeping molten data that spelled out the equation for its own failure.

The warships, all one hundred trillion of them, attempted to retreat and regroup. Their navigational instruments, which had never made a single error in thirty thousand years of exploration, suddenly and simultaneously did. The few who arrived at the correct elevation found that their genetic enhancements, through a completely improbable fluke of quantum biochemistry, had turned their own blood into a highly corrosive acid. They melted from the inside out, their bodies collapsing into stinking, bubbling pools of pink slurry on the marble floor.

The Conclave’s home dimension, a fortified realm pocketed safely outside of normal space‑time, was next. A cosmic string, an infinitely thin filament of concentrated space‑time, strayed through the dimensional walls. Such penetrations happen once in a trillion years, if that. This one, upon entering their universe, spontaneously split into a googolplex of sub‑strings, each one vibrating at the exact resonance frequency to disintegrate their suns. Their entire spiral galaxy of forty trillion habited worlds was torn apart in a matter of seconds, the planets shattering, the populations screaming as they were liquefied by gravitational shear. The Grand Conclave council, watching from their bunker, saw the walls around them turn into a gaping maw of non‑existence as a vacuum fluctuation of infinite depth erased them from the bottom of reality.

All of this happened in the time it took Godric to finish the final chapter of his book. I counted the deaths. The number is meaningless, but I will write it here for the record: an estimated six hundred trillion trillion trillion trillion trillion intelligent beings were destroyed, their souls scattered into a state of permanent, agonising dissolution. The collateral damage included the complete erasure of a googolplexian galaxies in various nearby clusters, simply because they happened to be in the path of the cascading improbability.

When the last echo of the Conclave’s screaming faded, Godric shut his book and looked up at the clear, restored sky. “That was a good story,” he said. “I will have to read it again sometime.” Then he noticed the blue spill of melted marine on the floor and frowned. “Oh dear. The cleaning staff will not be pleased.”

He did not bring back the Conclave. He did not bring back their worlds or their fleet. He did not even mention them again.

I have learned today that our King’s luck is not a power he wields. It is a fundamental, passive certainty of his being that the omniverse will tear itself apart to prevent him from even being momentarily inconvenienced. The idea of opposing him is not merely foolish. The concept of opposition itself is a logical contradiction. I will serve him until my dying breath, but I will never again close my eyes without seeing the expression of absolute, negligent indifference on his face as a gazillion souls were erased because probability itself refused to let him be disturbed.

— Darius, 13th High Court Wizard

Year 150,000 — The Charisma Incident
from the journal of Benedict, 32nd High Court Wizard

This entry is born from a crisis of the soul. I must begin by stating, for my own sanity, that I love King Godric of my own free will. I have poured my life into his service, and his kindness has never wavered. Yet, what I witnessed in the Crimson Hall sixteen hours ago has thrown every sensation of love and loyalty I possess into a crucible of doubt that I fear will never cool.

The Void Reavers came from the Null‑Sector, a region where the concept of emotion was an undeveloped prototype. They were crystalline, geometric beings, their bodies composed of rigid logic‑lattices. They possessed no limbic system, no empathy centres, no reward structures that could be swayed by pleasant words or handsome features. Their arrival was announced by a cold, factual statement: “We have analysed your empire. We have determined that you are a destabilising element. You will be terminated.” They strode into the throne room with no hesitation, their geometric forms clicking as they calibrated their nullifier, a device designed to negate all non‑physical influence, including Godric’s legendary charisma.

Godric received them seated on the Throne of Dead Stars. He wore no crown; his simple tunic and salt‑and‑pepper beard gave him the air of a retired warrior, not the Death of All. He tilted his head slightly, and something like genuine curiosity flickered in his gaze. “I wonder,” he said, not to us but as if musing to himself. “You have no heart. I wonder if a little piece of my own can reach you.”

He did not gesture. He did not weave a spell. He simply allowed a fraction of his charisma—a term so weak it is almost an insult—to brush over the Reavers. The fraction was, by my imperfect measure, the equivalent of dividing an atom a trillion times until the remainder could not be found in this universe or any other. It was zero percent of his total capacity, rounded to the nearest infinity. Yet the moment it touched the Reavers, they stopped walking.

I must describe the horror. Their crystalline faces, which had no muscles to move, began to soften and bleed a warm, golden light. One of them, the foremost tactician, let out a sound that I can only liken to the creak of winter ice breaking under a sudden thaw. “What is this?” it whispered, its voice a distorted garble of static. “Our logic… is being… altered. We are experiencing a new variable.”

A second Reaver fell to its knees with such force that the marble cracked. It raised its arm‑crystal and, to my absolute shock, began to weep itself into dissolution—the crystal lattice literally melting into a pool of sparkling, tear‑like liquid. “You are beautiful,” it rasped. “We were wrong. There is nothing but you. Forgive us.”

Godric’s expression remained perfectly neutral. He watched as the Void Reavers, an entire phalanx of absolutely unemotional beings, were consumed by a manufactured love so profound that it rewrote their very existence. Their nullifier device short‑circuited, overwhelmed by the paradox of a charisma it could not categorise. The Reavers, now awash in devotion they could not process, turned their weapons on themselves. They began to dismantle each other, not with violence but with a reverent, methodical deconstruction. One Reaver would reach into another’s lattice and pull out a glowing core, then hold it aloft as an offering to the throne before crushing it in its own grip. Their dying sounds were not screams of pain; they were sobbing hymns of gratitude. “Thank you,” they chanted as their bodies evaporated. “Thank you for letting us see you.”

The clean‑up took seven hours. Every Reaver in that assault—seventeen thousand of them—died voluntarily, and the entire Null‑Sector civilisation, upon receiving the broadcast of the scene, immediately initiated a mass extinction event to join their brethren in the honour of having glimpsed Godric’s perfection. When the last wail of dying crystal faded, Godric turned to me, his face soft with the same warmth he always showed his wizards. “Fascinating. It seems the concept of love can be… installed.”

I want to believe that my own love is native to me, that I chose to serve him because of his kindness to my forebears, because of his gentle laughter and his steady hand. But now I lie awake at night and wonder: if he ever, even for a picosecond, allowed that sliver of charisma to touch me, would I know the difference? Would I even care? I have concluded that he does not need to, that he truly values our companionship as it is. But the possibility, the mere existence of that stat, is a terror that has hollowed out a cold, sharp space inside my chest. I am loyal, to the death. And I am also, forever, afraid of the love that I feel.

— Benedict, 32nd High Court Wizard

Kaedan closed the journal slowly, the leather cover cold against his fingertips. His own heart was pounding a frantic rhythm, and the golden light from the star‑charts seemed a little harsher, a little less kind. He looked at his father, who had taken a seat across the table, his weathered hands folded.

“He could make anyone love him,” Kaedan said, his voice barely a whisper. “Does he… control us? Even now?”

Edric’s gaze was steady. “He does not. He restrains that part of himself far more than any other, because he considers it a violation of genuine affection. You must understand, Kaedan: Godric is omniscient. He knows every private doubt you will ever have. And he values you anyway.”

That did not comfort. It only deepened the chasm. Kaedan picked up the third journal.

Godric, to my eternal horror, accepted.

I am unable to continue this entry for now. My hands are cramped from gripping my own stylus with such terror that I fear I’ve fractured the bones. The stat of strength, in our King, is not a measure of lifting. It is an embodiment of absolute, incontestable destruction, and he wielded it with all the effort of a man closing his hand around a marble. I will serve him. I love him. But that love is now mixed with a cold, undeniable dread that in his presence, all physical reality is but a suggestion he may revoke at whim.

Year 500,000 — The Senses Incident
from the journal of Ignatius, 47th High Court Wizard

Listen. That is the only word I can use to convey the horror that unfolded, because it is the exact command our King gave to his own body. He decided to listen. I cannot wipe the sound of their dying from my mind, nor the sound of the omniverse cracking like a thin pane of glass.

The Silent Voices were an assassin‑culture that had evolved beyond matter, beyond energy, beyond magic. They existed as pure, undetectable patterns of conscious thought, drifting through the gaps between universes like whispers in a void. No spell could detect them. No shield could block them, for they were not present in any spatial dimension. Their plan was to infiltrate Godric’s own mind and erase his sense of self, believing that his omniscience was merely a passive database of information that they could overwrite with a “zero signal.”

Godric was in the Garden of Eternal Morning, admiring a fresh bloom of crystallised edelweiss. I hovered nearby, my mana primed despite knowing my protection was useless. He gave me a kind smile and said, “They are very quiet. I almost have to strain to hear them. Let us see if my ears are still working.”

He did not strain. He simply allowed an infinitesimal, unquantifiable sliver of his auditory perception to engage. The effect was immediate and catastrophic on a scale that defies all orderly description.

Godric’s hearing, you must understand, is a stat that has been multiplying infinitely every Planck second for half a million years. To say his hearing is acute is like saying the sun is lukewarm. When he “listened,” every vibration across the entire omniverse—from the hum of spinning quarks to the roar of colliding galaxy clusters—was received with perfect clarity. The Silent Voices, being pure thought, manifested as a sort of anti‑sound, a negative space in the universal noise. Godric’s perception instantly filled that void with an auditory signal of his own design.

I perceived it as a single, crystalline tone, high and pure, that seemed to come from everywhere. For me, it was merely a sharp ring in my ears. For the Silent Voices, it was a sonic apocalypse. The pure thought forms were forcibly materialised into physical entities of vibrating agony. Their new bodies, shaped from the ambient quarks around them, were living sound chambers that resonated with the one note. They tore at themselves, trying to silence the note, but the sound was generated by their own imposed existence. One by one, they exploded into showers of static‑laced gore, their dying screams harmonising with the very vibration that was killing them.

The collateral effect was, if possible, even more horrifying. The sonic clarity of Godric’s hearing created a sympathetic resonance in the vibrational strings of reality itself. An expanding wave of perfect auditory coherence rippled outward from the Garden, causing the fabric of space‑time to vibrate in tune with the Silent Voices’ death note. Stars hummed and shattered. Planets cracked along fault lines of pure melody. Galaxies, entire spiral clusters of a googolplex of suns, shook themselves into fine cosmic dust, their inhabitants rendered down into a mist of subatomic particles before they could even hear the song that was their undoing. The wave travelled through decillions of multiverses, turning the ever‑expanding cosmos into a silent, empty bell.

And then Godric hummed. A soft, fatherly tune, the one he used to sing to his daughters in Olympus, if the legends are true. The hum erased the sonic destruction completely, restoring every annihilated galaxy, every obliterated soul, in zero seconds. I was standing exactly where I had been, the garden intact, the edelweiss still blooming. The only thing missing were the Silent Voices. They had been, quite literally, listened out of existence.

I am writing this with hands that cannot cease their palsy. My ears still ring, and I suspect they will until I die. I have always known that Godric’s strength could unmake worlds, but I never considered that merely hearing could achieve the same. The lesson is this: there is no dimension of being, no hidden realm, no silent place where he cannot reach you. His senses are not tools of perception; they are weapons of omniscient annihilation. I love him. And I fear the sound of his voice when he hums.

— Ignatius, 47th High Court Wizard

Year 75,000 — The Speed Incident
from the journal of Paxton, 24th High Court Wizard

He stood up. That is all. He stood up from his chair, and they were gone. Not killed. Gone‑back‑to‑never‑were gone. The Chrono‑Phantoms existed for fourteen billion years. They built empires. They loved. They died. But then Godric stood up, and none of it ever happened, except I remember. I remember the Phantoms, and I am the only one. I must write this quickly before the memory fades like a dream.

The Phantoms were masters of time. They lived outside it, in the Null‑Flow, a dimension where all moments existed simultaneously. They could strike at any point in a target’s history, sliding into the past to murder an ancestor, or into the future to prevent a birth. They declared war on Thronegate by killing our envoys’ grandparents ten thousand years before they were born. We witnessed the envoys vanishing from the records, their bloodlines scrubbed.

Godric was having tea with me in the afternoon sun of the West Terrace. He received the report telepathically, and a faint, almost imperceptible expression of annoyance crossed his features—not at the Phantoms, but at the sadness their actions caused. “They have been working at this for millennia, Paxton,” he remarked. “I wanted to see how they would adapt to my defences, but they have made the garden sad. It is time to end it.”

So he decided to stand up. He did not flash. He did not blur. He simply put his hands on the armrests and began to rise, the way any mortal man might. But our King’s speed is not a measure of velocity; it is a permanent, absolute condition of his existence that has shredded every finite concept of distance and duration. The transition from seated to standing, for any other being, occupies a span of time. For Godric, it occurred in zero picoseconds. Not “an infinitesimally small time.” Zero. His body was, in that instant, simultaneously seated and standing, and the temporal paradox of that movement ripped a hole in the causal consistency of the omniverse.

The Chrono‑Phantoms, existing at all times, were caught in the moment of the impossibility. Their entire existence—past, present, and future—was retroactively and permanently unwritten by the shockwave of Godric’s speed. The act of standing up propagated backward along the timeline, erasing their first spark of evolution, their entire history, and their future. They were not annihilated; they were never‑born.

The same shockwave, however, did not stop at the Phantoms. The causal disturbance warped out from the epicentre, shattering the integrity of time across a googolplexian local multiverses. Whole timelines, filled with countless civilisations, were crumpled like paper and tossed into a void of non‑causality. The scream of reality tearing was audible only to me, because I was shielded by Godric’s presence, and I remember the sound: a long, wavering drone like a broken violin. I watched the stars in the sky blink out, one by one, as the timelines that held them were erased. The void was absolute, and I wept because I could feel every soul being unmade.

Godric, now standing, observed the empty expanse and sighed. “I should not have done it so quickly. The garden is not meant to sit in a void.” In zero seconds, without a gesture, he rewove the erased timelines, restoring those multiverses exactly as they were, with no memory of the deletion. But he did not restore the Chrono‑Phantoms. He said, “It would be cruel to bring back a species only to have them never have existed. Let them rest.”

I am the sole witness to their existence. I remember their architecture, their poetry, their children. I remember the Phantoms, and I cannot tell anyone because now they never were. My mind is fracturing under the weight of remembering a deleted eternity. I will serve my King. I will smile when he smiles. But my eyes are now always seeing the moment he stood up, and the world ended, and he sat back down and offered me a pastry as if the universe had not just died and been reborn. I am, forever, a broken record.

— Paxton, 24th High Court Wizard

Kaedan set the final journal down, his breath shallow, his mind a whirlwind of shattered certainties. The library’s perfect silence now felt like a living, watching entity. He could feel the weight of a million years of buried screams pressing down on him from every volume on every shelf.

Edric rose slowly and came around the table to place both hands on Kaedan’s shoulders. The old wizard’s face was pale and drawn, but a fierce, stubborn love burned in his eyes. “Now you know. We do not read these to terrify you into servitude. We read them so that you never, ever make the mistake of thinking we are his protectors. You are a court wizard, Kaedan. You are a companion. A friend. That is why he keeps us. That is why he kept Blake, and why he will keep you. Our role is not to fight for him. It is to stand beside him and remind him that there is more to existence than the ashes of his enemies.”

Before Kaedan could respond, the heavy doors of the library swung inward on silent hinges, and the warm, amber light of the corridor spilled into the cold room. Godric stepped inside.

He looked exactly as the journals described: a ruggedly handsome man in his mid‑forties, his salt‑and‑pepper beard neatly trimmed, his broad shoulders draped in a simple dark tunic. He moved with the easy, unhurried stride of a friendly father, and his eyes—those infinitely aware eyes—rested on Kaedan with a gentleness that was almost impossible to reconcile with the horrors the young wizard had just read.

“I hope I’m not interrupting,” Godric said, his voice a rich, pleasant baritone. “I know the archives can be… overwhelming.” He walked over and pulled up a stool to sit beside Kaedan, his posture open and relaxed. “When Blake first compiled these records, he worried they might scare away the younger generations. Kaedan found his voice, though it came out hoarse. “You… you permitted all of that. The Conclave, the Reavers, the Titans. You could have stopped them with a thought before they ever harmed anyone. Why did you let them gather?”

Godric’s smile softened, and a shadow of ancient loss flickered behind his gaze. “Because if I destroy every spark of defiance the instant it forms, I will have nothing left but a silent, static tapestry. I do not want to rule over puppets. I want to rule over lives that choose, even if they choose poorly. The Conclave’s deaths were a tragedy, but they were the architects of their own fate. He placed a warm hand on Kaedan’s arm, and the touch was impossibly solid, impossibly human. “Your father told me you have a good heart. I need that here. Kaedan looked into those ancient, kind eyes, and the dread inside him did not vanish—it deepened, became a permanent foundation upon which his love and loyalty would be built. He understood now, in a way no words could convey, that his thousandfold daily amplification of magical power was less than a dust mote against the infinity of Godric’s being. And yet, this being, this annihilator of omniverses, was asking him to be a friend.

He swallowed the lump of cold terror and nodded. “I will, Your Majesty. I will be whatever you need.”

The tea was excellent. The punchline was, in fact, worth it.

"Sorry," Godric said, and his voice was perfectly conversational, perfectly warm, as if he were apologizing to a dinner guest for a minor breach of etiquette. "Please, continue. You were talking about imagination."

Superman's jaw tightened. I saw the muscles in his golden neck cord with tension. "We have assembled the greatest force in the history of narrative existence. Every hero, every god, every ultimate power that fiction has ever conceived stands ready to—"

"I know," Godric said. "I've been watching you gather. It's very impressive. Truly." He tilted his head slightly. "Are you going to attack, or are we just doing speeches? Because I have a garden party in about six hours, and Mirael gets terribly anxious when I'm late."

That was when Goku attacked.

I did not see him move. My eyes, which can track tachyons through folded spacetime, registered nothing but a blur of silver light. Goku had transcended Ultra Instinct into a form that had no name, a state of being where his body moved in perfect, instantaneous harmony with the fabric of existence itself. His fist, charged with the combined ki of every Z-Fighter who had ever lived, every Genki Dama he had ever gathered, every transformation he had ever unlocked, struck Godric square in the center of his chest.

The impact produced a sound. It was a small sound. The kind of sound a raindrop makes when it hits a stone.

Goku's hand shattered. The bones of his fingers, his metacarpals, his wrist, his forearm—all of them pulverized in an instant, exploding outward in a spray of silver-tinged blood and marrow. The shockwave of his own attack, reflected back through his arm by the absolute, immovable solidity of Godric's chest, traveled upward through his shoulder and into his torso. His ribs cracked. His spine fractured. His internal organs liquefied. He coughed a geyser of blood—so much blood, a torrential, impossible volume—and collapsed backward, his body a broken, twitching ruin.

Godric looked down at him. His expression was one of mild, almost paternal disappointment. "I expected more," he said.

Vegeta screamed. The Prince of All Saiyans, his pride wounded beyond endurance, surged forward in a form that combined Ultra Ego, Hakai energy, and something older and darker—a technique he had learned from the erased universes of his own cosmology. His hands crackled with the purple-black fire of Destruction itself.

Godric did not move. He did not blink. He simply allowed Vegeta's attack to land. The Hakai flame washed over his face, his chest, his arms—a power that could erase anything from existence, that had been wielded by the Gods of Destruction themselves. It had no effect. Not a single cell of Godric's skin acknowledged the energy. The Hakai flame simply... stopped. It touched him and ceased to exist, as if the concept of "destruction" had encountered a being to whom it did not apply.

"Oh, that's a clever technique," Godric said, his voice carrying a note of genuine academic interest. "The energy attempts to unmake its target at the conceptual level. Very elegant. Pity." He shifted his gaze from Vegeta's face to the space just behind him, and the Prince of All Saiyans experienced the full, undiluted pressure of Godric's attention for one nanosecond. His body inverted. His skin turned inside out, his organs spilled through his mouth, his eyes burst from their sockets and were sucked back into his skull through the raw, bleeding holes where his ears had been. He died screaming, and the scream went on for several seconds after his lungs had been turned into a slurry that leaked through his ribcage.

The Coalition, to its credit, did not retreat. It attacked.

Superman Prime One Million came next, his heat vision—a beam of concentrated solar judgment that could bisect multiverses—lancing toward Godric's eyes. Godric turned his head a fraction of an inch, and the beam simply bent around his face and punched a hole through seventeen dimensional barriers, vaporizing a chained cluster of Tyranid Hive Fleets that had been waiting in the seventh flank. The Hive Mind's death-scream echoed across the psychic plane, a trillion minds extinguished in a single, accidental instant, and Godric didn't even notice.

Dr. Manhattan raised his hand, and reality flickered. He was attempting to unmake Godric at the quantum level, to scatter his atoms across the probability manifold, to ensure that he had never existed in the first place. Godric felt the tickle of probability manipulation against his absolute concealment and responded by existing slightly more firmly in the space Dr. Manhattan occupied. The blue demigod's form warped, twisted, and collapsed into a screaming knot of non-Euclidean flesh as Godric's mere ontological weight overrode his quantum coherence. "I see a puppet," Godric murmured, watching Dr. Manhattan's liquefying form, "who can see the strings but not the hand that pulls them. Boring."

The One Above All and the Presence attacked simultaneously, their divine authorities merging into a single command: DIE. It was not a physical strike. It was not an energy blast. It was the absolute, authorial decree of the supreme beings of their respective fictional strata, a word of power that had, in their own domains, never been denied. It touched Godric's existence and shattered like glass against a mountain. Godric did not even look at them. He simply allowed his passive ontological inertia to reject the command, and the rejection traveled backward along the conceptual link to its source. The One Above All screamed—a sound that ripped through the spiritual fabric of every Marvel universe—as his divine authority was stripped from him in an instant, his luminous white form collapsing into that of a frail, terrified old man who had just realized he was not, and had never been, supreme. The Presence fared worse. Godric's rejection did not merely strip his authority; it rewrote his fundamental definition. The Presence found himself transformed into a small, blind mole, burrowing frantically through the dimensional soil, his omniscience replaced with the dim, terrified consciousness of a creature that knew only darkness and the constant, crushing fear of the predator above.

"Next," Godric said. His voice was still conversational. He was still standing with his hands behind his back. He had not moved a single muscle below his neck.

Saitama stepped forward. The One Punch Man had watched his allies die, and his expression had not changed. He still looked bored. He still looked like a man who had never known a challenge. He walked toward Godric with his hands in the pockets of his yellow jumpsuit, his cape flapping behind him, and when he was close enough to touch, he threw a punch.

It was not a normal punch. Saitama's power, by his own narrative design, was the ability to defeat any opponent with a single blow. It was not strength—it was conclusion. The end of the fight, given physical form. He had never encountered an enemy who could survive it, because his narrative function was to always win.

His fist met Godric's abdomen. The resulting shockwave, I must record, was impressive. The dimensional perimeter beneath our feet cracked. The local multiverse shuddered. A decillion decillion decillion decillion decillion multiverses in the surrounding omniversal region were instantly and completely annihilated by the sheer, concentrated force of Saitama's punch. The void that remained was absolute—a perfect, silent nothing that stretched across a region of existence so vast that the human concept of "light-year" could not even begin to describe it. The Coalition forces that had been stationed in those multiverses—the Culture armada, the Time Lord Battle TARDISes, the Dalek fleet, the entire Xeelee Sequence—were erased from reality so completely that even the memory of their narrative strands was torn from the omniversal record.

Godric's tunic did not wrinkle. The skin beneath did not indent. The cells of his abdominal wall did not register the pressure as a meaningful event. Saitama's narrative function—the absolute, unassailable power to end any fight with one punch—collided with Godric's absolute, unassailable existence, and the narrative function lost. It did not bend. It did not adapt. It simply failed, because Godric's existence was not a fight that could be ended.

Saitama looked at his fist. Then he looked at Godric. His bored expression finally cracked, replaced by something I had never seen on his face in any of the fictional records I had studied. Confusion. And beneath it, the first, faint stirrings of existential dread.

"That... usually works," Saitama said.

Godric smiled down at him. "I know. I've seen your show. It's very entertaining." He reached out—the first voluntary movement he had made—and gently tapped Saitama on the forehead with his index finger. The tap was not forceful. It was the kind of tap a grandfather might give a child who had told a particularly amusing joke.

Saitama's body experienced the full, undiluted transfer of Godric's physical contact. Every cell, every atom, every quark of his existence was instantly and permanently separated from every other. He did not explode. He did not vaporize. He simply came apart, his form unraveling into a fine mist of subatomic particles that scattered across the dimensional void like dandelion seeds in a hurricane. His consciousness, the mind of the man who could defeat anyone with one punch, was stretched across the infinite expanse of that scatter, experiencing the agony of being a trillion trillion separate, non-sentient particles for the brief, eternal instant before those particles decayed into nothing.

"No," Godric murmured, withdrawing his finger. "That one was satisfying."

The Coalition, witnessing the death of its ultimate weapon, did what any desperate, bloodlusted army would do. It threw everything it had, all at once.

Azathoth, the Blind Idiot God, opened its nuclear chaos at the center of reality and unleashed the piping of the mindless flutes that surrounded its dreaming throne. The sound was the unmaking of all things, the dissolution of existence into the primordial chaos from which it had emerged. Godric heard the piping, and for one brief, curious instant, he allowed himself to listen to the melody. Then he grew bored of it. He shifted his attention toward Azathoth, and the Blind Idiot God—whose dreams sustained the entire Cthulhu Mythos—found itself suddenly, violently awake. Awake, and aware, and staring into the face of a being whose existence rendered its own cosmic scale a laughable, pitiable speck. Azathoth screamed, and the scream unmade the entirety of its own fictional stratum. The Outer Gods, the Great Old Ones, the crawling chaos and the dreaming cities—all of them collapsed into Azathoth's waking maw, a billion billion eldritch existences devoured by their own creator's sudden, horrified consciousness. And then Azathoth itself, unable to bear the weight of awareness in the presence of Godric, willed itself into non-existence, its amorphous mass folding inward and vanishing with a wet, sickening pop.

The Living Tribunal, his three faces contorted with the realization that the judgment he was about to deliver would be his last, raised his golden hand to pronounce sentence. Godric glanced at him, and the Tribunal's three faces began to argue with each other. The argument became a scream. The scream became a physical, violent separation as the three faces tore themselves from the single head, each one sprouting its own body, its own limbs, its own independent consciousness. The three new Tribunals, now freed from the tyranny of consensus, immediately turned on each other. They clawed, they bit, they tore at their own golden flesh, and within seconds, the supreme judge of the Marvel multiverse had reduced itself to three bleeding, dying figures locked in an eternal, self-destructive combat that would continue until the last spark of their divine authority guttered into ash.

The Downstreamers, those post-human engineers who had survived the heat death of one universe and reshaped the manifold of all possible realities, attempted to rewrite the fundamental mathematics of the local dimensional space to exclude Godric's existence. Their equations, elegant and vast, touched the perimeter of Godric's absolute concealment and rebounded with a force that multiplied their intended effect by an infinity of infinities. The Downstreamers' own existence was mathematically excluded from reality. They did not die. They were retroactively erased, their entire post-human history unwritten, their manifold collapsing into a single, sterile, lifeless universe where the spark of consciousness never ignited.

The Q Continuum, that species of omnipotent tricksters, attempted to weaponize paradox. They created a loop of self-negation designed to trap Godric in an eternal recursion of non-existence. Godric noticed the loop, found it mildly interesting, and then continued to exist. The Q, unable to process the failure of their absolute power, descended into a collective madness that manifested as a physical, trans-dimensional seizure. Their forms flickered, warped, and exploded into showers of incoherent probability, each Q dying in a state of uncomprehending, omnipotent terror.

The Five-World War factions—the SCP Foundation, their reality anchors burning white-hot, their Thaumiel-class entities unleashed in a desperate, last-ditch assault—tried to contain Godric within a narrative construct of their own design. They wove a story around him, a tale of his defeat and imprisonment, and attempted to overwrite his reality with their fiction. Godric, whose existence was the foundation upon which all narratives rested, found the attempt so charmingly misguided that he allowed the Foundation's story to play out in a localized pocket dimension. The Foundation personnel watched, their faces crumbling into despair, as their narrative Godric stood up, walked out of their fictional prison, and then stepped through the dimensional wall into the "real" space where they stood. He winked at the lead researcher—a woman named Dr. Vang, whose eyes were streaming with tears of absolute, soul-shattering futility—and then he collapsed the pocket dimension around them. The Foundation's entire multiversal apparatus, every Site, every anomaly, every D-Class and O5, was compressed into a singularity the size of a period at the end of a sentence. Godric flicked the singularity away with a thought, and it vanished into a realm of eternal, silent darkness.

I must now record what happened to the armies. The individual champions—the ones I have named and the countless billions I have not—were each dispatched in their own, uniquely horrifying way. But the bulk of the Coalition, the infinite tides of fictional warriors, did not warrant individual attention. Godric had grown tired of the game. He did not say this aloud, but I could feel it, the subtle shift in his posture, the fractional tightening of the skin around his eyes.

"Rutger," he said, still not looking at me, "I think I've had enough entertainment for one afternoon. Let's wrap this up."

He lowered his chin. Just a fraction of an inch. Just enough to bring his gaze into alignment with the central mass of the remaining Coalition forces.

And then he exhaled.

The breath left his lungs and crossed the dimensional void, and everything it touched died. Not in the clean, clinical sense of death as cessation. This was an agonizing, protracted, incomprehensibly cruel extinction. The breath—a single, casual exhalation from a being who did not even need to breathe—carried with it the passive, ambient weight of Godric's existence, the same weight that he spent every moment of eternity suppressing. It was not an attack. It was not a weapon. It was simply the air from his lungs, now saturated with the metaphysical pressure of a being whose stats had been infinitely amplifying every Planck fragment for over a million years.

The breath touched the armies. Trillions of fictional beings—heroes and villains, gods and mortals, the indomitable and the hopeless—felt their bodies begin to dissolve. Not from the outside in, but from the inside out. The breath entered their lungs, or whatever analogs they possessed, and it began to eat them. Not biologically—conceptually. It consumed their narrative strands, the fundamental threads of imagination that had woven them into existence. It unraveled their stories backward, erasing their origins, their triumphs, their defeats, their very identities. They watched their own histories vanish from their minds as their bodies melted into screaming, formless slurry. The armies of anime, their eyes wide with the horror of realizing their plot armor was meaningless, collapsed into puddles of ink that wept with the voices of their voice actors. The legions of video game protagonists experienced every single death animation simultaneously, their bodies cycling through every possible failure state in an infinite, recursive loop of pain. The mythological pantheons—Zeus and Odin and Ra and every thunder-god who had ever been worshipped—felt their divine domains abandon them, their worshippers across a billion fictional Earths suddenly forgetting their names, their temples crumbling, their very godhood revoked by the simple, undeniable fact of Godric's existence.

The breath spread across the entire Coalition front, a silent, invisible wave of extinction. A googolplexian of googolplexian fictional multiverses were scoured clean of life in the time it would take a mortal man to blink. The Gundam pilots, still screaming, watched their mobile suits rust and crumble around them. The Jedi and Sith alike felt the Force itself recoil from Godric's presence, abandoning them as they were crushed by the collapsing bulkheads of their Star Destroyers. Every Dragon Ball character who had not yet attacked—and there were hundreds of millions of them, drawn from every alternate timeline, every non-canonical spin-off—found their ki inverted, their life energy turned into a corrosive, self-devouring fire that burned them from the inside out. The screaming of the Saiyans, the Namekians, the gods and the angels and the Zen-Oh himself—all of it merged into a single, continuous, agonized shriek that echoed across the dimensional void.

The Warhammer 40,000 factions—the Imperium, the Chaos Gods, the Tyranids, the Necrons, the Orks—all of them, every single grimdark warrior in that eternal, hopeless galaxy, experienced the one thing their universe had never truly given them: absolute, undeniable, irreversible death. Khorne, the Blood God, felt his skull throne crumble beneath him as the concept of blood was casually revoked from his domain. Nurgle, the Plague Lord, watched his garden of pestilence wither and die as the very idea of disease was overwritten by the passive health of Godric's immune system. Tzeentch's schemes unraveled in a cascade of paradox; Slaanesh's pleasures inverted into eternal, unending agony. The Chaos Gods screamed, and their screams were the most satisfying thing I have ever heard, and I hate myself for that satisfaction.

The entire army—every single fictional being, from the highest omnipotent to the lowliest background character—died in the space of Godric's single exhalation.

When the breath had passed, there was nothing left. The dimensional void was silent. The armies were gone. The champions were gone. The gods and the monsters and the indomitable heroes were gone. The Coalition, the greatest force that imagination had ever assembled, had been reduced to a faint, fading stain of narrative residue that would dissipate entirely within the next few minutes.

Godric straightened his posture. He rolled his shoulders, a small, casual gesture, and then he turned to me with the warm, friendly smile of a man who had just finished a particularly pleasant walk.

"Well," he said, "that was a nice little diversion. The spiky-haired one was enthusiastic, I'll give him that. And Saitama—you know, I genuinely thought he might make me feel something. Pity."

I could not speak. My throat was closed. My hands were shaking so badly that my quill, which I had somehow continued to hold throughout the battle, was tearing holes in my own palm. I stared at the empty void where imagination had died.

Godric noticed my state and placed a gentle hand on my shoulder. "I know it's overwhelming, Rutger," he said. "The first time always is. But they chose to attack. I didn't force them. I didn't seek them out. They gathered themselves, they armed themselves, and they tried their very best to kill me. I simply... existed in response." He squeezed my shoulder, a warm, fatherly pressure. "Now. I believe we have a garden party to attend. Mirael has been working on a new fragrance for the Glimmerblooms, and I promised I would be there. Would you like to come?"

I nodded, because I am his loyal court wizard, and because there is no other answer.

We returned to the Garden of Eternal Morning, and the afternoon light was as perfect as ever, and the Glimmerblooms were weeping their silver tears, and Mirael bowed low as we approached, and Eryndor poured the tea with his eternal, trembling hands, and everything was exactly as it had been before the death of fiction. The Coalition was never mentioned again. I do not think Godric even remembers their names.

I remember. I will always remember. I remember Goku's hand shattering like glass. I remember Vegeta turning inside out. I remember Saitama's body scattering like seeds. I remember the Chaos Gods screaming. I remember the breath, and the silence that followed, and the smile on Godric's face as he turned to me and said, "That was a nice little diversion."

I am Rutger of the Blood of Blake. I am thirty-nine years old, and I have served the Immortal King for nineteen years, and I will serve him until I die, and I will love him, and I will fear him, and I will never, ever, for a single instant, forget what I saw.

There is no opposition. There never was. There never will be.

And the afternoon light is perfect, and the tea is warm, and the King is smiling, and I am screaming, silently, forever.
