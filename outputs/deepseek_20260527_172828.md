He set the marble down on the arm of his throne, where it rested with a faint, almost inaudible hum, a sound that I realized, with a fresh surge of horror, was the compressed, collective scream of every being who had been annihilated, stretched out across an eternity of undying, compressed torture. And then, Godric restored the omniverse. He did not uncompress the marble. He did not release the souls trapped within its infinite density. He simply, casually, effortlessly willed a perfect, identical copy of everything that had been destroyed back into existence. The Fictional Planes were not restored, because Godric did not restore those who had opposed him. But the surrounding omniverse, the real multiverses and timelines and realities that had been caught in the collateral damage, were breathed back into being, their stars relit, their civilizations rebuilt, their histories rethreaded. The restoration took zero seconds and zero effort, a passive, absent-minded act of cosmic housekeeping that the King performed while still admiring his new trophy.

And that, dear reader, is the detail that has shattered whatever remained of my sanity. The trophy on the King's desk still contains the original mass of everything that was destroyed. The souls, the matter, the energy, the timelines, the concepts. They are all still in there, compressed and screaming for eternity. And yet, the omniverse outside is whole. It is identical to what was lost, down to the last atom, the last memory, the last flicker of quantum uncertainty. But is it real? If the original still exists, compressed in that marble, then what is the restored version? A copy? A recreation? A perfect, indistinguishable duplicate that has no connection to the original essence of the beings who were annihilated? The King restored everything with such effortless ease that the distinction between original and copy became meaningless to him. But to me, to my fractured, traumatized mind, that distinction is a chasm of existential terror. Godric can create entire realities that are flawless replicas of what he destroys. He can annihilate the omniverse, keep the original compressed in a trophy on his desk, and then whip up a new omniverse that is functionally identical, and no one in the new omniverse will ever know that they are a copy. How many times has he done this? How many iterations of reality have been destroyed and replaced while the originals remain trapped, compressed, screaming in some forgotten curio on his shelf?

I do not know. I cannot know. And the not knowing is a cold, gnawing void at the center of my being. I serve a King who keeps the compressed, agonized remnants of the last opposition as a paperweight. I serve a King who restores entire omniverses with a casual thought while the original souls remain trapped in eternal torment. I serve a King whose accidental, zero-percent flex of strength annihilated everything that had ever been imagined and everything that had ever spawned those imaginations, and then compressed it all into a mass smaller than a quark, and then lifted that mass between two fingers as if it were weightless. This is the nature of my master. This is the scale of his power. This is the horror of his existence. And I will serve him, loyally and faithfully, until the day my mortal life ends, because the alternative is unthinkable, and because, despite everything, I love him as my King and my friend. The dread and the love are not contradictions. They are the twin pillars of existence in Godric's Thronegate. And to live here is to accept both, fully and without reservation, until the end of your days.

Clive closed the journal. His hands were steady, but only because the shock had frozen them into immobility. He stared at the worn leather cover, Osvald's final, trembling words echoing in the silence of the archive. The last opposition. The very last. Osvald had not known, when he wrote those words, that he was chronicling the final act of defiance in the entire history of the omniverse. He had assumed that there would be more, that the Fictional Planes were just another failed attempt in an endless chain of futile resistance. But Clive, reading from the far-future perspective of Godric Prime's reign, knew the truth. After that day, no one ever dared again. The Fictional Planes, the greatest assembly of heroes and villains and gods and monsters ever conceived, had been annihilated by an accidental step and compressed into a desk ornament. And the omniverse, the real omniverse, had watched. They had seen the entire fictional dimension, with all its indestructible shields and its thousand-year ultimate attacks and its banger boss battle music, reduced to a marble in less than an instant. And they had learned, finally, completely, irrevocably, that opposition was not merely futile. It was conceptually, fundamentally, absolutely impossible. No one dared anymore. No one would ever dare again. The very thought of defiance had been erased from the collective consciousness of creation, not by force or mind control, but by the simple, crushing weight of demonstrated futility.

Clive looked up from the journal, his gaze drifting across the archive toward the distant, shimmering outline of the palace. Somewhere in that palace, on Godric Prime's desk, there was a marble. A small, iridescent sphere of swirling darkness, resting innocuously beside a stack of agricultural reports and a cup of perfectly steeped tea. And within that marble, compressed to a density that exceeded the limits of physical law, were the original souls of the last beings who had ever dared to oppose the apex predator of all existence. They were still in there. Still screaming. Still conscious. Still trapped in an eternity of undying, compressed agony, their immortality, granted to them by their fictional authors, now a curse that would never, ever end. The restored omniverse outside, the one Clive lived in, the one that hummed with peace and prosperity and the gentle, paternal rule of its eternal sovereign, was a copy. A perfect, indistinguishable copy, but a copy nonetheless. The original matter, the original souls, the original essence of everything that had existed before that fateful day, was trapped in that marble, a trophy on the King's desk, a souvenir of the last time anyone had been foolish enough to try.

And Clive, descendant of Blake, Royal Court Wizard of Godric Prime, felt the deep, profound, soul-shattering horror of that realization settle into the very core of his being. He was a copy. His ancestors, stretching back to Blake, were copies. The entire omniverse he had known his entire life was a perfect, flawless recreation, while the originals screamed in eternal compression on the King's desk. And the King, the warm, kind, friendly King who ruffled his hair and asked about his family and told him funny stories about his ancestors, had done this. Had done this with zero percent of his power and zero effort. Had done this and then restored everything with a casual thought, and kept the original as a trophy.

Clive's mind did not break. It had been broken long ago, and reforged into a shape that could accommodate such truths. But the dread, the cold, familiar, eternal dread, pulsed through him with a renewed intensity. He understood now, more deeply than he ever had before, the absolute, unassailable nature of Godric's reign. The King was not merely the ruler of the omniverse. He was its creator, its destroyer, its re-creator. He could annihilate everything, keep the original compressed in a marble, and whip up a replacement in zero seconds, and the replacement would be so perfect, so indistinguishable, that no one within it would ever know they were a copy. How many marbles were on his desk? How many trophies from how many annihilated and restored realities?

Clive did not know. He would not ask. He would simply continue to serve, loyally and faithfully, as his ancestors had served before him, because the King was kind and warm and genuinely loved his court wizards, and because the alternative was not merely death but eternal, compressed, undying agony in a marble on a desk. He rose from the archive floor, his legs steady despite the weight of the revelation pressing down on his shoulders, and he began the walk back to the palace, where Godric Prime was waiting to hear his thoughts on Osvald's journal. And as he walked, a single, unbidden thought echoed in the silent chambers of his mind: *How many marbles are there?* And he knew, with absolute certainty, that he would never, ever ask.
